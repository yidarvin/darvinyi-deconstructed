# Eliot Porter — source shortlist

Eliot Porter made color nature photography into an inquiry into how a frame can hold dense, small-scale life: bark and water become graphic fields, a bird becomes a measured accent, and a canyon becomes a sequence of enclosing planes. This twelve-work set spans the woodland, bird, river, and conservation landscapes central to that achievement. Ten files are direct, public Art Institute of Chicago IIIF display renditions; two are public exhibition renditions from the J. Paul Getty Museum’s *Eliot Porter: In the Realm of Nature*. All were retrieved directly and verified as opening JPEGs.

**Rights basis:** **fair-use** for every selection. ## Research context

The Getty exhibition describes Porter as a photographer who bridged fine art and the sciences, championed color photography from the 1940s into the mid-1970s, and used his photographs as a visual argument for conservation: https://www.getty.edu/art/exhibitions/porter/. Its exhibition objects supply the early *East Penobscot Bay* and the bird photograph as direct historical counterpoints to the later dye-transfer landscapes. The Art Institute records provide the other ten authoritative object pages, object credits, and publicly served display renditions. The resulting sequence keeps Porter’s core concerns—season, habitat, vertical woodland density, close color, bird life, and canyon scale—available for close compositional comparison without presenting the source files as print substitutes.
