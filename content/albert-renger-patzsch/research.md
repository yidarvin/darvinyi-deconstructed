# Albert Renger-Patzsch — research

Scope: technique, era, printing, and recorded camera information for the ten
selected works. The collection record is clear about the objects being
reproduced, but not about the original capture settings; this note keeps those
two kinds of evidence separate.

## Era and approach

The selection spans the early 1920s to the mid-1940s: *Throat of an Orchid*
(1922/23), *Path in the Vineyards near Eller on the Moselle River* (1925),
*Slag Heaps in an Iron Work* (c. 1927), *Blades of Grass in Snow* (1927/28),
the self-portrait-like *Untitled* (1928/29), the c. 1930 fungi study, the
1930s forest picture, *Country Road in Snowstorm* (c. 1936), and *Oak Preserve
in Wamel* (1945/46). That range is broad enough to test a consistent visual
method across plant, reflective object, working landscape, industry, road, and
river scene. The project registry identifies *Die Welt ist schön* (1928) and
New Objectivity as the relevant historical context; the AIC source record
documents the individual dates and titles used here. [Source notes](sources.md)

## Technique and printing

Every selected AIC object is catalogued as a gelatin silver print. The AIC
records distinguish the historical photograph date from the particular print
when they can: *Beech Forest in Fall* is from the 1930s and printed c. 1960;
*Slag Heaps in an Iron Work* is c. 1927 and printed c. 1955–65. The remaining
records give the work dates listed above without establishing a common printing
date or support. The chapter therefore names gelatin silver prints while not
treating the ten reproductions as one uniform set of first-generation prints.

The visible method is sharp selection and controlled framing: trunks become a
screen, rails and rows become directional edges, and a flower or reflective
surface becomes a compact arrangement of symmetry and curve. Those are
composition readings from the images, not evidence of a particular lens,
negative format, or development recipe.

## Camera settings

No AIC record in the local source set gives a verified camera model, lens,
focal length, aperture, shutter speed, film stock, plate stock, sensitivity, or
exposure for these pictures. The ingested manifest contains no retained EXIF
fields, and the source files are modern collection reproductions rather than
capture records. Specific settings are therefore not inferred from the visible
sharpness, depth of field, blur, or tonal range.
