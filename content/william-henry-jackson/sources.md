# William Henry Jackson — sources

Jackson's 1871–72 Hayden Survey photographs gave the Yellowstone landscape a public visual form at the moment Congress was deciding whether to preserve it. The pictures make geology readable through scale, figure-like trees, rivers, and deep plunging viewpoints: terraces become layered architecture, canyon walls become nested bands, and falls become vertical force fields. All ten files below were downloaded as the Library of Congress's master TIFFs from the Prints & Photographs collection and converted directly to high-quality JPEGs for the local raw workspace. They are public domain in the United States as nineteenth-century published photographs (all pre-1929); LOC serves the scans without a rights restriction.

- **Why it matters:** The falls plunge through a tall vertical composition, while the distant canyon and tiny trees turn depth and geological scale into the picture's subject.
