# Roger Fenton — research

Scope: technique, era, printing, and recorded camera settings for the eleven
plates in this chapter. This is wet-plate collodion work from 1855 with no
per-frame exposure record; where a number is not documented, this says so.

## Who and when

Roger Fenton (28 March 1819 – 8 August 1869) trained first for the law and
then for painting, studying in Paris in the early 1840s (accounts place him
in the studio circle around Paul Delaroche, the same milieu that produced
several of the first serious photographic converts among French painters).
He took up the camera in earnest after seeing photography's showing at the
Great Exhibition of 1851, travelled to Paris to learn the wet-collodion
process at first hand, and by 1853 was a founding and leading figure of the
Photographic Society (later the Royal Photographic Society), serving as its
first honorary secretary. Through the mid-1850s he photographed cathedrals,
landscapes, the royal family (with Prince Albert's encouragement), and
Russia, establishing himself as one of Britain's most technically accomplished
photographers before he was ever a war photographer.

In February 1855 the print publisher Thomas Agnew & Sons sent him to the
Crimea to photograph the war for the British public, with backing that
reportedly included Prince Albert's interest in countering the grim written
dispatches then coming from the front (notably William Howard Russell's for
The Times). Fenton was in the Crimea from 8 March to 26 June 1855 — barely
four months — working from a horse-drawn former wine merchant's van
converted into a mobile darkroom, with a young assistant, Marcus Sparling,
who appears seated on the van itself in image 03. In that time he exposed
roughly 360 collodion glass plates covering officers, ordinary soldiers,
camps, terrain, and the aftermath of siege works, but — by choice or by the
limits of his contract and his equipment — no actual combat and none of the
war's dead or wounded. He fell ill (a form of cholera then common at the
front) and broke ribs in a fall before returning to England in June 1855.
Agnew & Sons published and sold the prints from autumn 1855; the show was a
critical success but a commercial disappointment. Fenton continued
photographing landscapes, still life, and orientalist studio tableaux for a
few more years, but gave up the medium entirely in 1862 — partly discouraged
by the trade's economics and partly, by his own account, by the passage of
that year's Fine Arts Copyright Act — selling his equipment and returning to
practicing law until his death in 1869.

## Technique

This is wet-plate (wet collodion) glass-negative photography, the dominant
process of the 1850s onward and the only practical option for fieldwork of
this kind at the time. A glass plate was coated with collodion carrying
iodide and bromide salts, sensitized immediately before use in a bath of
silver nitrate, exposed while still wet, and developed within minutes of
exposure — which is why Fenton's van was not just transport but a complete
portable darkroom: coating, sensitizing, exposing, and developing all had to
happen at the same location, in that order, before the plate dried. Every
exposure ran to several seconds at minimum on a stand camera; there was no
possibility of catching motion, which is why group scenes read as static
friezes — officers, horses, and units arranged and held in place for the
plate rather than caught in the middle of anything. Fenton worked with
large-format cameras producing glass negatives on the order of whole-plate
size, leveled and set back to hold deep, wide fields of camp and terrain in
sharp focus (04, 08, 10), and used the same stand-camera discipline for
posed studio-style portraits in the field (02, 05, 06, 07) as for landscape.
The single vanishing-point corridor of image 01, The Valley of the Shadow of
Death, is a deliberate composition on this same static, tripod-mounted
apparatus, not an incidental framing.

## Printing

The exposed glass negatives were contact-printed — enlargers did not yet
exist — onto salted paper or albumen paper, using paper coated with
light-sensitive silver salts and printed out by direct sunlight exposure
through the negative, then fixed, toned, and mounted. Agnew & Sons issued
selections from the resulting prints as a commercial portfolio from late
1855. The Library of Congress holds 263 of Fenton's salted-paper and
albumen prints from the Crimean campaign, acquired in 1944 from Fenton's
grandniece; this set — including the master files for all eleven images in
this chapter — is unmounted and appears to preserve Fenton's or Agnew's own
sequencing rather than the standard mounted presentation Agnew sold at
retail. The Library of Congress carries its own rights statement of "no
known restrictions on publication" for the collection. Fenton's original
glass negatives, where they survive, are held across several institutions,
including the Royal Collection and the National Media Museum's collections
now under the Victoria and Albert Museum; this chapter's source files are
the full-resolution LOC print scans, not negative scans.

## Camera settings

Not recorded. Wet-plate collodion photography of this era predates any
per-frame aperture, shutter-speed, or sensitivity log; there was no light
meter, and an operator judged exposure by experience with a given plate's
fresh sensitivity, the available light, and the lens at hand. What is
documented is qualitative only: collodion plates of the period were slow,
so a stand-camera exposure in the field — whether a static group portrait
(02, 05, 06, 07) or an open landscape or camp view (01, 03, 04, 08, 09, 10,
11) — required several seconds to perhaps tens of seconds with the lens
stopped down, consistent with the deep, evenly held focus and the complete
absence of motion or blur across every plate in this set, including men and
horses posed to hold still. No specific f-stop, shutter speed, or
sensitivity value survives for any of the eleven images, and none is stated
here.
