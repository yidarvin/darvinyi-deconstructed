# Roger Fenton — sources

Fenton spent barely four months in the Crimea (March–June 1855), commissioned by the print publisher Thomas Agnew & Sons and photographing under the wet-collodion process from a horse-drawn van that doubled as his darkroom. Every exposure ran to several seconds and required coating, exposing, and developing a glass plate on the spot — there is no decisive-moment aesthetic here, only deliberate arrangement. Group portraits are built as shallow, static friezes (officers and their horses lined up as if for inspection); camps and terrain are recorded as flat, layered bands receding to a horizon; and his single most famous image collapses an entire battlefield into one perspectival corridor — a sunken road narrowing to a vanishing point, its verges scattered with round shot. Because nothing could be caught mid-motion, Fenton's compositions read as staged tableaux even when the subject is candid camp life, making this body of work a clean case study in pre-photojournalistic, wholly considered picture-making — frontal groupings, single-point recession, and panoramic horizontal banding rather than any spontaneous geometry.

**Rights basis:** Public domain. All eleven images below were made in 1855 by a photographer who died in 1869 (UK copyright, life+70, expired 1939), and were published in Britain that same year — long out of copyright under any jurisdiction. They are drawn from the Library of Congress's Fenton Crimean War Photographs collection (263 salted-paper and albumen prints acquired from Fenton's grandniece in 1944), each item carrying LOC's own rights statement of "no known restrictions on publication." Files were pulled as the full-resolution master TIFFs served directly from tile.loc.gov and converted losslessly to JPEG.

## Shortlist

### 01 — The Valley of the Shadow of Death (1855)
- **Why it matters:** The defining image of the set and arguably of 19th-century war photography — a sunken road plunges to a single vanishing point between bare hills, round shot scattered across the full width of the road, the perspectival corridor itself doing all compositional work.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2001698869/
- **Rights:** Public domain (LOC Fenton Crimean War Photographs collection; no known restrictions)
- **File:** raw/roger-fenton/01-valley-of-shadow.jpg (6782px)

### 02 — Council of War held at Lord Raglan's Head Quarters (1855)
- **Why it matters:** Lord Raglan, Maréchal Pélissier, and Omar Pasha seated around a small map table the morning before the Mamelon assault — a loose triangular grouping of the three Allied commanders-in-chief, uniform dress against Raglan's civilian coat marking rank without a word of caption.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2001697658/
- **Rights:** Public domain (LOC Fenton Crimean War Photographs collection; no known restrictions)
- **File:** raw/roger-fenton/02-council-of-war.jpg (4672px)

### 03 — The Artist's Van (1855)
- **Why it matters:** Fenton's own horse-drawn photographic van/darkroom, assistant Marcus Sparling perched on the driver's seat — a self-referential frame in which the apparatus of the whole project becomes the frontal subject, centered and squared to the camera like any other sitter.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2001697111/
- **Rights:** Public domain (LOC Fenton Crimean War Photographs collection; no known restrictions)
- **File:** raw/roger-fenton/03-artists-van.jpg (4858px)

### 04 — Balaklava, from Guard's Hill (1855)
- **Why it matters:** A high vantage compresses the harbor town into stacked horizontal bands — foreground tents, the crowded anchorage, the ruined hilltop fortress beyond — the topographic panorama Fenton used repeatedly to document terrain rather than incident.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2001697672/
- **Rights:** Public domain (LOC Fenton Crimean War Photographs collection; no known restrictions)
- **File:** raw/roger-fenton/04-balaklava-guards-hill.jpg (4657px)

### 05 — Vivandière (1855)
- **Why it matters:** A full-length studio-style portrait of a French cantinière in Zouave dress, a soldier planted at the frame's far edge for scale and counterweight — genre performance staged with the same frontal formality Fenton gave his officers.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2001698819/
- **Rights:** Public domain (LOC Fenton Crimean War Photographs collection; no known restrictions)
- **File:** raw/roger-fenton/05-vivandiere.jpg (4512px)

### 06 — A Zouave (1855)
- **Why it matters:** A single Zouave soldier posed against a plain ground, costume and bearing doing the compositional work — the studio-portrait counterpart to Fenton's camp and landscape work, isolating a figure the way his group shots isolate a unit.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2001697657/
- **Rights:** Public domain (LOC Fenton Crimean War Photographs collection; no known restrictions)
- **File:** raw/roger-fenton/06-a-zouave.jpg (4664px)

### 07 — Field Marshal Lord Raglan (1855)
- **Why it matters:** The British commander-in-chief seated informally in a doorway, the frame's dark architectural opening acting as a portrait mount around him — a threshold composition rather than a studio backdrop, taken weeks before his death.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2002695364/
- **Rights:** Public domain (LOC Fenton Crimean War Photographs collection; no known restrictions)
- **File:** raw/roger-fenton/07-lord-raglan.jpg (4503px)

### 08 — Camp of the 5th Dragoon Guards, Looking Towards Kadikoi (1855)
- **Why it matters:** Tents and picketed horses spread in a flat foreground frieze with the valley and hills receding behind — the camp-as-typology view Fenton used across the Crimean plateau to record encampments as pure horizontal terrain.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2001697682/
- **Rights:** Public domain (LOC Fenton Crimean War Photographs collection; no known restrictions)
- **File:** raw/roger-fenton/08-camp-5th-dragoon-guards.jpg (4632px)

### 09 — The Tombs of the Generals on Cathcart's Hill (1855)
- **Why it matters:** A cemetery of officer graves with a single mourning figure planted among the markers for scale — the war's cost rendered through still, ordered geometry rather than any depiction of combat.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2001697093/
- **Rights:** Public domain (LOC Fenton Crimean War Photographs collection; no known restrictions)
- **File:** raw/roger-fenton/09-cathcarts-hill-tombs.jpg (4735px)

### 10 — Balaklava Harbour, the Cattle Pier (1855)
- **Why it matters:** Dockside structures, penned livestock, and moored ships arranged in overlapping geometric planes — the logistics of the siege recorded with the same frontal, deadpan clarity Fenton gave his portraits.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2001696137/
- **Rights:** Public domain (LOC Fenton Crimean War Photographs collection; no known restrictions)
- **File:** raw/roger-fenton/10-balaklava-cattle-pier.jpg (4637px)

### 11 — Cooking House, 8th Hussars (1855)
- **Why it matters:** Soldiers and a cook grouped loosely around cooking pots with Fenton's own van visible at the frame's edge — an informal genre scene that still resolves into a balanced cluster, the survivors of the Light Brigade charge photographed at their most mundane.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2001700318/
- **Rights:** Public domain (LOC Fenton Crimean War Photographs collection; no known restrictions)
- **File:** raw/roger-fenton/11-cookhouse-8th-hussars.jpg (4531px)
