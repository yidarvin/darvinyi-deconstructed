Étienne-Jules Marey makes time visible without dividing it into a row of separate photographs. In these chronophotographs, successive phases coexist on the same dark field. The overlay therefore treats arcs, rows, and repeated contours as the composition's real structure. It does not repeat the analyzer's strong edge detections when those are simply print borders, page divisions, or the apparatus of the experiment.

## a body becomes a diagram

In *Man Wearing a Black Suit Marked with White Lines*, the overlay follows the marked upper arm and legs, not the background horizon that the analyzer proposes at y0.123. The subject is a standing figure, but its white joint marks are already a graphic shorthand for posture and balance. The plate's mean tone of 0.48 and broad 0.25 tonal deviation keep that body from dissolving entirely into the gray field.

<Plate slug="etienne-jules-marey" id="01-man-black-suit" label="Man Wearing a Black Suit Marked with White Lines, 1883" caption="The marked limbs isolate posture and weight from the ordinary photographic background." />

*Pole Vault* is the clearest statement of Marey's method. The rising and descending positions form one large arc around the fixed vault standard. The analyzer measures a high edge density of 0.129, appropriate to the repeated bodies and poles; the overlay makes that visual density legible as a single trajectory rather than a collection of unrelated diagonals.

<Plate slug="etienne-jules-marey" id="02-pole-vault" label="Pole Vault, 1890" caption="Repeated positions form a continuous rise and fall around the fixed vault standard." />

## flight as rhythm

The two bird studies exchange athletic space for a nearly black field. In the Flying Heron, repeated pale birds cross a horizontal photographic field in a stable flight path, with a second line describing the changing wingbeat. Its warm dark palette, from #2b2a26 to #e3dbcf, and low 0.019 edge density make each pale bird register as a pulse rather than an isolated portrait.

<Plate slug="etienne-jules-marey" id="03-flying-heron" label="Chronophotograph of a Flying Heron, c. 1883–87" caption="The repeated birds make a measured wingbeat rhythm across the dark photographic field." />

The compact *Bird in Flight* uses the same principle at a much tighter scale. Upper and lower wing rhythms bracket the successive bright forms. Its tonal spread is exceptionally narrow, 0.076, so the small variations in the birds' contours do almost all the compositional work.

<Plate slug="etienne-jules-marey" id="04-bird-in-flight" label="Bird in Flight, 1886" caption="Upper and lower wing rhythms turn the strip into a chain of bright flight contours." />

## rotation in rows

The falling-cat material makes the photographic sequence explicit. In the compact sequence, a central run of cat positions is bracketed by upper and lower rows of related phases. Its 0.289 edge density is the highest of this set because every phase supplies another small contour. The longer printed study page then places several sequences one above another. The overlay marks the page as an object and distinguishes the top and central rows, rather than treating the white gutters and caption strip as a landscape.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Draw one motion, not every frame">
Record a repeated action, then select one arc or axis that makes the action readable. If the line does not explain the change from one position to the next, remove it.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Use a dark field">
Place a moving pale object or marker against a simple dark background. Test whether the repeated forms read as one rhythm before adding any extra visual information.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Treat a printed sequence as an object">
Photograph or scan a page of images. Identify the rows, panels, and gutters first; a strong detected border is often part of the page design, not a feature inside the depicted scene.
</ExerciseCard>
