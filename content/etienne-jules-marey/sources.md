# Étienne-Jules Marey — sources

Marey used chronophotography not to break motion into separate pictures, as Muybridge did, but to accumulate successive phases within one image. The body often disappears into a black ground; reflective joints, pale clothing, a wing, a pole, or a rod remains as a trace of force through time. This compact surviving-open-access set is enough to establish that compositional language: gridded measurement gives way to superimposed arc, wave, and repeated silhouette. Four clean, high-resolution institutional or public-domain downloads are available; six further canonical works are documented in `NEEDED.md` because their openly available scans fall below the 1200px working floor or do not provide a clean source file.

## Shortlist

### 01 — *Man Wearing a Black Suit Marked with White Lines* (1883)
- **Why it matters:** The dark body is erased against the dark ground so only the white joint-lines remain—a skeletal drawing made by motion rather than a conventional portrait.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Mann_tr%C3%A4gt_f%C3%BCr_ein_Experiment_einen_schwarzen_Anzug,_der_wei%C3%9Fe_Linien_entlang_der_Glieder_hat_1883.jpg
- **Rights:** Public domain (Marey d. 1904; 1883 work).
- **File:** raw/etienne-jules-marey/01-man-black-suit.jpg (1469px long edge)

### 02 — *Pole Vault* (1890)
- **Why it matters:** A single plate records the athlete's changing body around the pole, a chain of diagonals that turns athletic motion into a continuous geometric arc.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Stangenhochsprung_1890.jpg
- **Rights:** Public domain (Marey d. 1904; 1890 work).
- **File:** raw/etienne-jules-marey/02-pole-vault.jpg (2048px long edge)

### 03 — *Chronophotograph of a Flying Heron* (c. 1883–1887)
- **Why it matters:** The heron's wingbeat is accumulated across a long horizontal plate, its pale repeated form becoming both subject and a visual graph of flight.
- **Source:** https://clevelandart.org/art/2009.349 (Cleveland Museum of Art; open-access download endpoint)
- **Rights:** CC0, Cleveland Museum of Art Open Access.
- **File:** raw/etienne-jules-marey/03-flying-heron.jpg (3400px long edge)

### 04 — *Bird in Flight* (1886)
- **Why it matters:** A compact chronophotographic flight study balances a bright bird against a dark field, using superimposition to turn wing motion into a continuous contour.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/286544
- **Rights:** CC0, Metropolitan Museum of Art Open Access.
- **File:** raw/etienne-jules-marey/04-bird-in-flight.jpg (3819px long edge)

### 05 — *Falling Cat* (1894)
- **Why it matters:** The successive phases of the cat's righting reflex make rotation visible without a fixed external support; it is the canonical late Marey motion study.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Falling_cat_1894.jpg
- **Rights:** Public domain (Marey d. 1904; 1894 publication).
- **Availability:** See `NEEDED.md`.

### 06 — *Shaking a Flexible Rod* (1886)
- **Why it matters:** A vibrating rod writes a luminous wave across a long dark plate, transforming force and frequency into a directly photographic line.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Marey,_der_einen_flexiblen_Stab_sch%C3%BCttelt_1886.jpg
- **Rights:** Public domain (Marey d. 1904; 1886 work).
- **Availability:** See `NEEDED.md`.

### 07 — *Throwing a Wheel* (1891)
- **Why it matters:** The wheel's shifting circle and the figure's repeated stance turn a practical action into a layered study of rotation and balance.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Ein_Rad_loswerfen_1891.jpg
- **Rights:** Public domain (Marey d. 1904; 1891 work).
- **Availability:** See `NEEDED.md`.

### 08 — *Heron in Flight* (1886)
- **Why it matters:** A second flying-bird study emphasizes the alternation of extended and folded wings, translating animal flight into repeated bright diagonals.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Flatternder_Reiher_1886.jpg
- **Rights:** Public domain (Marey d. 1904; 1886 work).
- **Availability:** See `NEEDED.md`.

### 09 — *High Jump* (1886)
- **Why it matters:** The body clears the bar in successive positions within one frame, an explicit precursor to the motion arcs that modernist painting later adopted.
- **Source:** https://www.christies.com/en/lot/lot-1694342
- **Rights:** Public domain underlying work; no open institutional master located.
- **Availability:** See `NEEDED.md`.

### 10 — *Horse Bixio at a Walk* (c. 1890)
- **Why it matters:** Marey's serial horse study keeps animal movement continuous across a film strip, contrasting with the fixed-plate accumulation of his better-known human experiments.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Anim_Marey,_Cheval_au_pas.gif
- **Rights:** Public domain (Marey d. 1904); only a small open derivative is readily available.
- **Availability:** See `NEEDED.md`.
