# Hill & Adamson — sources

David Octavius Hill (a painter) and Robert Adamson (a chemist and calotypist) worked together in Edinburgh from 1843 until Adamson's death in 1848, producing roughly 3,000 calotype negatives and becoming, in Beaumont Newhall's phrase, the first photographers to treat the medium as a fine art rather than a curiosity. Hill's training governs the compositional signature: sitters are posed as they would be for oil portraiture, built into closed triangular and pyramidal groupings under a single raking north light that the calotype's soft paper-negative grain renders as broad Rembrandt-like masses rather than fine documentary detail. Three recurring subjects carry that signature through the partnership's four years — the Newhaven fishwife series, in which women in striped dress are posed as sculptural, hip-cocked silhouettes against plain ground; the Free Church of Scotland ministers photographed around the 1843 Disruption, arranged in the stacked, overlapping rows of a group-portrait problem Hill was simultaneously solving for a history painting; and the Greyfriars Churchyard scenes, where a stone monument anchors the frame's vertical axis and small figures are set along its diagonal base. Genre and documentary work — a fisherman coiling a line, three men over a glass of ale, a half-built monument, a self-portrait in a studio doorway — fills out the range.

**Rights basis:** Public domain. Both partners worked entirely within 1843–1848 and both are long dead (Adamson in 1848, Hill in 1870); nothing in their joint output is in copyright anywhere. Seven of the images below come from the Metropolitan Museum of Art's Open Access program (CC0) — the Met holds one of the largest catalogued Hill & Adamson collections outside Scotland. Four come from Wikimedia Commons original files: high-resolution Google Art Project scans, a National Gallery of Art open-access scan (mirrored on Commons since nga.gov's own site blocks automated fetches behind a bot challenge), and a presbytery print digitized by a Scottish university library. National Galleries of Scotland holds the primary Hill & Adamson archive and is the best home for further research, but its own site (nationalgalleries.org) returned 403 to automated requests and was not used as a direct download source — see the file-by-file source notes below, which point to the NGS catalog record for context where a Commons mirror was used for the actual download. All eleven shortlisted images were fetched cleanly; no NEEDED.md was required for this chapter.

## Shortlist

### 01 — Newhaven Fishwives (c. 1845)
- **Why it matters:** Two women in striped Newhaven dress stand shoulder to shoulder, one's raised creel and the other's crossed shawl closing into a single triangular silhouette against a plain ground — the compositional cell Hill repeats across the entire fishwife series.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/282022
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/hill-adamson/01-newhaven-fishwives.jpg (3628px)

### 02 — Mrs. Elizabeth (Johnstone) Hall, Newhaven Fishwife (c. 1845)
- **Why it matters:** The single most reproduced Hill & Adamson image — full length, hand on hip, striped skirt filling the lower two-thirds of the frame while the plain backdrop isolates her profile against open ground; the calotype's soft grain reads her as sculptural mass rather than a documentary specimen.
- **Source:** https://www.nationalgalleries.org/art-and-artists/27652 (catalog record; downloaded via the Google Art Project scan on Wikimedia Commons — https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Hill_%26_Adamson_-_Mrs._Elizabeth_Johnstone_Hall_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg — since nationalgalleries.org blocks automated fetches)
- **Rights:** Public domain (created 1843–48; Google Art Project high-resolution scan, Wikimedia Commons)
- **File:** raw/hill-adamson/02-elizabeth-johnstone-hall.jpg (4605px)

### 03 — Study for the Disruption Picture: Signing of the Deed of Demission (October 18, 1843)
- **Why it matters:** A calotype record made the day the Free Church split from the Church of Scotland, arranging dozens of black-gowned ministers into stacked, overlapping rows that Hill would later transcribe into his 1866 history painting — composition functioning as a working diagram of a crowd.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/285808
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/hill-adamson/03-signing-deed-of-demission.jpg (4000px)

### 04 — Dumbarton Presbytery (1845)
- **Why it matters:** A formal ensemble of Free Church ministers seated and standing in two ranked rows before a plain backdrop — the era's solution to the group-portrait problem, bodies stacked by height into a stable rectangular block.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Dumbarton_Presbytery.jpg
- **Rights:** Public domain (created 1845; Wikimedia Commons)
- **File:** raw/hill-adamson/04-dumbarton-presbytery.jpg (2000px)

### 05 — Rev. Dr. Thomas Chalmers (c. 1843)
- **Why it matters:** A close three-quarter portrait of the Free Church's founding leader, seated and turned off-axis in the manner of a painted moderator's portrait — the single-sitter counterpart to the mass ministerial group shots, and the individual face behind the Disruption group work.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/282017
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/hill-adamson/05-thomas-chalmers.jpg (3722px)

### 06 — Three Unknown Boys and James Linton, Newhaven (1843–47)
- **Why it matters:** A fisherman and three boys are arranged around a coiled net and a boat's gunwale, heads stepping down in a diagonal that ties the family group to the working gear at their feet — genre composition built from the tools of the trade rather than studio props.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/306228
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/hill-adamson/06-linton-boys-newhaven.jpg (4000px)

### 07 — "Redding the Line," Willie Liston, Newhaven (1845)
- **Why it matters:** A fisherman shown mid-task, coiling a fishing line, his body bent into a diagonal that cuts the frame and turns a multi-second exposure into an implied instant of labor — one of the earliest photographs to stage motion itself as the subject.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/306225
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/hill-adamson/07-willie-liston-redding-line.jpg (3794px)

### 08 — Greyfriars Churchyard: the Dennistoun Monument, with D. O. Hill and His Nieces (c. 1845)
- **Why it matters:** A stone obelisk plants the frame's vertical axis while three small figures are arranged along the diagonal base of the tomb — architecture and portrait sitters locked into a single triangulated composition, the churchyard series' signature device.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Hill_%26_Adamson_(Scottish,_active_1843_-_1848)_-_(Greyfriars%27_Churchyard,_the_Dennistoun_Monument_with_D._O._Hill_and_His_Nieces)_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg
- **Rights:** Public domain (created c. 1843–48; Google Art Project scan, Wikimedia Commons)
- **File:** raw/hill-adamson/08-greyfriars-dennistoun-monument.jpg (4303px)

### 09 — David Octavius Hill at the Gate of Rock House, Edinburgh (1843–47)
- **Why it matters:** Hill's self-portrait in the doorway of the partnership's Calton Hill studio, framed square by the stone gatepost and stair rail — the architecture itself centers the sitter, doubling as an advertisement for the studio's address.
- **Source:** https://www.nga.gov/artworks/137700-david-octavius-hill-gate-rock-house-edinburgh (catalog record; downloaded via the Wikimedia Commons mirror of the NGA open-access scan — https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:David_Octavius_Hill_and_Robert_Adamson,_David_Octavius_Hill_at_the_gate_of_Rock_House,_Edinburgh,_1843-1847,_NGA_137700.jpg — since nga.gov's own site returns a bot challenge to automated requests)
- **Rights:** Public domain (National Gallery of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/hill-adamson/09-hill-self-portrait-rock-house.jpg (4000px)

### 10 — Edinburgh Ale (c. 1844)
- **Why it matters:** Three men — James Ballantine, Dr. George Bell, and Hill himself — grouped around a table and a raised glass, heads at three different heights forming an unstable triangle that reads as convivial, unposed conversation despite minutes-long exposure times.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Edinburgh_Ale_by_Hill_%26_Adamson_c1844.png
- **Rights:** Public domain (created c. 1844; Wikimedia Commons)
- **File:** raw/hill-adamson/10-edinburgh-ale.jpg (2329px)

### 11 — The Scott Monument, Edinburgh (1845)
- **Why it matters:** Construction scaffolding and masons on the half-built spire, shot straight-on to hold the tower dead-center — an architectural documentary image that anticipates later fascinations (Blossfeldt, the Bechers) with structure caught mid-assembly.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:David_Octavius_Hill_and_Robert_Adamson_-_The_Scott_Monument,_Edinburgh_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg
- **Rights:** Public domain (created May 1845; Google Art Project scan, Wikimedia Commons)
- **File:** raw/hill-adamson/11-scott-monument-construction.jpg (4001px)
