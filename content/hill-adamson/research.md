# Hill & Adamson — research

Scope: technique, era, printing, and recorded camera settings for the eleven
plates in this chapter. This is calotype (talbotype) paper-negative work from
1843–1848 with no per-frame exposure record; where a number is not
documented, this says so.

## Who and when

David Octavius Hill (1802–1870) was an established Scottish landscape and
genre painter and the secretary of the Royal Scottish Academy when, in 1843,
he took on a commission no painter had faced before: a group portrait of the
roughly 450 ministers who had just walked out of the Church of Scotland to
found the Free Church, an event known as the Disruption. Painting that many
individual likenesses from life was impractical, so Hill turned to the new
paper-negative process for a faster way to record faces. He was introduced
to Robert Adamson (1821–1848), a St Andrews-trained chemist who had learned
William Henry Fox Talbot's calotype process from his older brother John, an
early experimenter with the method. Adamson had opened a calotype studio at
Rock House, on Calton Hill in Edinburgh, in May 1843; within weeks the two
had entered a formal partnership, Adamson supplying the technical craft and
Hill the eye and the painter's sense of pose and grouping. Over the next
four and a half years, until Adamson's death in January 1848 at age 26, the
partnership produced close to 3,000 calotypes and is generally credited,
following Beaumont Newhall, as the first sustained body of work to treat
photography as a fine art rather than a scientific curiosity or a
documentary convenience. Hill continued to paint after the partnership
ended — the Disruption picture itself was not finished until 1866 — but he
never photographed again with the same standing; the Rock House
collaboration is the entirety of his significant photographic output.

## Technique

This is calotype, or talbotype, work: Talbot's 1841 paper-negative process,
in which a sheet of writing paper is sensitized with silver iodide, exposed
in the camera while still translucent, and developed out to a negative image
on the paper itself rather than on glass. Hill and Adamson used a good
watercolor paper for both negative and printing stages, and it was the
paper's own fiber structure — visible as a soft, broken grain under
enlargement — that gave the calotype its characteristic broad masses of
tone in place of the fine, crisp detail a later glass-plate negative would
render. That softness suited Hill's aims exactly: he posed sitters as he
would for oil portraiture, building them into closed triangular and
pyramidal groups under Rock House's raking north daylight, and the
calotype's grain read those groups as sculptural masses of light and shadow
rather than as documentary inventories of individual detail. The studio
portraits and posed genre scenes in this chapter (01–02, 05, 06–07, 09–10)
were made at or near Rock House or in the fishing village of Newhaven,
immediately north of Edinburgh; the large ministerial group scenes (03–04)
and the churchyard and monument views (08, 11) were made on location outdoors,
where the same slow paper process and a leveled stand camera held rows of
seated and standing figures, or a static stone monument, in even focus
across the full depth of the scene.

## Printing

Calotype negatives of this kind were contact-printed — enlarging was not
practical technology yet — onto salted paper: sheets sensitized with a
plain silver-salt solution rather than the albumen coating that would
become standard in the 1850s, producing prints with a matte, slightly
textured surface rather than an albumen print's sheen. Surviving material
for this chapter is split across several institutional holdings. Seven
images are Open Access (CC0) prints at the Metropolitan Museum of Art, which
holds one of the largest catalogued Hill & Adamson collections outside
Scotland. The primary Hill & Adamson archive — the bulk of the surviving
negatives and prints, including the Newhaven series and the Disruption
studies — is held by the National Galleries of Scotland in Edinburgh; two of
the images here (02, 08) are reproduced from high-resolution Google Art
Project scans mirrored on Wikimedia Commons rather than fetched directly
from the Scottish archive's own site. One image (09) survives as a National
Gallery of Art (Washington) open-access scan, also mirrored on Commons, and
one (04) as a print digitized by a Scottish university library and hosted
directly on Commons.

## Camera settings

Not recorded. Calotype photography of the 1840s predates any per-frame
aperture, shutter-speed, or sensitivity log by decades — there was no light
meter, no shutter mechanism beyond a lens cap removed and replaced by hand,
and exposure was judged by the operator's experience with the day's light
and the paper's own variable sensitivity. What can be said is qualitative
and well documented: calotype paper was far slower than the wet-collodion
glass negatives that would follow it in the 1850s, so studio sittings at
Rock House (01–02, 05, 09–10) required a sitter to hold a pose for several
seconds to perhaps a minute or more under strong north light, sometimes with
a headrest or a support to hold the pose steady, and the outdoor group and
monument scenes (03–04, 06–08, 11) required similarly long exposures in full
daylight with the camera held level and stopped down enough to keep a
crowded or deep scene in even focus. Willie Liston's coiled fishing line
(07) is a genre scene built around a task, not a captured instant — the
exposure was still several seconds long, with the "action" a held pose
rather than motion frozen by a fast shutter, since no shutter of the era
could do that. No specific f-stop, exposure duration, or sensitivity value
survives for any of the eleven images, and none is stated here.
