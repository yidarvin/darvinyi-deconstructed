# Mathew Brady — research

Scope: technique, era, printing, and recorded camera settings for the ten
plates in this chapter. This body of work spans two processes with no
per-frame exposure record; where a number is not documented, this says so.

## Who and when

Mathew B. Brady (c. 1822–January 15, 1896) was born in Warren County, New
York, likely to Irish immigrant parents, and moved to Saratoga as a teenager,
where he studied painting under the portraitist William Page. Page introduced
him to Samuel F. B. Morse, who had met Louis Daguerre in Paris in 1839 and
brought the daguerreotype process back to New York; Brady learned the
process in Morse's circle and opened his own studio on lower Broadway in
1844. Through the late 1840s and 1850s he built the best-known portrait
gallery in the country, publishing engraved plates drawn from his
daguerreotypes as the "Gallery of Illustrious Americans" (1850) and
expanding to a second gallery in Washington, D.C. in 1858, which he placed
under the management of a young Scottish immigrant, Alexander Gardner. By
the time of the Cooper Union sitting (image 03, February 1860) and the war
that followed, Brady increasingly directed rather than operated the camera
himself, employing a roster of field and studio operators — Gardner,
Timothy H. O'Sullivan, and others — under the "Brady" name; his own hand is
documented for some of the sittings in this set (notably the earliest,
Calhoun) and is a matter of studio, rather than individual, authorship for
much of the rest, consistent with the mass-production gallery practice of
the era. Brady financed an ambitious, self-funded documentation of the
Civil War through his field operators, a venture that bankrupted him; a
depleted Congress bought his surviving negative archive in 1875 for
$25,000, well short of what he had spent. He lost his eyesight and his wife
in his last years and died in the charity ward of Presbyterian Hospital in
New York on January 15, 1896.

## Technique

This chapter spans the two processes that defined Brady's working life. The
earliest plate, the 1849 Calhoun portrait (01), is a daguerreotype: a
sheet of silver-plated copper sensitized with iodine (and, by this date,
usually also bromine) fumes, exposed directly in the camera with no
intervening negative, then developed over mercury vapor and fixed — a
one-of-a-kind, laterally reversed positive image on a mirror-polished
metal surface, produced under a top skylight or reflector arrangement
typical of a late-1840s New York gallery. Everything else in this set (02
through 10, 1856–1865) is wet-plate (wet collodion) glass-negative work,
the process that had displaced the daguerreotype in American commercial
galleries by the mid-1850s: a glass plate was flowed with collodion
carrying iodide salts, sensitized in a silver nitrate bath, exposed while
still wet, and developed within minutes, which meant a darkroom — a fixed
room in the gallery, or a horse-drawn wagon in the field (image 09 shows
that field darkroom directly) — had to travel with the camera. The studio
sittings here (02, 03, 04, 06, 07, 10) and the field images (05, 08, 09)
apply the same wet-plate discipline in different settings. Stand cameras,
skylight lighting, head braces, and portable darkrooms are well documented
parts of the period's practice, but no per-image camera arrangement or
exposure choice is recorded for this set.

## Printing

The Calhoun daguerreotype (01) has no separate negative or print: the
exposed plate is itself the final image, a unique object rather than a
reproducible one. Per Sotheby's 6 April 2011 sale record, the physical
plate is in private hands; the file used here is a photographic
reproduction of that plate mirrored on Wikimedia Commons. The wet-plate
images were contact-printed — there being no enlargers in this era —
directly from the glass negative at the negative's own size. The Perry
portrait (02) is documented by the National Portrait Gallery, Smithsonian
Institution as a salted-paper print, the paper process (plain paper
sensitized with silver nitrate over a salt bed, without albumen's glossy
coating) still in use alongside albumen paper in the mid-1850s. The
carte-de-visite and cabinet-format portraits and field views (03–10) are
consistent with the albumen paper — egg-white-bound silver salts on a thin
paper stock, mounted to card — that had become the standard commercial
printing surface for Brady's gallery output by the Civil War years.
Surviving material is split across institutions: eight images (03–10) derive
from Library of Congress holdings — the Brady-Handy Collection (glass
negatives purchased from Levin C. Handy's estate in 1954, roughly 4,000
original wet-plate negatives from Brady's studios) and the separate Civil
War Photographs, 1861–1865 collection — served as full-resolution master
scans; 01 is a Wikimedia Commons reproduction sourced from Sotheby's sale of
the privately held daguerreotype, and the Perry print (02) is a Smithsonian
National Portrait Gallery CC0 Open Access scan of the salted-paper original.

## Camera settings

Not recorded. Neither the daguerreotype era nor the wet-plate era that
followed it had any per-frame aperture, shutter-speed, or sensitivity log,
and there was no light meter — exposure was judged by the operator against
the available light, the plate's freshly mixed sensitivity, and the lens in
use. What can be said is qualitative and well documented: daguerreotype
portraiture generally required a sitter to hold still under strong light,
while wet-collodion studio practice was faster but still depended on
careful posing. Outdoor wet-plate work likewise required a portable
darkroom and careful setup, but exposure and lens choices varied with light
and materials. No specific f-stop, shutter speed, or sensitivity value
survives for any of the ten images, and none is stated here.
