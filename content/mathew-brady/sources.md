# Mathew Brady — sources

Brady rarely stood behind the camera himself after the 1850s — Alexander Gardner ran the Washington gallery and later Antietam field work, Timothy O'Sullivan and a dozen other named operators covered the front — but every sitting, whether shot by Brady's own hand or one of his men, followed the same house style he set as a daguerreotypist in the 1840s: the subject squared frontally to the lens, three-quarter or bust length, centered in a plain or lightly furnished field, with a prop (a column, a stack of books, a chair back, a tree trunk) planted beside the figure to anchor the composition and signal gravity. Group portraits extend the same logic into a shallow triangulated cluster — one seated principal flanked by standing subordinates, as in the Lee sitting below. It is a studio grammar so consistent that Brady could relocate it wholesale to a working headquarters tent (Grant at Cold Harbor) without altering its bones. To keep this chapter distinct from the Gardner and O'Sullivan chapters, which will draw on the same Civil War Collection for battlefield-aftermath and landscape-survey material, this shortlist favors images that are explicitly studio sittings, Brady-credited portraits of principals, or self-referential views of Brady's own field operation — not the battlefield frames those two chapters are expected to use.

**Rights basis:** Public domain. Nine of the ten images below come directly from the Library of Congress Prints & Photographs Division — the Brady-Handy Collection and the Civil War Photographs, 1861–1865 collection — each carrying LOC's own "no known restrictions on publication" rights statement; files were pulled as the full-resolution master TIFFs served from tile.loc.gov and converted losslessly to JPEG. The earliest image, the 1849 Calhoun daguerreotype, is public domain on the same age basis (made and first circulated well over a century ago) and was pulled from a Wikimedia Commons upload sourced from a Sotheby's auction record of the physical plate. The Perry salted-paper print was pulled from a Wikimedia Commons mirror of the National Portrait Gallery, Smithsonian Institution's own CC0 Open Access release.

**A note on attribution:** the widely repeated claim that Brady daguerreotyped Edgar Allan Poe in 1848 was checked and set aside. The celebrated 1848 "Ultima Thule" plate was made by Edwin H. Manchester in Providence, R.I.; Brady's studio later reproduced/copied that image, but the sitting Brady himself publicized never took place, per the Edgar Allan Poe Society's study of the portraits. It is omitted here rather than included on the strength of a popular but unsupported credit line. Similarly, a second Brady sitting of Lee alone (seated, LOC ppmsca.39293) was fetched but dropped from the shortlist: the surviving object is a framed exhibition print (a period wood-and-gilt frame photographed as part of the item), which would put a physical frame edge inside the analysis frame; the cleaner *Lee and Staff* group below carries the same sitting's compositional signature without that artifact.

## Shortlist

### 01 — John C. Calhoun (1849)
- **Why it matters:** The earliest image in this set and one of Brady's most celebrated daguerreotypes — Calhoun's face and shock of white hair fill a plain dark field, no prop but his own folded arms, the frontal bust format Brady would carry forward into the "Gallery of Illustrious Americans" engravings and, later, the carte-de-visite trade.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:John_C_Calhoun_by_Mathew_Brady,_1849.jpg (whole-plate daguerreotype, per Sotheby's 6 April 2011 sale, lot 41)
- **Rights:** Public domain (daguerreotype made 1849; pre-1931)
- **File:** raw/mathew-brady/01-john-calhoun.jpg (2442px)

### 02 — Matthew Calbraith Perry (c. 1856)
- **Why it matters:** A three-quarter-length antebellum studio portrait in full dress uniform, epaulette and double row of buttons squared to the lens — the commercial-portrait formula Brady's gallery sold by the thousand, years before the war made his name synonymous with it.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Matthew_Calbraith_Perry_by_Mathew_B._Brady,_c._1856,_salted_paper_print,_from_the_National_Portrait_Gallery_-_NPG-9101248C_1.jpg
- **Rights:** Public domain / CC0 (National Portrait Gallery, Smithsonian Institution, Open Access)
- **File:** raw/mathew-brady/02-matthew-perry.jpg (6038px)

### 03 — Abraham Lincoln, Cooper Union portrait (1860)
- **Why it matters:** The defining Brady studio formula at maximum stakes — Lincoln standing, hand resting on a stack of books, a classical column beside him signaling institutional weight; Brady retouched the plate to steady Lincoln's roving eye, and Lincoln credited the resulting image, alongside the Cooper Union speech itself, with putting him in the White House.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/98504529/
- **Rights:** Public domain (Library of Congress, Brady-Handy Collection; no known restrictions)
- **File:** raw/mathew-brady/03-cooper-union-lincoln.jpg (5915px)

### 04 — General Winfield Scott (1861)
- **Why it matters:** A bust-length carte-de-visite, uniform and epaulettes filling the card, Brady's facsimile signature caption "Gen'l Scott" printed beneath — the standard wartime CDV format his galleries mass-produced to satisfy public appetite for portraits of the high command.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2016651681/
- **Rights:** Public domain (Library of Congress, Brady-Handy Collection; no known restrictions)
- **File:** raw/mathew-brady/04-winfield-scott.jpg (3292px)

### 05 — Brady, the Photographer, Returned from Bull Run (1861)
- **Why it matters:** A rare instance of the operator becoming the sitter — dated in-frame July 22, 1861, Brady stands in profile in a duster coat and slouch hat fresh from the war's first battle, the same frontal, plainly-grounded formality he gave every other subject turned on himself.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2018667216/
- **Rights:** Public domain (Library of Congress, Civil War Photographs, 1861–1865; no known restrictions)
- **File:** raw/mathew-brady/05-brady-returned-bull-run.jpg (14646px)

### 06 — Walt Whitman (1862)
- **Why it matters:** A half-length seated carte-de-visite, beard and open collar filling a plain backdrop — the gallery's frontal bust format applied to a literary sitter rather than a general or statesman, extending the same studio grammar into Brady's civilian register.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/99406018/
- **Rights:** Public domain (Library of Congress, Brady-Handy Collection; no known restrictions)
- **File:** raw/mathew-brady/06-walt-whitman.jpg (3298px)

### 07 — Major General William T. Sherman (1861–65)
- **Why it matters:** A bust-length uniform portrait, arms folded, gaze fixed off-camera — Brady's National Photographic Portrait Galleries applying the identical formula used on Grant, Lee, and Scott, so that the four read as one deliberate visual set: "the Union and Confederate high command."
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2018666487/
- **Rights:** Public domain (Library of Congress, Civil War Photographs, 1861–1865; no known restrictions)
- **File:** raw/mathew-brady/07-william-sherman.jpg (7500px)

### 08 — General Ulysses S. Grant at Cold Harbor (1864)
- **Why it matters:** The studio portrait's formality relocated to a working headquarters — Grant plants one hand on his hip and the other against a tree trunk laced with tent-rope, canvas standing in for a backdrop; the Civil War's best-known field-commander portrait and proof the Brady grammar didn't need a studio to function.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2002736661/
- **Rights:** Public domain (Library of Congress, Brady-Handy Collection; no known restrictions)
- **File:** raw/mathew-brady/08-grant-cold-harbor.jpg (4155px)

### 09 — Brady's Photographic Outfit in Front of Petersburg (1864)
- **Why it matters:** The traveling darkroom wagon itself, tarpaulin-draped among pines — the apparatus behind every other image in this set becomes the subject, a rare candid, unposed camp view credited directly to Brady's field operation rather than a studio sitting.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2012649239/
- **Rights:** Public domain (Library of Congress, Brady-Handy Collection; no known restrictions)
- **File:** raw/mathew-brady/09-photo-outfit-petersburg.jpg (5753px)

### 10 — General Robert E. Lee and Staff (1865)
- **Why it matters:** Lee seated centrally on the back porch of his Richmond home, flanked by his son Custis Lee and aide Walter Taylor standing — a triangulated three-figure group made two days after the surrender at Appomattox, the last formal portrait session of the war and among the most reproduced Brady images of all.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2011648617/
- **Rights:** Public domain (Library of Congress, Brady-Handy Collection; no known restrictions)
- **File:** raw/mathew-brady/10-robert-e-lee-staff.jpg (5370px)
