# Walker Evans — sources

Evans built his 1935–38 pictures on a single discipline: the camera set square to its subject, eye-level and frontal, the film plane parallel to the wall. Faces, shopfronts, and clapboard facades are pinned dead-center, and the frame is emptied of incident so structure reads clean — bilateral symmetry about a central axis, the wall dissolving into a flat rectangular grid, hand-lettered signage flattened into planar typography. He worked in typologies, photographing the same kind of thing again and again — sharecropper portraits, frame houses, company rows — until repetition itself becomes the subject. It is the most rigorously geometric body of documentary photography in America, and an ideal overlay test bed for symmetry axes, grids, and modular rhythm.

**Rights basis:** Public domain. Evans made this work in 1935–1938 as a staff photographer for the U.S. Resettlement Administration / Farm Security Administration (later merged into the Office of War Information); as works of the U.S. federal government they carry no copyright and are in the public domain. Nine of the images below come directly from the Library of Congress FSA/OWI Black-and-White Negatives collection (LC-USF / LC-DIG numbers), digitized from Evans's own 8×10 nitrate negatives. Note that this PD basis does **not** extend to Evans's pre-FSA work (his 1930–34 New York and architectural photographs, e.g. *License Photo Studio, New York*, 1934) or his later independent work — copyright in those is held by the Walker Evans Archive at the Metropolitan Museum of Art (Evans d. 1975, so life+70 runs to 2045). One image here, *Penny Picture Display, Savannah* (06), is a 1936 photograph that falls outside the FSA negative file; it is included on the basis of the Metropolitan Museum's Open Access (CC0) release of its print. See NEEDED.md for canonical Evans images that are **not** cleanly available as public domain.

## Shortlist

### 01 — Allie Mae Burroughs, wife of a cotton sharecropper. Hale County, Alabama (1936)
- **Why it matters:** The defining Evans frontal portrait — face centered and level, mouth set, the vertical clapboard behind her turning the whole ground into a ruled grid against which her bilateral symmetry reads.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2017762301/
- **Rights:** Public domain (U.S. government work; FSA/OWI Collection, Library of Congress)
- **File:** raw/walker-evans/01-allie-mae-burroughs.jpg (5878px)

### 02 — Floyd Burroughs, cotton sharecropper. Hale County, Alabama (1936)
- **Why it matters:** Head-and-shoulders straight to camera, centered on the frame, plain board wall behind — the sharecropper rendered as a typological specimen, all incident stripped away.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2017762300/
- **Rights:** Public domain (U.S. government work; FSA/OWI Collection, Library of Congress)
- **File:** raw/walker-evans/02-floyd-burroughs.jpg (3209px)

### 03 — Bud Fields and his family at home. Hale County, Alabama (1936)
- **Why it matters:** The family arranged in a shallow frontal frieze across the bed and the bare board wall, figures spaced like a row of portraits — a group composed as flat, symmetrical typology.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/96516419/
- **Rights:** Public domain (U.S. government work; FSA/OWI Collection, Library of Congress)
- **File:** raw/walker-evans/03-bud-fields-family.jpg (1461px)

### 04 — Fireplace and wall detail in bedroom of Floyd Burroughs' cabin. Hale County, Alabama (1936)
- **Why it matters:** The wall-as-grid in its purest form — newspaper-papered planks and mantel objects flattened dead-frontal into a rectilinear lattice, a still life read entirely as geometry.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2017762297/
- **Rights:** Public domain (U.S. government work; FSA/OWI Collection, Library of Congress)
- **File:** raw/walker-evans/04-fireplace-wall-burroughs.jpg (12085px)

### 05 — Roadside stand near Birmingham, Alabama (1936)
- **Why it matters:** A shopfront plastered edge-to-edge with hand-painted signs and fish names — vernacular typography arrayed across a single flat plane, letterforms as the whole composition.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2017762372/
- **Rights:** Public domain (U.S. government work; FSA/OWI Collection, Library of Congress)
- **File:** raw/walker-evans/05-roadside-stand-birmingham.jpg (3604px)

### 06 — Penny Picture Display, Savannah (1936)
- **Why it matters:** A literal grid — a studio window tiled with rows of penny portraits under the word STUDIO, the typological lattice made subject; the found grid Evans most admired.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/265556
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/walker-evans/06-penny-picture-display-savannah.jpg (4096px)

### 07 — Bethlehem graveyard and steel mill. Pennsylvania (1935)
- **Why it matters:** Three stacked horizontal bands — headstones, a row of identical company houses, the steel mill behind — the American scene compressed into frontal strata.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2017759355/
- **Rights:** Public domain (U.S. government work; FSA/OWI Collection, Library of Congress)
- **File:** raw/walker-evans/07-bethlehem-graveyard-steel-mill.jpg (2322px)

### 08 — Negro church. South Carolina (1936)
- **Why it matters:** Dead-frontal, bilaterally symmetric clapboard facade with the steeple planted on the mirror axis and the porch columns framing a central door — vernacular architecture as pure symmetry.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2017762234/
- **Rights:** Public domain (U.S. government work; FSA/OWI Collection, Library of Congress)
- **File:** raw/walker-evans/08-negro-church-south-carolina.jpg (6062px)

### 09 — Frame houses. New Orleans, Louisiana (1936)
- **Why it matters:** Near-identical two-story double-gallery frame-house facades set frontally side by side, repeating windows and clapboard flattened to a planar grid — a typology of the vernacular street.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2017762240/
- **Rights:** Public domain (U.S. government work; FSA/OWI Collection, Library of Congress)
- **File:** raw/walker-evans/09-frame-houses-new-orleans.jpg (8761px)

### 10 — Frame houses. Fredericksburg, Virginia (1936)
- **Why it matters:** A row of identical two-story house fronts marching across the frame, each a repeated module — pure typological repetition photographed square to the street.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2017762233/

### 11 — Steelmill workers' houses (company owned). Birmingham, Alabama (1936)
- **Why it matters:** Rows of identical workers' houses read as a repeating modular grid stretched before the steel mill — industrial housing as typology, the frame filled with rhythm.
