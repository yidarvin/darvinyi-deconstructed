# Walker Evans — research

Scope: technique, era, printing, and recorded camera settings for the eleven
plates in this chapter. This is 1930s film work with no per-frame exposure
record; unrecorded values are named as unrecorded rather than guessed.

## Who and when

Walker Evans (1903–1975) built the style seen here between 1935 and 1938 as a
staff photographer for the Resettlement Administration and Farm Security
Administration under Roy Stryker. The eleven images are from that federal work
and are public domain as U.S. government works. The most famous of them, the Hale
County, Alabama portraits and interiors, come from the summer of 1936 spent with
James Agee living among three tenant families, which became *Let Us Now Praise
Famous Men* (1941). Evans's earlier New York and architectural work of 1930–1934
and his later independent work are not public domain; their copyright sits with
the Walker Evans Archive at the Metropolitan Museum of Art.

## Technique

Evans's discipline is a large-format discipline. For this body of work he used an
8×10 view camera on a tripod, and also a 4×5, composing on the ground glass with
the film plane held parallel to the wall or facade so verticals stay vertical and
the subject reads dead-frontal and flat. The big negative and small working
aperture give the enormous depth of field and the fine descriptive detail (the
grain of clapboard, the newsprint on a papered wall, the lettering on a
storefront) that let structure carry the picture. He worked in available light,
patiently, on static subjects. Some of the 1936 street and sign pictures were
also made on smaller formats, but the frontal view-camera look defines the group.

## Printing

Gelatin silver prints, many of them contact prints from the 8×10 negatives, which
is part of why the tonal scale is so long and the detail so exact. The surviving
FSA negatives are held by the Library of Congress; the modern scans here derive
from those negatives, except *Penny Picture Display, Savannah* (06), which is
reproduced from the Metropolitan Museum of Art's Open Access (CC0) print.

## Camera settings

Not recorded. The FSA negatives carry no per-frame aperture or shutter log, and
the digital files hold only archival numbers and scanner metadata. The record
supports only the qualitative reading: 8×10 and 4×5 view-camera work on slow
1930s sheet film, stopped well down for depth of field, on a tripod, in available
light. That is consistent with the front-to-back sharpness of every plate here,
but no specific f-stop or shutter speed survives, so none is stated.
