Saul Leiter does not treat the city as a clear view waiting to be taken. In these pictures, seeing is always mediated: a screen opens briefly, glass carries a reflection, a vehicle window divides a body, a canopy makes weather into a small aperture. The overlays follow that mediation rather than imposing decorative geometry. Their few lines, frames, and ellipses identify where a photograph lets the street through and where it stubbornly refuses to.

## the image as a partial view

<Plate slug="saul-leiter" id="01-red-curtain" label="Red Curtain, 1956" caption="A red foreground screen opens two small views of the same walker: once on the street, once as a reflection." />

In *Red Curtain*, the screen edge slopes through the frame and divides a pale pavement from a dense red foreground. The overlay names the two walkers separately: the distant body on the street and its reflected counterpart inside the triangular reveal. Those two small intervals are more important than a complete description of the street. The analyzer finds a low edge density of 0.0119 and a palette moving from near-black red-browns (#27141e, #482126) to pale grey (#c6c6c5), which helps explain why the picture reads as broad color and value masses before it resolves into people.

<Plate slug="saul-leiter" id="02-canopy" label="Canopy, 1958" caption="A black canopy reduces the snowy street to a low, angled opening populated by small figures." />

*Canopy* makes the obstruction nearly absolute. The outlined canopy edge and snowy curb meet at the lower part of the frame; the nearest pedestrian is held in that thin accessible zone. The dark field is not empty background but the pressure that makes the people and cars feel miniature. Its tonal distribution is extreme: p05 0.012 against p95 0.875, and its 0.0737 edge density comes largely from the busy snow street below the almost featureless black mass.

<Plate slug="saul-leiter" id="11-through-boards" label="Through Boards, 1957" caption="Boards leave a narrow horizontal aperture in which a white car and pedestrians become a compressed street stage." />

The boards in *Through Boards* perform the same withholding more severely. The overlay frames only the narrow slit and marks a pedestrian rather than pretending the red and dark bands are scenery. A white convertible carries the eye through that slit, but the decisive fact is how much is denied. The muted palette includes #29232f and #3b2c31 beside a pale #e2d5bf; with edge density just 0.0137, the few exposed figures and the car gain unusual force.

## objects become the structure

<Plate slug="saul-leiter" id="03-foot-on-el" label="Foot on El, 1954" caption="A woven seat back, floorboards, and a cropped shoe turn public transit into a field of competing diagonals." />

In *Foot on El*, a passenger is present only as a shoe. The seat-back frame holds a luminous woven plane while the floorboard run drives down toward the right edge; the shoe interrupts that system without completing a conventional portrait. The analyzer registers the set's highest edge density, 0.1107, consistent with the tight texture of the seats, rails, and boards. Its dark green-browns and warm tan clusters (#1a2320 through #dec895) make the object-world feel both material and slightly abstract.

<Plate slug="saul-leiter" id="06-taxi" label="Taxi, 1957" caption="Taxi windows and the yellow roof arc divide driver, wheel, and reflections into red, yellow, and black blocks." />

*Taxi* is built from relations between surfaces before it becomes a narrative. The window frame, yellow roof arc, and taxi beltline give the driver and steering wheel a precise compartment, while a blurred foreground vehicle crosses the lower third. Its palette report catches the construction: deep blue-black #061d1f, red-brown #98533e, yellow-orange #ce8915, and pale beige #c2b5a5. At 0.0311 edge density, the large color blocks retain authority over incidental detail.

<Plate slug="saul-leiter" id="09-bus" label="Bus, 1954" caption="A passenger silhouette is isolated by a bus window and counterweighted by the cropped wheel arch." />

The bus in *Bus* is a stack of rectangles and a partial circle. The outlined upper rail and window make the passenger's silhouette a distinct picture inside the picture; the cropped wheel arch supplies a larger dark countershape below. The image is not symmetrical because the obstructing sign at right matters, but it is carefully held between those geometric fragments. The restricted palette of dark green-black (#111a19), orange-red (#8c2e0c), tan (#b4947e), and muted grey-green (#31322f) keeps the bus body from reading as mere local color.

## reflection delays recognition

<Plate slug="saul-leiter" id="04-graffiti-heads" label="Graffiti Heads, 1950" caption="Painted profiles and a reflected street share a single unsettled field of marks and faces." />

*Graffiti Heads* refuses a quick answer to the question of what is in front. The overlay traces two painted profiles and anchors the central drawn head, while the horizontal field break separates neither image layer cleanly. Architecture and reflected sky remain visible through the painted swirls. Mean luminance is relatively high at 0.6333 and the palette is blue-grey (#a3b4b8, #c8d3d2, #526367) with a dark #222f31 accent; that cool, open range lets the black drawn lines insist without making the layers easy to sort.

<Plate slug="saul-leiter" id="07-mirrors" label="Mirrors, c. 1962" caption="Three mirror apertures repeat and isolate a red-coated figure amid a reflected street." />

The three ellipses in *Mirrors* are literal objects, not a generalized claim of symmetry. The central elongated mirror catches the red-coated figure; the two round mirrors interrupt it with different reflected spaces. That repetition makes the eye move from object to object before it locates the person. The tonal range is broad, from p05 0.031 to p95 0.729, with a low mean of 0.3502; pale beige (#d4ccb5) and soft grey (#a49a86) hold against near-black blue (#0b1322).

<Plate slug="saul-leiter" id="08-phone-call" label="Phone Call, 1957" caption="Glass dividers and window panels fragment a telephone gesture across reflected transit and lettering." />

In *Phone Call*, the caller's window is only one panel in a larger arrangement of reflections, vehicle glazing, bars, and reversed text. The overlay's two rectangles and central divider make that partitioning explicit. A horizontal lower window band keeps the composition from dissolving into a pile of reflections. The image's edge density of 0.0790 is among the highest here, appropriate to its many frames and signs, while its muted mauves and browns (#332839, #75544f, #bbad9e) prevent the information from becoming visually loud.

<Plate slug="saul-leiter" id="12-walking" label="Walking, 1956" caption="A walker is softened by a central glass seam, reflected verticals, and a low band of light." />

*Walking* turns a passerby into a slow, translucent presence. The central glass seam is a real division in the view, and the ellipse contains the softened reflected body rather than claiming a sharply legible gesture. The lower reflection band anchors the otherwise drifting blue-grey fields. The palette moves from deep blue-black #101a27 through #2a4451 and #536067 to pale #b2ac9a; p05 0.067 and p95 0.667 keep the image compressed but not opaque.

## surfaces make the weather visible

<Plate slug="saul-leiter" id="05-cracks" label="Cracks, 1957" caption="Two fissures and a dark edge make the glass surface more present than the distant blurred figure." />

The strongest subject in *Cracks* is not the figure behind the pane but the surface in front of it. The overlay follows two separate cracks at the left and contains the distant dark figure as an incomplete form. The measured horizontal break at y 0.457 divides the warm, blurred distance from the denser foreground surface. With mean luminance 0.2765 and p95 only 0.667, the image remains dark and compressed; the palette's charcoal #1c2326, grey #2e3839, brown #695b53, and pale pink-beige #beaaa9 make blur and fracture feel equally material.

<Plate slug="saul-leiter" id="10-snow" label="Snow, dated 1970 in the supplied Foundation shortlist" caption="Condensation tracks and a soft street band turn a figure and yellow field into a weather-filtered image." />

*Snow* takes the screen from glass itself. The broad figure is not described as a crisp subject: it is an ellipse seen through condensation, while two running tracks and the soft street band describe the intervening surface. The provided source notes conflict on the date: its Foundation shortlist says 1970 while a cited NGA note says 1960. This chapter leaves that discrepancy visible rather than choosing between records. Its relatively high mean luminance of 0.4819 and p95 of 0.886 describe the glowing grey field; the dark #19231a and yellow-grey #817b73, #a09e9d, #d8d7da palette clusters locate the figure within it.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Photograph through a constraint">
Choose a window, railing, awning, or other foreground surface that blocks most of a street view. Make the obstruction occupy at least half the frame, then wait for a figure or vehicle to give the remaining opening a specific scale. Ask whether the obstruction is a meaningful plane, not just an accidental blur.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Build a picture inside a picture">
Use a car window, mirror, bus window, or shop display to make an internal frame. Photograph until the inner picture changes the reading of the outer one. Keep the boundaries legible enough that you can name the actual edge or pane doing the work.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Let color organize the view">
Find three large color or value fields, perhaps a vehicle, a reflection, and a wall. Make one picture where they merely decorate the scene and another where a line, window edge, or threshold makes them hold the frame together. Prefer a small, precise interruption to a completely described scene.
</ExerciseCard>
