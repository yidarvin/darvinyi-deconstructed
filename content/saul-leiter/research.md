# Saul Leiter — build research

## Scope and evidence

The supplied Saul Leiter Foundation Color gallery shortlist identifies twelve color photographs dated 1950–c. 1962, except for *Snow*, which its Foundation shortlist dates 1970. The source notes also say that an NGA artist-page reference catalogs *Snow* as 1960; this disagreement is unresolved here, so the chapter does not present a single settled date for that image. The set is therefore described as mid-century color work extending to the later *Snow* image, rather than as one uniform early period.

The supplied display files are Foundation-hosted web JPEGs (two source files report WebP delivery despite their `.jpg` names), not original negatives, slides, scans, or print-production files. They contain no useful camera EXIF. The records provide no camera body, lens, focal length, film stock, exposure, development method, lighting arrangement, or print process. None of those details is inferred from the pictures.

## Visible method

The selected images support a consistent compositional account: urban scenes are often approached through an intervening surface rather than as unobstructed views. Glass, painted or cracked surfaces, reflections, a canopy, vehicle windows, boards, and soft weather turn the photograph into layered planes. Figures are partial, reflected, blurred, or made small against large fields of red, black, yellow, blue, and grey. The recurring structural devices are apertures, nested frames, vertical divisions, narrow horizontal slits, and cropped circular or diagonal forms.

This is a description of the surviving display images, not a claim about a fixed technical recipe. The analyzer's normalized-image reports provide tonal percentiles, edge density, palette clusters, and detected geometry for the chapter's composition discussion; they do not establish historical camera or darkroom facts.

## Prints and settings

No original print process, paper, edition, or printing date is documented for these twelve selections. No camera settings are available. The chapter explicitly treats framing, color relationships, tonal range, reflection, and occlusion as visible evidence only.
