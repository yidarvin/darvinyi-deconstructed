David Levinthal makes staged miniatures look less like illustrations of a past event than like memories arriving through fog. Across *Modern Romance*, *American Beauties*, *Barbie*, *Wild West*, *Baseball*, and *History*, dolls and toy figures are never hidden as props; scale, blur, and an insistent photograph-within-a-paper-edge make their artificiality part of the image's pressure. The overlays name the few structural decisions that survive the softness: a marquee field, a pose, a dress axis, a fence, a flagpole, a raised arm. They do not turn blur into a technical defect. They show how blur removes descriptive certainty while leaving direction, color, and hierarchy intact. [Source notes](../../content/david-levinthal/sources.md)

## miniature stages, large fields

<Plate slug="david-levinthal" id="01-modern-romance-1985" label="Untitled, from Modern Romance, 1985" caption="A red pool and small lit interior puncture a nearly black field." />

In the 1985 *Modern Romance*, the small illuminated window hangs above a toy car and the red pool below it. The overlay bounds the dark photographic field, then anchors the car-and-pool focus within its lower-left quadrant: nearly all of the meaningful illumination falls there. Mean luminance is 0.353, but the p05–p95 span of 0.024–0.906 is enormous. Black (#09090b) can therefore operate as a large container while the warm red and pale light carry the event.

<Plate slug="david-levinthal" id="02-modern-romance-1984" label="Untitled, from Modern Romance, 1984" caption="Lit theater signs and small figures emerge from a dark street field." />

The 1984 scene converts cinema signage into a shallow proscenium. The paired-marquee ellipse holds the competing signs together, while the two anchors insist on their lateral asymmetry. Its edge density is only 0.025, so the small bulbs and lettering matter less as crisp information than as a rhythm of local lights. The muted palette of near-black, dusty white, red-brown, and tan helps the signs feel recalled rather than legibly reported.

<Plate slug="david-levinthal" id="03-american-beauties-1990" label="Untitled, from American Beauties, 1990" caption="A raised doll gesture breaks a broad pale-and-dark stage." />

*American Beauties* makes a single pose do the work of a plot. The ellipse embraces the doll as a whole while the short polyline traces the raised arm's visible contour: it is the gesture, not facial detail, that activates the wide field. The dark upper ground, pale floor, and body sit within a 0.051-0.816 tonal range; salmon, cream, and deep red-brown palette notes make the doll's form hover rather than settle into ordinary space.

<Plate slug="david-levinthal" id="04-american-beauties-1990-b" label="Untitled, from American Beauties, 1990" caption="A reclining doll turns a bare floor into a stage for one bent leg." />

The second 1990 image is even more withholding. Its frame and ellipse isolate the reclining body, and the bent-leg polyline turns a small joint of the pose into the picture's strongest directional turn. The image has a mean luminance of 0.319 and modest 0.031 edge density: the floor remains smooth enough that the doll's limbs read as placed marks. What looks empty is doing compositional work, giving the figure room to appear both exposed and deliberately arranged.

## portraits that refuse naturalism

<Plate slug="david-levinthal" id="05-barbie-1998" label="Untitled, from Barbie, 1998" caption="A centered Barbie face and red dress are held against a yellow field." />

The red-dressed *Barbie* is almost diagrammatically frontal. The portrait axis lands through the face and bow, the face ellipse identifies the tiny area that can answer back to the viewer, and the horizontal break establishes where the red dress begins to outweigh the yellow ground. Mean luminance is 0.389, with a p95 of 0.886. Yellow-brown (#aa832b), red (#8e210f), and black make a blunt palette; the slight softness stops that bluntness from becoming merely graphic.

<Plate slug="david-levinthal" id="06-barbie-1998-b" label="Untitled, from Barbie, 1998" caption="A black sequined dress forms a narrow, centered silhouette." />

The second *Barbie* makes the body itself a vertical measure. The shoulder line and portrait axis keep the figure centered, while the dress ellipse declares the dress, not the cropped face, as the main mass. A high mean luminance of 0.579 and low edge density of 0.019 leave the pale field unusually quiet. Against it, the near-black dress (#19161c) reads as a column whose fine highlights make an apparently simple silhouette unstable.

## western scale and staged history

<Plate slug="david-levinthal" id="07-wild-west-1988" label="Untitled, from Wild West, 1988" caption="A red-hatted figure and fence sit in layers of orange western space." />

In *Wild West* (1988), the red-hatted figure is small enough that the broad orange ground initially seems to be the subject. The ellipse gives the miniature its place; the fence polyline shows how a low lateral barrier makes it legible as a Western stage rather than a color field alone. This is among the darker selections (mean luminance 0.284), and its palette is built from brown-reds, dark maroon, tan, and a small pale accent. The staged world is cinematic because its layers are so few.

<Plate slug="david-levinthal" id="08-wild-west-1989" label="Untitled, from Wild West, 1989" caption="A rearing horse overwhelms a small cowboy in a warm, blurred field." />

The 1989 horse is not a normal animal portrait: its scale against the cowboy is the premise. Two ellipses make that unequal pairing explicit, and the rising contour carries the eye from the cowboy toward the horse's head. With only 0.021 edge density, the silhouette has to do more than detail could. Orange-brown ground and the horse's dark mass produce a toy battle of proportions that feels more persuasive because the image refuses sharp proof.

<Plate slug="david-levinthal" id="09-wild-west-sepia-1998" label="Untitled, from Wild West (sepia), 1998" caption="A cowboy occupies a centered doorway above converging floor boards." />

The sepia *Wild West* substitutes frontal containment for the earlier open field. The doorway axis, inner frame, and cowboy anchor align: a staged figure is placed inside an already staged architectural box. The analyzer finds a 0.379 mean luminance and a restrained palette of black-brown, tan, and gray-white. That limited range makes the soft verticals and floor boards feel like an old image recalled through a set, not an authentic period document.

## gestures that remake historical pictures

<Plate slug="david-levinthal" id="10-baseball-2004" label="Untitled, from Baseball, 2004" caption="A hitter's torso and bat sweep diagonally through a blurred stadium field." />

The *Baseball* player is a rotation rather than a portrait. The torso ellipse and anchor identify the mass that turns; the polyline follows the nearly horizontal extended swing arm toward the right edge, where the pose seems to outrun the frame. The measured edge density is the chapter's highest at 0.037, yet the p05–p95 span of 0.012–0.871 still leaves a heavily softened field around the striped uniform. Black-blue, gray-violet, and pale uniform tones convert movement into a broad tonal gesture.

<Plate slug="david-levinthal" id="11-iwo-jima-2013" label="Iwo Jima, from History, 2013" caption="The flagpole rises from a compact model figure group into a broad blue sky." />

In *Iwo Jima*, Levinthal retains the famous upward action while making it visibly a model scene. The figure-group ellipse gathers the clustered bodies; the flagpole ascent is the crucial line, rising into a field of blue with almost no competing detail. Edge density falls to 0.006, the lowest in the selection, while mean luminance rises to 0.552. Blue palette values (#5490b3, #8bb4c7, #486586) make the open sky an active expanse that the figures have to push against.

<Plate slug="david-levinthal" id="12-washington-crossing-delaware-2013" label="Washington Crossing the Delaware, from History, 2013" caption="Washington's bright figure and raised gesture organize a crowded historical tableau." />

*Washington Crossing the Delaware* is dense where *Iwo Jima* is spare. The Washington ellipse holds the bright central figure, while the raised-gesture polyline links him to the forward-pointing arm and the picture's leftward pull. The image's 0.102–1.000 tonal range makes white trousers and face blaze against black, red-brown, and blue groups. It is a crowded miniature, but hierarchy remains immediate: a single bright body turns a historical crowd into a staged command.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Make scale uncertain">
Build a scene from small figures and one large simple backdrop. Photograph it from low enough that the ground becomes a stage, then test how much selective focus you can remove before the figure stops carrying the image.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Let one gesture organize the frame">
Use a posed arm, tool, or body turn to make a single directional line. Keep the background broad and quiet; if the gesture does not still read when detail softens, change the pose rather than adding decoration.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Use color as the set">
Choose two dominant colors for a miniature scene and allow the palette to establish place before any prop does. Make one small contrasting accent carry the narrative focus.
</ExerciseCard>
