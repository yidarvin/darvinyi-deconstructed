# Minor White — build research

## Scope and evidence

This chapter uses the eleven Princeton University Art Museum collection records and their publicly served IIIF display renditions documented in `sources.md`. The selection begins with *Portland* (1938) and *Water Street, Portland* (1940), moves through California and San Francisco photographs made from 1947 to 1951, and ends with the Rochester window pictures (1958), *Santa Fe, New Mexico* (1966), and *Schoodic Point, Maine* (1969). Those titles and dates are catalog facts from the cited records.

Princeton identifies the Minor White Archive as the artist's most significant primary-source collection and describes its proof cards as important evidence of his creative process. The Museum's account of *The Sound of One Hand Clapping*, cited in `sources.md`, describes White's idea of equivalence as a search for formal relationships in nature that can reflect inner experience, and calls a sequence a “cinema of stills.” This chapter therefore treats the move from streets to windows, walls, rocks, and water as an argument made by the supplied pictures, rather than as a claim about a single fixed subject matter.

## Technique and printing

The supplied collection records identify photographs, dates, credits, and public display files. They do not establish a camera system, negative format, film stock, development method, paper, print process, edition state, or printing recipe for these individual works. The web JPEGs are later museum display reproductions, not technical records of White's negatives or prints. The chapter makes visual observations about their framing, tonal organization, and extracted measurements only; it does not infer unrecorded production methods.

## Camera and exposure information

The ingested manifest records no capture EXIF. Neither the cited Princeton records nor the archive context in `sources.md` supplies a camera body, lens, focal length, aperture, shutter speed, film speed, or exposure for these eleven photographs. No camera settings are asserted in the chapter.

## Analyzer observations for the supplied web reproductions

The deterministic analyses describe the normalized, 1600px-long-edge museum renditions rather than historical prints. They provide a consistent way to compare the early urban frames' converging edges, the reduced tonal fields of the window and wall pictures, and the directional rock-and-water structures in the later landscape. Palette and luminance statistics in the chapter are likewise measurements of these display files, not claims about the original print materials.
