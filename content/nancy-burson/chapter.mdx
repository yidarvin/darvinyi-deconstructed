Nancy Burson's composite portraits make the face look self-evident just long enough for that certainty to become suspicious. Across this selection, beauty, demographic classification, political authority, and age are all presented as frontal likenesses: centered heads, matching eye-lines, and controlled fields around the face. The overlays stay with those visible decisions. They do not claim that the machine has found a natural type; they show how symmetry and crop make a constructed aggregate read as a single person. [Source notes](../../content/nancy-burson/sources.md)

## A face made to stand for beauty

<Plate slug="nancy-burson" id="01-first-beauty-composite" label="First Beauty Composite: Bette Davis, Audrey Hepburn, Grace Kelly, Sophia Loren, Marilyn Monroe, 1982 (printed 1999)" caption="Five celebrity portraits resolve into a centered facial field whose eye-line and bilateral axis make an averaged ideal appear singular." />

*First Beauty Composite* makes averaging feel less like arithmetic than like a studio portrait. The overlay's vertical axis runs through the nose and mouth; its facial ellipse holds the blended hairline, cheeks, and chin as one continuous field. Princeton's record identifies these early 1982 Beauty Composites with what became facial-morphing software, while the work's title keeps the sources of the blend in view. The analyzer measures strong vertical symmetry (0.940) but very low edge density (0.007): facial difference is smoothed into broad gray transitions. Near-black #1f1c1a hair and a light #cfcfce face let the eyes and mouth emerge as a few concentrated interruptions.

## The frontal composite as a claim to neutrality

<Plate slug="nancy-burson" id="03-mankind" label="Mankind, 1983–85" caption="A frontal composite face is held on a central axis, while a shallow eye-line makes its apparent neutrality feel carefully constructed." />

*Mankind* places a demographic premise inside the quiet authority of a headshot. The source identifies it as a computer-generated silver print, but the composition is what makes that premise persuasive at first glance: the centered axis and broad facial field give both sides of the head nearly equal weight. Its vertical symmetry is 0.935, and the palette ranges from dark #090704 through gray-green #898d80 to pale #f0f4f5. With only 0.011 edge density, the picture gives little descriptive clutter to compete with the face. The result is not an objective portrait; it is a deliberately stabilized surface on which a social classification can appear natural.

<Plate slug="nancy-burson" id="04-mankind-ii" label="Mankind II, 1983" caption="A tight rectangular crop presses a frontal composite face against a pale, grainy field instead of allowing a neutral surrounding space." />

The gelatin silver print *Mankind II* makes the same frontal logic more forceful by tightening the pressure at the frame. The overlay's axis holds the nose and mouth while the facial field nearly fills the rectangle; this is an enclosure, not merely a centered subject. The analyzer again finds high vertical symmetry (0.929), but the low edge density (0.006) and p95 luminance of 1.000 give the surface a bleached, grainy openness. Pale #edeeec and #c5c6c2 surround the dark #131414 hair and eyes. The closer format changes the rhetorical temperature: the blend does not sit for inspection so much as confront the viewer.

## Authority and age applied to an icon

<Plate slug="nancy-burson" id="02-the-president-second-version" label="The President (second version), 1988" caption="A tall, dark color field, centered face, and jacket V make a five-president composite borrow the formal authority of an official portrait." />

In *The President (second version)*, the central head is small enough to be surrounded by a commanding dark reserve, then stabilized by the jacket's rising V. The source identifies a color photoprint and a composite of five U.S. presidents; the overlay marks the portrait axis, face, and jacket contour rather than mistaking the dark field for a receding room. Its mean luminance is only 0.115, with #000000 and dark purple #3f1a40 dominating the palette. The color face and shirt flashes therefore acquire an institutional gravity: authority here is not discovered in a face but staged by vertical crop, centered gaze, and dress.

<Plate slug="nancy-burson" id="05-aged-barbie" label="Aged Barbie, 1994" caption="A symmetrical doll face is enclosed in a dark inner field, making the simulation of age conspicuous against an already standardized consumer icon." />

*Aged Barbie* closes the sequence by treating a manufactured face as an equally manufactured age. The documented medium is a computer composite. Within the outer pale surround, the overlay frames the dark portrait field, then places the doll axis and eye-line through the aging face. This is the group's strongest symmetry reading (0.974), and the frame makes that artificial regularity visible before the wrinkles or altered skin are read. Blue-black #0d171b surrounds pale #c8d2ce and teal-gray #63847f, keeping the face isolated like a small display image. The simulation does not hide its intervention; the centered, toy-like format makes it something to inspect.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Test the authority of an axis">
Make two portraits of the same person against a simple background. In the first, align nose, mouth, and both eyes exactly to a vertical centerline. In the second, move the face just far enough off that line for the balance to loosen. Compare which version begins to sound like a type rather than a person, and identify the crop or background change that produces that effect.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Make an aggregate visibly constructed">
Without blending faces, arrange several images or details of one subject into a single frontal field. Preserve one seam, mismatch, or tonal shift instead of smoothing everything away. Photograph or scan the result, then decide whether its remaining discontinuity exposes the assembly or simply looks accidental.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Use the surrounding field">
Photograph a face with a large dark or pale reserve around it. Change only the amount of empty field and the height of the crop. Keep the version in which the surrounding tone alters the social role the portrait seems to propose: official, commercial, intimate, or diagnostic, without adding props.
</ExerciseCard>
