# Nancy Burson — source shortlist

This focused working set follows Burson's computer-generated portrait practice from the early Beauty Composites through composite race, political identity, and age. The five selected museum display files are all direct, publicly accessible JPEG responses and were verified as opening successfully. Each is at least 900px on its long edge; no image was enlarged, generated, or retouched.

**Rights basis:** **fair-use** for every selection. These are copyrighted works, not open-licensed files. Each individual use is limited to a credited, linked, web-sized copy for this nonprofit book's transformative criticism, scholarship, and composition teaching. The complete published frame is needed to analyze the image's facial blending, crop, and figure/ground structure; the copies neither replace the collection record, original print, nor a licensed reproduction.

## Shortlist

### 01 — *First Beauty Composite: Bette Davis, Audrey Hepburn, Grace Kelly, Sophia Loren, Marilyn Monroe* (1982, printed 1999)
- **Why it matters:** Burson turns five celebrity portraits into one frontal, almost icon-like face, making the centered crop and the smoothing of distinct features visible as an argument about constructed beauty.
- **Source:** https://www.clevelandart.org/art/2020.75 (Cleveland Museum of Art, accession 2020.75; public display JPEG: `https://piction.clevelandart.org/cma/ump.di?e=AB15031B79956439D98BDCA4F950CBC83A5266A503633EB6F906523AF5C03394&s=24247294&se=685090774&v=1&f=2020.75_w.jpg`)
- **Rights / fair-use assessment:** Nancy Burson; © Nancy Burson; Cleveland Museum of Art identifies the work as copyrighted. **Factor 1:** transformative composition criticism examines the frontal composite and its beauty standard. **Factor 2:** a highly creative photograph. **Factor 3:** the complete square-ish portrait is needed to assess hair, eyes, mouth, and facial outline together. **Factor 4:** the credited 900 × 859px public display rendition is collection-linked and cannot substitute for the print or a licensed reproduction.
- **File:** `raw/nancy-burson/01-first-beauty-composite.jpg` (900 × 859px)

## Research context

Princeton University Art Museum's record for *First and Second Beauty Composites* documents that the early 1982 works used what became facial-morphing software and explicitly contrasts 1950s and 1980s beauty ideals: https://artmuseum.princeton.edu/art/collections/objects/137156. The Smithsonian American Art Museum biography records Burson's early collaboration with engineers and the 1981 face-aging patent: https://americanart.si.edu/artist/nancy-burson-679. The Met's *Warhead I* record confirms the political-composite branch of the same early practice, though its public display image is deliberately not acquired here because the museum states it cannot be downloaded or enlarged: https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/266955.
