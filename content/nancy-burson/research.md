# Nancy Burson — research note

## What the supplied sources establish

The supplied museum records identify five works made between 1982 and 1994 and
document their public display derivatives. Princeton University Art Museum
describes the 1982 Beauty Composites as using what became facial-morphing
software; Smithsonian American Art Museum records Burson's early collaboration
with engineers and a 1981 face-aging patent. These sources establish a practice
of computer-generated composite portraiture, not a camera-based account of each
individual work. [Source notes](sources.md)

The individual records identify *First Beauty Composite* as a 1982 work printed
in 1999; *Mankind* as a computer-generated silver print; *Mankind II* as a
gelatin silver print; *The President (second version)* as a color photoprint;
and *Aged Barbie* as a computer composite. Those are work-specific medium or
presentation facts and are not generalized across the selection.

## Technical limits for this chapter

The supplied records do not establish a camera body, lens, focal length,
aperture, shutter speed, sensitivity, film or sensor format, lighting scheme,
capture workflow, or per-work processing workflow. The ingested museum-display
JPEGs retain no camera EXIF. This chapter therefore treats the documented
compositing practice, frontal alignment, crop, visible grain, measured tone,
and palette as evidence; it does not convert the digital files' appearance into
invented capture settings or undocumented print processes.
