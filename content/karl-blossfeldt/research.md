# Karl Blossfeldt — research

Scope: technique, era, printing, and recorded camera settings for the twelve
plates in this chapter. Where the historical record is silent (as it is for
almost every per-plate exposure value), this says so rather than inventing a
number.

## Who and when

Karl Blossfeldt (1865–1932) was not a professional photographer. He trained as a
sculptor and modeller and taught "modelling from living plants" at the
Unterrichtsanstalt des Kunstgewerbemuseums (the teaching institute of the Berlin
Museum of Decorative Arts) from 1898 to 1930. The photographs were built as
teaching aids: a reference archive of plant forms his students could draw from.
He made them steadily across roughly three decades, from the late 1890s into the
1920s. Only late in life were they seen as art, when a 1926 gallery showing led
to the book that made him famous.

- *Urformen der Kunst* (Art Forms in Nature), 1928 — 120 plates, the source of
  most of the specimens here.
- *Wundergarten der Natur* (The Wonder Garden of Nature), 1932.

## Technique

Blossfeldt built his own cameras. They were plain vertical or horizontal copy
stands fitted with home-made bellows and simple lenses, designed to do one thing:
photograph a single dried or living plant part, frontally, against a plain
cardboard ground, at high magnification. Reported magnifications for the series
run from about 3× to as much as 30×–45× depending on the specimen. He worked in
even, diffuse daylight in the studio, which is why the grounds are flat gray and
the modelling on each stem is soft and continuous rather than raked by hard
shadow. Specimens were often stripped, dried, and sometimes wired or trimmed to
show structure clearly, which is part of why the geometry reads so cleanly.

## Printing

The original working images are gelatin silver prints. The 1928 and 1932 books
reproduced them in high-quality collotype/photogravure, which preserved the long,
even gray scale that makes the specimens look carved. The digitizations used here
are from museum holdings of the plates (Getty, Rijksmuseum, and other public-
domain scans on Wikimedia Commons).

## Camera settings

Not recorded. Blossfeldt's cameras were bespoke and undocumented at the level of
individual exposures; no per-plate aperture, shutter, or focal-length data
survives, and the digital files carry only scanner metadata. What can be stated
on the record is qualitative and follows from the method: high-magnification
close-up work in diffuse light implies small working apertures for depth of field
and correspondingly long exposures of static, dead-still specimens. Any specific
f-stop or shutter speed would be a guess, so none is given.
