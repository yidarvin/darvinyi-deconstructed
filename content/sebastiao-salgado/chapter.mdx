Sebastião Salgado's photographs do not make labour or hardship legible by reducing people to symbols. They make a bodily scale hold against something much larger: a mine wall, a field, a cemetery, a door, or a sky. This selection moves from the dense excavation of *Serra Pelada* to compressed portraits and a radically low viewpoint. The overlays mark visible routes, containers, faces, tools, and points of pressure; the underlying museum records and rights rationale are in the [source notes](../../content/sebastiao-salgado/sources.md), and the documented print medium and production limits are in the [research notes](../../content/sebastiao-salgado/research.md).

The supplied display JPEGs give the chapter a broad tonal range, but not a single uniform look. *Ecuador* is darkest by measured mean luminance (0.325) and has the widest standard deviation (0.313); *Serra Pelada, east slope* has the greatest edge density (0.208), while *Guatemala* is much sparser (0.069). Those are measurements of credited web renditions, not claims about the original gelatin silver prints. The source records identify the prints as gelatin silver, but do not provide camera, lens, film, exposure, or darkroom settings for these photographs.

## a body gives the field its measure

<Plate slug="sebastiao-salgado" id="01-serra-pelada-east-slope" label="Serra Pelada, State of Para, Brazil, 1986" caption="A low procession and an upright timber give the crowded excavation a sequence of bodily measures." />

The first *Serra Pelada* plate refuses an aerial abstraction. A procession crosses the lower foreground, while the upright timber holds one abrupt vertical amid hundreds of smaller movements. The overlay uses the carrier group as a local anchor and follows the low diagonal through the bodies rather than trying to inventory every route in the pit. Its very high edge density is visible in the granular crowd, tools, rock, and garments. Yet the pale-to-dark range, from p05 0.133 to p95 0.945, stops the image from becoming visual noise: the workers in front remain recognisable as people, not texture.

<Plate slug="sebastiao-salgado" id="02-serra-pelada-procession" label="Serra Pelada, State of Para, Brazil, 1986" caption="Rail, climbing bodies, and the bowl-like excavation turn repeated circulation into the image's scale." />

The second view moves farther back. The pit makes a rough enclosing bowl and the rail cuts across it like a low, provisional bridge. The right-hand climbers are small, but the overlay gives them a precise place against the enclosure; that is enough to make the excavation's scale felt as effort rather than spectacle. At edge density 0.234, the densest measured file in the set, the picture is packed with routes that almost become pattern. The lower rail interrupts that pattern, making the eye travel across the mine before it drops toward the climbers.

<Plate slug="sebastiao-salgado" id="04-serra-pelada-ladder" label="Serra Pelada, State of Para, Brazil, 1986" caption="A reaching hand, weighted body, and improvised ladder make ascent feel unstable and exact." />

The ladder plate brings that same system to one body. The far rim gives the background a thin, high register, but the photograph's force comes from the hand at left, the man's bent shoulders, and the ladder rungs beneath him. The overlay's strap and ladder contours do not decorate the scene; they explain its counterweights. The file is lighter on average (0.593) and comparatively spare in edges (0.092), so the climber is not swallowed by the mine. He occupies an open enough field for balance, fatigue, and the risk of the step to be read separately.

## small frames, concentrated relations

<Plate slug="sebastiao-salgado" id="03-guatemala" label="Guatemala, 1978" caption="A fruit tray and a face in a small window establish two unequal but answering fields of attention." />

In *Guatemala*, the division is architectural instead of geological. The child and fruit tray occupy the open left side; a second face appears within the framed opening at right. The large door does not merely fill space between them. It makes the distance active, so the two faces feel linked without being brought together. The overlay keeps its claims small: a tray, a face, and the literal window frame, because the picture's power lies in that unequal exchange. Its restrained edge density and pale #e1e3de-to-dark #333332 palette help the broad wall remain a real field of separation.

<Plate slug="sebastiao-salgado" id="05-mexico" label="Mexico, 1980" caption="A resting dog occupies the central tomb step while candles and monuments recede into haze." />

*Mexico* turns a cemetery into a stepped theatre. The dog is quiet at the centre, but the scene is not still: tomb edges climb toward a small mourner, and candles make a bright low band against the foreground black. The overlay's ellipse is deliberately modest; it names the dog without pretending that the animal alone explains the image. The supplied file's p05 0.102 and p95 0.941 show how far its black-and-white range extends. Those dark tomb faces and pale mist allow the dog to act as a pause inside a much larger ritual space.

<Plate slug="sebastiao-salgado" id="06-three-coal-miners-india" label="Three Coal Miners, India, from the series Workers, 1989" caption="A pickaxe and a receding line of faces compress a worksite into a direct, frontal encounter." />

The miners' portrait changes scale again. Three faces recede from left to right, but the foremost face nearly fills the frame; the pickaxe cuts up from the lower edge and refuses the calm of a conventional head-and-shoulders portrait. The overlay separates the bright turban fold, the frontal face, the three-person sequence, and the tool because each carries a different kind of pressure. The file's relatively low p95 of 0.816 keeps the highlight range restrained. Rather than setting the men against a luminous distance, the photograph holds them in a close, tactile tonal register.

## a low viewpoint reverses the hierarchy

<Plate slug="sebastiao-salgado" id="07-ecuador" label="Ecuador, 1982" caption="A prone face fills the foreground while standing figures press across the bright field above." />

In *Ecuador*, a prone face takes the whole lower frame and four standing figures look down from the bright upper field. The low viewpoint turns the usual social relation inside out: the face closest to us is physically dominant, yet it is watched from above. The overlay follows the foreground hairline into the face and the shallow row of heads above it. These are visible directional relations, not claims about a literal horizon or a calibrated perspective system. With mean luminance 0.325, p05 0.051, and p95 0.941, this is the chapter's most forceful black-and-white split: the bright field reads less as atmosphere than as exposure around the silhouetted group.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Give a large field one human measure">
Photograph a person in a space that could otherwise read as pattern: a construction site, crowd, stairwell, field, or market. Make the person small, then adjust your position until one route or upright structure lets the viewer judge the field through that body.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Build an unequal exchange">
Make a picture with two subjects held apart by a wall, doorway, window, or other hard division. Keep one subject small or enclosed. The aim is not symmetry: test whether the empty interval makes the two presences answer each other.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Reverse the viewpoint">
Photograph one person close to the lens and several people above or behind them. Keep the background simple enough that the near face and the group compete clearly. Compare a level viewpoint with a low one and note how authority changes.
</ExerciseCard>
