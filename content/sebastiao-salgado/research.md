# Sebastião Salgado — research notes

## Scope and record

This chapter studies seven photographs made between 1978 and 1989: five Art
Institute of Chicago (AIC) works and one Getty work, plus a second AIC view of
*Serra Pelada*. The AIC collection records identify the six AIC objects as
gelatin silver prints, and Getty identifies *Ecuador* as a gelatin silver print.
The source records therefore support discussing these as black-and-white
silver-gelatin exhibition prints; they do not document a common print date,
paper, developer, or darkroom workflow for the group.

The dates and subjects form a compact late-1970s/1980s sequence: *Guatemala*
(1978), *Mexico* (1980), *Ecuador* (1982), the three *Serra Pelada* photographs
(1986), and *Three Coal Miners, India* from *Workers* (1989). The chapter uses
those dates only to order the photographs historically, not to claim that every
formal choice is programmatic across the series.

## Technique and production limits

The source records document gelatin silver prints and dimensions of roughly
29–30 cm by 44–45 cm for the image areas represented by the museum copies. The
available web JPEGs are monochrome display renditions, so the chapter can make
measured observations about their luminance, palette, edge density, and visible
framing. Those measurements are not a substitute for inspecting the original
prints.

Neither the linked AIC and Getty records nor the ingested files document a
camera body, lens, focal length, aperture, shutter speed, film stock, negative
format, exposure method, lighting setup, print date, paper, developer, or
darkroom procedure for these individual photographs. The ingested files contain
no EXIF. No camera setting or undocumented production method is inferred here.

## Visual through-line

The selection alternates between two scales. In the *Serra Pelada* plates,
individual bodies make an excavation measurable: routes, ladders, timber, and
the mine rim turn labour into a dense field whose immensity is never abstract.
In *Guatemala*, *Mexico*, *Three Coal Miners*, and *Ecuador*, an isolated face,
animal, tool, or prone figure gives a larger social space its point of pressure.
Across both modes, Salgado's black-and-white framing depends on a controlled
exchange between near, tactile dark masses and more distant, lighter structure.
