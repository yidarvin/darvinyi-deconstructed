# Hippolyte Bayard — sources

Bayard's tiny surviving oeuvre reads as a set of deliberate stagings rather than casual records: a self-portrait arranged as a corpse, a garden self-portrait built around a barrel and a trellis-grid backdrop, statuary posed dead-center behind a parted curtain like a stage set. His calotype-paper Paris views carry the era's oval vignette mask, which itself imposes a soft centered frame on whatever sits inside it — a circus rotunda, a column, a fountain — pushing his subjects toward strict frontal symmetry on the vertical axis. Where the mask is absent, as in the panorama and the street view, he instead uses a level horizon and an even, atmospheric gray to flatten deep space into horizontal bands. It is a small body of work, but a compositionally disciplined one: staged tableau, oval-framed symmetry, and flattened perspective recur across every genre he touched.

**Rights basis:** Public domain worldwide. Bayard died in 1887, so all work is long out of copyright under any life+70 term. Seven of the ten images below are salted-paper prints from the J. Paul Getty Museum, released through the Getty Open Content Program (no known copyright restrictions); two — one a direct-positive print (02), one an albumen silver print from a glass negative (03) — are from the Metropolitan Museum of Art's Open Access program (CC0). *Self-Portrait as a Drowned Man* — the one image whose surviving prints are held by the Société française de photographie rather than an Open Access museum — is sourced from a Wikimedia Commons scan credited to the Musée de l'Elysée / Rudolfinum, both of which mark the work public domain; no NEEDED.md entry was required since every shortlisted image downloaded cleanly.

## Shortlist

### 01 — Self-Portrait as a Drowned Man (1840)
- **Why it matters:** Photography's first staged fiction — Bayard poses as his own corpse, head tipped onto his shoulder, hands crossed at the wrist, a hat propped beside him like a grave marker; the whole tableau centered and lit flat so the staging reads as documentary fact.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Bayard,_Hippolyte_1801-1887_-_Selfportrait_as_a_Drowned_man_1840_b.jpg
- **Rights:** Public domain (original held by Société française de photographie, Paris; scan credited to Musée de l'Elysée / Rudolfinum)
- **File:** raw/hippolyte-bayard/01-self-portrait-drowned-man.jpg (1500px)

### 02 — Windmills, Montmartre (1839)
- **Why it matters:** One of Bayard's earliest surviving direct-positive prints — the Montmartre windmill sails sit as dark silhouettes against a pale, atmospheric sky, roofline and horizon both held level so the whole print reads as a study in tonal recession rather than incident.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/285909
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/hippolyte-bayard/02-windmills-montmartre.jpg (3007px)

### 03 — Still Life with Statuary (early 1850s)
- **Why it matters:** A niche-like arrangement of plaster casts — a standing male nude, the Venus de Milo, two putti medallions and small figurines on brackets — framed by a parted curtain into a strict bilateral composition, the statuary standing in for portrait sitters.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/285907
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/hippolyte-bayard/03-still-life-statuary.jpg (3812px)

### 04 — Self-Portrait in the Garden (1847)
- **Why it matters:** Bayard stands beside a barrel that both anchors the composition and steadies his long exposure, a ladder and lattice trelliswork behind him breaking the background into a rigid grid against which his figure reads in silhouette.
- **Source:** https://www.getty.edu/art/collection/object/104KS0
- **Rights:** Public domain (J. Paul Getty Museum, Getty Open Content)
- **File:** raw/hippolyte-bayard/04-self-portrait-garden.jpg (4364px)

### 05 — Construction Worker, Paris (1845–1847)
- **Why it matters:** A rare social-documentary frame for its date — a worker stands on a low scaffold beneath a shuttered, broken-paned window, the oval vignette mask and the barred window below compressing the scene into a tight gridlike composition softened at the edges.
- **Source:** https://www.getty.edu/art/collection/object/71399
- **Rights:** Public domain (J. Paul Getty Museum, Getty Open Content)
- **File:** raw/hippolyte-bayard/05-construction-worker-paris.jpg (8852px)

### 06 — Cirque National, Paris (1847)
- **Why it matters:** The circular rotunda is placed dead-center beneath the oval mask, its colonnade and cornice tracing concentric curves that echo the frame itself — architecture and vignette locked into a single radial symmetry.
- **Source:** https://www.getty.edu/art/collection/object/102025
- **Rights:** Public domain (J. Paul Getty Museum, Getty Open Content)
- **File:** raw/hippolyte-bayard/06-cirque-national-paris.jpg (6006px)

### 07 — Panorama of Paris with Louvre and Tuileries in Foreground (1847)
- **Why it matters:** An extreme letterboxed format — rooftops, domes and spires strung along a single level horizon in flat atmospheric gray — Bayard building depth entirely through tonal recession rather than converging lines.
- **Source:** https://www.getty.edu/art/collection/object/101999
- **Rights:** Public domain (J. Paul Getty Museum, Getty Open Content)
- **File:** raw/hippolyte-bayard/07-panorama-louvre-tuileries.jpg (6062px)

### 08 — Rue Royale, Paris (about 1840–1849)
- **Why it matters:** A raking view down a row of shopfronts and apartment facades — repeating window bays and hand-painted signage ("DENTISTE," "GALERIE DE LA MARINE") recede toward a vanishing point, an early instance of the flattened, sign-covered streetscape later genres would return to.
- **Source:** https://www.getty.edu/art/collection/object/101988
- **Rights:** Public domain (J. Paul Getty Museum, Getty Open Content)
- **File:** raw/hippolyte-bayard/08-rue-royale-shopfronts.jpg (6018px)

### 09 — Column of barrière du Trône, Paris (November 1849)
- **Why it matters:** A single fluted column plants itself on the frame's vertical axis, its statue-topped capital nearly touching the top edge — the purest possible expression of Bayard's centered, symmetrical monument studies.
- **Source:** https://www.getty.edu/art/collection/object/101996
- **Rights:** Public domain (J. Paul Getty Museum, Getty Open Content)
- **File:** raw/hippolyte-bayard/09-column-barriere-du-trone.jpg (5480px)

### 10 — La Fontaine des Quatre Évêques, Place St. Sulpice, Paris (about 1848)
- **Why it matters:** Shot dead-on and close, the fountain's niche, seated bishop and flanking columns fill the frame in strict mirror symmetry, the shallow foreground water adding a soft horizontal band beneath the vertical architecture.
- **Source:** https://www.getty.edu/art/collection/object/101984
- **Rights:** Public domain (J. Paul Getty Museum, Getty Open Content)
- **File:** raw/hippolyte-bayard/10-fontaine-quatre-eveques.jpg (6431px)
