# Henri Cartier-Bresson — source shortlist

Cartier-Bresson's early Leica photographs make framing feel like an event rather than a container: stair rails, doorways, paving, shadows, and bodies arrive in a briefly exact relation. This working set emphasizes the 1930–38 European and Mexican photographs where his geometrical, surrealist-inflected street practice became the basis for the later shorthand of the “decisive moment.” It includes twelve authoritative collection-record display renditions, each retrieved directly from the Art Institute of Chicago's public IIIF service and checked as an opening JPEG. The supplied IIIF endpoint constrains these web copies to roughly 843px on their long edges—above the 640px normal floor, but deliberately modest rather than production-print substitutes.

**Rights basis:** **fair-use** for every selection. The Art Institute API marks each listed work `is_public_domain: false`; the author is Henri Cartier-Bresson (1908–2004), and collection records for this corpus credit the work to Henri Cartier-Bresson / Magnum Photos. Each assessment below is individual: this nonprofit book uses the complete frame for transformative criticism, scholarship, and composition teaching; the whole frame is necessary to discuss the pictured spatial relation; only a web-sized derivative is retained; and the chapter credits and links to the authoritative Art Institute record rather than replacing the record, an original print, or a licensed reproduction.

## Research context

The Fondation Henri Cartier-Bresson identifies *Place de l'Europe, behind the Saint-Lazare station, Paris, France* (1932) as a signature work and its 2017 *Images à la Sauvette* press material describes the 1952 book as the project whose American title made “The Decisive Moment” inseparable from Cartier-Bresson's reputation: https://www.henricartierbresson.org/en/publications/a6-notebooks/ and https://www.henricartierbresson.org/wp-content/uploads/2016/12/Press-Kit-Images-a%CC%80-la-Sauvette-FHCB-GB.pdf. The present acquired set deliberately broadens that single famous jump into a teachable sequence of architecture, delay, crowding, and shifting human scale, while retaining only publicly served, web-sized institutional display copies.
