# Henri Cartier-Bresson — research note

## Record and period

The supplied Art Institute of Chicago records date this selection primarily to 1930–38, with later print dates recorded for *Hyères, France*, *Allées du Prado, Marseilles*, *Juvisy, France*, and *Arsila, Spanish Morocco*. The source shortlist locates these photographs in Cartier-Bresson's early, geometrical street practice and notes its surrealist inflection. Fondation Henri Cartier-Bresson's material in the source notes identifies the 1952 *Images à la Sauvette* as the project whose American title, *The Decisive Moment*, became inseparable from his reputation.

## Technique and printing

The Art Institute records identify all twelve works as gelatin silver prints and provide dates and, in several cases, a print period. They do not document a camera model for a particular exposure, lens, focal length, aperture, shutter speed, film stock, negative format, enlarger, paper, or developer for these web reproductions. The ingested JPEGs contain no capture EXIF. Those absent particulars are not reconstructed here. The selection should not be called uniformly Leica-made: Trieste is dated 1930, whereas the Fondation HCB biography dates Cartier-Bresson's first Leica to 1931–32.

What the images show is a black-and-white practice responsive to found geometry: railings, pavement seams, tree rows, walls, tables, stacked textiles, and body positions act as timing devices. The composition analysis confirms that the plates range from sparse, broad tonal fields (Mexico City has edge density 0.015) to denser textured accumulations (Quai de Javel, Paris has edge density 0.069). This is evidence about the supplied reproductions' visible structure, not a claim about historical exposure settings or original print materials.
