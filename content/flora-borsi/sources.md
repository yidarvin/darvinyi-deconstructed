# Flóra Borsi — source record

## Selection and acquisition note

This twelve-image working set is drawn from the artist's official public
*Animeyed* project display.  The project describes the ongoing self-portrait
series as a dialogue between humans and animals, built by sharing one eye with
an animal and using makeup, costume, and digital post-production.  That stated
method makes the group a particularly concentrated source for a chapter about
visual substitution, facial symmetry, and the way colour and contour can fuse
two subjects into one portrait.  The site presents the images without individual
captions; the bracketed animal descriptors below are descriptive working titles,
not asserted catalogue titles, and the displayed project page does not supply a
date for an individual file.  The site was accessed on 2026-07-24.

All twelve files were retrieved as direct, publicly visible official display
responses, converted only from the server's WebP delivery where necessary to
ordinary JPEG files, opened locally, and verified at 1,186–1,750px on their long
edge.  No image was upscaled, retouched, or cropped.

**Rights basis:** **fair-use** for every item.  The official site footer reads
“Copyright © 2026 Flóra Borsi”; the project page identifies Flóra Borsi as the
artist and author of the series.  Each use is a web-sized, credited critical and
scholarly reproduction for transformative composition teaching, not a decorative
or commercial substitute.  The complete frame is necessary for the lessons below
because the animal, face, field, and exact point of overlap form the work's
composition.  The works are highly creative, which weighs against use, but every
copy links to the artist's own project record and does not replace an original,
limited edition, licensed reproduction, or the artist's sales channels.

## Research context

The [official *Animeyed* project statement](https://www.floraborsi.com/projects/animeyed)
explains that the series shares one of Borsi's eyes with an animal and treats the
animals as equal protagonists.  The [Savina Museum exhibition record](https://www.artsy.net/article/savina-museum-of-contemporary-art-flora-borsi-anime)
corroborates the series' process: Borsi studies each animal's colour and form,
photographs herself with complementary makeup and lighting, then joins the
images in post-production.  That record supports an analysis of these works as
precise constructed portraiture rather than uncredited animal photography.
