# Louis Daguerre — sources

Daguerre's surviving photographic output is not a body of work in the sense later photographers built one — it is a handful of experimental plates made in 1837–39 to prove that his process could hold texture, tone, and depth at all. The compositional signature that emerges from them is nonetheless real: a camera fixed at a high window looking down and out along a street or river (the only way to get a stationary subject during exposures of five to fifteen minutes), or a tabletop still life lit from one side and built for maximum tonal range — plaster casts, shells, fossils, arranged the way a painter would block a study. Because the plate could not stop motion, Daguerre's outdoor views are inadvertent studies in erasure: traffic and pedestrians vanish, and whatever remains legible does so only because it held still long enough. That accident — long exposure as a compositional filter — is the through-line of this chapter.

**Rights basis:** Public domain. Daguerre died in 1851; every surviving plate was made in 1836–39, decades before any copyright term in any jurisdiction could still apply, and all are published pre-1928. Fewer than 25 daguerreotypes worldwide are securely attributed to Daguerre himself (most of his 1824–39 experimental work was destroyed when the Diorama burned on 8 March 1839), so this shortlist is short by nature — three plates cleared the download bar at usable resolution; three more canonical survivors are real but only available as small reproduction scans, and are logged in NEEDED.md rather than padded in here.

## Shortlist

### 01 — Boulevard du Temple (c. 1838)
- **Why it matters:** The exposure ran long enough to erase every moving cart and pedestrian on a normally crowded boulevard, leaving a clean diagonal recession of building facades down to a vanishing point — and one man in the lower left, motionless getting his boots shined, who became the first person ever captured in a photograph purely by holding still. Composition here is a side effect of exposure time, not a photographer's manual choice about a moving subject — the earliest demonstration that duration itself frames a picture.
- **Source:** https://www.bayerisches-nationalmuseum.de/en/collection/00222500 (Bayerisches Nationalmuseum, Munich — holds the original plate, part of the triptych gifted by Daguerre to King Ludwig I of Bavaria in 1839); reproduction via https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Boulevard_du_Temple_by_Daguerre.jpg
- **Rights:** Public domain (pre-1928 work; Daguerre d. 1851)
- **File:** raw/louis-daguerre/01-boulevard-du-temple.jpg (3441px)

### 02 — L'Atelier de l'artiste / Still Life with Plaster Casts (1837)
- **Why it matters:** The earliest surviving daguerreotype Daguerre made — plaster casts, a framed drawing, and a wicker-cased bottle built into a tight pyramidal still life under raking side light, evidently staged to wring out every register of tone the new process could hold, from chalk-white cast to black shadow. It is a painter's studio arrangement translated directly into the photographic vocabulary of value and edge.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Daguerreotype_Daguerre_Atelier_1837.jpg (plate held by the Société française de photographie, Paris)
- **Rights:** Public domain (pre-1928 work; Daguerre d. 1851)
- **File:** raw/louis-daguerre/02-atelier-plaster-casts.jpg (3200px)

### 03 — View of the Pont Neuf, Paris (c. 1836–39, with Mathurin-Joseph Fordos)
- **Why it matters:** Reportedly the first daguerreotype ever attempted outdoors, made with the chemist Mathurin-Joseph Fordos: the bridge parapet and the statue of Henri IV drive a strong diagonal across the plate toward the Louvre and the quay, and two motionless figures resting in the statue's shadow survive the long exposure the same way the bootblack does in Boulevard du Temple — stillness as the price of legibility.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:1836_39_Pont_Neuf_-_Daguerre_and_Fordos.jpg (plate held by the Musée des Arts et Métiers, Paris)
- **Rights:** Public domain (pre-1928 work; joint credit Daguerre/Fordos, both long deceased)
- **File:** raw/louis-daguerre/03-pont-neuf.jpg (1310px)
