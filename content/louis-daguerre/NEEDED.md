# Louis Daguerre — needed / flagged (not cleanly available at usable resolution)

Canonical Daguerre plates that fit the chapter's signature but where every open-access reproduction found tops out well under the 1200px long-edge floor — the plates themselves are tiny (several under 6 x 5 cm) and no institution has published a high-resolution scan through Wikimedia Commons, Gallica, Getty, or the Met. Listed here for reference; do not ingest a low-resolution scan just to fill the slot.

## Coquillages et fossiles / Shells and Fossils (c. 1839)
- **Why it matters:** A cabinet still life shot inside the Muséum national d'Histoire naturelle — three shelves of shells and fossils (gastropods, an ammonite, a sea-urchin imprint, a petrified sponge) arranged and lit exactly like the plaster-cast atelier study, confirming the tabletop-still-life half of Daguerre's compositional range against the street-view half.
- **Rights:** Public domain (pre-1928 work; Daguerre d. 1851) — rights are not the obstacle, resolution is.
- **Where a good print/scan lives:** Musée des Arts et Métiers, Paris (holding institution). The only reproductions found online (Wikimedia Commons "Zeno Fotografie" scans, USC Annenberg-sourced copy) max out at roughly 1000 x 733px — well under the usable floor. A high-resolution museum scan may exist behind a paywall/reading-room request but was not found openly downloadable.

## Portrait of M. Huet (1837)
- **Why it matters:** A tightly cropped head-and-shoulders daguerreotype made in the Natural History Museum's Cabinet, rue du Jardin-du-Roi — if the 1837 date on the verso is accepted, this is the oldest surviving photographic portrait of a human being, frontal and plainly lit, the origin point of the photographic portrait as a genre.
- **Rights:** Public domain (pre-1928 work; Daguerre d. 1851) — rights are not the obstacle, resolution is. (Note: attribution to Daguerre was called "problematic" by some scholars for lacking firm provenance, though more recent technical/signature analysis is reported to support it — flagged here for resolution, not for rights or authenticity doubt.)
- **Where a good print/scan lives:** Private collection (sold at auction; art-market valuation over EUR 1 million). Every open reproduction located (Wikimedia Commons, PICRYL) is a small secondary scan, largest found being 754 x 854px — under the usable floor.

## Notre-Dame et l'Île de la Cité (c. 1838)
- **Why it matters:** A view from Daguerre's apartment window across the Seine to Notre-Dame and the Île de la Cité — likely the earliest surviving photographic image of the cathedral, and a companion outdoor view to Boulevard du Temple and the Pont Neuf plate, extending the elevated-window vantage point across the chapter.
- **Rights:** Public domain (pre-1928 work; Daguerre d. 1851) — rights are not the obstacle, resolution is.
- **Where a good print/scan lives:** Comparable plates are held by the History of Science Museum, Oxford, and in private French collections; one related Notre-Dame daguerreotype view is credited to the Centre des monuments nationaux via Google Arts & Culture, which does not offer a direct high-resolution download. The only openly downloadable Commons copies max out at 800 x 597px — under the usable floor.
