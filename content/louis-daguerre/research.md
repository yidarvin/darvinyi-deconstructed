# Louis Daguerre — research

Scope: technique, era, printing, and recorded camera settings for the three
plates in this chapter. The supplied source record identifies them as
experimental daguerreotypes from 1837–1839: two outdoor views made while
testing what a long exposure could retain, and one arranged studio still
life. It does not provide a per-image technical log.

## Technique and era

The 1837 still life belongs to the formative years of the daguerreotype; the
two Paris views date to about 1836–39, before or around the public launch of
the process in 1839. The sources describe these as plates rather than paper
prints, and place the outdoor views at a high window or riverside prospect
where an immobile camera could hold architecture long enough to register.
The Pont Neuf image carries a joint credit with the chemist Mathurin-Joseph
Fordos; that joint attribution is retained rather than assigning the plate to
Daguerre alone.

## Printing and object history

These are unique daguerreotype objects, not negative-to-paper print chains.
The chapter uses reproductions of the original plates: the Boulevard plate is
held by the Bayerisches Nationalmuseum, the atelier still life by the Société
française de photographie, and the Pont Neuf plate by the Musée des Arts et
Métiers. Their reproduction surfaces retain scratches, vignetting, and
plate-scale tonal irregularities; those are not treated as scene geometry in
the overlays.

## Camera settings

No aperture, focal length, or sensitization record is supplied for these
individual plates, and the ingested files carry no EXIF data. The historical
source record gives only a qualitative range for the outdoor work: Boulevard
du Temple required an exposure of roughly five to fifteen minutes. The
chapter does not turn that range into a precise shutter speed, and does not
invent settings for the atelier or Pont Neuf images.
