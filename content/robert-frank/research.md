# Robert Frank — research notes

## Record-backed context

The source shortlist identifies eleven selected American photographs as made in 1954–56, and *Marching Band, Valencia, Spain* as made in 1951/52 and printed about 1956. The National Gallery of Art's *Looking In: Robert Frank's The Americans*, cited in `sources.md`, describes *The Americans* (1958/59) as the outcome of Frank's Guggenheim-supported 1955–56 journey. It records more than 25,000 exposures edited to 83 photographs. That makes the American road-trip group a documented historical context, not a stylistic assumption imposed on the images.

The supplied Art Institute of Chicago records are web-sized display renditions. The selected images are monochrome, but this source set does not document original camera body, lens, film stock, exposure, aperture, shutter speed, lighting setup, development process, paper, or edition for the individual works. The normalized files contain no camera EXIF. This chapter therefore describes visible framing, subject placement, edges, tone, and recurring motifs rather than inventing technical settings.

## Dating and printing

The object information in `sources.md` gives these dated works: *Fourth of July, Jay, New York* (1954); *Trolley, New Orleans*, *U.S. 285, New Mexico*, *Barbershop through Screen Door*, *Restaurant, U.S. 1*, and *Movie Usherettes* (1955); *Political Rally, Chicago*, *View from Motel Window*, and *Movie Usherettes* (1956); *Drugstore, Detroit* and *Cafeteria, San Francisco* (1955/56); *Elevator, Miami Beach* (1955, printed 1977); and *Marching Band, Valencia, Spain* (1951/52, printed c. 1956). No other print dates or processes are asserted here.

## Visual method observed in this set

Across these selected frames, a public scene is often made through a partial view: a flag becomes a screen, trolley windows partition passengers, a restaurant contains a television picture and a bright window, and curtains flank a motel view. Frank also makes groups uneven rather than ceremonial: marching musicians are cropped by oversized instruments, usherettes repeat without becoming symmetrical, and diners are crowded by plates, signs, and counters. The analysis files support description of tonal compression and edge density, but their detections are treated as measurements to check—not evidence for claims about Frank's intent.
