# Seydou Keïta — research note

## Scope and records consulted

This chapter draws on eight Art Institute of Chicago collection records and their public IIIF display images, itemized in [sources.md](sources.md). The selected works date from about 1950 through 1956–57, with two later collection-print dates recorded there. The source notes also cite the Metropolitan Museum's account of Keïta's Bamako studio, including its use of props and changing textile backdrops. Those records support descriptions of a staged studio practice and its material vocabulary; they do not identify individual sitters beyond the catalog titles.

## Technique and visual method

Keïta's studio portraits make backdrop, dress, pose, jewelry, furniture, and modern consumer objects active elements of the picture. The recurring patterned cloth is not neutral decoration. It can flatten the field behind a sitter, make a frontal pose more emphatic, or set a dense repeated rhythm against a smooth face, pale robe, or dark radio. Group portraits are carefully distributed rather than casually observed: bodies form triangles or a centered spine, and hands, arms, chairs, and feet connect the figures across the frame.

The source set also shows a precise play between symmetry and slight imbalance. A triple portrait may be organized around a central figure while the outer figures differ in height and stance; a seated person may be offset by a radio, handbag, or wicker chair. The compositional overlays identify these visible relations, not an implied social narrative beyond the records.

## Process, camera, and settings

The supplied collection records and normalized image files do not establish a camera model, lens, focal length, aperture, shutter speed, film stock, negative format, lighting setup, or original printing process for these individual photographs. The ingested files have empty EXIF fields. The catalog does identify later print dates for two works, but that is not evidence for the historical capture or darkroom process. It would be false to derive settings from the finished pictures.

The reliable technical account is therefore limited to the visible studio method and the documented context: black-and-white studio portraiture in Bamako during the 1950s, arranged around sitters, props, and textile backdrops. This chapter discusses framing, tonal mass, patterned surfaces, and bodily gesture without assigning unverified exposure values or equipment.
