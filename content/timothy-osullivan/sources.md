# Timothy O'Sullivan — sources

O'Sullivan's signature is scale collapse: he set a small, legible human trace — a boat, a wagon, a line of footprints, a tiny figure on a bench — against geology or event too large to otherwise be read, and let the disproportion do the compositional work. In the canyon work this becomes a converging V of sheer rock walls driving the eye to a single vanishing point, the water a flat mirror splitting the frame in two; in the desert work it becomes a nearly blank plane of sand or basin with one dark, measuring mark on it. He worked dead-frontal only when documenting a surface as a record (an inscription, a ruin), and worked from odd, elevated, or oblique vantage points when the subject was event rather than geology — the Massaponax Church council of war shot from a commandeered gallery window is the clearest case. It is a body of work built for measurement rather than the picturesque, and a strong test bed for vanishing-point, horizon-line, and figure-ground-scale overlays.

**Rights basis:** Public domain. O'Sullivan photographed as a hired civilian photographer for U.S. government expeditions throughout his career — the Union Army's Civil War photographic corps (1861–65, under Brady and then credited individually via Alexander Gardner's publishing operation) and, after the war, the King (Fortieth Parallel) and Wheeler (surveys west of the 100th meridian) geological surveys for the War Department, Corps of Engineers (1867–74). All of it is a U.S. government work and carries no copyright. Nine of the images below come directly from the Library of Congress Prints and Photographs Division (LC-DIG / LOT negatives, no known restrictions on publication); two come from the Metropolitan Museum of Art's Open Access program (CC0), which holds clean full prints of two frames whose LOC records are otherwise split across duplicate negatives or partial halves. O'Sullivan's Civil War work overlaps directly with Alexander Gardner's *Photographic Sketch Book of the War* — Gardner published many of O'Sullivan's Gettysburg negatives under the Sketch Book imprint. The two Civil War frames chosen here (*A Harvest of Death* and *Council of War, Massaponax Church*) are both distinctly credited on the negative and in the LOC record to O'Sullivan as photographer, not to Gardner's own camera work, and are not duplicated in the alexander-gardner chapter.

## Shortlist

### 01 — A Harvest of Death, Gettysburg, Pennsylvania (1863)
- **Why it matters:** An elevated vantage across a stripped battlefield lays receding rows of the fallen flat under raking dawn light — the archetype of the Civil War death-field composition, and the negative is explicitly credited to O'Sullivan (Gardner supplied only the positive print).
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2006685384/
- **Rights:** Public domain (U.S. government-sponsored Civil War photography, July 1863; negative by T.H. O'Sullivan; Library of Congress, no known restrictions)
- **File:** raw/timothy-osullivan/01-harvest-of-death.jpg (6647px)

### 02 — General Grant's Council of War, Massaponax Church, Virginia (1864)
- **Why it matters:** Shot from an upper gallery window O'Sullivan commandeered inside the church, the elevated, oblique vantage compresses Grant's staff, the pews, and the wagon train behind them into one deep, informally structured field — a sharp break from the static tripod-level Civil War tableau.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/264640
- **Rights:** Public domain (U.S. government-sponsored Civil War photography, May 21, 1864; Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/timothy-osullivan/02-council-of-war.jpg (2000px)

### 03 — Ancient Ruins in the Cañon de Chelle, N.M. (1873)
- **Why it matters:** The cliff dwelling is reduced to a dark horizontal seam tucked into a shadowed niche beneath towering, diagonally streaked sandstone — geology overwhelms architecture in scale, the ruin legible only as a thin band of masonry.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/96507688/
- **Rights:** Public domain (Wheeler Survey, War Department Corps of Engineers, 1873; Library of Congress, no known restrictions)
- **File:** raw/timothy-osullivan/03-ancient-ruins-chelle.jpg (8105px)

### 04 — Black Cañon, Colorado River, from Camp 8, Looking Above (1871)
- **Why it matters:** His rented boat "Picture," grounded on the near shore, is the only scale marker against sheer converging canyon walls and their mirror reflection in dead-calm water — a symmetrical V driving the eye into a small notch of daylight.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/99404085/
- **Rights:** Public domain (Wheeler Survey, War Department Corps of Engineers, 1871; Library of Congress, no known restrictions)
- **File:** raw/timothy-osullivan/04-black-canon.jpg (8037px)

### 05 — Sand Dunes, Carson Desert, Nevada (1867)
- **Why it matters:** A lone horse-drawn darkroom wagon and its trailing double line of footprints supply the only scale against an otherwise blank, wind-scoured dune field — emptiness itself is the subject, tracked and measured.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/96513065/
- **Rights:** Public domain (Fortieth Parallel Survey / Clarence King, War Department Corps of Engineers, 1867; Library of Congress, no known restrictions)
- **File:** raw/timothy-osullivan/05-sand-dunes.jpg (8825px)

### 06 — The Pyramid and Domes, Pyramid Lake, Nevada (1867)
- **Why it matters:** A receding row of near-identical dome-shaped tufa formations marches back along the shoreline toward the terminal pyramid rock — repetition and diminishing scale used as a measuring stick for geologic time.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/98503891/
- **Rights:** Public domain (Fortieth Parallel Survey / Clarence King, War Department Corps of Engineers, 1867; Library of Congress, no known restrictions)
- **File:** raw/timothy-osullivan/06-tufa-domes.jpg (8847px)

### 07 — Fissure Vent at Steamboat Springs, Nevada (1867)
- **Why it matters:** Steam venting from a bare fumarole is caught mid-billow against a flat basin floor — one of the only Fortieth Parallel frames built on an atmospheric, ephemeral event rather than static rock, the plume standing in for the missing human scale-mark.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/283217
- **Rights:** Public domain (Fortieth Parallel Survey / Clarence King, War Department Corps of Engineers, 1867; Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/timothy-osullivan/07-fissure-vent.jpg (3811px)

### 08 — Historic Spanish Record of the Conquest, South Side of Inscription Rock, N.M. (1873)
- **Why it matters:** A dead-flat, frontal elevation of the inscribed sandstone face — O'Sullivan sets aside the diagonal drama of his canyon work for a document shot, treating the rock itself as a page to be copied.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2007682546/
- **Rights:** Public domain (Wheeler Survey, War Department Corps of Engineers, 1873; Library of Congress, no known restrictions)
- **File:** raw/timothy-osullivan/08-inscription-rock.jpg (7412px)

### 09 — Vermillion Creek Cañon (1872)
- **Why it matters:** Two sheer, near-identical cliff faces frame a thin ribbon of stream disappearing into haze at the vanishing point — a textbook convergent-canyon composition from King's Green River season.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2002719247/
- **Rights:** Public domain (Fortieth Parallel Survey / Clarence King, War Department Corps of Engineers, 1872; Library of Congress, no known restrictions)
- **File:** raw/timothy-osullivan/09-vermilion-creek.jpg (8958px)

### 10 — Shoshone Falls, Idaho (1868)
- **Why it matters:** The full curtain of the falls spans the frame edge to edge beneath a flat mesa silhouette; the long exposure smooths the water into a continuous pale sheet against black basalt — his most classically sublime Western landscape.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2004666654/
- **Rights:** Public domain (Fortieth Parallel Survey / Clarence King, War Department Corps of Engineers, 1868; Library of Congress, no known restrictions)
- **File:** raw/timothy-osullivan/10-shoshone-falls.jpg (8878px)

### 11 — Old Mission Church, Zuni Pueblo, N.M. (1873)
- **Why it matters:** The ruined adobe mission sits slightly off-center against the pueblo's stacked terraces, a worn wagon-rut trail leading the eye in from a near-empty foreground — architecture read as one more eroding geological form.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2007684826/
- **Rights:** Public domain (Wheeler Survey, War Department Corps of Engineers, 1873; Library of Congress, no known restrictions)
- **File:** raw/timothy-osullivan/11-zuni-mission.jpg (1536px)
