# Timothy O'Sullivan — research

Scope: technique, era, printing, and recorded camera settings for the eleven
plates in this chapter. This is wet-plate collodion work from the 1860s–70s
with no per-frame exposure record; where a number is not documented, this
says so.

## Who and when

Timothy H. O'Sullivan (c. 1840–1882) was born in Ireland (or possibly New
York, to Irish immigrant parents — his birth record is not settled) and grew
up in Staten Island. As a teenager he apprenticed in Mathew Brady's New York
gallery under Brady's operator Alexander Gardner, and when Gardner left to
open his own Washington studio in 1863, O'Sullivan went with him as a field
operator. He photographed for both operations across the Civil War,
including Antietam, Fredericksburg, and Gettysburg, and two of his negatives
appear individually credited to him in Gardner's 1866 *Photographic Sketch
Book of the War* — the convention that book set of naming the field
photographer separately from the publisher-printer. After the war, rather
than returning to studio work, O'Sullivan signed on as official photographer
to two federal geological surveys: Clarence King's Fortieth Parallel Survey
(1867–69, then again 1872), covering a corridor of the Great Basin from the
Sierra Nevada to the Rockies, and Lieutenant George Wheeler's survey of
territory west of the 100th meridian (1871, 1873–74), covering Arizona, New
Mexico, and the Colorado Plateau, including a boat descent of the Colorado
River through Black Canyon. Both were War Department, Corps of Engineers
commissions. He later spent a short stint (1880) as photographer for the
Treasury Department before dying of tuberculosis in 1882, largely unrecognized
in his own lifetime — his survey plates were official government records, not
gallery prints, and his wider reputation as an artist was built retroactively
by twentieth-century curators and historians.

## Technique

This is wet-plate (wet collodion) glass-negative photography, the same
process used across the Civil War corps and standard for all serious
large-format work of the 1850s–1870s. A glass plate was coated with
collodion carrying iodide and bromide salts, sensitized immediately before
use in a bath of silver nitrate, exposed while still wet, and developed
within minutes of exposure — which meant a portable darkroom had to travel
with the camera at all times. In the field this took the form of a
horse- or mule-drawn ambulance wagon fitted out as a darkroom (the wagon and
its trailing tracks are themselves the subject of 05); on the Colorado River
descent (04) the same darkroom equipment traveled by boat. O'Sullivan worked
large-format survey cameras producing glass negatives roughly 8x10 inches
and up, occasionally supplemented by a stereo camera for the same scene. The
canyon and desert work depends on the same wet-plate discipline as the
studio: the lens stopped well down and the camera leveled and set back far
enough to hold a deep field — cliff face, water, and sky — all in focus at
once, with no telephoto compression available to any camera of the era. Long
exposures on water (10) render moving current as a smoothed, continuous
sheet rather than the arrested spray a fast shutter would show.

## Printing

The glass negatives were contact-printed — there being no enlargers in this
era — onto albumen paper coated with egg-white-bound silver salts, then
gold-toned, washed, and mounted; survey plates were typically issued as
large mounted album prints distributed to Congress and government offices
alongside the printed survey reports, rather than sold commercially. Some of
O'Sullivan's negatives did not survive: several Fortieth Parallel and
Wheeler Survey plates are known today only from a single surviving print,
the glass having broken or been discarded afterward, which is part of why
institutional holdings for this body of work are split and inconsistent.
Nine of the eleven images in this chapter are Library of Congress Prints and
Photographs Division holdings (the Civil War Glass Negatives collection for
01 and various Fortieth Parallel / Wheeler Survey lots for 03, 04, 05, 06,
08, 09, 10, 11); two (02, 07) are Open Access (CC0) albumen prints held by
the Metropolitan Museum of Art, whose collection includes complete prints of
frames whose Library of Congress holdings are otherwise split across
duplicate or partial negatives.

## Camera settings

Not recorded. Wet-plate photography of this era predates any per-frame
aperture, shutter-speed, or sensitivity log; there was no light meter, and
exposure was judged by the operator's experience with a given plate's fresh
sensitivity, the available light, and the lens in use. What can be said is
qualitative and well documented: collodion plates were far slower than
modern film, so O'Sullivan's landscape and survey work required a
stopped-down lens on a leveled stand camera and an exposure of several
seconds to tens of seconds even in strong daylight, long enough that moving
water reads as smoothed and continuous (10) and that any human figure placed
in a scene (04, 05, 11) had to hold still or be caught only as a small,
static mark rather than a blur. Interior or dim-light frames such as the
Massaponax Church gallery view (02) would have required a longer exposure
still, consistent with the static, held poses of everyone in frame. No
specific f-stop, shutter speed, or sensitivity value survives for any of the
eleven images, and none is stated here.
