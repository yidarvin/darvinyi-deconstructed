# Craig Kalpakjian — research notes

## Verified process and period

The selected works date from 1996 to 2002. The artist’s Works index is the primary
record for the titles, dates, media, dimensions, and display images collected in
`sources.md`. SFMOMA’s account of the related *Corridor* states that it is
computer-generated and that its visible details were programmed. Together, those
records support describing this group as computer-generated architectural images,
not as camera-made documents of occupied buildings.

No supplied record identifies a camera body, lens, film or sensor, focal length,
aperture, shutter speed, sensitivity, or lighting plan; the ingested artist-site
JPEGs contain no useful EXIF. The artist’s Works record does document three
work-specific display formats: *Dark Hall II* (2000), Cibachrome mounted on
aluminum, 29.5 × 39.5 in.; *Stair* (2001), Fujiflex print mounted on aluminum,
30.5 × 59.5 in.; and *C.O.* (2002), inkjet print mounted on plexiglas, 46.5 ×
78.5 in. (recorded in `sources.md`). The chapter therefore treats the rendered
surfaces as visible image structure, not as evidence for invented camera settings.

## Visual working method supported by the selection

Across this group, Kalpakjian builds office-like interiors from repeating ceiling
panels, floor seams, thresholds, ducts, fixtures, glazing, and blank planes. The
spaces mimic the cues of ordinary institutional architecture while withholding the
camera-world evidence of a particular building or inhabitant. Corridors bend or
narrow, reflective surfaces fold one interior into another, and a cropped fixture
or corner can take over the role usually assigned to a human subject. The complete
frame matters because these effects arise from the relation between the enclosing
planes and the small interruption within them.
