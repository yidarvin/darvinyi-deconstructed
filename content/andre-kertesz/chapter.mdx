André Kertész made a photograph's subject answer first to its placement. Across
these works, a stair rail, pipe, fork, shadow, body, bow, chair, or tower becomes
a way to direct looking. Paris in the 1920s gave him studios, streets, friends,
and chance encounters; the later *Distortions* make the same lesson explicit:
the frame can remake what is seen without ceasing to show it. The overlays name
visible structures only: rails, rims, shadows, bodies, and trusses, rather than
inventing technical history. [Source notes](../../content/andre-kertesz/sources.md)

## interiors and small things

<Plate slug="andre-kertesz" id="01-chez-mondrian" label="Chez Mondrian, 1926" caption="An open doorway and curving stair rail divide the interior into a dark frontal plane and a recess that draws the eye inward." />

In *Chez Mondrian*, the doorway is more than an architectural subject: it
forms an internal frame that separates the dark cabinet and flower vase from
the lit stair. The overlay follows the curve of the rail to the stair turn,
making that bend the hinge between the two spaces. A modest edge density of
0.025 and p05–p95 luminance span of 0.184–0.745 keep the photograph spare;
dark brown #3f352a and warm #ab9681 make the room read as blocks of light and
shadow rather than as a deep inventory of objects.

<Plate slug="andre-kertesz" id="02-mondrians-glasses-and-pipe" label="Mondrian's Glasses and Pipe, 1926" caption="Spectacle lenses, ashtray rim, and pipe stem set curves against a large, nearly empty tabletop." />

The still life makes ordinary tools carry its entire rhythm. The paired lenses
and ashtray ellipse repeat one another at different scales, while the pipe stem
breaks their circles with a sharp rising diagonal. At edge density 0.015, the
plate is extremely economical; the 0.094–0.788 tonal range lets near-black
#1d1b16 frames sit firmly against a pale #c5c0ba tabletop. The overlay's phi
grid is a measured fit, but the important visual fact is the deliberate
imbalance of small objects inside broad blank ground.

<Plate slug="andre-kertesz" id="03-fork" label="Fork, 1928" caption="Fork handle, cast shadow, and cropped plate rim turn a table setting into three competing diagonals." />

*Fork* is even more concise. The handle and its shadow run in separate but
related diagonals, while the cropped plate edge insists on a large circular
counter-force at the left. The overlay marks all three without pretending that
they converge in architectural space. Its bright mean luminance of 0.654 and
low edge density of 0.015 make the fork's dark #403a31 contour feel unusually
decisive against cream #cfc9ba and #d9d5c9. Kertész converts a utensil into an
unstable graphic event.

## figures rearranged

<Plate slug="andre-kertesz" id="04-shadows-paris" label="Shadows, Paris, 1931; printed 1970s" caption="Three long shadows and a brick edge make the street a vertical field in which bodies appear as projections." />

In *Shadows, Paris*, the people are present both as small figures and as their
larger projections. The overlay's tall and right-hand shadow paths clarify how
the high viewpoint makes the pavement a vertical stage, checked by the brick
edge at left. A p05–p95 span of 0.227–0.906 separates charcoal #41413f from
light #c5c6c1 pavement, while edge density 0.027 preserves the few necessary
contours. The source record identifies this as a 1931 picture printed in the
1970s; the chapter does not infer the original camera or exposure.

<Plate slug="andre-kertesz" id="05-distortion-102" label="Untitled (Distortion #102), 1932/33" caption="A mirror bends the body into a continuous contour while hard mirror edges hold the deformation inside the frame." />

The mirror in *Distortion #102* does not make the body abstract by itself; the
fixed edges at both sides are what let the altered contour register as an
experiment. The overlay traces that long body sweep and the restraining mirror
edges, with an anchor at hand and torso. At edge density 0.012, the result is
more tonal than linear: #4a4943 and #62625a surround a pale #c1c2b7 body,
whose p95 reaches 0.953. The deformation is legible because Kertész leaves
enough stable geometry around it.

<Plate slug="andre-kertesz" id="06-satiric-dancer-paris" label="Satiric Dancer, Paris (variant), 1927" caption="A couch arc contains the sitter while her splayed legs form a comic V-shaped counter-rhythm." />

*Satiric Dancer* makes pose into structure. The broad couch ellipse contains
the body, but the two leg paths insist on escaping it; the right-hand anchor
adds the playful tension of the splayed gesture. The image is dark overall,
with mean luminance 0.411 and dark brown #483d32 dominant against #d6bda9
highlights. Its 0.216–0.773 tonal span prevents the scene from becoming a
flat silhouette, retaining the couch, dress, limbs, and floor as separate
planes.

<Plate slug="andre-kertesz" id="07-quartet" label="Quartet, 1926" caption="A centered music stand, one bow, and a strong vertical axis turn the cropped players into an interlocking ensemble." />

In *Quartet*, the music stand is the literal center and a visual metronome. The
overlay's internal frame and center axis make clear how Kertész crops the
players to bows, hands, instruments, and score rather than offering a group
portrait. At edge density 0.014, its activity depends on a few dark contours
against #dcc7b5 paper tone; the 0.180–0.808 luminance span keeps each hand and
instrument legible. The large blank lower margin is not a mistake: it makes
the compressed action above feel suspended.

<Plate slug="andre-kertesz" id="08-the-dancing-faun" label="The Dancing Faun, June 1919" caption="The airborne figure is isolated against pale sky just above the low, dark line of shrubs." />

*The Dancing Faun* reduces dance to a silhouette and interval. The outline
follows the compact airborne figure while the low shrub horizon holds it in
place; the figure's small scale is what makes the leap feel unlikely. It has a
relatively high edge density of 0.040 for this selection, yet the palette stays
within charcoal #4a4a44 and muted #868577 beneath pale #f1f1e5. The p95 of
0.953 gives the sky enough lift to make the black body read immediately.

## paris as a field of marks

<Plate slug="andre-kertesz" id="09-paris-the-eiffel-tower" label="Paris, The Eiffel Tower, 1925; printed 1970s" caption="The tower's slim axis and the storm's lightning paths make the city silhouette a low, steady baseline." />

The Eiffel Tower in this view is not a monumental silhouette but a slim
vertical answer to lightning. The overlay follows the tower axis and a single
bright lightning path, while the city silhouette makes a low horizontal stop.
The dark mean luminance of 0.391 and palette of #5e5d5d, #484646, and #292524
make the light marks unusually forceful within a p05–p95 span of 0.145–0.937.
The collection record dates the picture to 1925 and the print to the 1970s;
no capture settings are supplied by the source dossier.

<Plate slug="andre-kertesz" id="10-chairs-luxembourg-gardens" label="Chairs, Luxembourg Gardens, 1926" caption="Chairs and their shadows cross in a shallow, receding field instead of resolving into a quiet garden view." />

*Chairs, Luxembourg Gardens* is the densest plate here, with edge density
0.099. The overlay follows one chair row and a parallel shadow row toward the
measured receding field; the crowded crossings make empty furniture feel
active. Dark #392e27, light #cbc0b6, and #554640 divide the view into hard
alternations, while the 0.165–0.796 tonal span preserves structure in both the
sunlit path and dark seating. Kertész finds motion in repetition rather than
in a moving subject.

<Plate slug="andre-kertesz" id="11-paul-armas-hands" label="Paul Arma's Hands, Paris, 1928" caption="Upside-down glasses and two hands create a sparse arrangement of oval, wrist edge, and hanging fingers." />

Here the glasses are a second pair of eyes held in front of hands. The overlay
marks the spectacle pair, the gripping wrist, and the hanging hand contour,
letting the image's tactile lines do the work. Its edge density is only 0.013,
but a 0.251–0.851 tonal span makes pale skin and gray field distinct from the
dark #4b4944 frames. The picture's restraint is compositional: nearly half the
frame is empty, so the hands appear to suspend the glasses rather than merely
hold them.

<Plate slug="andre-kertesz" id="12-under-the-eiffel-tower" label="Under the Eiffel Tower, 1929" caption="A high iron arch and a diagonal tower brace compress the street into a framed field of small pedestrians and shadows." />

The final plate reverses the prior tower view. Instead of watching it from a
distance, *Under the Eiffel Tower* looks down through its truss canopy. The
overlay's top arch and diagonal brace lead to the measured truss convergence,
while the visible pedestrians remain small enough to establish scale. Edge
density 0.081 is second only to the chairs; near-black #272419 iron presses
against #c9cac2 pavement across a 0.110–0.820 tonal range. Massive structure
does not erase the everyday street; it composes it.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Make the small object directional">
Photograph a fork, key, glasses, or other ordinary object on a plain surface.
Move it until its shadow produces a second directional line, then crop so a
larger curved edge counters both diagonals.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Photograph a body through its projection">
At low sun, frame people and their shadows from a high viewpoint. Let the
projected forms take more space than the figures, and use one wall or curb to
prevent the pavement from becoming an undifferentiated field.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Turn structure into a canopy">
Photograph beneath a bridge, tower, or awning. Keep one large overhead arc or
brace at the frame edge and allow people or vehicles below to remain small;
make the structure feel like a device for measuring scale, not just a subject.
</ExerciseCard>
