# Frederick H. Evans — source shortlist

This 11-image institutional set centers Evans’s cathedral platinum prints, with one river view and one portrait as counterpoints. Sources are LACMA, LOC, the Met, Getty/Cleveland Museum of Art mirrors on Wikimedia Commons; each is documented as public domain or CC0.

1. **A Sea of Steps—Wells Cathedral: Stairs to the Chapter House** — 1903. An ascending S-curve turns stair geometry into an almost abstract current, balanced by arch and landing. [LACMA record](https://collections.lacma.org/node/218970). **Rights:** LACMA believed-public-domain download.
2. **“In Sure and Certain Hope.” York Minster** — 1902. A dark off-axis nave leads upward into an illuminated spatial climax. [Met record](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/286643). **Rights:** Met Open Access / CC0.
3. **Height and Light in Bourges Cathedral** — negative 1899; published 1903. Vertical convergence and a high bright window make the architecture immaterial. [Met record](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/271766). **Rights:** Met Open Access / CC0.
4. **Late Afternoon Across Octagon and Transepts, Ely Cathedral** — 1903. Nested arches and raking light organize a huge interior into tonal planes. [LOC record](https://www.loc.gov/pictures/item/2004673270/). **Rights:** Public domain.
5. **Ely Cathedral: A Memory of the Normans** — negative 1897; published 1903. Pier-and-arch cropping emphasizes Norman weight, relieved by a small interval of light. [Getty collection asset](https://artsandculture.google.com/asset/ely-cathedral-a-memory-of-the-normans-frederick-h-evans-british-1853-1943/6820492). **Rights:** Commons public-domain institutional scan.
6. **Ely Cathedral: Across Nave and Octagon** — negative 1897; published 1903. Axial recession is paced by crossing arches and a measured rhythm of depth. [Getty collection asset](https://artsandculture.google.com/asset/ely-cathedral-across-nave-and-octagon-frederick-h-evans-british-1853-1943/6820496). **Rights:** Commons public-domain institutional scan.
7. **York Minster: Into the North Transept** — negative 1899; published 1903. Low vantage and lateral opening create an asymmetric, light-led study of scale. [Getty collection asset](https://artsandculture.google.com/asset/york-minster-into-the-north-transept-frederick-h-evans-british-1853-1943/6830128). **Rights:** Commons public-domain institutional scan.
8. **Bishop Alcock’s Chapel from Retrochoir, Ely Cathedral** — 1897. Gothic tracery frames a deep shadowed view whose vault grid supplies decisive leading lines. [Cleveland Museum of Art record](https://clevelandart.org/art/2019.100). **Rights:** CC0.
9. **Gloucester Cathedral, Cloisters: South and West Alleys** — c. 1900. Repeated fan-vault cells and a turning passage make rhythm and directional pull the subject. [Cleveland Museum of Art record](https://clevelandart.org/art/1975.37). **Rights:** CC0.
10. **On a French River** — c. 1902. Riverbanks frame a bright water corridor, a useful non-cathedral counterpoint to the architectural images. [Met record](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/286415). **Rights:** Met Open Access / CC0.
11. **Aubrey Beardsley** — c. 1894. A hand-to-face triangle and deep negative space make this the strongest portrait complement. [Met record](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/283248). **Rights:** Met Open Access / CC0.

Context: Getty’s [Evans exhibition](https://www.getty.edu/art/exhibitions/frederick_evans/) identifies *A Sea of Steps* among his best-known cathedral photographs and is useful for interpreting the platinum-print architectural sequence.
