# Alfred Stieglitz — sources

Stieglitz’s archive spans both early Pictorialism and a later, often estate-controlled modernist period. This source set deliberately stays with the historically central photographs made and published through 1917: the works most useful for tracing his shift from atmospheric street scenes to hard urban geometry. The Library of Congress supplies the master scan for *The Steerage*; the other scans are open releases from the Rijksmuseum or Metropolitan Museum of Art, obtained through their Wikimedia Commons records. The later *Equivalents* are not included: they are central to the story, but the registry correctly flags the later corpus as mixed-rights.

**Rights basis:** The 1907 *Steerage* record is marked “no known restrictions on publication” by the Library of Congress. The Rijksmuseum scans are CC0, and the Metropolitan Museum of Art scan is CC0/Open Access. *The Last Joke, Bellagio* is a pre-1929 published photograph and is marked public domain on its Commons record. Every downloaded file opens successfully and has a long edge of at least 1,200px.

## Shortlist

### 01 — The Steerage (1907)

- **Why it matters:** Stieglitz’s canonical modernist breakthrough: the ship’s gangways, mast, hats, and railings lock a crowded human scene into a tightly interleaved grid of diagonals and rectangles.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/89715190/
- **Rights:** Library of Congress, “no known restrictions on publication.”
- **File:** `raw/alfred-stieglitz/01-the-steerage.jpg` (1494px)

### 02 — Winter, Fifth Avenue (1893)

- **Why it matters:** A horse-drawn carriage is held against the snow’s blank field, while the avenue’s receding edge turns bad weather into a deep, tonal corridor.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Gezicht_op_een_besneeuwde_straat_in_New_York_met_paardenkarren_Winter_5th_avenue_N.Y._(titel_op_object),_RP-F-2001-7-968-3-192.jpg
- **Rights:** Rijksmuseum Open Data / CC0.
- **File:** `raw/alfred-stieglitz/02-winter-fifth-avenue.jpg` (5882px)

### 03 — The Hand of Man (1902)

- **Why it matters:** The locomotive, rails, poles, smoke, and freight cars make an industrial composition whose converging tracks carry the eye straight into the frame.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Spoorlijnen_met_stoomtrein_The_Hand_of_Man_(titel_op_object),_RP-F-2001-7-1184B-220.jpg
- **Rights:** Rijksmuseum Open Data / CC0.
- **File:** `raw/alfred-stieglitz/03-the-hand-of-man.jpg` (2022px)

### 04 — The Flatiron (1903)

- **Why it matters:** The wedge-like building anchors a vertical composition while bare trees and mist soften its sharp geometry into a pictorialist cityscape.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:The_Flat-iron_MET_DT244585.jpg
- **Rights:** Metropolitan Museum of Art Open Access / CC0.
- **File:** `raw/alfred-stieglitz/04-the-flatiron.jpg` (3722px)

### 05 — Spring Showers, the Coach (1901)

- **Why it matters:** A wet avenue, carriage, and rows of trees create a long vanishing corridor, with rain and reflected light simplifying the scene into tonal planes.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Spring_1901,_RP-F-F17770.jpg
- **Rights:** Rijksmuseum Open Data / CC0.
- **File:** `raw/alfred-stieglitz/05-spring-showers.jpg` (5924px)

### 06 — Reflections, Night, New York (1896)

- **Why it matters:** A sparse nocturne where wet pavement doubles lights and trees, using the frame’s dark mass and its reflected counterpart as an almost symmetrical abstraction.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Gezicht_op_een_leeg_plein_met_een_aantal_bomen_Reflections,_Night_(titel_op_object),_RP-F-2001-7-968-3-81.jpg
- **Rights:** Rijksmuseum Open Data / CC0.
- **File:** `raw/alfred-stieglitz/06-reflections-night.jpg` (5608px)

### 07 — The Street — Design for a Poster (1896)

- **Why it matters:** Umbrellas, pedestrians, and building fronts are compressed into emphatic light-and-dark silhouettes; the picture reads as a poster before it reads as a street.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Straatgezicht_in_New_York_The_Street_-_Design_for_a_Poster_(titel_op_object),_RP-F-2005-107-220.jpg
- **Rights:** Rijksmuseum Open Data / CC0.
- **File:** `raw/alfred-stieglitz/07-the-street-design-for-a-poster.jpg` (5694px)

### 08 — The City of Ambition (1910)

- **Why it matters:** Low camera position and tall buildings turn Manhattan into an upward thrust of verticals, with a street opening held as a narrow central channel.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Stadsgezicht_van_New_York_The_City_of_Ambition_(titel_op_object),_RP-F-2005-107-218.jpg
- **Rights:** Rijksmuseum Open Data / CC0.
- **File:** `raw/alfred-stieglitz/08-the-city-of-ambition.jpg` (5798px)

### 09 — Old and New New York (1910)

- **Why it matters:** New vertical construction is set against the low, irregular street—an explicit composition of stacked eras and competing scales.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Straatgezicht_in_New_York_Old_and_New_New_York_(titel_op_object),_RP-F-2005-107-224.jpg
- **Rights:** Rijksmuseum Open Data / CC0.
- **File:** `raw/alfred-stieglitz/09-old-and-new-new-york.jpg` (4956px)

### 10 — A Snapshot, Paris (1911)

- **Why it matters:** A street scene is caught as a quick, asymmetric slice, with architectural edges and small figures balanced across the long vertical frame.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Straatgezicht_in_Parijs_A_Snapshot_Paris_(titel_op_object),_RP-F-2005-107-222.jpg
- **Rights:** Rijksmuseum Open Data / CC0.
- **File:** `raw/alfred-stieglitz/10-a-snapshot-paris.jpg` (5704px)

### 11 — The Last Joke, Bellagio (1887)

- **Why it matters:** An early, tightly staged social scene whose clustered figures and strong foreground gesture show Stieglitz already organizing attention through a single human focal point.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Alfred_Stieglitz_-_The_Last_Joke_Bellagio,_1887.jpg
- **Rights:** Public domain (pre-1929 publication; Commons record).
- **File:** `raw/alfred-stieglitz/11-the-last-joke-bellagio.jpg` (4000px)
