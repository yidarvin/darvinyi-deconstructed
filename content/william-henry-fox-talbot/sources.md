# William Henry Fox Talbot — sources

Talbot composed as an inventor cataloguing what a camera could newly do, and the discipline shows: subjects are set frontally and centered, close enough to fill the frame, and chosen because their surface texture or repeating structure would prove the calotype's descriptive power — straw on a haystack, china on a shelf, spines on a shelf of books, leading in a window. Where a figure or event enters the frame (workmen on a ladder, scaffolding rising over Trafalgar Square) it is still built from stacked horizontals and verticals, the camera held square to its subject rather than picturesquely angled. The result is the first vocabulary of photographic composition — grid, repetition, frontal symmetry, and the still life read as specimen — laid down before "composition" was even a photographic idea.

**Rights basis:** Public domain. Talbot died in 1877 and every image below was made between 1835 and 1844, so all are long out of copyright under any life+70 or life+100 rule and were published well before 1930 in any case. Files come from open-access institutional programs — the Metropolitan Museum of Art (Open Access / CC0, several via its Gilman Collection of early photography), the Science Museum Group / National Science and Media Museum, Yale Center for British Art, and the Rijksmuseum — mirrored at full resolution on Wikimedia Commons, or pulled directly from the Met's collection API. All are public-domain photographic reproductions of two-dimensional public-domain works.

## Shortlist

### 01 — Latticed Window, South Gallery, Lacock Abbey (1835)
- **Why it matters:** The oldest surviving camera negative — a small leaded oriel window shot dead square, its diamond panes and lead cames forming a literal grid; composition and the medium's origin are the same object.
- **Source:** https://collection.sciencemuseumgroup.org.uk/objects/co17291/windows-from-inside-south-gallery-lacock-abbey
- **Rights:** Public domain (Science Museum Group / National Science and Media Museum collection)
- **File:** raw/william-henry-fox-talbot/01-latticed-window.jpg (1536px)

### 02 — The Open Door (1844)
- **Why it matters:** A stone doorway with the door ajar and a broom leaning in it, staged consciously after Dutch genre painting — the rectilinear frame of the doorway set against the diagonal broom and the fall of light across stone and wood.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/283068
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0; Gilman Collection)
- **File:** raw/william-henry-fox-talbot/02-the-open-door.jpg (2983px)

### 03 — The Haystack (1844)
- **Why it matters:** A monumental haystack and leaning ladder fill the frame edge to edge, every straw resolved — texture and repetition made the whole subject, proof that the calotype could out-describe a painted picturesque.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/289179
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/william-henry-fox-talbot/03-the-haystack.jpg (3873px)

### 04 — Articles of China (1844)
- **Why it matters:** Shelves of china stacked and arrayed frontally, each piece isolated like a specimen — an inventory shot that reads as a literal grid of shelves and repeated object-forms, the prototype of the photographic still-life-as-catalogue.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/289173
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0; higher-resolution scan via Google Art Project)
- **File:** raw/william-henry-fox-talbot/04-articles-of-china.jpg (4341px)

### 05 — The Ladder (1844)
- **Why it matters:** Three men climb a ladder leaned against the abbey wall in a stepped diagonal, one of Talbot's rare figure compositions — the ladder's repeated rungs cut across the flat frontal wall, rhythm built from a single line.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/306335
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0; Bequest of Maurice B. Sendak)
- **File:** raw/william-henry-fox-talbot/05-the-ladder.jpg (3416px)

### 06 — Nelson's Column under Construction, Trafalgar Square (1844)
- **Why it matters:** Among the earliest photographs of a news event — scaffolding and derricks stack into a vertical lattice against open sky while onlookers at the base give it scale, the temporary structure read purely as geometry.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/289387
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/william-henry-fox-talbot/06-nelsons-column.jpg (3136px)

### 07 — A Scene in a Library (1844)
- **Why it matters:** Shelves of books shot outdoors (moved into daylight for the long exposure), a dense grid of vertical spines each legible — repetition and taxonomy again the true subject, an ancestor of later typological grids (Blossfeldt, Ruscha).
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/283066
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/william-henry-fox-talbot/07-scene-in-library.jpg (3283px)

### 08 — Leaf of a Plant (1844)
- **Why it matters:** A single leaf laid directly against sensitized paper and printed by contact, its veins and serrated edge rendered as the entire image — a cameraless demonstration of photogenic drawing where the specimen is both subject and printing plate.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/289176
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0; Gift of Jean Horblit in memory of Harrison D. Horblit)
- **File:** raw/william-henry-fox-talbot/08-leaf-of-a-plant.jpg (3813px)

### 09 — Lacock Abbey in Wiltshire (1844)
- **Why it matters:** The Gothic south front of Talbot's own house, photographed frontally and symmetrically through a screen of trees — his home estate treated with the same disciplined, camera-square framing he brought to every specimen in the book.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/289183
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/william-henry-fox-talbot/09-lacock-abbey-wiltshire.jpg (3873px)

### 10 — Bust of Patroclus (1843)
- **Why it matters:** A plaster cast bust set against a dark ground and modeled by raking light — an early study of photography's capacity to render sculptural volume through tone alone, the bust held near-centered and bilaterally symmetric in the frame.
- **Source:** https://collections.britishart.yale.edu/catalog/tms:74639 (Yale Center for British Art, object B2018.20.5)
- **Rights:** Public domain (Yale Center for British Art collection)
- **File:** raw/william-henry-fox-talbot/10-bust-of-patroclus.jpg (1920px)

### 11 — The Boulevards at Paris (1843)
- **Why it matters:** An elevated view down a Parisian boulevard, matched building facades receding to a single vanishing point — Talbot's earliest urban perspective study, and a direct forerunner of Atget's street compositions six decades later.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/283070 (Gilman Collection; also digitized via Wikimedia Commons)
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/william-henry-fox-talbot/11-boulevards-at-paris.jpg (3007px)

### 12 — The Bridge of Orleans (1843–44)
- **Why it matters:** A distant view of a stone bridge, its repeated arches forming a rhythmic horizontal band across the frame — an early study of engineered repetition set against open river and sky.
- **Source:** https://www.rijksmuseum.nl/en/collection/RP-F-F80147
- **Rights:** Public domain (Rijksmuseum, Amsterdam; open collection)
- **File:** raw/william-henry-fox-talbot/12-bridge-of-orleans.jpg (3288px)
