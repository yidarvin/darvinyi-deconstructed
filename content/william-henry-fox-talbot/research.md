# William Henry Fox Talbot — research

Scope: technique, era, printing, and recorded camera settings for the twelve
plates in this chapter. This is paper-negative work from the 1830s–40s —
photogenic drawing and calotype — with no per-frame exposure record; where a
number is not documented, this says so.

## Who and when

William Henry Fox Talbot (1800–1877) was an English gentleman scientist —
mathematician, chemist, classicist, and Member of Parliament — who owned
Lacock Abbey in Wiltshire, the estate his family had held since the
Reformation. He was not trained as an artist or a professional image-maker;
his path into photography ran through mathematics and chemistry. Frustrated
in 1833 by his own inability to draw with a camera lucida on the shore of
Lake Como, he began experimenting at Lacock with paper sensitized to darken
under light, seeking a way to let light "fix" an image without a hand
tracing it. By August 1835 this work had produced the small paper negative
of a leaded, diamond-paned window in the abbey's south gallery (image 01) —
today recognized as the oldest surviving camera negative in existence, made
years before photography was public knowledge. Talbot did not exhibit or
announce this work at the time; he set it aside, and it was the news of
Daguerre's rival process reaching London in January 1839 that spurred him to
present his own paper-based method to the Royal Institution within weeks.
Images 02–09 and 11–12 here (1843–44) belong to the mature phase of that
work, after he had patented an improved process in 1841 and begun issuing
his prints commercially and in book form; image 10 (1843) is contemporary
with them. Talbot's chief public achievement of this period was *The Pencil
of Nature* (1844–46), issued by Longman in six paper-covered fascicles with
real photographic prints tipped in by hand — the first commercially
published book illustrated with photographs — and several of the images in
this chapter were made for it or alongside it. He continued to work in
photography, photomechanical printing, and etymology and Assyriology until
his death at Lacock in 1877.

## Technique

Two distinct processes are represented here. Image 01 (1835) is a
**photogenic drawing** negative: writing paper brushed with alternating
weak washes of common salt and silver nitrate to form light-sensitive silver
chloride, loaded into a small handmade wooden camera (Talbot's household
nicknamed these miniature boxes "mousetraps" for their size), and exposed
directly in the camera obscura. Photogenic drawing is a print-out process —
the image darkens progressively under light with no chemical development —
so it demanded very long in-camera exposures, on the order of tens of
minutes to an hour even in full sun, which is why Talbot's earliest camera
subjects are static architecture rather than anything living. Images
02–09 and 11–12 (1843–44) and image 10 (1843) use the **calotype** (also
"talbotype") process he patented on 8 February 1841: paper sensitized with
silver iodide, then coated just before use with a solution of silver
nitrate and gallic acid ("gallo-nitrate of silver"). This combination
recorded a latent, invisible image after a much shorter exposure in the
camera, which was then brought out chemically by re-applying the same
developing solution — the same negative-development principle that
underlies every later film process. The calotype's latent-image development
cut required exposure times sharply compared to photogenic drawing,
into the range of roughly a minute to a few minutes outdoors in good light,
though Talbot did not log exposure durations per plate. For the 1843–44
work Talbot used a larger stand camera built for him with lenses supplied
by opticians including Andrew Ross, rather than the small mousetrap boxes of
1835.

## Printing

Both processes here end in paper negatives, but the finished prints in this
chapter are **salted paper prints**: the developed and fixed negative (waxed
or oiled in some cases to make the paper more translucent for printing) was
laid in contact with a second sheet of paper sensitized with silver
chloride and exposed to sunlight until the positive image printed out, then
fixed — Talbot originally used a strong salt solution and later adopted
sodium thiosulfate ("hypo"), on the recommendation of his colleague John
Herschel, who discovered its fixing property in 1819 and shared it with
Talbot in 1839. The soft-edged, slightly diffused look typical of these
plates comes from printing through the paper negative's own fibers, since
there was no glass or film base yet to hold a sharper edge. Surviving
material for this chapter is spread across the institutions that hold
Talbot's negatives and prints today: the original Latticed Window negative
(01) is held by the National Science and Media Museum / Science Museum
Group in the UK, which holds the bulk of Talbot's surviving glass and paper
material passed down through the Talbot family and the Royal Photographic
Society collection; most of the 1843–44 salted paper prints (02–09, 11)
are Metropolitan Museum of Art Open Access holdings, largely from its
Gilman Collection of early photography; the Bust of Patroclus print (10) is
held by the Yale Center for British Art; and the Bridge of Orleans print
(12) is held by the Rijksmuseum, Amsterdam.

## Camera settings

Not recorded. Photogenic drawing and calotype negatives from the 1830s–40s
predate any per-frame aperture, shutter-speed, or sensitivity log — there
was no shutter mechanism to speak of (exposures were timed by uncapping and
recapping the lens by hand) and no light meter or standardized sensitivity
rating for the paper in use. What can be said is qualitative and well
documented: image 01 required a very long in-camera exposure, minutes to
the better part of an hour, using the slow print-out photogenic drawing
paper of 1835; the 1843–44 calotype plates, thanks to gallic-acid
development, needed much shorter but still multi-second-to-multi-minute
exposures on a stand camera in daylight, consistent with the static,
frontally squared subjects Talbot chose across this body of work — nothing
in it moves within the frame except where blur itself is visible (see image
05, the men on the ladder). No specific f-stop, shutter speed, or
sensitivity value survives for any of the twelve images, and none is stated
here.
