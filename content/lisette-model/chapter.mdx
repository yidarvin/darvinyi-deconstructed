Lisette Model makes proximity physical. In this selection, a street becomes a band at shoe height, a memorial display grows into a vertical chamber, circus apparatus opens into a black drop, and a man with a newspaper fills the frame before any surrounding city can settle the encounter. The six Art Institute of Chicago records span 1938–1950. They are not presented as a comprehensive account of Model's work; together they show a recurring decision to make cropping and interval carry as much force as the depicted subject.

## a street cut at the ankle

The first *Running Legs* makes a very low viewpoint feel less like a trick than a discipline. The dominant footfall band sits near the lower edge, and the overlay follows the alternating shoes through it instead of inventing a distant horizon. The foreground shoe at right is so large that it nearly becomes a border; the smaller feet behind it keep entering and withdrawing. In the present derivative, mean luminance is 0.210, with p05 0.035 and p95 0.580. The dark #14100b and brown-black #2f271a masses make the narrow pale pavement and the few bright shoe edges feel unusually exposed.

<Plate slug="lisette-model" id="01-running-legs-1940-41" label="Running Legs, 1940–41" caption="The overlay names the low footfall band and the sequence of alternating feet rather than turning the close sidewalk view into a false perspective diagram." />

*Running Legs, New York Public Library* swaps that compressed horizontal crowd for a crossing between body and stairs. The overlay's rising tread and parallel steps are literal surfaces in the frame. A cropped hand at left is held against them, while the blurred leg moves diagonally through their ordered repetitions. The derivative is much brighter, with mean luminance 0.528, p05 0.247, and p95 0.953; its modest edge density of 0.0203 lets the few broad step bands remain legible despite the moving figure.

<Plate slug="lisette-model" id="03-running-legs-nypl-1940" label="Running Legs, New York Public Library, 1940" caption="A blurred moving body crosses a repeated stair pattern, so architectural order and bodily interruption share the frame." />

The second *Running Legs* is more crowded still. Large cropped legs block both sides while a shallow corridor opens in the center. The overlay's sidewalk plane is not a horizon claim; it identifies the strip of bright pavement on which the shoes are measured. Its p05 of 0.157 and p95 of 0.937 describe the hard alternation of black clothing and bright pavement, while the palette rises from #33322f to #e9eae3. The people are not separate portraits. Their partial bodies create the rhythm.

<Plate slug="lisette-model" id="05-running-legs-1940-41" label="Running Legs, 1940/41" caption="Cropped bodies leave a narrow walking corridor, with the bright pavement pacing the shoes at the bottom of the frame." />

## displays and drops

*We Mourn Our Loss* moves from street motion to a public arrangement. Drapery, the framed portrait, and the hand-lettered placard are stacked one above another, but Model does not smooth them into a tidy monument. The overlay marks the memorial field and the placard because their scale relationship is the picture's pressure: the portrait appears above, while the text forces itself forward at the bottom. The scan has the broadest light range in this set after the circus plate, from p05 0.184 to p95 0.949; its near-white #ebecdf paper and deep #363733 fabric keep both the message and the surrounding folds active.

<Plate slug="lisette-model" id="02-we-mourn-our-loss-1945" label="We Mourn Our Loss, 1945" caption="Portrait, drapery, and placard form a compressed memorial stack in which the lower text claims the viewer's attention." />

In *Circus, The Wallendas, New York City*, the upper action has an opposite relation to its ground. Two wires run almost level across the top third, with performers and a right-side platform held above a vast dark arena. The lines do not promise a vanishing point; they make a precarious lateral route. Far below, the pale diamond of the ring and its seated perimeter make the distance count. Mean luminance is only 0.303, but p95 reaches 0.945, so the pale performers and ring flare against a palette beginning at #2a2926. The composition depends on keeping that void open.

<Plate slug="lisette-model" id="04-circus-wallendas-1950" label="Circus, The Wallendas, New York City, 1950" caption="Nearly level wires suspend the performers above a dark arena, turning the blank interval into part of the circus act." />

## a close frontal encounter

*Man with Newspaper, Paris* makes the opposite decision: almost no interval is left. The body fills the vertical frame, while the tie forms a narrow axis and the newspaper presses against the right edge. The overlay names the waistline and tie knot as visible anchors, not a supposed one-point system in the shirt's folds. This is the set's busiest measured derivative, with edge density 0.1335. Its p05 of 0.082 and p95 of 0.753 hold the dark jacket, pale shirt, newspaper edges, and dappled background in separate layers, while the palette moves from blue-black #0f2522 through #636262 to #c8bdba.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Photograph a shared footfall band">
Work from a low but safe position in a public walkway. Make three frames in which shoes cross the same narrow lower strip. Keep at least one large foreground foot and one smaller, receding foot, then compare whether the crop creates rhythm or merely cuts people off.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Make an interval do the work">
Photograph a small, bright subject against a large dark or empty field. Do not fill the gap afterward. Test how much blank area is needed before the distance becomes an active part of the picture, as it does between the wire and ring in *Circus*.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Crowd a frontal portrait">
Photograph a person close enough that one edge object, such as a newspaper, bag, or sleeve, presses against the frame. Use one vertical feature of clothing or posture as an axis, but keep the subject's body from becoming a perfectly centered diagram.
</ExerciseCard>
