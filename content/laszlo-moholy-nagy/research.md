# László Moholy-Nagy — research

Scope: technique, era, printing, and documented camera information for the
twelve selected photographs. The supplied object records establish titles,
dates, and print medium; they do not supply camera bodies, lenses, apertures,
shutter speeds, film stock, or exposure settings. This chapter does not infer
them.

## Era and approach

These works span 1922–30, with one later print explicitly identified below.
They belong to interwar New Vision photography: an approach that embraced
unconventional viewpoints, experiments with light and form, photograms,
photomontage, and the visual language of modern technological culture. The
Metropolitan Museum identifies Moholy-Nagy as a major New Vision advocate and
his Bauhaus period as 1923–28; MoMA places his photogram and photomontage
experiments from mid-1922. [Met: New Vision](https://www.metmuseum.org/de/essays/the-new-vision-of-photography)
[Met: Fotogramm](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/265200)
[MoMA chronology](https://www.moma.org/interactives/objectphoto/artists/4048.html)

## Technique and printing

Eleven selected objects are catalogued as gelatin silver prints in the linked
Met records in `sources.md`. *Fotogramm* (1922) is a cameraless photogram:
objects were placed on photographic paper and exposed to light, producing an
image through contact, transparency, and tonal overlap rather than lens
perspective. *From the Radio Tower, Berlin* is dated 1928 and explicitly
catalogued as printed circa 1940; for the other works the record gives one
object date, so this chapter treats it as a date rather than asserting a
separate print date.

## Camera settings

No verified camera model, focal length, aperture, shutter speed, film or
sensitized-paper brand, or sensitivity is available for this selection in the
supplied records. For *Fotogramm*, camera settings are not applicable because
the process is cameraless. Specific equipment or exposure figures would be
invented and are omitted.
