# László Moholy-Nagy — sources

This shortlist focuses on Moholy-Nagy's 1922–30 photographic work: photograms, radical high and low viewpoints, and found industrial structure. All twelve masters were downloaded from Wikimedia Commons files donated by the Metropolitan Museum of Art. The corresponding Met records identify Moholy-Nagy as artist; the Commons records identify the files as Met Open Access **CC0**. Each local master was opened after download and exceeds the project’s 1200px long-edge floor.

**Rights basis:** CC0 (Metropolitan Museum of Art Open Access, served through Wikimedia Commons). Moholy-Nagy died in 1946; the Met’s explicit CC0 dedication governs reuse of these supplied digital files. See the Met’s [Open Access policy](https://www.metmuseum.org/about-the-met/policies-and-documents/open-access).

## Shortlist

### 01 — *[Cat, Seen From Above]* (c. 1926)

- **Why it matters:** An ordinary animal becomes a New Vision experiment in extreme viewpoint: the body is compressed into a steep, graphic downward view and the surrounding negative space makes scale unstable.
- **Source:** [Met collection record](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/264515); [Wikimedia Commons master](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:-Cat,_Seen_From_Above-_MET_DP369950.jpg).
- **Rights:** CC0 — Met Open Access.
- **File:** `raw/laszlo-moholy-nagy/01-cat-seen-from-above.jpg` (3884px long edge).

### 02 — *[Climbing the Mast]* (1928)

- **Why it matters:** The diagonal climb and the mast’s repeating metal members turn a worker in motion into an abstract construction, using the camera’s upward angle to make the frame feel vertiginous.
- **Source:** [Met collection record](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/285429); [Wikimedia Commons master](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:-Climbing_the_Mast-_MET_DP110380.jpg).
- **Rights:** CC0 — Met Open Access.
- **File:** `raw/laszlo-moholy-nagy/02-climbing-the-mast.jpg` (3979px long edge).

### 03 — *7 A.M. (New Year's Morning)* (c. 1930)

- **Why it matters:** A deliberately oblique morning view makes architecture and cast shadow the picture’s real subject, demonstrating Moholy-Nagy’s conversion of everyday urban light into hard-edged geometry.
- **Source:** [Met collection record](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/265194); [Wikimedia Commons master](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:7_A.M._(New_Year%27s_Morning)_MET_DP105547.jpg).
- **Rights:** CC0 — Met Open Access.
- **File:** `raw/laszlo-moholy-nagy/03-7-am-new-years-morning.jpg` (3809px long edge).

### 04 — *Behind the Back of the Gods* (1928)

- **Why it matters:** The title’s reversal is formal as well as conceptual: a close, off-axis view turns monumental form into an ambiguous overlap of dark silhouette, pale surface, and cropped edge.
- **Source:** [Met collection record](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/265277); [Wikimedia Commons master](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Behind_the_Back_of_the_Gods_MET_DP111634.jpg).
- **Rights:** CC0 — Met Open Access.
- **File:** `raw/laszlo-moholy-nagy/04-behind-the-back-of-the-gods.jpg` (3822px long edge).

### 05 — *Decorating Work, Switzerland* (1925)

- **Why it matters:** Worksite structure becomes a lattice of diagonals and rectangles; the framing makes labor, tools, and architectural surface read as one constructed visual system.
- **Source:** [Met collection record](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/283290); [Wikimedia Commons master](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Decorating_Work,_Switzerland_MET_DP210177.jpg).
- **Rights:** CC0 — Met Open Access.
- **File:** `raw/laszlo-moholy-nagy/05-decorating-work-switzerland.jpg` (3689px long edge).

### 06 — *Dolls on the Balcony* (1926)

- **Why it matters:** The balcony’s rails divide the frame into a severe grid while the dolls introduce a small, uncanny cluster of forms, balancing repeated structure against irregular figures.
- **Source:** [Met collection record](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/283291); [Wikimedia Commons master](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Dolls_on_the_Balcony_MET_DP165114.jpg).
- **Rights:** CC0 — Met Open Access.
- **File:** `raw/laszlo-moholy-nagy/06-dolls-on-the-balcony.jpg` (3850px long edge).

### 07 — *Fischernetze auf Isola Bella* (c. 1930)

- **Why it matters:** Fishing nets supply a dense foreground web through which the eye reaches water and shore, a concise demonstration of Moholy-Nagy’s use of a patterned screen to activate depth.
- **Source:** [Met collection record](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/264891); [Wikimedia Commons master](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Fischernetze_auf_Isola_Bella_MET_DP209275.jpg).
- **Rights:** CC0 — Met Open Access.
- **File:** `raw/laszlo-moholy-nagy/07-fischernetze-isola-bella.jpg` (3769px long edge).

### 08 — *Fotogramm* (1922)

- **Why it matters:** This early camera-less image isolates translucent and opaque objects as overlapping tonal silhouettes, making contact, scale, and edge rather than conventional perspective do the compositional work.
- **Source:** [Met collection record](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/265275); [Wikimedia Commons master](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Fotogramm_MET_DP103107.jpg).
- **Rights:** CC0 — Met Open Access.
- **File:** `raw/laszlo-moholy-nagy/08-fotogramm.jpg` (3756px long edge).

### 09 — *From the Radio Tower, Berlin* (1928; printed c. 1940)

- **Why it matters:** One of the New Vision’s canonical high-angle photographs: the tower converts streets, vehicles, and pedestrians below into a steeply foreshortened field of lines and tiny counterweights.
- **Source:** [Met collection record](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/285731); [Wikimedia Commons master](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:From_the_Radio_Tower,_Berlin_MET_DP146680.jpg).
- **Rights:** CC0 — Met Open Access.
- **File:** `raw/laszlo-moholy-nagy/09-from-the-radio-tower-berlin.jpg` (3688px long edge).

### 10 — *Pont Transbordeur, Marseille* (1929)

- **Why it matters:** The transport bridge’s vast truss creates a sharply segmented industrial frame, with the harbor’s smaller forms arranged beneath it to make engineering scale immediately legible.
- **Source:** [Met collection record](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/285944); [Wikimedia Commons master](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Pont_Transbordeur,_Marseille_MET_DP139561.jpg).
- **Rights:** CC0 — Met Open Access.
- **File:** `raw/laszlo-moholy-nagy/10-pont-transbordeur-marseille.jpg` (1890px long edge).

### 11 — *Scandinavia* (1930)

- **Why it matters:** A close, angled view makes the subject read first as a collision of planes, railings, and shadows; the photograph pushes documentary description toward an almost Constructivist arrangement.
- **Source:** [Met collection record](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/283698); [Wikimedia Commons master](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Scandinavia_MET_DP106537.jpg).
- **Rights:** CC0 — Met Open Access.
- **File:** `raw/laszlo-moholy-nagy/11-scandinavia.jpg` (3893px long edge).

### 12 — *Target Practice (In the Name of the Law)* (c. 1927)

- **Why it matters:** The target’s circular signal and its surrounding hard diagonals make a compact visual argument about looking, aiming, and control; it is a pointed use of industrial signage as composition.
- **Source:** [Met collection record](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/265278); [Wikimedia Commons master](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Target_Practice_(In_the_Name_of_the_Law)_MET_DP103109.jpg).
- **Rights:** CC0 — Met Open Access.
- **File:** `raw/laszlo-moholy-nagy/12-target-practice.jpg` (3735px long edge).
