# Alexander Gardner — research

Scope: technique, era, printing, and recorded camera settings for the eleven
plates in this chapter. This is wet-plate collodion work from the 1860s–70s
with no per-frame exposure record; where a number is not documented, this
says so.

## Who and when

Alexander Gardner (1821–1882) was born in Paisley, Scotland, and worked as a
journalist and an actuary before photography became his trade; he learned the
medium partly through Scottish contacts and through the photographic
department he helped run for a Glasgow newspaper venture. He emigrated to the
United States in 1856 and by 1858 was managing Mathew Brady's Washington,
D.C. gallery. In 1862, in part over Brady's practice of publishing every
employee's negative simply as "Photographed by Brady," Gardner split from the
firm, and on May 26, 1863 opened his own studio at 7th and D Streets NW,
hiring away several of Brady's field operators. He photographed the Antietam
battlefield in September–October 1862 (images 05–07, 11, made while he was
still nominally attached to Brady's operation but in the field himself),
covered the trial, scaffold, and execution of the Lincoln conspirators in
1865 (02, 08), sat Lincoln for his last formal portrait about ten weeks before
the assassination (01), photographed the crowd at the 1865 second inauguration
(09) and Walt Whitman in his Washington studio (10), and later worked as
official photographer to the Union Pacific Railroad's Eastern Division before
returning to studio portraiture, including the 1872 Indian delegation
sittings for the Interior Department (04). He died in Washington in 1882.
His 1866 two-volume *Photographic Sketch Book of the War* is notable in the
history of the medium as one of the first American photographic publications
to credit each plate individually — negative by the field operator, positive
by Gardner as printer/publisher — rather than folding every image into a
single studio byline.

## Technique

This is wet-plate (wet collodion) glass-negative photography, the dominant
process of the 1850s–1870s. A glass plate was coated with collodion holding
iodide and bromide salts, sensitized in a bath of silver nitrate, exposed
while still wet, and developed within minutes — which meant a portable
darkroom (a covered wagon in the field, a fixed darkroom in the studio) had
to travel with the camera. Gardner's field and studio work used large-format
cameras producing glass negatives on the order of whole-plate size and
larger, including a wide format around 7x9 inches for landscape and group
work; the studio portraits here (01, 02, 04, 10) were made on a stand camera
with the sitter held in place, often with a head brace, for the duration of
the exposure. In the field (05–07, 09, 11) the same wet-plate discipline
applied to landscapes, battlefield ruins, and outdoor group and crowd scenes,
with the camera leveled and set back far enough to hold a wide, deep field in
focus — the "acre of ground" scale relationships the sources note depend on
that depth of field, not on a telephoto effect no camera of the era had.

## Printing

The finished glass negatives were contact-printed — there being no enlargers
in this era — onto albumen paper coated with egg-white-bound silver salts,
then toned, typically with gold chloride, washed, and mounted. The Sketch
Book plates (many of the images here) were tipped into an album with a
facing page of text and a credit line naming both the field photographer and
Gardner as the positive-printer/publisher. Surviving material for this
chapter is split across institutions: five images are direct Library of
Congress Prints and Photographs Division records (the Civil War Glass
Negatives collection and the Brady-Handy collection), three are Open Access
(CC0) albumen prints at the Metropolitan Museum of Art (02, 03, and 05), and
three are high-resolution reproductions or mirrors: the National Portrait
Gallery's "cracked plate" print (01) via Wikimedia Commons, a Beinecke Library
scan of the Library of Congress-catalogued Red Cloud sitting (04), and a
Wikimedia Commons mirror of a Library of Congress scan (10). The NPG print
(NPG.81.M1) shows the diagonal fracture in the glass negative that occurred
when the emulsion cracked, limiting Gardner to the single surviving pull
before he discarded the plate.

## Camera settings

Not recorded. Wet-plate photography of this era predates any per-frame
aperture, shutter-speed, or sensitivity log; there was no light meter, and
exposure was judged by the operator's experience with a given plate's fresh
sensitivity, the light, and the lens in use. What can be said is qualitative
and well documented: collodion plates were far slower than modern film, so
studio portrait sittings (01, 02, 04, 10) required the sitter to hold still
for several seconds under strong studio light, often braced, and outdoor work
(05–09, 11) required a stopped-down lens and several seconds of exposure on
a leveled stand camera even in full daylight, which is consistent with the
deep, evenly focused fields the sources describe across both the battlefield
and studio plates. No specific f-stop, shutter speed, or sensitivity value
survives for any of the eleven images, and none is stated here.
