# Alexander Gardner — sources

Gardner's compositional signature is documentary, not painterly: a level camera, a deep, evenly lit field, and figures or bodies distributed across the frame as evidence rather than arranged as design. He worked in the widescreen 7x9 wet-plate format at eye level or slightly above, letting a horizon line or a fence line run nearly the full width of the frame and using scale contrast — a tiny standing figure against an acre of dead, a president dwarfed by his own generals — to do the compositional work that framing devices do for other photographers. He was also the first Civil War photographer to insist on individual authorship: where Mathew Brady's studio published everything simply as "Brady," Gardner's own 1866 *Photographic Sketch Book of the War* prints a named credit line — negative by X, positive by A. Gardner — under every one of its hundred plates. That credit line matters for this chapter. Two of the album's most reproduced images, *A Harvest of Death* and *Home of a Rebel Sharpshooter* (both Gettysburg, July 1863), are catalogued by the Library of Congress and by Gardner's own contemporaneous sales catalog as negatives by his field operator Timothy H. O'Sullivan, with Gardner credited only as the publisher/printer of the positive. Since O'Sullivan is a separate chapter in this project, both images are deliberately excluded here in favor of the roughly seventeen Sketch Book plates and the many later Washington photographs where Gardner himself is the credited photographer of record — the Antietam battlefield work he shot personally in September–October 1862, the Washington studio and prison-yard portraiture of 1865, and the 1872 Indian delegation sittings, none of which overlap the Brady or O'Sullivan chapters.

**Rights basis:** Public domain. All eleven images were made between 1862 and 1872, more than a century past any copyright term, and survive as glass-plate negatives or vintage prints held by U.S. federal or Open Access institutions. Five are direct Library of Congress Prints and Photographs Division records (Civil War Glass Negatives / Brady-Handy collections, digitized at full master-TIFF resolution); three are Metropolitan Museum of Art Open Access (CC0) prints (02, 03, and 05); and three are high-resolution reproductions or mirrors: 01 is a Wikimedia Commons scan of the National Portrait Gallery print, 04 a Beinecke Library scan of a Library of Congress-catalogued sitting, and 10 a Wikimedia Commons mirror of a Library of Congress scan. All eleven shortlisted images were fetched cleanly at long-edge resolutions between 2700px and 7500px; no NEEDED.md was required for this chapter.

## Shortlist

### 01 — Abraham Lincoln, "Cracked Plate" portrait (February 5, 1865)
- **Why it matters:** A level, dead-center bust portrait against a plain studio ground, lit from the sitter's left so one side of Lincoln's face falls into deep shadow — the last formal sitting before his assassination, and the starkest use of plain-ground isolation in Gardner's studio work.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2009630692/ (Library of Congress); original negative/print at the National Portrait Gallery
- **Rights:** Public domain (created 1865; Wikimedia Commons high-resolution scan of the surviving print)
- **File:** raw/alexander-gardner/01-lincoln-cracked-plate.jpg (5262px)

### 02 — Lewis Powell [alias Lewis Payne], Lincoln assassination conspirator (April 27, 1865)
- **Why it matters:** Full-length, standing, plain ground, Powell's direct stare and folded coat filling the vertical frame with almost no incident around him — Gardner's prison-yard portrait format stripped to its barest geometry, the same discipline he later applied to the delegation sittings.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/283201
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/alexander-gardner/02-lewis-powell-conspirator.jpg (3966px)

### 03 — Ruins of Richmond & Petersburg Railroad Bridge, Richmond, Virginia (ca. 1865)
- **Why it matters:** The burned trestle's repeating piers recede on a strong diagonal into the Richmond skyline across the James — a rare Gardner landscape built entirely on receding structural rhythm rather than incident, and formerly misattributed to Brady before the Met's own re-cataloging.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/268013
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/alexander-gardner/03-ruins-richmond-bridge.jpg (3917px)

### 04 — Red Cloud, Oglala Lakota delegation portrait (May 1872)
- **Why it matters:** Seated three-quarter view against a plain backdrop, blanket folds and hands built into a closed pyramidal mass — Gardner's later Indian Bureau studio portraiture applying the same frontal, uncluttered discipline he used on Powell seven years earlier, now to a sitter treated with evident formal respect.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/pictures/item/2004665242/ (Blackmore Museum sitting, cataloged at the Library of Congress); high-resolution scan via Beinecke Library, Yale
- **Rights:** Public domain (created 1872)
- **File:** raw/alexander-gardner/04-red-cloud-portrait.jpg (4875px)

### 05 — Burnside Bridge, Across Antietam Creek, Maryland (September 1862)
- **Why it matters:** The stone bridge's three arches sit low and dead-center under a wide sky, the creek's reflection doubling the arcade — the clearest instance of Gardner using pure architectural repetition, undisturbed by a single figure, to compose a battlefield landscape.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/259600
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/alexander-gardner/05-burnside-bridge-antietam.jpg (3873px)

### 06 — President Lincoln on the Battle-field of Antietam, with Gen. McClellan and Staff (October 3, 1862)
- **Why it matters:** Sixteen men strung out in a single shallow line before the headquarters tent, Lincoln and McClellan isolated at the composition's center by a gap in the row — Gardner using pure lateral spacing, not a frame-within-a-frame, to single out his two principal subjects.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2002713085/
- **Rights:** Public domain (Library of Congress, Prints and Photographs Division)
- **File:** raw/alexander-gardner/06-lincoln-mcclellan-antietam.jpg (6659px)

### 07 — Bodies of Confederate Artillerymen near Dunker Church, Antietam (September 19, 1862)
- **Why it matters:** A dead battery crew and their horse lie scattered across the near ground while the whitewashed Dunker Church sits small and level on the horizon behind them — Gardner's signature scale device, an acre of foreground evidence set against one calm architectural anchor.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2012647801/
- **Rights:** Public domain (Library of Congress, Prints and Photographs Division)
- **File:** raw/alexander-gardner/07-dunker-church-dead-antietam.jpg (3166px)

### 08 — Washington, D.C. Execution of the Conspirators: View of the Scaffold (July 7, 1865)
- **Why it matters:** A near-symmetrical timber gallows fills the frame under a crowd ranked in tiers behind a fence line — Gardner was the only photographer permitted at the hanging, and the composition's rigid geometric staging turns the scaffold itself into the subject.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2018672087/
- **Rights:** Public domain (Library of Congress, Prints and Photographs Division)
- **File:** raw/alexander-gardner/08-execution-scaffold-conspirators.jpg (7500px)

### 09 — Crowd at President Lincoln's Second Inauguration, Washington, D.C. (March 4, 1865)
- **Why it matters:** The East Portico's columns divide the frame into even vertical bays while a dense crowd fills the plaza below in an unbroken horizontal mass — one of Gardner's few architecture-plus-crowd compositions, built on the same grid discipline as his portrait backdrops, scaled up to a civic stage.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2018670563/
- **Rights:** Public domain (Library of Congress, Prints and Photographs Division)
- **File:** raw/alexander-gardner/09-crowd-second-inauguration.jpg (5120px)

### 10 — Walt Whitman, head-and-shoulders portrait (1863)
- **Why it matters:** Whitman turned slightly off-axis against a plain ground, coat and open collar filling the lower frame in soft tonal masses — Gardner's studio portrait discipline applied to a literary sitter rather than a soldier or a president, the same frontal economy carried into peacetime.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/resource/ppmsca.07545/ (Library of Congress); high-resolution scan mirrored on Wikimedia Commons
- **Rights:** Public domain (created 1863)
- **File:** raw/alexander-gardner/10-walt-whitman-portrait.jpg (5209px)

### 11 — Antietam, Md. President Lincoln and Gen. McClellan in the General's Tent (October 3, 1862)
- **Why it matters:** Just two men and a folding chair inside the close canvas walls of the tent — the intimate counterpart to the wide staff-line composition (06), showing Gardner switching registers from open-field group tableau to a tight two-figure interior study within the same sitting.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/item/2018666252/
- **Rights:** Public domain (Library of Congress, Prints and Photographs Division)
- **File:** raw/alexander-gardner/11-lincoln-mcclellan-tent.jpg (7500px)
