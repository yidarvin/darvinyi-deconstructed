# Source notes — Manuel Álvarez Bravo

## Acquisition and rights approach

This source pass uses twelve canonical photographs that can be directly viewed
and downloaded from public collection pages. Eleven are web-sized display
images supplied by the Art Institute of Chicago (AIC); AIC's object records
identify Manuel Álvarez Bravo as their maker but do not publish a copyright
notice or an open licence for these works. They are therefore used here as
`fair-use`, with credit to Manuel Álvarez Bravo and the holding institution; no
open licence is inferred from the public display service.

For every `fair-use` item below: (1) this is a critical, scholarly, and
transformative composition-teaching use rather than a substitute for the
photograph's ordinary market; (2) the complete frame is necessary to discuss
Bravo's cropping, tonal structure, and spatial relationships; (3) the use is a
web-sized reference copy; and (4) the chapter credits and links to the museum
record, and does not replace the original print, the museum record, or a
licensed reproduction. This assessment is made for each identified work.

## Selected photographs

1. **Two Pairs of Legs (Dos pares de piernas), 1928/29 (printed 1977)**
   — The radically cropped legs turn a street fragment into a stacked,
   alternating rhythm, making it a clear early statement of Bravo's oblique
   framing. Source: [Art Institute of Chicago object 63471](https://www.artic.edu/artworks/63471).
   **Use basis: fair-use.** Creator: Manuel Álvarez Bravo; credit line: Gift of
   Frederick M. and Elizabeth L. Myers, AIC. AIC displays no copyright notice;
   the four-factor criticism, complete-frame necessity, web-size, and
   credit/link rationale stated above applies specifically to this image.

2. **Laughing Mannequins (Maniquís riendo), 1930**
   — Repeated shop-window heads turn an urban detail into an unsettling
   pattern; it teaches his use of serial forms and shallow planes. Source:
   [Art Institute of Chicago object 92150](https://www.artic.edu/artworks/92150).
   **Use basis: fair-use.** Creator: Manuel Álvarez Bravo; credit line: Julien
   Levy Collection, gift of Jean Levy and the estate of Julien Levy, AIC. AIC
   displays no copyright notice; the four-factor criticism, complete-frame
   necessity, web-size, and credit/link rationale above applies specifically.

3. **Optical Parable (Parábola óptica), 1931 (printed later)**
   — The image's compressed shadow, wall, and object relationships make a
   lesson in how a modest encounter can be made visually enigmatic. Source:
   [Art Institute of Chicago object 48888](https://www.artic.edu/artworks/48888).
   **Use basis: fair-use.** Creator: Manuel Álvarez Bravo; credit: AIC. AIC
   displays no copyright notice; the four-factor criticism, complete-frame
   necessity, web-size, and credit/link rationale above applies specifically.

4. **The Dreamer (El Soñador), 1931**
   — A figure, diagonals, and tight tonal zones turn an observed sleeper into a
   poised Surrealist-like scene without abandoning the everyday. Source:
   [Art Institute of Chicago object 48891](https://www.artic.edu/artworks/48891).
   **Use basis: fair-use.** Creator: Manuel Álvarez Bravo; credit: AIC. AIC
   displays no copyright notice; the four-factor criticism, complete-frame

5. **The Daughter of the Dancers (La hija de los danzantes), 1933/34 (printed later)**
   — The frontal child and surrounding dark mass show how Bravo gives a
   documentary encounter the stillness and weight of a formal portrait. Source:
   [Art Institute of Chicago object 48876](https://www.artic.edu/artworks/48876).
   **Use basis: fair-use.** Creator: Manuel Álvarez Bravo; credit: AIC. AIC

7. **The Crouched Ones (Los agachados), 1934**
   — A row of bowed figures and the nearly continuous bench create a social

8. **Portrait of the Eternal (Retrato de lo eterno), 1935**

## Local acquisition record

The source files were retrieved without authentication or access-control
bypass: eleven from AIC's public IIIF display service and one from the public
Commons original-file redirect. All twelve files open as JPEGs. Their long
edges range from 843 to 2,772 pixels, exceeding the 640-pixel source floor.
