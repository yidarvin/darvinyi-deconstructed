# Horst P. Horst — research note

## Scope and records consulted

The local source dossier identifies eleven photographs by Horst P. Horst, dated 1934–1952, from the Victoria and Albert Museum's Horst collection and object records. It includes fashion commissions for Vogue, two Vogue covers, celebrity portraits, a nude, and the *Bacchanale* costume study. The public display files are credited either © Condé Nast/Horst Estate or © Victoria and Albert Museum, London, as recorded in [the source notes](sources.md). They are retained here solely as web-sized, credited fair-use reproductions for criticism and composition teaching.

## Visual method and era

The documented selection begins with Paris and Vogue work of the 1930s, includes the 1941 cover and 1942 Dietrich portrait, and reaches the 1947 Schiaparelli picture and 1952 *Male Nude*. The V&A characterizes Horst as a master of light, composition, and atmospheric illusion, and its collection spans fashion, celebrity portraiture, nudes, travel, and botanical studies. Within this chapter, claims about the studio's theatricality, silhouette, pose, internal framing, and tonal hierarchy are readings of the visible photographs and their deterministic analyses; they do not presume undocumented direction or intent.

## Process, printing, and camera settings

The cited V&A collection and object records, the source dossier, and the normalized files do **not** establish camera model, lens, focal length, aperture, shutter speed, film stock, negative format, lighting equipment, original print process, paper, print date, or darkroom method for these individual frames. The ingest manifest records image dimensions and no reliable capture EXIF; one malformed web-file date value is not historical capture evidence. No technical setting or printing recipe is inferred from the pictures' contrast, grain, color, or studio effects.

It is supportable to describe the pictures' visible light, pose, furniture, backdrop, costume, and tonal separation. It is not supportable here to turn those visual effects into claims about a particular camera, exposure, negative, or print process.
