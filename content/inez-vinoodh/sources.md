# Inez & Vinoodh — source shortlist

This working set pairs Inez van Lamsweerde and Vinoodh Matadin's early, digitally altered body image with seven public display photographs from Kunstmuseum Den Haag's 2026 career retrospective. The museum page identifies the exhibition as a forty-year retrospective and explicitly describes the artists as pioneers of digital image-making whose work moves between the familiar and unsettling. Its carousel serves several image files without individual captions; where the page does not publish a work title or date, the descriptive bracketed title and “date not supplied” below are deliberately not asserted as catalogue metadata. All eight JPEGs were retrieved directly from the public display endpoints and opened successfully.

**Rights basis:** **fair-use** for every selection. These are copyrighted contemporary photographs. The listed authoritative display pages credit Inez van Lamsweerde / Vinoodh Matadin (the Christie's record is headed “Inez Van Lamsweerde”; the Kunstmuseum exhibition identifies “Inez & Vinoodh”). Each use is independently limited to transformative criticism, scholarship, and composition teaching: the chapter needs the complete frame to analyze the artists' staging, digital alteration, body/ground relation, and fashion-image intervention; only a web-sized copy is kept; and the chapter credits and links to the source rather than replacing the artist's work, museum record, original, or licensed reproduction. The four-factor assessment is stated separately for each image below.

## Research context

Kunstmuseum Den Haag's exhibition page, *Can Love Be a Photograph: 40 years of Inez & Vinoodh*, describes the artists as pioneers of digital image-making and characterizes their practice through the tension between the familiar and the unsettling. Museum Boijmans' collection record for *Thank You Thighmaster Serie / Pam* likewise explains the 1993 series through digitally modified nude figures and the boundary between fashion, advertising, television, and everyday reality. These sources support reading the early altered body and later fashion/editorial pictures together as a study of construction, not as uncredited fashion illustration.
