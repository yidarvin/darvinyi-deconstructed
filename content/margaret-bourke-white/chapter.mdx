Margaret Bourke-White makes scale feel made rather than simply found. Her 1936 Fort Peck sequence moves from workers' faces and barroom encounters to pipe liners, scaffolds, rails, and a temporary town, but the visual system remains exact: bodies give machinery a unit of measure, repeated structural edges make depth readable, and signs are allowed to dominate the people beneath them. The 1937 *Louisville Flood* gives that system an ethical charge. A billboard's promise of the “American Way” is not background to the relief queue; it is the bright, enclosing upper half of the picture that makes the queue impossible to read innocently.

The source records identify the Fort Peck cover as the first **LIFE** cover and document the Louisville photograph's social contrast, but they do not preserve per-frame camera or exposure settings. No aperture, shutter speed, lens, film stock, camera, negative, or original printing process is claimed here. The tonal and palette observations below describe the supplied web renditions, and the overlays mark only visible, measured structures.

*Workers on Montana's Fort Peck Dam blow off steam at night* is a close horizontal group rather than an industrial panorama. The overlay follows the lit contour of the woman at left and marks the compact central pair; these linked arms and faces make the photograph's crowdedness physical. *At the Time of the Louisville Flood* holds two incompatible images in one rectangle: the idealized car-and-family billboard above, the actual relief line below. ## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Give a large object a human measure">
Photograph a large built structure with people visibly inside, beside, or beneath it. Before adding an overlay, decide whether the people are merely scale marks or the actual subject. Draw only the structural edge that connects those two readings.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Let text change the picture">
Photograph a sign whose wording matters, then make a second frame that includes the person or place the sign affects. Treat the lettering as a shape with visual weight, not a caption pasted onto the photograph.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Test a detector's horizon">
Photograph a framed print, poster, or screen with a strong horizontal edge. When analysis finds a horizon, check whether it is actually the scene's ground plane, a counter, a billboard edge, or the border of an object. Name only what the line is.
</ExerciseCard>
