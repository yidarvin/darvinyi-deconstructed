# Margaret Bourke-White — source shortlist

Margaret Bourke-White made industrial scale and social observation legible through emphatic framing: an architectural mass can dwarf a worker, while a bar, street, or relief line becomes an equally deliberate arrangement of faces, signs, and thresholds. This source set pairs eleven publicly displayed frames from LIFE's complete Fort Peck Dam feature with *The Louisville Flood*, the image that makes her social juxtaposition most explicit. All files are direct, public web renditions, verified as readable JPEGs with long edges from 692px to 1024px.

## Research context

The [National Gallery of Art collection record](https://www.nga.gov/artworks/165921-fort-peck-dam-montana) identifies *Fort Peck Dam, Montana* as the 23 November 1936 inaugural LIFE cover and describes Bourke-White's construction imagery as a confrontation between monumental structures and workers. The [Whitney record](https://whitney.org/collection/works/8061) explains the Louisville image's contrast between flood-relief recipients and the billboard's “American Way” message. Those authoritative records anchor a chapter that can move from machine-age mass to the ethical force of a social documentary frame.
