# Margaret Bourke-White — build research

## Scope and evidence

This chapter draws eleven 1936 Fort Peck photographs from LIFE's public feature and the 1937 *At the Time of the Louisville Flood* from the Whitney Museum of American Art. The local [source shortlist](sources.md) records the direct public renditions, credits, and a separate fair-use rationale for each frame. The National Gallery of Art identifies Bourke-White's *Fort Peck Dam, Montana* as the inaugural 23 November 1936 **LIFE** cover and frames the work as an encounter between monumental construction and the people who made it. The Whitney collection record identifies the Louisville image's opposition between flood-relief recipients and the billboard's “American Way” message.

## Technique and visual method

Bourke-White's published Fort Peck sequence moves between broad industrial views, close portraiture, workplace interiors, and domestic scenes. In this source set, scale is never merely a matter of distance: workers climb inside a vast circular liner, line up in a construction site, or crowd the foreground of a vertical frame; pipes and braces turn the engineered site into curves, diagonals, and repeating structural rhythms. Her social photographs use the same graphic discipline. A signboard overwhelms the people below it; a bar's posted words, door opening, and counter make text into a compositional force; the Louisville billboard and relief queue are held in deliberately opposed horizontal registers.

The available collection records establish dates, subjects, and institutional context, but do **not** provide per-image camera model, lens, negative format, aperture, shutter speed, film stock, or exposure. No such setting is inferred from the digitized JPEGs or their modern metadata. The analyses in this chapter therefore describe visible geometry, measured tonal behavior, and palette only; they do not claim a technical setting the record does not preserve.

## Printing and object history

The supplied source records describe publicly displayed reproductions and image rights, not the original negative and print process for each plate. This build consequently does not identify an original printing process. The 12 ingested files are normalized web renditions, and the manifest records only their present image dimensions and any surviving file metadata, not Bourke-White's camera settings or original print specifications.
