Eadweard Muybridge's *Animal Locomotion* plates are not single instants. They are printed systems for comparing instants: a black sequence field holds repeated photographic phases inside rows and columns, so a movement becomes legible as variation against a stable measure. The overlays therefore mark the plate field and its row structure, not the gray-scale calibration bars, scan margins, or the false horizons and symmetries those artifacts induce in automated measurement.

## the plate is the composition

In the horse-and-rider plate, a centered sequence field holds four horizontal bands of successive exposures. The overlay's dividers make the plate's real order visible: each row resets the comparison while the changing gait provides the difference. The analyzer finds a broad grayscale palette from #0d0d0d through #e8e8e8 and a modest 0.038 edge density; that combination lets the dark field and pale individual panels read as large, stable masses before the small changes of the horse are inspected.

<Plate slug="eadweard-muybridge" id="01-man-riding-horse-plate-636" label="Animal Locomotion, plate 636: man riding a horse, 1887" caption="A four-band sequence field makes the changing horse-and-rider phases comparable without treating the scan border as a horizon." />

The woman-with-children plate makes a sharper two-row contrast. The upper and lower sequences retain a recurring adult-and-children grouping, yet each panel changes the distance and contact among the figures. Its 0.361 tonal deviation, the widest in this small selection, comes from bright dresses and the deep plate field; those value extremes make the grouping register before individual poses do.

## rows make time comparable

The incline study makes the serial grammar unusually explicit. Four stacked rows place the walker against the same sloping support, turning posture into a repeated test rather than a single expressive figure. The overlay marks the field and row dividers because that is the plate's strongest compositional fact. Its low 0.038 edge density and palette from near-black #0f0f0f to #dddddd keep the many small cells from becoming visually chaotic.

<Plate slug="eadweard-muybridge" id="03-man-walking-incline-plate-489" label="Animal Locomotion, plate 489: man walking on an incline, 1887" caption="Four time-rows use the repeated panel field to make changes in gait and incline comparable." />

The jumping plate pairs two views of the same action in stacked rows. The side-view row gives the jump its changing silhouette; the rear-view row tests the movement from a second direction. The overlay labels those row identities rather than drawing an invented smooth trajectory across panel boundaries. A 0.319 tonal spread and 0.037 edge density again describe an image governed by broad dark and light compartments, not by a dense field of incidental detail.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Build a sequence field">
Photograph one small action in several separate frames. Keep the viewpoint and panel size stable enough that changes in the action, not changes in the container, become the main information.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Let rows reset attention">
Arrange a sequence in two or more rows. Compare what a row break does: it can turn continuous looking into a set of deliberate comparisons.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Separate the object from its scan">
Photograph or scan a printed page with margins, targets, or labels. Identify the internal picture field before making compositional claims. A strong measured edge may belong to the scan apparatus rather than to the pictured work.
</ExerciseCard>
