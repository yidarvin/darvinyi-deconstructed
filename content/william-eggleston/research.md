# William Eggleston — research notes

## Record-backed context

This source set spans photographs made from the later 1960s through about 1985: the grocery-store picture is dated c. 1965–74; several selected works date from c. 1970–71; *Shiloh National Military Park* is dated 1978; and *Tennessee* c. 1985. The object records in `sources.md` distinguish capture dates from later print dates, including 1980, 1996, 2002, 2004, and 2007. The Art Institute’s record for *Memphis* places Eggleston’s 1976 Museum of Modern Art exhibition and *William Eggleston’s Guide* at a decisive moment for serious art photography in color. The Whitney describes the “democratic” approach as bringing supposedly banal subjects into sustained photographic attention. See `sources.md` and https://www.artic.edu/artworks/188843/memphis and https://whitney.org/collection/works/9204.

## Printing and technique

The selected works do not share one universal print process. Art Institute records identify *Memphis*, *Near Jackson, Mississippi*, *Halloween, Morton, Mississippi*, *Untitled* (1974), *Tennessee*, and *Rosa Sleeping* as dye-imbibition prints; *Shiloh National Military Park* is a chromogenic print. The Whitney identifies *Greenwood, Mississippi*, *Untitled (Grocery Store)*, *Untitled (Car Wreck)*, and *Untitled (Baby Doll Cadillac)* as dye-transfer prints, and *Huntsville, Alabama* as chromogenic. The Whitney explains that dye transfer can produce unusually pure, intense color; it is accurate to discuss the red ceiling as a dye-transfer color field, but not to call every plate here dye transfer. For the 2002 *Memphis* and *Near Jackson* editions, the Art Institute identifies Nino Mondhe, printing under Eggleston’s supervision in Hamburg. Collection links are recorded individually in `sources.md`; supporting records include https://api.artic.edu/api/v1/artworks/188843, https://api.artic.edu/api/v1/artworks/58364, and https://whitney.org/collection/works/7845.

## Camera and settings

The supplied museum records document works, dates, media, dimensions, and rights; they do not document Eggleston’s camera body, lens, film stock, aperture, shutter speed, lighting, or exposure settings for these photographs. The manifest’s Hasselblad EXIF on several Whitney downloads belongs to the later collection digitization (with 2012/2021 timestamps), not the historical exposure. This chapter therefore makes no capture-setting claim and stays with visible framing, color, tone, and spatial relations.

## Visual method observed in this set

Eggleston makes ordinary sites exact without making them tidy. An oven becomes a nested rack lattice; a garment becomes a small color event in a blank room; a road, ceiling wire, bed, bridge, or supermarket aisle directs attention without producing a conventional heroic subject. The analysis files measure tonal spread, palette clusters, and edge density, but the overlays only name visible structures after the presentation borders in the museum display files were excluded. Measurements support description; they do not prove intention.
