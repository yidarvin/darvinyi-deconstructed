# Jacob Riis — source shortlist

The files are highest-resolution masters downloaded from the Library of Congress Prints & Photographs Division. LOC’s catalog wording is **“No known restrictions on publication”**; it does not itself grant rights, so retain credit to the collection and make a final reuse assessment at publication.

1. **Five cents a spot—unauthorized immigration lodgings in a Bayard Street tenement** — c. 1890. Flash-lit sleepers compress into a shallow, dark enclosure, with the central face as the eye’s anchor. [LOC record](https://www.loc.gov/item/2002710259/). **Rights:** LOC “No known restrictions on publication.”
2. **Baxter Street Alley in Mulberry Bend** — 1888. Steep walls and receding figures make an exceptionally clear corridor and vanishing-point composition. [LOC record](https://www.loc.gov/item/2002710255/). **Rights:** LOC “No known restrictions on publication.”
3. **Bohemian cigarmakers at work in their tenement** — 1889. Worktable and bodies form a dense multi-figure cluster within a claustrophobic room. [LOC record](https://www.loc.gov/item/2002710251/). **Rights:** LOC “No known restrictions on publication.”
4. **Twelve year old boy “pulling threads” in a “sweatshop”** — 1889. A single child is set against compact workshop geometry, making subject scale and labour lines explicit. [LOC record](https://www.loc.gov/item/2002710250/). **Rights:** LOC “No known restrictions on publication.”
5. **Court at 24 Baxter Street** — 1888. The courtyard’s dark window grid and figures around a central void make negative space and enclosure visible. [LOC record](https://www.loc.gov/item/2002710268/). **Rights:** LOC “No known restrictions on publication.”
6. **Mullen’s Alley, Cherry Alley** — 1888. Stacked façades and foreground figures emphasize the narrow social space’s compression. [LOC record](https://www.loc.gov/item/2002710261/). **Rights:** LOC “No known restrictions on publication.”
7. **Children’s playground, Poverty Gap** — 1888. An irregular open foreground populated by children offers a revealing spatial contrast with Riis’s trapped alleys. [LOC record](https://www.loc.gov/item/2002710264/). **Rights:** LOC “No known restrictions on publication.”
8. **Sweatshop in Ludlow Street Tenement, New York** — 1889. Repeating workers and tables create rhythm and depth within the room’s hard perimeter. [LOC record](https://www.loc.gov/item/2002710271/). **Rights:** LOC “No known restrictions on publication.”
9. **Italian mother and her baby in Jersey Street** — 1890. A doorway edge frames a quiet two-figure arrangement and directs attention to the maternal focal point. [LOC record](https://www.loc.gov/item/2002710272/). **Rights:** LOC “No known restrictions on publication.”
10. **Baby in slum tenement hallway, N.Y.C.** — 1888. A small solitary subject is placed in a long dark hall whose lines amplify scale and isolation. [LOC record](https://www.loc.gov/item/2002710274/). **Rights:** LOC “No known restrictions on publication.”
11. **Flat in Hell’s Kitchen** — 1889. Diagonal furnishings and door openings organize constrained domestic depth around several figures. [LOC record](https://www.loc.gov/item/2002710279/). **Rights:** LOC “No known restrictions on publication.”
12. **Going to bed in the Five Points House of Industry** — 1890. Receding beds and repeated children make a graphic pattern punctuated by flash-lit accents. [LOC record](https://www.loc.gov/item/2002710296/). **Rights:** LOC “No known restrictions on publication.”

Context source: the [LOC/MCNY exhibition](https://www.loc.gov/exhibits/jacob-riis/overview.html) identifies Riis’s tenement photographs as central to his reform work. The chapter should distinguish Riis’s reformist intent from the unequal, often intrusive conditions under which people were photographed.
