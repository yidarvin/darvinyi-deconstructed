# Jacob Riis — research

## Era and context

Jacob Riis (1849–1914) was a New York police reporter, writer, lecturer, and reformer working at the end of the nineteenth century, at the transition from the Gilded Age to the Progressive Era. His tenement pictures circulated with prose and lectures as arguments for housing reform. They are therefore not transparent records of urban poverty: Riis and his collaborators selected the spaces, captions, framing, timing, and methods of circulation. The people pictured are not illustrations of a category.

That caution is especially necessary for *Five cents a spot*. Museum of the City of New York teaching material describes that Bayard Street picture as a late-night police investigation made with newly invented flash powder while its subjects were unaware of the photograph. The other supplied images should not all be called candid: Riis also made posed photographs, and the early work could be collaborative. The Library of Congress documents that he initially worked with Richard Hoe Lawrence and Dr. Henry G. Piffard; later he photographed himself while commercial firms handled darkroom work. The chapter describes visible arrangement without assuming consent, authorship, or a single mode of encounter where the record does not establish one.

## Technique and printing

Riis adopted early low-light flash photography. The abrupt foreground illumination and dark recesses in several supplied images are consistent with that historically documented use, but the images alone do not identify a precise flash formula or quantity. The supplied LOC records catalogue photographic prints; for *Five cents a spot*, the record describes a photographic print and the online reproduction as a black-and-white film-copy negative. They should not be relabelled as original camera negatives. A separately catalogued MCNY tenement image is a gelatin dry-plate negative, but that object-specific medium cannot be generalized to every image here.

Riis's first presentation format also matters: he assembled one hundred images as "Other Half" lantern slides. Contemporary print publication commonly translated photographs into wood engravings or flat halftones. An illustrated *Scribner's Magazine* article appeared in December 1889, and *How the Other Half Lives* followed in 1890, chiefly illustrated from photographs. This does not establish that every selected image appeared photographically in the book, and the chapter makes no such claim.

## Camera settings

No authoritative record located for this selected group gives a camera model, lens, focal length, aperture, shutter or exposure time, plate/film speed, or flash-powder quantity. Those settings are not inferred from the pictures. The firm historical claim is limited to Riis's documented adoption of low-light flash photography and the collaborative conditions of some early work.
