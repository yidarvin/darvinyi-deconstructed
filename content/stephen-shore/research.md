# Stephen Shore — research notes

## Scope and sources

This chapter uses the ten 1973–75 road and street photographs documented in
`sources.md`, all from the Art Institute of Chicago. The supplied source record
describes them as large-format views associated with *Uncommon Places* and
records precise exposure dates for most of the group. It records 1976 print
dates for *Holden St.*, *U.S. 10*, *U.S. 93*, *El Paso St.*, *M 1/2 Ave.*, and
*Alley off Sunset Strip*. For the remaining four works, the supplied record
does not establish a print date.

## Method and historical facts

The source set supports a discussion of Shore's matter-of-fact color street
photographs: roadside businesses, parked cars, facades, signs, porches, and
service spaces are made to share the frame rather than serve as anecdotal
detail. That is the chapter's interpretive focus, grounded in the acquired
works and their collection records.

The supplied materials do **not** document a camera body, lens, film stock,
aperture, shutter speed, ISO, lighting setup, or exposure settings for these
original photographs. The ingested files contain no retained original-camera
EXIF. They are collection renditions, so later digitization metadata would not
be evidence about the original exposure in any event. The chapter therefore
describes visible framing, space, tonal distribution, and palette rather than
inventing technical settings.

## Image-specific teaching cues

- *Holden St.*: fire-escape edge, curb line, and closely opposed buildings
  produce a deep street pocket around the distant white house.
- *U.S. 10*: onion bags, an open car, trucks, boxes, and duplicated signage
  distribute attention instead of giving the scene a hierarchy.
- *U.S. 93*: a huge concrete apron makes the highway businesses and distant
  ridge feel deliberately small and evenly weighted.
- *El Paso St.*: a foreground tree, signs, pedestrians, and the crossing road
  layer several scales of civic space.
- *M 1/2 Ave.*: stair, porch rail, lower doorway, and car show how adjoining
  lateral planes prevent a domestic facade from settling into symmetry.
- *Alley off Sunset Strip*: the alley opening and road bend create a narrow
  stage for a sunlit, textured hillside.
- *Meeting St.*: repeated windows supply an all-over rhythm, broken by the
  fire stair and parked cars.
- *Second Street East*: road recession is interrupted by poles, signs, and
  pedestrian-scale traffic signals.
- *Bay Theater*: a vertical marquee organizes an otherwise broad street band
  of cars, lettering, and entrance activity.
- *Church Street*: side walls, curbs, awnings, and a cross street give an
  ordinary intersection a measured, planar order.
