# Carleton Watkins — research

Scope: technique, era, printing, and recorded camera information for the
selected Getty photographs. Where the catalogue record does not document a
setting, this does not supply one.

## Era and process

The selected work ranges from the 1861 Yosemite negative of *Tutocanula, or
El Capitan* to a view made around 1880. The physical prints do not all date
with their negatives: *Mirror Lake* is a 1861 negative printed around 1867,
*Multnomah Falls* is a 1867 negative printed in 1881–85, and *Among the
Treetops* is a 1878 negative printed in 1880–90. The chapter therefore spans
Watkins's 1860s–70s image-making and prints extending to about 1890.

The Getty records catalogue all nine selected objects as albumen silver prints.
Watkins's western large-format landscapes were made from wet-collodion glass
negatives: a plate was coated, exposed, and developed while its collodion was
still wet, a field process that required a traveling darkroom. This describes
the negative process, not a claim that an albumen print itself is
"wet-collodion." In period practice the negative was contact-printed at its
intended size; these were not later enlargements. The Metropolitan Museum of
Art's overview of Watkins's western work documents that large-format
wet-plate practice; individual dates, formats, and print media in this set
come from the Getty records cited in `sources.md`.

## Recorded camera handling

Getty specifically records an 18 × 22-inch camera for *Multnomah Falls*, its
lens tilted upward so both tiers of the falls, the trees, and the gorge could
enter the frame; the leaning trunks are a consequence of that camera angle.
For *Among the Treetops*, Watkins put the same 18 × 22-inch camera on its back
for the upward-looking composition. In *View on Lake Tahoe*, the visible
shadow is identified as Watkins and his mammoth-plate camera. Getty also
notes that Watkins combined a second negative for the clouds in *Yosemite
Valley from the Best General View*, so that sky is a deliberate composite
element rather than a single instantaneous exposure.

## Settings

No aperture, lens focal length, shutter speed, or exposure duration is
documented for these individual photographs in the supplied records. None is
invented in this chapter.
