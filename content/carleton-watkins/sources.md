# Carleton Watkins — sources

Watkins made the nineteenth-century western landscape into a deliberately composed large-format picture. His Yosemite mammoth plates use foreground trees, rock, and riverbanks as hard anchors for immense recessional space; the later railroad, mine, city, and forest work carries that same exacting placement into engineered and settled terrain. The ten files below are direct, highest-resolution IIIF JPEGs from the J. Paul Getty Museum. Each Getty record declares the image **CC0** through Getty Open Content, and the underlying photographs were made between 1861 and 1885 by Watkins (1829–1916), so the chapter's rights basis is public domain.

## Shortlist

### 01 — *Yosemite Valley from the Best General View* (1866)
- **Why it matters:** Watkins's signature panoramic synthesis: a foreground tree rises from the lower edge while Bridalveil Fall, Cathedral Rocks, Half Dome, El Capitan, and a tiny centered Yosemite Fall articulate extraordinary depth; he even combined a second negative for the clouds.
- **Source:** https://www.getty.edu/art/collection/object/104EEP
- **Rights:** CC0, Getty Open Content; public-domain 1866 photograph.
- **File:** raw/carleton-watkins/01-yosemite-valley-best-general-view.jpg (4241px long edge)

### 02 — *Tutocanula, or El Capitan, 3600 Feet, Yosemite Valley, Mariposa County, Cal. (#46)* (1861)
- **Why it matters:** A low, wide mammoth-plate view turns El Capitan into a massive lateral wall, using the valley floor and small foreground vegetation to make scale legible rather than merely scenic.
- **Source:** https://www.getty.edu/art/collection/object/107RKN
- **Rights:** CC0, Getty Open Content; public-domain 1861 photograph.
- **File:** raw/carleton-watkins/02-el-capitan-tutocanula.jpg (6442px long edge)

### 03 — *Dams and Lake, Nevada County, California, Distant View* (c. 1871)
- **Why it matters:** A bold dark foreground bank and flooded skeletal trunks hold the lower frame against a delicate, atmospheric distance—Watkins's landscape grammar applied to an engineered intervention in nature.
- **Source:** https://www.getty.edu/art/collection/object/104G26
- **Rights:** CC0, Getty Open Content; public-domain c. 1871 photograph.
- **File:** raw/carleton-watkins/03-dams-and-lake-nevada-county.jpg (4352px long edge)

### 04 — *The Lake / Mirror Lake and Mt. Watkins, Yosemite* (negative 1861; print c. 1867)
- **Why it matters:** Mirror Lake makes a nearly bilateral reflection field while the rock face and tree banks provide dark, asymmetrical edge weights; it is a precise study in reflection, stillness, and recession.
- **Source:** https://www.getty.edu/art/collection/object/104EDS
- **Rights:** CC0, Getty Open Content; public-domain 1861 negative / c. 1867 print.
- **File:** raw/carleton-watkins/04-mirror-lake-mount-watkins.jpg (4942px long edge)

### 05 — *Multnomah Falls, Columbia River, Oregon / Multnomah Falls, Side* (negative 1867; print 1881–1885)
- **Why it matters:** An upward-tilted camera crams both tiers of the falls, leaning trunks, and the gorge wall into a skyless vertical enclosure—the opposite of Watkins's panoramic Yosemite expanses.
- **Source:** https://www.getty.edu/art/collection/object/104EFK
- **Rights:** CC0, Getty Open Content; public-domain 1867 negative / nineteenth-century print.
- **File:** raw/carleton-watkins/05-multnomah-falls-side.jpg (4272px long edge)

### 06 — *View on Lake Tahoe* (1877)
- **Why it matters:** Curving rail lines, trestle, steam engines, and the photographer's own shadow create a layered sequence of diagonals; industrial movement is staged across the same deep terrain Watkins used for Yosemite.
- **Source:** https://www.getty.edu/art/collection/object/104EH7
- **Rights:** CC0, Getty Open Content; public-domain 1877 photograph.
- **File:** raw/carleton-watkins/06-view-on-lake-tahoe.jpg (4297px long edge)

### 07 — *Among the Treetops, Calaveras Grove* (negative 1878; print 1880–1890)
- **Why it matters:** Watkins put his 18 × 22-inch camera on its back to make an unusually upward view: giant trunks thrust from the corners toward a bright central opening, turning the forest canopy into radial architecture.
- **Source:** https://www.getty.edu/art/collection/object/104KYC
- **Rights:** CC0, Getty Open Content; public-domain nineteenth-century photograph.
- **File:** raw/carleton-watkins/07-among-the-treetops-calaveras.jpg (6133px long edge)

### 08 — *Mt. Shasta, 14,400 Feet. View from the West.* (c. 1870)
- **Why it matters:** The mountain's triangular snow form dominates a broad horizontal field, a crisp example of Watkins building monumental scale from a restrained foreground and a single, distant peak.
- **Source:** https://www.getty.edu/art/collection/object/104FXV
- **Rights:** CC0, Getty Open Content; public-domain c. 1870 photograph.
- **File:** raw/carleton-watkins/08-mount-shasta-view-west.jpg (9983px long edge)

### 09 — *Views in Los Angeles* (c. 1880)
- **Why it matters:** A city overview organized in stacked horizontal bands—plaza, low roofs, street grid, and distant hills—shows the wide-format survey logic carried from wilderness to the growing city.
- **Source:** https://www.getty.edu/art/collection/object/104FY5
- **Rights:** CC0, Getty Open Content; public-domain c. 1880 photograph.
- **File:** raw/carleton-watkins/09-views-in-los-angeles.jpg (10030px long edge)

### 10 — *Smelting Works, New Almaden* (1863)
- **Why it matters:** An oblique view exposes the factory's repeated roofs, rails, and smoke stacks as an industrial diagonal grid—the photographic geometry of extraction and manufacture rather than sublime nature.
- **Source:** https://www.getty.edu/art/collection/object/104G23
- **Rights:** CC0, Getty Open Content; public-domain 1863 photograph.
- **File:** raw/carleton-watkins/10-smelting-works-new-almaden.jpg (5367px long edge)
