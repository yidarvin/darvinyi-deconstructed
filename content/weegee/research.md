# Weegee (Arthur Fellig) — research note

## Scope and records consulted

The Art Institute of Chicago collection records listed in [sources.md](sources.md) identify ten photographs by Weegee (Arthur Fellig) made between 1939 and 1945, with the supplied title dates or date ranges. They establish the work titles, collection credit, authorship, public display routes, and that the collection API does not mark the works public domain. The local set consists of unaltered, web-sized IIIF JPEG deliveries from those records; the ten files are 843px on their long edge.

## Technique and visual method

The visible method in this selection is direct flash used as an editing instrument. It gives faces, hands, fabric, bagels, smoke, and polished metal a decisive local brightness while allowing surrounding street or stage space to remain dark. The photographs do not use darkness as empty backdrop: it isolates a public relation. A fire engine presses two women together in *Tenement Fire, Harlem*; a sleeping circus spectator is answered by a child at the edge of the frame; *The Critic* makes a bright pair of opera patrons and a solitary onlooker occupy one shallow social field.

The sequence also moves beyond crime and disaster. The cannonball performer rises out of a smoke burst, while the flower peddler and bagelman become frontal night portraits. In *Easter Sunday in Harlem*, hats, formal clothes, and a tightly spaced crowd turn the street into a patterned public stage. These readings describe the visible organization of the selected frames, not an assertion that each photograph is neutral reportage.

## Process, printing, and settings

The sources dossier does not document a camera model, lens, focal length, aperture, shutter speed, film stock, negative format, exposure index, or original print process for these particular photographs. Every ingested manifest entry has an empty EXIF object. The supplied JPEGs are contemporary museum display derivatives, so any delivery-file metadata would concern later reproduction rather than the historical exposure. No settings or darkroom recipe can responsibly be inferred from flash falloff, grain, contrast, or depth of field.

It is accurate to discuss the visible consequences of the method—frontal flash, compressed groups, dark surrounding space, and sharply separated bright details—without converting them into unsupported technical specifications. The chapter therefore treats technique as framing, social distance, and tonal organization, while leaving the historical camera and printing particulars explicitly unknown.
