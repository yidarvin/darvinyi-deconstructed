# Helen Levitt — source shortlist

Helen Levitt made the New York street into a zone of play, improvised theatre, and sharply observed social spacing. This ten-image set moves from her late-1930s children’s pictures through a 1971 colour scene and 1980s Brooklyn photographs, so the chapter can study her use of thresholds, frames within frames, gesture, and the way an ordinary street becomes a stage. Every file is a public, web-sized Art Institute of Chicago IIIF rendition retrieved directly from the museum’s public service and checked as an opening JPEG. The service supplies 843px on the long edge—above the project’s 640px floor, but too modest to substitute for an exhibition or publication reproduction.

**Rights basis:** **fair-use** for every selection. The Art Institute API marks each work `is_public_domain: false`. The photographer is Helen Levitt and the registry identifies the known rightsholder as the Estate of Helen Levitt; the Art Institute records below supply collection credit lines but do not state a separate copyright notice. Each assessment is deliberately per-image: this nonprofit book uses a complete, web-sized copy for transformative criticism, scholarship, and composition teaching; the complete frame is necessary to analyze the stated spatial relation; the copy credits and links to the museum record; and it does not replace the original, the museum record, or a licensed reproduction.

## Research context

The Metropolitan Museum of Art describes Levitt as an expert in the “enigmatic ballet” of neighborhood subcultures, including children, women on stoops, and young men on street corners, and identifies her 1938 *New York* as a key early work: https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/267341. Its 1939 *New York* record further explains that Levitt treated children’s games and chalk drawings as spontaneous, archetypal art: https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/265603. The Art Institute’s collection records provide an unusually coherent public-display set across Levitt’s early street pictures, later colour work, and 1980s Brooklyn scenes; the acquired copies preserve that breadth while remaining small, credited, complete-frame references for visual analysis.
