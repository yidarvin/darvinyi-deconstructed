# Helen Levitt — build research

## Scope and chronology

This source set spans Levitt's New York street photographs of about 1939–40, a 1941 Mexico City picture, a New York colour photograph made in 1971 and printed in 1998, and Brooklyn pictures dated 1982 and 1985. The dates, titles, collection credits, source records, and print-date qualifications are recorded individually in `sources.md` from Art Institute of Chicago collection pages. The Met identifies Levitt's early New York work with children, chalk drawings, stoops, and street life; the source record describes these as an "enigmatic ballet" of neighborhood subcultures. That phrase is useful only as a curatorial description, not evidence of a particular camera technique.

## Technique and visual method

The selected pictures visibly work through street observation: a fixed doorway becomes a proscenium, a mirror makes an interior picture inside a crowd, a rail gives a row of masked children a descending rhythm, and passing figures activate otherwise ordinary walls and pavements. This is an analysis of visible framing and timing, not a claim that Levitt staged the people pictured. The 1971 colour image uses the storefront's blue field, red dispensers, and patterned dress as color blocks; the black-and-white images instead organize by value intervals, dark thresholds, bright pavement, and graphic figure silhouettes.

## Printing and process

`sources.md` supports only the historical dates and, for three works, later-print qualifications: *Gypsy, New York* (c. 1939, printed later), *Untitled (Little Boy & Car)* (1940, printed later), and *New York* (1971, printed 1998). The cited collection summaries do not supply a negative format, film stock, camera, lens, aperture, shutter speed, lighting setup, paper, print process, or an edition-specific printing method for this selected group. The chapter therefore does not infer them from grain, contrast, or the museum's web reproductions.

## Digital files

The ten supplied Art Institute display renditions are 843px on their long edge and have no retained EXIF camera data after normalization; see `manifest.json`. Measurements in the chapter come from the normalized files' visible tone, palette, edge density, and measured geometry. They describe these small reference renditions, not original negatives or exhibition prints.
