Jack Delano’s FSA/OWI colour photographs make work and leisure feel physically built. Across this 1940–43 selection, silos, banners, train hardware, rails, and sky are not background: colour gives each mass a separate job in the frame. The overlays name only visible structures, and the analyzer’s measurements make the contrast clear. The fair pictures are dense (edge density 0.124–0.127); the Stonington coast relaxes to 0.026; the Calumet engine is an exceptionally dark, sparse field (mean luminance 0.135, edge density 0.019). No camera or exposure settings are documented for these plates; see the research note and linked LOC records.

## colour makes a stage

At the Derby auction, two round silos, the wagon, and a crowd turn farm work into a layered theatre. The overlay circles the central silo and wagon crowd, then marks the roofline band that links them. The analyzer’s palette moves from near-black #18150f through rust and wood browns to blue #3f5e78; its high edge density of 0.124 keeps the wheel spokes, roof seams, and figures articulate.

The Rutland barker is placed beneath the painted snake banner, not isolated from it. A phi grid, banner seam, and barker anchor show how the tent’s diagonal pressure resolves at the figure. With mean luminance 0.278 and a broad tonal spread to p95 0.780, dark booth space can coexist with the bright lettering and shirt.

<Plate slug="jack-delano" id="02-barker-state-fair-rutland" label="Barker at the state fair, Rutland, Vermont, September 1941" caption="A banner seam and awning edge make the barker a live point within the booth’s painted geometry." />

The backstage portrait is closer and quieter, but it remains a stage: the central sitter and the figure behind her occupy two unequal oval fields. The shoulder line provides a low horizontal interruption beneath the faces. Its palette moves from #10110a and #292c1a to costume pink and pale skin (#e4c5b1), which is why the three faces read as separate depths rather than a single cluster.

<Plate slug="jack-delano" id="03-backstage-girlie-show-rutland" label="Backstage at the 'girlie' show, Rutland, Vermont, September 1941" caption="Two overlapping figure fields and a shoulder line make a backstage portrait into a shallow proscenium." />

In Stonington, Delano gives the cloud field most of the image and lets people remain small at the water. The stone shore, far shoreline, and figures are sufficient anchors; they do not compete with the weather. Edge density is only 0.026, while blue-grey palette entries #60748b, #8a949c, and #bcc0be keep cloud, water, and buildings visibly separate.

## railroads: scale and labour

The Calumet switch engine is almost a two-part picture: a low sun at left and a smoke-and-engine mass at right. The overlay does not invent perspective; it isolates the actual plume and sun. Mean luminance is just 0.135, with dark browns #131106 and #211f12 dominating, so the small warm sun becomes a precise counterweight.

<Plate slug="jack-delano" id="05-switch-engine-calumet-city" label="Switch engine near Calumet Park stockyards, Calumet City, Illinois, January 1943" caption="A low sun and a dark smoke mass make the locomotive’s scale legible through imbalance." />

Mrs. Viola Sievers is small against a locomotive, yet the water and steam make her action unmistakable. The running-gear band and steam cloud identify the machine’s horizontal weight and its temporary white interruption. Edge density rises to 0.103, while the palette’s steel blues and pale #d6ded1 steam preserve separation in a scene that could otherwise collapse into black machinery.

<Plate slug="jack-delano" id="06-mrs-viola-sievers-washing-locomotive" label="Mrs. Viola Sievers washing a locomotive, Clinton, Iowa, April 1943" caption="A steam cloud interrupts the locomotive’s long mechanical bands and makes the worker’s action visible." />

The Clinton lunch scene reverses that scale: the shared table, rather than the machinery, is the binding structure. The overlay follows its edge and encloses the seated workers without assigning them a false symmetry. Dark work clothes and the nearly black #17170d base are relieved by blue-grey walls and the small orange on the table; mean luminance is 0.207 and edge density 0.083.

<Plate slug="jack-delano" id="07-women-wipers-lunch-clinton" label="Women wipers having lunch, Clinton, Iowa, April 1943" caption="The table edge turns a crowded meal into a continuous social line." />

At South Water Street, freight cars make a dense lower field and the Pabst sign makes a second, elevated figure against the skyline. The warehouse roof band divides the two. The analyzer finds edge density 0.108 and a palette that ranges from dark #191a12 through blue #7096a5 to pale #a7c0be, explaining how the crowded yard stays legible beneath the open sky.

<Plate slug="jack-delano" id="08-south-water-street-freight-depot" label="South Water Street freight depot, Chicago, Illinois, May 1943" caption="Freight cars, an elevated sign, and the warehouse band build a layered industrial cityscape." />

The rebuilt caboose is a red rectangle made emphatic by empty blue sky. Its lower edge and the near rail hold the shape to the yard, while the bracketed caboose field makes the colour block the subject. The palette records the red as #af4226 among blue-grey and pale values; the moderate 0.045 edge density leaves the colour mass room to dominate.

<Plate slug="jack-delano" id="09-rebuilt-caboose-proviso-yard" label="Rebuilt caboose, Proviso yard, Chicago, Illinois, April 1943" caption="A saturated red caboose holds a rail yard and a large sky in one rectilinear statement." />

The switchman photograph makes repetitive tracks into an action field. Two visible rail paths curve through the worker and toward the distant freight line; the overlay follows those paths rather than forcing them to converge at an invented point. This is the set’s most edge-dense railroad image (0.136), and its pale #f5efd1 horizon gives the brown and steel rails a clear upper boundary.

<Plate slug="jack-delano" id="10-switchman-proviso-yard" label="Switchman throwing a switch, Proviso yard, Chicago, Illinois, April 1943" caption="Parallel rails expand the switchman’s small act across nearly the whole frame." />

Mike Evans is held between the boxcar’s huge plane and the curving torch hose. The overlay frames the boxcar field and traces the hose as a concrete link from hand to equipment. With mean luminance 0.283, edge density 0.080, and a palette of brown, workwear blue-grey, and skin-toned #907869, the portrait has enough tonal separation to remain direct rather than decorative.

<Plate slug="jack-delano" id="11-mike-evans-welder-proviso-yard" label="Mike Evans, welder, Proviso yard, Chicago, Illinois, April 1943" caption="Boxcar plane, worker, and torch hose make an industrial portrait from real working parts." />

The Santa Fe freight closes on scale. A signal structure at left, a curving rail, and the long train make the open sky feel measured rather than vacant. The rail annotation is intentionally interpretive: it follows the visible curve despite a detector warning, and the rendered proof confirms the line remains on the rail. Mean luminance is 0.441 with low edge density 0.043; slate blue #4b5d6e and pale #cbc1c2 carry the sky while the train supplies the dark horizontal fact.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Separate colour masses">
Photograph a scene with three clearly different colour or tonal masses, such as sky, vehicle, and building. Make each mass touch or oppose another one. Then draw only the boundaries that actually organize the frame.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Make labour visible through structure">
Photograph a task without isolating the worker from tools or setting. Keep one physical line, hose, rail, table edge, or machine band that shows how the person occupies the work.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Use empty space as a measure">
Place a small figure, vehicle, or signal against a large sky or wall. Keep an anchor at the edge of the open field so the space has scale, then test whether moving it closer to the center weakens the relation.
</ExerciseCard>
