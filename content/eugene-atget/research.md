# Eugène Atget — research

Scope: technique, era, printing, and recorded camera settings for the twelve
plates in this chapter. Film-and-plate work of this period carries no per-frame
exposure record, and this says so plainly rather than inventing values.

## Who and when

Eugène Atget (1857–1927) came to photography late, after failed careers as an
actor and a painter, and from about 1890 until his death in 1927 he made a vast
documentary survey of "Vieux Paris" — old streets, shopfronts, doorways,
staircases, parks, and trades — selling prints as reference material to painters,
architects, decorators, and archives. The images here span roughly 1898 to 1925.
He was largely unknown as an artist in his lifetime; Berenice Abbott and Julien
Levy preserved his archive after his death and built his posthumous reputation,
and Man Ray and the Surrealists had already seized on the strangeness of the
empty, mannequin-haunted streets.

## Technique

Atget used a large-format wooden view camera on a heavy tripod, exposing glass
plates (his working format was around 18×24 cm). The lens was an ordinary
rapid-rectilinear type whose image circle did not always cover the plate, which
is why many frames vignette or soften toward the corners — a signature accident,
not a choice. He worked in the early morning to get streets emptied of traffic
and people, in flat, even light that suppresses hard shadow and lets architecture
read as frontal geometry. The long exposures on slow plates are also why moving
figures blur or vanish and why water in the park basins goes glassy and still.

## Printing

Atget contact-printed his glass negatives on printing-out paper, chiefly albumen
and later matte albumen and gelatin silver, gold-toned, by daylight. The prints
are warm brown, with the long tonal scale of a contact print off a large
negative. He never enlarged; the print is the size of the plate. The digitized
files here are from open-access museum programs (the J. Paul Getty Museum's Getty
Open Content and the Metropolitan Museum of Art's Open Access release) via
Wikimedia Commons.

## Camera settings

Not recorded. There is no per-plate log of aperture or exposure time for Atget's
negatives, and the digital files carry only scanner metadata. What the record
does support is qualitative: view-camera work on slow glass plates in dim morning
light means small apertures for depth of field and long exposures measured in
seconds, which is consistent with the empty streets, the blurred or absent
passersby, and the mirror-still water. No specific f-stop or shutter speed is
claimed, because none is documented.
