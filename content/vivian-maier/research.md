# Vivian Maier — research notes

## Record-backed context

The supplied Maloof Collection shortlist identifies five self-portraits dated 1954, 1955, 1961, and 1971; a New York street photograph dated Fall 1953; and a Chicago photograph dated March 31, 1966. Two further New York and one Milwaukee image are explicitly undated in the source record. These dates and place names are retained as the historical record gives them; undated works are not assigned speculative dates.

The source notes identify the selected images as public display copies from the Maloof Collection and document the Estate of Vivian Maier's copyright claim. Their use here is a per-image fair-use case for transformative criticism, scholarship, and composition teaching, not an assertion that the works are openly licensed. The square frames are visible evidence in the supplied files, but the selected records do not identify a camera body or lens for any individual exposure.

## Printing and settings

The source records do not provide negative format, film stock, exposure, aperture, shutter speed, lighting setup, development method, original print process, paper, edition, or print date for these ten works. The normalized web display files contain no camera EXIF. This chapter therefore does not turn the square format into a technical claim, or infer darkroom practice from digital delivery files. It discusses only observable framing, reflection, tone, figure placement, architecture, and foreground interruption.

## Visible method in this selection

The self-portraits repeatedly make a camera-bearing figure available through another surface: shop glass, a window, a tabletop mirror, or a round mirror. The person is rarely simply centered and presented; lettering, mesh, reflections, seated strangers, and hard object edges intervene. The street pictures use related pressure. A library façade dwarfs a passerby, steam opens a pale zone around a walker, a shopfront divides two smokers, a dark wall releases a man into a narrow street canyon, and pigeons occupy the foreground before a young man's face can settle the image.

The analysis files give normalized-image measurements—tonal percentiles, palette clusters, edge density, and candidate geometry. They are useful checks on visible claims, not evidence of Maier's intent or of a historical camera workflow.
