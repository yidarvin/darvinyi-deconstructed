Imogen Cunningham makes a flower answerable to the edge of a frame. These four botanical close-ups, from 1920 through about 1925, do not offer a specimen at a comfortable viewing distance. They bring petal, throat, stem, and leaf so near that their names give way to contour, interval, and tone. The overlays follow that visual argument rather than treating every machine measurement as a fact about a plant: the analyzer's suggested horizons and vanishing points are tonal or organic-pattern false positives, so none is used. What remains is more revealing: an enclosing petal field, two unequal openings, a trunk that rises into cropped foliage, and a stem held against empty ground.

## the flower as a field

*Magnolia Blossom* (1925) organizes the flower around a dark, articulated stamen. The overlay's vertical axis at x 0.512 and its enclosing left-petal arc make the pressure of the crop legible: pale petals surround the central structure without becoming a neutral background. The analyzer measures strong vertical mirror correlation (0.901), but the overlay calls it a **near**-bilateral field; the live petal shapes are not a diagrammatic symmetry. Its broad grayscale range, p05 0.180 to p95 0.925 and mean 0.550, lets the stamen remain dark and sharply legible against a much lighter body. The measured palette stays almost entirely gray (#373737 through #e5e5e5), so volume and edge, rather than color, do the separating.

The same refusal of botanical illustration drives *Two Callas* (about 1925), but this time there is no useful axis. Two white flower throats are held as unequal ellipses in a dark field, while the traced rims show the forms folding and overlapping rather than mirroring one another. The analyzer reports a nominal vanishing point and a horizontal split, but both come from curved rims and luminance changes, not from a space that recedes. The relevant measurement is the contrast: mean luminance 0.375, p05 0.110, p95 0.902, and edge density 0.035. The near-black #232121 field makes the pale forms read as separate volumes; their different scales and openings prevent the repetition from settling into decoration.

## crop, branch, and interruption

*Plant Form* / *Succulent* / *Sedum Cristate* (1920s; printed 1971) is less a portrait of a plant than a compact growth system. The Norton Simon Museum records its print as *Plant Form*, dated “1920s (printed 1971),” while the Trust and Commons file use the alternate *Sedum Cristate* and *Succulent* labels; this chapter follows the documented 1920s date rather than asserting 1920. The overlay follows the pale rising trunk to an off-center rosette crown, then picks out the cascade of leaves at right. Cropped clusters keep entering from the frame edges, making the image feel larger than the selected fragment. Its high edge density, 0.101, is the set's clear evidence of an especially articulated surface; a mean of 0.240 and p05 of 0.027 keep much of the ground nearly black, while p95 0.773 reserves light for the trunk and leaf tips. The analyzer detects converging diagonals here, but organic branching is not architectural perspective, so the overlay names the visible contours instead.

<Plate slug="imogen-cunningham" id="03-succulent" label="Plant Form / Succulent / Sedum Cristate, 1920s; printed 1971" caption="A pale trunk rises into an off-center rosette, while cropped leaf clusters at the frame edge keep the dense plant field expanding outward." />

*Water Hyacinth* (1920s; published 1932) reverses the dense field. A single narrow stem holds the center and runs into two folded leaves at the bottom; broad pale negative space surrounds it. The stem is a genuine visual vertical even though the edge scorer gives the simple contour only a modest warning: the photographed form is clearer than its local contrast alone. The leaves meet at a compact bract junction, making a small dark hinge beneath the long stem. The analyzer's proposed line at y 0.183 is a boundary between the dark upper flower and pale ground, not a horizon. Its p05 0.086, p95 0.741, and mean 0.509 describe a quieter tonal range than *Two Callas*, with #1b1b1b concentrated in the flower and leaves against an otherwise gray field.

## what the close-up changes

Across the four plates, Cunningham uses cropping as a way to turn plant life into a set of deliberate relationships. The magnolia's pale masses surround a dense center; the callas make two openings negotiate a dark void; the succulent converts branching into directional accumulation; and the hyacinth lets one stem occupy an otherwise open field. Their shared black-and-white palette is not a historical claim about a single print process because this source set does not establish one. It is, however, a visible condition of these reproductions. The analyzer's grayscale palettes and tonal percentiles make the point concrete: each image distributes light and darkness so that a flower reads as an arrangement before it reads as a specimen.

The available sources document titles, dates, attribution, and reproduction routes, but not Cunningham's camera, lens, aperture, shutter speed, film stock, lighting setup, or original print process for these four photographs. ## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Make one opening carry the frame">
Photograph a flower closely enough that its center or throat becomes a dark anchor within a larger pale form. Make one version centered and one version with a second opening entering from the edge. Compare whether the frame reads as a single field or as a negotiation between two volumes.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Crop a growth system">
Choose a branching plant and make a close composition in which one stem supplies the main directional path. Let at least one cluster be cut by the edge. In review, trace only the trunk and one answering cluster; do not force a vanishing point onto organic growth.
</ExerciseCard>
