# Imogen Cunningham — source shortlist

This pass centers on Cunningham’s 1920s plant photographs: close, sharply focused studies in which a flower or leaf becomes a complete graphic field. The Imogen Cunningham Trust identifies itself as the official archive and offers both prints and reproduction licensing for this body of work. Its catalogue and the museums named below establish the works’ titles, dates, and attribution; the locally retained JPEGs are public, unaltered web-sized deliveries from the corresponding [Wikimedia Commons file pages](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Category:Imogen_Cunningham). They are not treated as open-licence works.

## Shortlist

### 01 — *Magnolia Blossom* (1925)

- **Why it matters:** The large blossom is pushed almost to the picture plane, while a dark folding leaf structure makes a counter-diagonal behind it. It is a precise lesson in converting botanical detail into a near-abstract relationship of pale volume and black shape.
- **Best source:** [Imogen Cunningham Trust, *Magnolia Blossom* credit in exhibition material](https://www.imogencunningham.com/usr/documents/exhibitions/press_release_url/19/bombas-gens-brochure-copy.pdf); [public delivery file page](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:MagnoliaBlossom1925.jpg).
- **Use basis — fair-use:** The Trust identifies the work as © Imogen Cunningham Trust. This chapter uses the complete, web-sized photograph for nonprofit criticism, scholarship, and transformative composition teaching; the whole frame is necessary to discuss its blossom/leaf balance. It does not replace the Trust’s print, reproduction licence, museum record, or other licensed reproduction, and credits and links to the rights source.
- **File:** `raw/imogen-cunningham/01-magnolia-blossom.jpg` (6000 × 4758 px).

### 02 — *Two Callas* (about 1925)

- **Why it matters:** Two curling white forms rise from a velvety dark ground, making negative space and the gap between the stems as active as the flowers themselves. The pair is a clear study in repetition with controlled asymmetry.
- **Best source:** [Imogen Cunningham Trust catalogue](https://www.imogencunningham.com/art/two-callas-about-1925-by-imogen-cunningham-i); [public delivery file page](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Imogen_Cunningham,_Two_Callas,_1925.jpg).
- **Use basis — fair-use:** The Trust sells estate prints made from Cunningham’s original negative and directs reproduction-rights requests to the archive. This use is a complete but web-sized copy for criticism, scholarship, and transformative composition teaching; the frame is needed to analyse the two forms and their surrounding dark field. It is credited and linked, and cannot substitute for a Trust print, licensed reproduction, or source record.
- **File:** `raw/imogen-cunningham/02-two-callas.jpg` (3354 × 4250 px).

### 03 — *Succulent* / *Sedum Cristate* (1920)

- **Why it matters:** The dense, fractal plant surface fills the frame with a highly articulated texture, but its curving crest keeps that detail from becoming uniform. It offers an unusually legible example of a close crop that holds both pattern and directional movement.
- **Best source:** [Norton Simon Museum record](https://www.nortonsimon.org/art/detail/PH.1971.147); [Imogen Cunningham Trust catalogue entry](https://www.imogencunningham.com/artworks/8499-cunningham-sedum-cristate-1920s/); [public delivery file page](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Succulent_Imogen_Cunningham_1920.jpg).
- **Use basis — fair-use:** The photograph is attributed to Cunningham and the Trust catalogues the related work. The chapter’s complete, web-sized copy is used only for criticism, scholarship, and transformative composition teaching; it is necessary to examine the edge-to-edge pattern and its visual anchor. The credited, linked use does not replace a museum record, Trust licence, print, or licensed reproduction.
- **File:** `raw/imogen-cunningham/03-succulent.jpg` (1549 × 2000 px).

### 04 — *Water Hyacinth* (1920s; published 1932)

- **Why it matters:** The bloom’s spiralling pale petals are offset by a hard, nearly black stem, producing a compact push-pull between soft radial volume and vertical structure. It is useful for showing how tonal contrast makes a flower read as a deliberately organized form rather than a specimen.
- **Best source:** [Imogen Cunningham Trust botanical catalogue](https://www.imogencunningham.com/library-botanicals); [specific Trust record](https://www.imogencunningham.com/art/blossom-of-water-hyacinth-2-1920s-by-imogen-cunningham); [public delivery file page](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Water_Hyacinth,_1920s.jpg).
- **Use basis — fair-use:** The Trust’s catalogue presents and licenses Cunningham’s botanicals. This is a complete but web-sized use for criticism, scholarship, and transformative composition teaching, because the complete flower/stem relation is the subject of the analysis. It links to and credits the Trust and does not substitute for a print, reproduction licence, museum source, or other licensed reproduction.
- **File:** `raw/imogen-cunningham/04-water-hyacinth.jpg` (1473 × 2000 px).
