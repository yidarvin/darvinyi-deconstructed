# Nicéphore Niépce — research

Scope: technique, era, printing, and recorded camera settings for the two
surviving images in this chapter. The historical record is unusually thin:
these are early experimental objects, not a documented body of routine
camera work, and no per-picture exposure or lens settings are recorded.

## Technique and era

The two images bracket Niépce's last photographic experiments. *View from
the Window at Le Gras* dates to 1826–1827 and is the surviving natural view
in the chapter; the Harry Ransom Center's 2020 rescan is the source used
here. *La Table Servie* dates from about 1832–1833 and survives as a
photomechanical reproduction of a now-lost glass plate. The source record
identifies both as heliographic / early photographic experiments, but does
not preserve a complete working recipe for either individual exposure.

## Printing and object history

The window image is reproduced from the pewter plate held by the Harry
Ransom Center. The table image is reproduced from an 1893 Société française
de photographie print, rather than from its lost original plate. That
difference matters: the first file records the surface and border of a
surviving photographic plate, while the second is an already-mediated
photomechanical reproduction. The chapter therefore treats the files as
historical objects as well as pictures, and does not infer detail that the
surfaces cannot support.

## Camera settings

No aperture, focal length, shutter speed, or sensitivity setting is recorded
for either image in the supplied source record, and the ingested files carry
no EXIF camera metadata. The sources describe the window exposure only as
lasting hours or days; that qualitative duration is retained, but no more
precise setting is invented.

Sources: [Harry Ransom Center — Niépce Heliograph](https://www.hrc.utexas.edu/niepce-heliograph/);
[La Table Servie source record](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/La_Table_servie).
