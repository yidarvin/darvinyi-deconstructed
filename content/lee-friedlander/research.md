# Lee Friedlander — research note

## Record and period

The supplied Art Institute of Chicago records date this twelve-image selection from 1963 to 1974; several also distinguish an exposure date from a later print date. They identify Lee Friedlander as the artist and document titles, places, collection credits, and public IIIF display renditions. The selection is concentrated in the 1960s and 1970s, when door glass, a rear-view mirror, roadside signs, a tourist window, or the photographer's own shadow can turn the apparently ordinary street into a layered field.

## Technique and printing

The supplied records establish neither a camera body, lens, focal length, aperture, shutter speed, film stock, negative format, developer, paper, nor a per-frame printing process for these particular photographs. The ingested JPEGs contain no capture EXIF. Those particulars are not reconstructed here. Where a record gives a print date, it is retained in the source notes; it does not license an inference about the original negative or an exposure setting.

What the web reproductions visibly show is a black-and-white method based on competing planes: reflection against scene, foreground obstruction against distant information, text against figure, and one frame inside another. The composition analyses record wide tonal ranges—for example, the Madison window ranges from p05 0.102 to p95 0.941—alongside very different surface densities, from the sparse New York window display (edge density 0.016) to the screened New York fence scene (0.307). Those numbers describe these normalized study files, not an original print's materials or Friedlander's camera settings.
