Lewis Hine's National Child Labor Committee photographs build an argument from scale. Machinery is repeated until it becomes a field; children stand against it, inside it, or walk away from it. The overlays name only visible structures: rails, bobbin rows, doors, paths, and clusters. The pictures were made as advocacy evidence, with captions and circulation designed to persuade. That gives the compositions force, but it does not dissolve the power difference between investigator and child, or turn historical caption language into a neutral fact.

The analyzer registers a sepia tonal set from #1a1a1a and #2f2f2f to pale #d2d2d2. Addie Card has the highest edge density, 0.136, because the spinning frame is a grid of repeated bobbins; the Tupelo path is only 0.018, where light and open ground carry the arrangement. These are formal observations, not a measure of harm.

## machine scale

<Plate slug="lewis-hine" id="01-sadie-pfeifer" label="Sadie Pfeifer, Lancaster Cotton Mills, 1908" caption="A long spinning-frame edge brings the eye to Sadie, with the window wall preserving the narrow aisle beside her." />

Sadie Pfeifer is not centered by a decorative grid. A machine contour runs from the near left toward her body, while the window wall keeps the aisle narrow. The image's mean luminance is 0.45 and edge density 0.059: the repeated frame carries the image more than isolated detail.

<Plate slug="lewis-hine" id="02-sweeper-doffer-boys" label="Sweeper and Doffer Boys, Lancaster Mills, 1908" caption="The broom cuts across a spinning frame and separates the foreground boy from the workers behind him." />

The broom is a real diagonal inside the factory, not an abstract arrow. The overlay makes it a contour between the foreground boy, the adult, and the repeated spindles. The analyzer's 0.100 edge density reflects that crowded equipment.

<Plate slug="lewis-hine" id="03-doffer-boy-kesler" label="Doffer boy, Kesler Manufacturing Company, 1908" caption="Opposing machine rows form a shallow corridor around the boy and his box of bobbins." />

Two machine runs close around the boy without pretending that the obscured center is a precise vanishing point. The visual claim is scale: his figure and work box sit between near-identical industrial rhythms.

<Plate slug="lewis-hine" id="04-little-spinner-globe" label="A little spinner in Globe Cotton Mill, 1909" caption="A portrait-scale spinner interrupts paired blurred machinery bands." />

The spinner is held in the center by mirrored pressure rather than literal symmetry. The overlay follows the two tangible machinery bands and keeps the person's face and torso together; it does not use the detector's false bottom-corner saliency.

<Plate slug="lewis-hine" id="05-addie-card" label="Addie Card, North Pownal Cotton Mill, 1910" caption="A frontal worker interrupts repeated spindle rows and the machine's near-center axis." />

The centered portrait is structured by the machine behind it. A vertical axis and upper bobbin row are visible supports for that claim. The period caption's medical term remains historical wording, not a diagnosis established by the image.

## groups and departures

<Plate slug="lewis-hine" id="06-breaker-boys-nanticoke" label="Breaker boys, Nanticoke, 1911" caption="A worker row holds at the rail in front of a larger crowd; a damaged reproduction crease is deliberately not treated as scene structure." />

The rail gives the row a horizontal base. The overlay circles the young workers as a group rather than extracting faces from the larger crowd; the diagonal crease belongs to the reproduction, so it is left out.

<Plate slug="lewis-hine" id="07-ewen-breaker" label="A view of Ewen Breaker, South Pittston, 1911" caption="A long bench and a high window organize a smoky interior around several workers." />

Hine's breaker image is made from uncertain visibility. The bench and worker chain remain readable while dust, shadows, and reproduction wear make the room's depth deliberately cautious.

<Plate slug="lewis-hine" id="08-newsies-boston" label="Newsies, Boston, 1909" caption="A doorway gathers the central cluster while the curb and facade distribute people across the street." />

The Boston street view works by small clusters rather than a single subject. A doorway supplies the strongest internal frame, and the sidewalk gives the figures a shallow lateral stage.

<Plate slug="lewis-hine" id="09-ny-newsies" label="N.Y. Newsies, 1910" caption="Two newspaper sellers stand against open pavement and a dark entrance." />

The two boys register at different scale and height against a large empty pavement. The overlay marks that pair and the entrance, avoiding the cropped adult at right that the detector mistakes for the subject.

<Plate slug="lewis-hine" id="10-doffer-boys-tupelo" label="Doffer boys going to dinner, Tupelo Cotton Mills, 1911" caption="A pale path links a front pair to the middle distance, with one boy separated at right." />

The lightest image here has mean luminance 0.68 and low edge density 0.018. Its path and spaced figures carry a procession without needing a false skyline or a fabricated vanishing point.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Measure scale with repetition">
Photograph one person beside repeated equipment, windows, or shelves. Mark one true contour in the repetition and one subject anchor. Do not use a grid if the repeated structure already makes the relationship clear.
</ExerciseCard>

Find an archival or damaged image and list every visible scratch, crease, border, and stain before adding an overlay. Mark only the photographed scene. Explain in one sentence why a conspicuous defect was excluded.
</ExerciseCard>
