# Lewis Hine — research

## Era and context

Between 1908 and 1924 Lewis Hine worked as an investigative photographer for the National Child Labor Committee (NCLC). He combined pictures, interviews, measurements, and extended captions in an advocacy campaign against child-labor abuses. Hine called this work both "detective work" and "photo-interpretation": it was evidence-directed, but never neutral or equivalent to consent. Captions may contain errors and period language; for example, the phrase "anaemic little spinner" is a historical captional claim, not a medical finding. These images supported NCLC publicity and reform efforts; they did not alone end child labor.

## Technique and printing

The NCLC archive comprises photographic prints and glass negatives. The Library of Congress says most surviving prints are small, single-weight contact prints, probably made from 4 × 6 or 5 × 7 inch negatives, and that Hine generally used printing assistants. NCLC circulated the photographs through reports, progressive publications, newspapers, stereopticon slides, exhibitions, and lectures. A Smithsonian-held Graflex used across Hine's career enabled reflex viewing to the plate edges; the National Museum of American History describes his NCLC-era equipment as a tripod-mounted 5 × 7 plate box-type camera and notes magnesium flash for indoor or low-light work.

## Camera settings

No per-image record for this selection establishes a camera model, lens, aperture, shutter speed, exposure time, plate stock, flash use, or darkroom recipe. General camera and flash history is not assigned to a specific photograph. No settings are inferred from the images.
