# Paul Strand — research

## Era and method

This chapter concentrates on Strand's 1915–22 breakthrough work: city views, direct New York portraits, still lifes, a porch-shadow abstraction, and the 1922 Akeley camera study. The local source record identifies the early works as photographs made in 1915–17 and the camera study as 1922; it does not support treating the group as one uniform series or one uniform printing process.

Strand's evident method in these pictures is direct, sharply selected framing rather than soft-focus pictorial effect. That is an interpretive description of the images, not a claim about a particular lens or exposure. The urban portraits are especially close and off-axis; the abstractions derive their force from ordinary architectural or mechanical forms reduced to arcs, planes, and tonal intervals.

## Prints and reproductions

The local source record names the physical form only where a catalog does: *Man, Five Points Square* and *Still Life, Pear and Bowls* are Yale photogravures printed in 1973 from 1916 photographs. The Library of Congress files for *New York*, *Woman, New York*, and *Man in a Derby* are digitizations of historical material, and the local raws were converted from publicly delivered master TIFFs without image alteration. The source record does not establish the original print process for every other image here, so this chapter does not assign one.

## Camera settings

No aperture, shutter speed, focal length, film stock, camera model, or exposure setting is documented in the local sources or retained EXIF. The EXIF in several downloaded files identifies modern scanning or editing software and dates, not Strand's equipment, and must not be mistaken for historical capture data. No camera settings are therefore asserted.
