# Paul Strand — sources

This source pass concentrates on Strand's breakthrough 1915–22 work: the urban studies, clandestine-looking portraits, machine image, and straight-photography abstractions that established his modernist vocabulary. All ten local JPEGs were opened after download and exceed the 1200px long-edge requirement (1500–4349px). The early works were published before 1931 and are marked public domain by Wikimedia Commons; the two Yale photogravures are explicitly catalogued there as **No Copyright — United States** and delivered through Yale's public IIIF service.

## Shortlist

### 01 — *Wall Street* (1915)
- **Why it matters:** The enormous black window bays turn the financial district into an abstract façade; tiny, diagonal pedestrians supply the scale and rhythm that make the frame a landmark of modernist photography.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Wall_Street_by_Paul_Strand,_1915.jpg
- **Rights:** Public domain in the United States (published before 1931; Commons PD-US-expired designation).
- **File:** `raw/paul-strand/01-wall-street.jpg` (3132px long edge)

### 02 — *Blind Woman, New York* (1916)
- **Why it matters:** The near-frontal head-and-shoulders crop makes the woman's face and the inscribed "BLIND" sign compete as two stark focal planes, an uncompromising counterpoint to Strand's architectural work.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Blind_Woman,_edit.jpg (Getty Museum digitization cited in the file record)
- **Rights:** Public domain in the United States (published before 1931; Commons PD-US-expired designation).
- **File:** `raw/paul-strand/02-blind-woman.jpg` (4349px long edge)

### 03 — *Bowls* (1916)
- **Why it matters:** Cropped circles, arcs, and cast shadows erase ordinary scale and turn a still life into a tightly packed field of overlapping geometry.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Bowls,_by_Paul_Strand,_1916.jpg
- **Rights:** Public domain in the United States (published before 1931; Commons PD-US-expired designation).
- **File:** `raw/paul-strand/03-bowls.jpg` (2775px long edge)

### 04 — *Abstraction, Porch Shadows, Twin Lakes, Connecticut* (1916)
- **Why it matters:** Hard porch shadows and balustrade diagonals convert a familiar domestic view into an almost cubist sequence of wedges and parallel bands.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/265133 (openly hosted Commons derivative: https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Paul_Strand._Abstraction,_Twin_Lakes,_Connecticut._1916.jpg)
- **Rights:** Public domain in the United States (1916 work, Commons PD-US-expired designation).
- **File:** `raw/paul-strand/04-abstraction-porch-shadows.jpg` (1500px long edge)

### 05 — *Akeley Motion Picture Camera, New York* (1922)
- **Why it matters:** Strand looks into the camera's circular housing rather than at it, using bright metal arcs and deep mechanical recesses to make the machine read as a monumental abstract construction.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Akeley_Motion_Picture_Camera,_New_York,_1922.jpg
- **Rights:** Public domain in the United States (published before 1931; Commons PD-US-expired designation).
- **File:** `raw/paul-strand/05-akeley-motion-picture-camera.jpg` (3196px long edge)

### 06 — *New York* (1916; LOC catalog title)
- **Why it matters:** The elevated view stacks roof planes, railings, bare branches, and snow into a high-contrast lattice, extending Strand's abstract geometry into a lived city view.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/pictures/item/90710540/
- **Rights:** Public domain (Library of Congress digitization; the matching Commons record marks the 1916 image PD-US-expired).
- **File:** `raw/paul-strand/06-new-york-1916.jpg` (4096px long edge; converted from LOC master TIFF without image alteration)

### 07 — *Woman, New York* (1917; LOC catalog title: *Photograph—New York*)
- **Why it matters:** The close, slightly off-axis portrait isolates an open-mouthed expression against a blurred street ground, a direct human study rather than a posed likeness.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/pictures/item/90710618/
- **Rights:** Public domain (Library of Congress digitization; Commons marks the 1917 Camera Work image PD-US-expired).
- **File:** `raw/paul-strand/07-woman-new-york.jpg` (4096px long edge; converted from LOC master TIFF without image alteration)

### 08 — *Man in a Derby, New York* (1917; LOC catalog title: *Photograph—New York*)
- **Why it matters:** A black bowler, face, white scarf, and dark overcoat form four compressed tonal masses; the shallow crop makes class signifiers into a graphic portrait structure.
- **Source:** https://www.loc.gov/pictures/item/90710619/
- **Rights:** Public domain (Library of Congress digitization; Commons marks the 1917 Camera Work image PD-US-expired).
- **File:** `raw/paul-strand/08-man-in-derby.jpg` (4096px long edge; converted from LOC master TIFF without image alteration)

### 09 — *Man, Five Points Square* (1916; photogravure printed 1973)
- **Why it matters:** The frontal urban portrait uses the man's face as a dense central anchor while the surrounding street remains a soft, indistinct field—the candid portrait language of Strand's 1916 New York series.
- **Source:** https://artgallery.yale.edu/collections/objects/290736
- **Rights:** Yale University Art Gallery catalogues this object as **No Copyright — United States**; downloaded through its public IIIF endpoint.
- **File:** `raw/paul-strand/09-man-five-points-square.jpg` (2456px long edge)

### 10 — *Still Life, Pear and Bowls, Twin Lakes, Connecticut* (1916; photogravure printed 1973)
- **Why it matters:** A pear and bowls are reduced to cropped curves and a few sharply lit edges, supplying a companion study to *Bowls* with a different balance of contour, mass, and shadow.
- **Source:** https://artgallery.yale.edu/collections/objects/290735
- **Rights:** Yale University Art Gallery catalogues this object as **No Copyright — United States**; downloaded through its public IIIF endpoint.
- **File:** `raw/paul-strand/10-still-life-pear-and-bowls.jpg` (2426px long edge)
