# Julia Margaret Cameron — source shortlist

All files were downloaded at original JPEG size from The Metropolitan Museum of Art’s Open Access collection. Every linked object is marked **Public Domain**; the Met makes these works available under CC0.

1. **Julia Jackson** — 1867. A pale near-frame-filling face emerges from a dark ground: Cameron’s intimate soft-focus portrait grammar at its clearest. [Met record](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/267426). **Rights:** Met Open Access, CC0/Public Domain.
2. **Zoe, Maid of Athens** — 1866. Blurred contour, loose hair, and slight motion make longing and breath rather than detail organize the portrait. [Met record](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/282043). **Rights:** Met Open Access, CC0/Public Domain.
3. **Sappho** — 1865. Head, hair, and face emerge from deep tonality in a concentrated, classically inflected study. [Met record](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/268696). **Rights:** Met Open Access, CC0/Public Domain.
4. **Alfred, Lord Tennyson** — May 1865. The celebrated “Dirty Monk” portrait gives beard, head, and shadow sculptural visual weight. [Met record](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/271077). **Rights:** Met Open Access, CC0/Public Domain.
5. **Sir John Herschel** — April 1867. A settled central figure and luminous facial mass make this elder portrait monumental. [Met record](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/283097). **Rights:** Met Open Access, CC0/Public Domain.
6. **Thomas Carlyle** — 1867. Weathered face and inward gaze are isolated against a restrained dark field. [Met record](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/268363). **Rights:** Met Open Access, CC0/Public Domain.
7. **The Madonna Penserosa** — 1864. A close devotional grouping translates Renaissance religious painting into a domestic photographic tableau. [Met record](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/268717). **Rights:** Met Open Access, CC0/Public Domain.
8. **Daughters of Jerusalem** — 1865. Faces and drapery link into a lyrical horizontal multi-figure cluster. [Met record](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/268694). **Rights:** Met Open Access, CC0/Public Domain.
9. **Christabel** — 1866. Long exposure and deliberate soft focus turn May Prinsep into Coleridge’s ethereal literary heroine. [Met record](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/268709). **Rights:** Met Open Access, CC0/Public Domain.
10. **Beatrice** — 1866. A painting-derived female portrait organized by a strong frontal facial axis. [Met record](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/271522). **Rights:** Met Open Access, CC0/Public Domain.
11. **Vivien and Merlin** — Sept. 1874. Costumed friends and physical proximity create compressed Arthurian narrative tension. [Met record](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/282121). **Rights:** Met Open Access, CC0/Public Domain.
12. **The Passing of Arthur** — 1874. Staged hierarchy, grouped gestures, and a full narrative tableau counterpoint Cameron’s intimate portraits. [Met record](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/266254). **Rights:** Met Open Access, CC0/Public Domain.

The Met’s [Open Access terms](https://www.metmuseum.org/policies/terms-and-conditions) provide the collection-wide CC0 basis. The chapter should distinguish the theatrical, literary staging from documentary portrait conventions.
