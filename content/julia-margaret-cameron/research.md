# Julia Margaret Cameron — research notes

## Record and process

The twelve selected Met Open Access photographs date from 1864 to 1874. Their
object records identify albumen silver prints; eleven are catalogued as made
from glass negatives, while *Thomas Carlyle* is catalogued simply as an albumen
silver print. The distinction is retained here rather than silently filling in
the latter record. The individual records and rights basis are listed in
[sources.md](sources.md).

Cameron's regular practice was wet-collodion photography: a glass plate was
coated and sensitized, exposed while wet in a large wooden tripod camera, then
developed and varnished; a negative was contact-printed in sunlight onto
sensitized albumen paper. The V&A records an early approximately 12 x 10 inch
format and her move in 1865 to a 15 x 12 inch camera, a scale suited to the
close, nearly life-size heads in this set. [V&A, “Working methods”](https://www.vam.ac.uk/articles/julia-margaret-camerons-working-methods)

## What can be said about the look

Close placement, soft focus, dramatic light, and—in some works—slight sitter
movement were purposeful parts of Cameron's practice, not a reason to treat
these images as failed sharp photographs. The Met's *Zoe* record specifically
notes her intentional soft focus and permitted slight movement. The Met's
technical study reports a general, period account of three-to-eight-minute
sittings, but that is context for her method, not an exposure value for any
individual plate here. [Met, *Zoe*](https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/282043) · [Met technical study (PDF)](https://resources.metmuseum.org/resources/metpublications/pdf/KennedySmieskaCentenoRuizMolina_Metropolitan_Museum_Journal_v_53_2018.pdf)

The swirls, fingerprints, streaks, and scratches visible in some prints may be
part of wet-collodion/negative handling or the historical object; they are not
called retouching or damage without object-specific evidence. Cameron began
photographing in 1863 and the chapter's dates belong to her Victorian rather
than later Pictorialist context. [Met essay](https://www.metmuseum.org/pt/essays/julia-margaret-cameron-1815-1879)

## Settings

No per-image aperture, shutter duration, ISO/plate sensitivity, focal length,
lens model, bellows extension, or lighting diagram is supplied by the Met
shortlist or the downloaded JPEG metadata; the manifest records empty EXIF for
all twelve files. This chapter therefore gives no invented camera settings.
