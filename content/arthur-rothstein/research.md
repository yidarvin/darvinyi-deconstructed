# Arthur Rothstein — research

Scope: technique, era, printing, and camera settings for this chapter's twelve
Library of Congress (LOC) records. The account stays with what the source
records establish; it does not turn scanner metadata into facts about the
1930s photographs.

## Era and assignment

The source shortlist identifies the Oklahoma and Texas Dust Bowl photographs
as Resettlement Administration/Farm Security Administration work from March and
April 1936. The two Alabama photographs are dated February 1937. LOC describes
the set as FSA/Office of War Information black-and-white negatives, and the
individual record links are retained in `sources.md`. The chapter therefore
uses the dates and place names supplied by those records, rather than inferring
them from the pictures.

## Technique and printing

The documented original medium for every plate is a federal black-and-white
negative. The supplied records do not identify a camera model, negative format,
film stock, developer, enlarger, paper, photographer's darkroom process, or a
specific vintage print for these twelve frames. They do document LOC master
renditions, from which the source set was downloaded and normalized. That makes
the files here modern digital derivatives of the negatives, not evidence for a
particular historical print surface or printing style.

## Camera settings

No original aperture, shutter speed, focal length, or exposure record is
provided for these photographs. `manifest.json` records `Leaf Aptus-II` or
`Stokes 75` values on the downloaded source files; those are digitization or
file metadata and are explicitly not treated as Rothstein's equipment. No
camera-setting claims appear in the chapter.

## Provenance and caution

LOC's FSA/OWI collection rights statement is the rights basis for the set, and
each item is identified in `sources.md` as public domain / no known
restrictions. The steer-skull image is discussed carefully: the source shortlist
flags the later controversy that Rothstein moved a skull between exposures. The
chapter does not use that fact to deny the drought conditions pictured; it
notes instead that the image's arrangement has an ethical history that belongs
beside its formal force.
