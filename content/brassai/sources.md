# Brassaï — source shortlist

Brassaï made the Paris night into a stage of fog, electric light, closed doors, and alert bodies. This set concentrates on the early-1930s pictures in which the city’s underworld and public surfaces become compositional problems: a face held against a mirror, bridge arches dissolved in mist, figures pulled apart by shadow, and space made theatrical by streetlight. Ten canonical or representative prints were retrieved from the Art Institute of Chicago’s public IIIF service and individually verified as opening JPEGs. The service supplies intentionally web-sized files (843px on the long edge), above the project’s 640px floor but far below a print-quality substitute.

**Rights basis:** **fair-use** for every selection. The Art Institute API identifies each work as `is_public_domain: false`. The works are by Brassaï (Gyula Halász, 1899–1984); the estate/rightsholder is identified where known as © Estate Brassaï-RMN (the Getty’s Brassaï collection record: https://www.getty.edu/art/collection/object/104EMG). ## Research context

The Metropolitan Museum of Art identifies *Paris de nuit* (1933) as the publication that established Brassaï’s reputation as a chronicler of the night, and describes the axial, lamp-lit bridge imagery of his early Paris work: https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/283311. The National Gallery of Art similarly notes that *Paris de Nuit* included street cleaners, sex workers, and other working-class Parisians, alongside his later portraits and graffiti: https://www.nga.gov/artists/13567-brassai. The acquired selection keeps that compositional range—street, river, nightclub, portrait, and graphic pavement—while using only the Art Institute’s publicly served, web-sized display renditions.
