# Brassaï — research

Scope: documented technique, era, printing, and camera information for the ten
selected works. This note distinguishes collection facts from readings of the
visible compositions.

## Era and project

The selected images range from *Pavés Parisiens (Parisian Paving Stones)*
(1929) through *Dans une Boite de Nuit (In a Nightclub)* (1935), with the main
concentration in 1932–34. The Metropolitan Museum of Art identifies *Paris de
nuit* (1933) as the publication that established Brassaï's reputation as a
chronicler of the city at night. The National Gallery of Art describes the
book's range as including street cleaners, sex workers, and other working-class
Parisians. The set here samples that period's night street, river, interior,
and close social scenes. [Source notes](sources.md)

## Technique and printing

The Art Institute of Chicago records supplied for this chapter establish the
artists, titles, dates, collection credits, and public display renditions for
these ten works. The supplied records and linked source notes do not establish
a print process, print date, print support, negative process, darkroom method,
or a distinction between vintage and later prints for this particular set.
The chapter therefore discusses only visible tonal structure, mirror use,
reflection, shadow, and frame geometry; it does not turn those observations
into undocumented technical claims.

## Camera information

No source recorded for this set identifies a verified camera model, lens,
focal length, aperture, shutter speed, film or plate stock, sensitivity, or
exposure for any selected image. The local manifest records only the dimensions
of the museum's normalized display derivatives and contains no historical EXIF.
Specific settings are not inferred from darkness, blur, sharpness, or the
night-time subject matter.
