# Edward S. Curtis — sources

Curtis made these photographs while producing *The North American Indian* (1907–30), a project whose pictorial beauty cannot be separated from its colonial framing. He frequently selected, posed, and sometimes altered the visible scene to construct a romanticized image of Indigenous life; the Library of Congress explicitly notes that critics have identified this erasure of contemporary Indigenous life. This shortlist therefore treats the photographs as works to analyze critically, retains the source catalog titles, and identifies people and nations as the holding records do. The recurring formal language is highly deliberate: isolated figures silhouetted against sky or water, broad triangular groupings, processional diagonals, and carefully staged ritual or labor scenes.

**Rights basis:** The ten files below are public-domain reproductions from open collection records or open institutional mirrors. The Library of Congress Curtis records for *The vanishing race*, *The three chiefs*, *The Apache reaper*, and *Dancing to restore an eclipsed moon* state “No known restrictions on publication” (the first also states no copyright renewal). Commons identifies the remaining original files as public domain; each is a photograph made and published in 1900–1914, or a public-domain collection reproduction. Commons is used as the lawful full-resolution delivery mirror where LOC throttled further catalog retrieval during this source pass. No access controls were bypassed.

## Shortlist

### 01 — The vanishing race—Navaho (1904)
- **Why it matters:** Five riders recede in a tightening diagonal between tall rock walls, their dust trail and shrinking silhouettes turning the canyon into a tunnel of depth; the image is Curtis’s most famous example of pictorial recession, while its “vanishing” premise must be read as colonial mythmaking.
- **Source:** [Library of Congress record](https://www.loc.gov/item/2004672871/) · [open master mirror](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:The_vanishing_race-Navaho_LCCN2004672871.jpg)
- **Rights:** Public domain; LOC: no known restrictions on publication and no copyright renewal.
- **File:** `raw/edward-s-curtis/01-vanishing-race-navaho.jpg` (5242 × 4246px)

### 02 — The three chiefs—Piegan (1900)
- **Why it matters:** Three mounted men are spread across an exceptionally wide horizon, their horses forming a low, alternating rhythm beneath a vast blank sky; it is Curtis’s classic solution for making group portraiture read as a landscape frieze.
- **Source:** [Library of Congress record](https://www.loc.gov/item/2001695852/) · [open master mirror](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Three_chiefs_Piegan_p.39_horizontal.jpg)
- **Rights:** Public domain; LOC Curtis collection record and Commons public-domain original.
- **File:** `raw/edward-s-curtis/02-three-chiefs-piegan.jpg` (4764 × 2382px)

### 03 — Apache mother and child (1903)
- **Why it matters:** The seated mother’s dark dress, cradleboard, and the child’s pale face lock into a compact vertical triangle, while the unoccupied ground around them makes the family group read as a single sculptural mass.
- **Source:** [Wikimedia Commons open file](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Apache_mother_and_child.jpg)
- **Rights:** Public domain (Commons identifies the 1903 Curtis image as public domain; original is a Library of Congress scan).
- **File:** `raw/edward-s-curtis/03-apache-mother-child.jpg` (2617 × 3202px)

### 04 — The Apache reaper (1906)
- **Why it matters:** A standing reaper and the long sweep of grain create a vigorous left-to-right diagonal, with the dense crop as foreground texture and the distant mesa held as a quiet horizontal counterweight.
- **Source:** [Library of Congress record](https://www.loc.gov/item/2003652893/) · [open master mirror](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Apache_reaper_Cp01008.jpg)
- **Rights:** Public domain; LOC Curtis collection record and Commons public-domain original.
- **File:** `raw/edward-s-curtis/04-apache-reaper.jpg` (4348 × 3478px)

### 05 — Bear’s Belly (Apsáalooke/Crow) (1908)
- **Why it matters:** The sitter fills the upright frame with a dense cloak silhouette while the face is turned just off center; fur, beadwork, and feather texture build an almost abstract field around a sharply lit focal face.
- **Source:** [Wikimedia Commons open file](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Bear%27s_Belly_by_Edward_Curtis,_1908.jpg)
- **Rights:** Public domain (1908 Curtis photograph; Commons public-domain original).
- **File:** `raw/edward-s-curtis/05-bears-belly.jpg` (3335 × 4492px)

### 06 — The scout in winter—Crow (1908)
- **Why it matters:** Horse and rider are isolated against a near-white snow field, with the horse’s dark side forming the image’s main mass and the diagonal rise of the snowy ground making the scene feel suspended rather than merely documentary.
- **Source:** [Wikimedia Commons open file](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:The_Scout_in_Winter,_Crow,_1908,_Edward_S._Curtis_(original).jpg)
- **Rights:** Public domain (c. 1908 Curtis photograph; Commons public-domain original).
- **File:** `raw/edward-s-curtis/06-scout-in-winter.jpg` (3262 × 4096px)

### 07 — Dancing to restore an eclipsed moon—Qagyuhl (1914)
- **Why it matters:** A dense group of masked dancers is packed into a shallow, almost theatrical rectangle; repeating masks, arms, and costume edges make ritual movement legible as a patterned surface rather than a single decisive gesture.
- **Source:** [Library of Congress record](https://www.loc.gov/item/2003652796/) · [open master mirror](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Dancing_to_restore_an_eclipsed_moon.jpg)
- **Rights:** Public domain; LOC Curtis collection record and Commons public-domain original.
- **File:** `raw/edward-s-curtis/07-dancing-eclipsed-moon.jpg` (6024 × 4781px)

### 08 — Mandan girls gathering berries (c. 1908)
- **Why it matters:** Two figures bend into a heavily textured vertical field of leaves and branches; their bodies repeat each other’s downward curve, with the abundant foreground intentionally flattening depth into a woven pattern.
- **Source:** [Wikimedia Commons open file](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Mandan_girls_gathering_berries.JPG)
- **Rights:** Public domain (c. 1908 Curtis photograph; Commons public-domain original).
- **File:** `raw/edward-s-curtis/08-mandan-girls-gathering-berries.jpg` (5884 × 7845px)

### 09 — At the water’s edge—Piegan (1910)
- **Why it matters:** A lone figure stands at the boundary of dark shore and luminous water, pushed slightly to one side so the horizontal bands of lake, bank, and distant land carry as much of the image as the person does.
- **Source:** [Wikimedia Commons open file](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Piegan-_At_the_Waters_edge.jpg)
- **Rights:** Public domain (1910 Curtis photograph; Commons public-domain original).
- **File:** `raw/edward-s-curtis/09-at-waters-edge-piegan.jpg` (1435 × 2071px)

### 10 — Big Mouth Spring (1910)
- **Why it matters:** The spring is framed as a dark oval opening inside a surround of pale rock, with the figures and reflected water creating nested tonal shapes; it is a compact example of Curtis using landscape as a stage-set enclosure.
- **Source:** [Library of Congress record](https://hdl.loc.gov/loc.pnp/cph.3c06743) · [Wikimedia Commons open file](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Big_Mouth_Spring.jpg)
- **Rights:** Public domain; Commons file identifies the LOC source and public-domain status.
- **File:** `raw/edward-s-curtis/10-big-mouth-spring.jpg` (2646 × 3531px)
