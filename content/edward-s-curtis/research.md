# Edward S. Curtis — research

## Record and process

The selected photographs date from 1900 to 1914 and were made during Curtis's long *The North American Indian* project (published 1907–30). The Library of Congress describes the Curtis collection as more than 2,400 first-generation silver-gelatin prints made from his original glass negatives; it also notes that some prints are sepia toned. The project itself was issued in twenty text volumes with portfolios of photogravures. For the original edition, copper plates were individually inked and printed by hand; the Library of Congress catalog identifies original photogravures made from 1903 to 1925 by Plimpton Press and Suffolk Engraving.

The collection record does not mean that every downloaded file here is an original silver-gelatin print or a photogravure: these are institutional digital reproductions, including scans of different physical formats. The visible border, plate lettering, and tone are therefore part of a particular reproduction as well as evidence of Curtis's image-making.

## Camera and settings

No source supplied for this chapter establishes a camera body, lens, focal length, aperture, shutter speed, plate sensitivity, or exposure for an individual Curtis photograph. The ingest manifest contains no historical capture metadata. One file carries Nikon D70 EXIF from a 2005 digital reproduction; it describes that later copy, not the 1908 Curtis exposure, and is not used as historical evidence.

## Historical caution

The Library of Congress says Curtis's project documented more than eighty tribes, and its collection guide notes the recurrent criticism that he manipulated subjects and surroundings to produce a romanticized view that removed signs of contemporary Indigenous life and blurred differences among communities. Formal analysis here therefore limits itself to visible frame relations and retains the collection titles and nation names supplied in `sources.md`; it does not treat Curtis's staging as transparent ethnographic evidence.
