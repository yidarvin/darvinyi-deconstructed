# Gustave Le Gray — sources

Le Gray built his reputation on a single technical problem that was also a compositional one: the wet-collodion and albumen processes of the 1850s could not register a bright sky and a dark sea on the same negative without blowing out one or crushing the other. His solution — expose two negatives, one for sea and one for sky, and print them together on one sheet, sometimes joining a genuine seascape to a stock cloud negative shot elsewhere entirely — turned a darkroom workaround into the defining feature of his marine pictures: a hard, almost drawn horizon line splitting the frame, with the sea's tonal register and the sky's held independently in balance. The same eye for large, simplified planes shows up away from the coast — in his Fontainebleau forest studies, where a single gnarled oak or a receding sand path is isolated against open middle-distance instead of picturesque clutter, and in the Mission Héliographique architecture, where stone ramparts are read as pure planar geometry. Le Gray was also Napoleon III's semi-official photographer in the mid-1850s, covering court yachts and the army's showcase manoeuvres at Camp de Châlons with the same large-plate clarity.

**Rights basis:** Public domain. Le Gray died in 1884, so his work is long out of copyright everywhere; every image below is additionally released by the Metropolitan Museum of Art under its Open Access policy (CC0), sourced directly from images.metmuseum.org at full exhibition resolution. Where a higher-resolution Wikimedia Commons mirror was checked (e.g. *The Great Wave, Sète*), it was lower-resolution than the Met's own master file, so the Met original was used throughout.

## Shortlist

### 01 — The Great Wave, Sète (1857)
- **Why it matters:** The canonical Le Gray image and the clearest demonstration of combination printing as composition: a dead-level horizon cuts the frame almost exactly in half, sea and sky each getting their own tonal treatment, with the sun's reflection landing off-center to break the symmetry.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/261941
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/gustave-le-gray/01-great-wave-sete.jpg (2000px)

### 02 — Brig on the Water (1856)
- **Why it matters:** A single ship in silhouette placed off-center against a glassy, single-negative sea rendered as twilight — proof that Le Gray could win the same reductive, high-contrast horizon effect without combination printing, purely through exposure and print timing.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/261937
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/gustave-le-gray/02-brig-on-water.jpg (3801px)

### 03 — Mediterranean with Mount Agde (1857)
- **Why it matters:** The most minimal of the seascapes — flat sea, flat sky, and a single low landform breaking the horizon near the frame's edge — an almost geometric abstraction built from two joined negatives.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/267425
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/gustave-le-gray/03-mediterranean-mount-agde.jpg (4000px)

### 04 — The French and English Fleets, Cherbourg (August 1858)
- **Why it matters:** A denser variant of the seascape formula — multiple moored warships strung along the middle distance under a heavily worked cloud negative — showing how Le Gray scaled the same horizon-and-sky discipline to a busy harbor subject.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/306320
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/gustave-le-gray/04-french-english-fleets-cherbourg.jpg (4000px)

### 05 — La Reine Hortense, Yacht de l'Empereur, Le Havre (1856)
- **Why it matters:** The Imperial yacht set diagonally across the harbor water, its rigging cutting hard verticals against a soft reflected sky — a court commission handled with the same reductive marine geometry as the seascapes made for their own sake.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/285467
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/gustave-le-gray/05-reine-hortense-yacht-havre.jpg (3859px)

### 06 — Tree Study, Forest of Fontainebleau (ca. 1856)
- **Why it matters:** A solitary tree isolated against open sky and ground rather than forest clutter — Le Gray applying the same figure-against-void logic of the seascapes to a single organic subject.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/265065
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/gustave-le-gray/06-tree-study-fontainebleau.jpg (3811px)

### 07 — Oak Tree and Rocks, Forest of Fontainebleau (1849–52)
- **Why it matters:** Le Gray's earliest Fontainebleau manner — paper negative texture and a gnarled oak wedged among boulders, the frame filled edge to edge rather than emptied, a useful contrast case to the later, sparer tree studies.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/283626
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/gustave-le-gray/07-oak-tree-rocks-fontainebleau.jpg (3811px)

### 08 — Hollow Oak Tree, Fontainebleau (1855–57)
- **Why it matters:** A single ancient oak trunk placed dead-center and lit to read almost as sculpture — the composition built entirely around one dominant vertical mass.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/306321
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/gustave-le-gray/08-hollow-oak-tree-fontainebleau.jpg (3545px)

### 09 — Fontainebleau, chemin sablonneux montant (ca. 1856)
- **Why it matters:** A sandy forest path climbing on a strong diagonal into the middle distance, trees framing it left and right — Le Gray's clearest use of a leading line to pull the eye into depth.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/283123
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/gustave-le-gray/09-fontainebleau-sandy-path.jpg (3829px)

### 10 — The Ramparts of Carcassonne (1851)
- **Why it matters:** Shot for the government's Mission Héliographique survey, the fortified wall is read frontally as a stack of horizontal stone courses — architecture reduced to the same planar, banded geometry Le Gray brought to the seascapes.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/283107
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/gustave-le-gray/10-ramparts-of-carcassonne.jpg (3863px)

### 11 — Étude de nuages / Cloud Study (1856–57)
- **Why it matters:** A study of cloud forms alone, made as raw material for combination printing — the underlying technical practice behind the seascapes, shown here as a composition in its own right.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/301885
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/gustave-le-gray/11-cloud-study.jpg (3667px)

### 12 — Cavalry Maneuvers, Camp de Châlons (1857)
- **Why it matters:** Mounted troops fan out across a flat plain in receding diagonal ranks — a rare Le Gray composition built on dynamic figure placement and depth-through-repetition rather than a still horizon.
- **Source:** https://www.metmuseum.org/art/collection/search/283125
- **Rights:** Public domain (Metropolitan Museum of Art, Open Access / CC0)
- **File:** raw/gustave-le-gray/12-cavalry-camp-chalons.jpg (3830px)
