# Gustave Le Gray — research

Scope: technique, era, printing, and recorded camera settings for the twelve
plates in this chapter. This is 1849–58 paper-negative and wet-collodion
glass-negative work with no per-frame exposure record; where a number is not
documented, this says so.

## Who and when

Gustave Le Gray (1820–1884) was born in Villiers-le-Bel, north of Paris, and
trained first as a painter in the Paris studio of Paul Delaroche, where he
met two other students who became major photographers in their own right,
Charles Nègre and Henri Le Secq. He was making photographs by the late 1840s
and opened a Paris studio in 1848; by 1855, capitalized by a family of
Norman aristocrats, he ran a large and fashionable establishment at 35
Boulevard des Capucines that doubled as one of the era's few formal
photography schools — among the roughly fifty students who passed through it
were Nègre, Le Secq, Maxime Du Camp, and Nadar. In 1851 the newly formed
Commission des Monuments Historiques selected Le Gray as one of five
photographers for the Mission Héliographique, sending him and Auguste
Mestral through the Loire valley and southwest France to document
architecture for preservation planning, including the fortified town of
Carcassonne before its later restoration (image 10). He had already begun
photographing the forest of Fontainebleau before that trip and returned to
it repeatedly through the 1850s (images 06–09). By the mid-1850s he was the
closest thing France had to an official court photographer under Napoleon
III, documenting the imperial yacht at Le Havre (05), the fleet review at
Cherbourg (04), and army maneuvers at the model camp of Châlons (12). His
seascapes of 1856–58, made chiefly at Sète and along the Normandy coast (01–
03), won him immediate acclaim in Paris and London and remain the work he is
best known for. The studio's finances collapsed in 1860; abandoned by his
backers and pursued by creditors, Le Gray left France that year on a
Mediterranean voyage with Alexandre Dumas and never returned, eventually
settling in Cairo, where from 1864 he taught drawing and painting at the
Khedive's Polytechnic school. He died there in poverty in 1884, and his
reputation was largely rebuilt only from the 1980s onward as scholars and
collectors reassessed the surviving prints.

## Technique

Two distinct negative processes sit behind this chapter, both used by Le
Gray within a few years of each other. The earliest image here, the Oak Tree
and Rocks study (07, 1849–52), and the Mission Héliographique plate of
Carcassonne (10, 1851) were made on paper negatives, in the improved "waxed
paper" variant Le Gray himself developed and published around 1851: a sheet
of paper was waxed before sensitizing with silver iodide, which made it more
transparent and gave a finer, softer grain than the unwaxed calotype paper
then in common use, while also letting a photographer coat several sheets in
advance, carry them dry, expose in the field, and develop only later — a
real practical advantage on a survey trip or in a forest far from a
darkroom. By the mid-1850s Le Gray had adopted the wet-collodion glass-plate
process that was fast becoming standard across the profession: a glass
plate coated with iodized collodion, sensitized in a silver-nitrate bath,
exposed while still wet, and developed within minutes of exposure, which
required a portable darkroom (tent or covered wagon) on location. All of the
seascapes (01–05), the later Fontainebleau studies (06, 08, 09), the cloud
study (11), and the cavalry scene at Camp de Châlons (12) are wet-collodion
glass-negative work, prized for the finer detail and greater tonal range the
process gave over paper. The seascapes are also where Le Gray's specific
technical signature shows: sea and sky routinely exceeded the tonal range a
single collodion negative could hold in one exposure, so he exposed the two
independently — sometimes sea and sky from the same scene, sometimes a
genuine seascape negative paired with a stock cloud negative shot elsewhere
entirely, as the cloud study here (11) was made to be — then printed both
onto one sheet using a mask to protect each half from the other's light,
joining them at the horizon. That combination-printing technique, not a
single fortunate exposure, is what produces the hard, independently toned
horizon line that defines images 01, 03, and 04.

## Printing

Le Gray printed from his negatives onto both salted-paper and, increasingly
through the 1850s, albumen paper, and he was known among contemporaries
specifically for the quality and richness of his printing — he published his
own formulas and practical manual, treating printing as inseparable from
picture-making rather than a mechanical afterthought, which is consistent
with the deliberate combination printing described above. All twelve prints
in this chapter are held by the Metropolitan Museum of Art and released
under its Open Access (CC0) policy; the Met's own attributions and dating
are followed here. Le Gray material more broadly survives in institutional
collections including the Bibliothèque nationale de France, the Musée
d'Orsay, the George Eastman Museum, and the J. Paul Getty Museum, but the
negatives and prints behind these twelve specific images are not otherwise
tracked down beyond the Met's records for this chapter.

## Camera settings

Not recorded. Neither the waxed-paper process nor wet collodion carries any
per-frame aperture, shutter-speed, or sensitivity log — there was no light
meter, and the operator judged exposure by experience with the light, the
lens, and a given batch of paper or plate. What is qualitatively well
documented: wet-collodion plates had to be exposed and developed within
roughly the same quarter-hour the collodion stayed wet, so images 01–06 and
08–12 required a portable darkroom on site, whether harbor, forest, or army
camp; exposures on a leveled stand camera ran from several seconds to tens
of seconds depending on the light, long enough that the wide, deep fields in
the landscape and architectural plates read sharp front-to-back while a
genuinely fast-moving subject would blur. That last point matters for the
cavalry maneuvers at Châlons (12), whose galloping riders would have posed a
real challenge for an exposure this slow, and it is also the practical
reason the seascapes needed combination printing in the first place: a
single exposure timed correctly for the sea would have overexposed the sky,
and one timed for the sky would have left the sea a featureless black, so
sea and sky were each given their own exposure and their own printing time
rather than one compromise setting for both. No specific f-stop, shutter
speed, or sensitivity value survives for any of the twelve images, and none
is invented here.
