# Robert Capa — research note

## Scope and records consulted

The chapter's image-specific titles, dates, credits, source links, and rights routes are recorded in [sources.md](sources.md). The supplied source set ranges from the Spanish Civil War in 1938 through North Africa, Italy, Normandy, Budapest, and Tel Aviv in 1948. The National Museum of American History collection record cited there describes Capa's wartime photographs of soldiers, civilians, and refugees and identifies the Normandy work among its holdings. Magnum's Capa collection is the supporting authority for the broader wartime corpus.

## Technique and visual method

These pictures support a visual description of Capa's method, not a recipe inferred after the fact. He repeatedly works from close range and low or unstable positions: figures are blocked by equipment, packed against a frame edge, or placed in shallow water; a rifle, crane boom, obstacle, helmet row, or map table supplies the picture's directional structure. The Normandy images make blur, surf, haze, and interrupted visibility part of the event rather than defects to be corrected. The postwar Budapest and Tel Aviv pictures extend the same method to construction and crowd scale, using ropes, ship silhouette, and a shore mass to make collective action legible.

## Process, camera, and settings

The supplied records do not establish a camera model, lens, focal length, aperture, shutter speed, film stock, negative format, or per-image printing process for these eleven web reproductions. The ingested files contain no usable capture EXIF; where raw files carry software or contemporary device metadata, it belongs to later reproduction or scanning rather than the 1938–48 exposure. The source notes identify publicly available reproductions and rights status, not the original print process. It would therefore be false to assign settings from the pictures' motion, grain, or depth of field.

What can be stated from the images themselves is qualitative: close physical position, wide fields of view, and fast-changing action force Capa to accept partial figures, blur, and oblique horizons as the picture's grammar. This chapter keeps technical claims at that level and names no unverified exposure values.
