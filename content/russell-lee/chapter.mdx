Russell Lee made the social room visible. Across this 1938–41 selection, people do not appear as isolated emblems of hardship or community; they press against a wall, gather around a car, sit in a rank, leave through a doorway, or arrange themselves around an improvised school. The overlays keep their claims close to that evidence: a doorway, desk rhythm, fence, board, mast, or arm. The analyzer’s tonal readings explain why those structures stay clear. Dark clothing and boards hold against bright walls and ground; in the two colour plates, browns, greens, and blue-grey sky do the separating.

## bodies make the public line

*Relief line waiting for commodities* turns a row of people into the picture’s route. The overlay follows the waiting line from the foreground at left toward the building’s far edge and calls out the window-sill band at y0.211. The figures are tightly staged between that horizontal and the lower wall: neither a dramatic vanishing point nor a sentimental isolated face is needed. The frame’s mean luminance is 0.448, with a broad range from p05 0.031 to p95 0.808, and its palette runs from nearly black #0c0c0c to #c6c6c6. Coats and window shadows therefore retain their separate weight against the pale masonry.

## groups held by a structure

The Chicago street portrait uses the car as an armature. Its centerline is x0.487; the measured street recession meets at x0.572, y0.507; and the curb-and-street band crosses at y0.388. The anchored seated boy is one of several unequal figures around the car, not a solitary hero. At 0.075, the edge density is substantial, and the tonal range runs from pure black to p95 0.894. Dark suits, chrome, road, and the pale street make a dense but ordered field.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Make a social line">
Photograph three or more people along a wall, counter, curb, or railing. Keep one continuous boundary visible behind them, then compare a version where that boundary breaks. Does the group still read as a shared action rather than several separate portraits?
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Build a lesson from a room">
Photograph a person teaching, showing, or repairing something. Include one real rectangle, a board, window, chart, or tabletop, and one visible gesture that connects the person to it. Mark only those physical facts afterward.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Find the structure inside a crowd">
Photograph a group around a car, door, table, ride, or building. First locate the centerline, route, or enclosing shape that lets the group cohere. If no real structure holds the figures together, move your position instead of drawing a decorative overlay.
</ExerciseCard>
