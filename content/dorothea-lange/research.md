# Dorothea Lange — research

Scope: technique, era, printing, and recorded camera settings for the twelve
plates in this chapter. These are film-era negatives with no per-frame exposure
record; where a number is not documented, this says so.

## Who and when

Dorothea Lange (1895–1965) trained in portrait photography and ran a successful
San Francisco portrait studio through the 1920s before the Depression turned her
outward onto the street. From 1935 to 1939 she worked for the Resettlement
Administration and its successor, the Farm Security Administration, under Roy
Stryker, documenting displaced farmers and migrant labor across the West and
South. Images 01–10 here are from that federal work (1935–1938). Images 11–12
(1942) were made for the War Relocation Authority during the incarceration of
Japanese Americans. All are U.S. government works and therefore public domain.

## Technique

For the FSA field work Lange worked mostly with a Graflex Series D single-lens
reflex on 4×5 sheet film, held at waist level so she could compose on the ground
glass while staying in contact with her subject, and she also used a Rolleiflex
(medium-format 2¼-inch roll film) and other cameras. *Migrant Mother* was made on
the 4×5 Graflex, in a sequence of exposures she edited down to the single frame
that became the icon. She composed deliberately in the field and captioned
carefully, but she also allowed the FSA lab to retouch (the famous removal of a
thumb from the *Migrant Mother* negative was done by the FSA, to her later
irritation).

## Printing

The negatives were developed and printed by the FSA/OWI photographic lab in
Washington; the surviving file is a set of black-and-white nitrate and safety
negatives held by the Library of Congress, from which the modern high-resolution
scans here derive. Lange also made her own exhibition prints later in life. The
1942 WRA images are held by the National Archives and the Library of Congress.

## Camera settings

Not recorded. FSA field negatives were not logged with aperture or shutter data,
and the digital files carry only archival identifiers and scanner metadata. What
can be said on the record is qualitative: reflex and view-camera work on slow
mid-1930s sheet and roll film, often outdoors in bright Western light, is
consistent with the deep focus and the crisp rendering of the pictures, but any
specific exposure value would be invented, so none is given. The one well-
documented technical intervention is editorial, not photographic: the retouched
thumb in *Migrant Mother*.
