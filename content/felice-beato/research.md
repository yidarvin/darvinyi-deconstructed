# Felice Beato — research

## Era and context

Felice Beato (1832–1909), an Italian-born British photographer, made the India pictures in this group during the aftermath of the 1857 uprising, chiefly in 1858–62, and made the Taku Fort and Prince Kung pictures during the 1860 Anglo-French campaign in China. Getty records that he sequenced and captioned such views for a Western audience; the photographs are therefore composed colonial-era representations of military campaigns, not transparent or neutral battlefield evidence. Getty also documents deliberate rearrangement of human remains at Secundrabagh, and a contemporary account connected with the North Fort sequence says Beato asked that casualties not be disturbed before photographing them. The chapter treats the structural choices visible in the prints without converting those choices into unqualified historical proof.

## Technique and printing

The institutional object records for the selected works identify them as albumen silver prints, generally from glass negatives. Albumen describes the paper print, not by itself the chemistry of every negative. Getty says Beato learned an albumen glass-plate negative process from James Robertson and carried chemicals and large, fragile glass plates during the 1860 campaign. LACMA describes his general field workflow as collodion-on-glass, made, exposed, and developed while wet in a portable darkroom, then contact-printed on albumen paper. That establishes a plausible general practice, but the supplied object records do not specify the exact negative chemistry for every one of these twelve images; individual works are described here conservatively as albumen silver prints from glass negatives.

Nineteenth-century albumen prints were contact prints: sensitized paper was placed directly against the negative and exposed to light, rather than enlarged. Although Beato later made hand-colored works, the supplied records do not establish hand-coloring for these twelve monochrome views.

## Camera settings

No camera model, lens, focal length, aperture, shutter speed, plate size, exposure duration, or sensitivity value is documented for these twelve images in the supplied records. The historical record supports only the broader constraints of a large-format glass-plate field practice and a portable darkroom. No numerical setting is inferred from the images.
