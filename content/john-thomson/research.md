# John Thomson — research

John Thomson (1837–1921) made these China photographs in 1868–72, in the late Qing treaty-port era. They belong to a British commercial photographer's project of selecting, captioning, and publishing Chinese people and places for Western readers. The archive does not generally document the sitters' names, consent, or the directions given during a sitting, so none is inferred here. The historical conditions matter without reducing every encounter to one motive: the resulting photographs can register presence and collaboration while also participating in a colonial system of representation.

The Wellcome source objects are wet-collodion glass negatives, not surviving period prints. Wet collodion required a plate to be coated, sensitised, exposed, and developed while still wet in a portable darkroom; the Hong Kong Museum of History records that Thomson employed eight workers to carry this equipment. That material constraint helps explain deliberate viewpoints and relative stillness, but it does not prove a particular pose was staged. Thomson used full-plate and stereo cameras, though no particular instrument survives; only the self-portrait stereo plate supports a stereoscopic-camera claim. Aperture, lens, focal length, shutter or exposure duration, filters, and plate chemistry for individual images are not documented in the reviewed records.

The 1873–74 four-volume *Illustrations of China and Its People* circulated the work in London through the Autotype Company; an MFA Houston catalogue describes Volume I as a book of carbon prints. This is publication history, not a description of the scanned negative assets here. Do not call these negatives albumen or collotype prints.
