Man Ray turns a portrait into a set of forces: a face can be pinned by a hat brim, a printed border can become a second frame, and a hand can make a mask a deliberate partner rather than background property. This selection moves from New York in 1917 to Paris in the 1920s, but its compositional question stays consistent: how can a small number of light and dark shapes make identity feel staged rather than simply recorded? The overlays make only visible claims: head, collar, border, mantel, mask, comb, and table edge. They avoid treating texture or a tonal split as a false horizon or perspective system. [Source notes](../../content/man-ray/sources.md)

## the portrait as a cropped construction

<Plate slug="man-ray" id="01-william-zorach" label="William Zorach, c. 1917" caption="The tilted head, tie knot, and left lapel keep the face from dissolving into the dark coat mass." />

In *William Zorach*, the large head ellipse is intentionally broad: it names the tilted mass, while the anchor sits at the tie knot where collar, shirt, and coat converge. The left-lapel path then returns that lower detail to the face. The image has a wide p05–p95 range of 0.098–0.831 but a low 0.027 edge density; light carves a few large forms instead of supplying descriptive detail. The analyzer's grayscale palette, from #222222 through #cfcfcf, reinforces the argument: a near-black coat is the counterweight that makes the face appear to lean forward.

<Plate slug="man-ray" id="02-hd" label="H.D., 1917" caption="The printed-image border encloses H.D.'s turned silhouette and makes the surrounding page part of the composition." />

*H.D.* is a photograph mediated by a page. The overlay follows the actual inner print boundary rather than pretending that its straight edges are architectural facts in the pictured room. H.D.'s face is kept to the right of the image field while a pale vertical opening separates it from the dark coat; the large surrounding margin makes that internal cut sharper. Mean luminance is 0.394, p05–p95 spans 0.122–0.867, and edge density is just 0.023. The image therefore works through a small number of dark and pale blocks, not through fine contour.

<Plate slug="man-ray" id="03-alice-toklas-gertrude-stein" label="Alice B. Toklas and Gertrude Stein, 1922" caption="Toklas and Stein sit at unequal ends of a dense salon stage, joined by the mantel and picture-wall rhythm." />

The two ellipses in *Alice B. Toklas and Gertrude Stein* deliberately resist a centered double portrait: Toklas is compressed into the lower left, Stein opens out in the right chair, and the mantel edge establishes a low horizontal stage between them. Above it, framed pictures multiply the room's internal rectangles. The brown-leaning palette (#423a2a to #ab9777), modest tonal span of 0.208–0.620, and 0.029 edge density make the many objects read as a single dense environment rather than as a catalogue of possessions.

<Plate slug="man-ray" id="04-rrose-selavy" label="Portrait of Rrose Sélavy (Marcel Duchamp), 1921" caption="A face is crowded by the feathered brim and fur collar, making costume a sequence of cropped textures." />

The *Rrose Sélavy* overlay avoids the detector's tempting perspective lines; this is not a room receding to a point. Its argument is local: the feathered brim arcs across the upper frame, the fur collar rises from below, and the face is the pale interruption between them. At 0.094, edge density is the highest among the portrait close-ups here, while p05–p95 runs from 0.075 to 0.843. That dense feather-and-fur texture makes the image feel insistently made, not transparent; the costume does not decorate an identity but helps produce it.

<Plate slug="man-ray" id="05-james-joyce" label="James Joyce, 1922" caption="Joyce's face and bright shirt front form a small lit relay inside the dark suit and sleeve edge." />

In *James Joyce*, the sleeve edge rises from the lower left toward the face, then the white shirt front continues the eye downward through the black suit. The face ellipse is a literal contour, not a claim that the sitter is symmetrically centered. This is the chapter's busiest portrait reproduction: edge density reaches 0.214 and the tonal range is extreme, p05 0.012 to p95 0.898. A narrow bright face-and-shirt path keeps that printed grain and dense dark coat from becoming visual noise.

## faces, masks, and a problem of rhyme

<Plate slug="man-ray" id="06-kiki-african-mask" label="Kiki with African Mask, 1926" caption="Kiki's reclining face and the upright mask make an unequal pair, held in place by the hand and forearm." />

*Kiki with African Mask* makes its comparison bluntly visible: a broad, pale horizontal face is answered by a narrow, dark vertical mask. The hand is not incidental; its traced bend fastens the mask upright and makes the difference in scale and orientation legible. The photograph is unusually sparse, with edge density 0.018 and p05–p95 only 0.141–0.584, so the paired ovals carry nearly all of the picture's pressure. The source title describes the object as an African mask, but the available record does not identify its maker or community. Formal rhyme should not erase the modernist appropriation that makes that pairing historically charged. [Research note](../../content/man-ray/research.md)

<Plate slug="man-ray" id="07-catherine-hessling" label="Catherine Hessling, 1927" caption="The magazine reproduction frames a pale face, scarf, and dark shoulder against a large open field." />

The magazine border in *Catherine Hessling* is retained as a real part of this reproduction, not cropped away as if it were photographic space. Inside it, a face ellipse, scarf path, and dark shoulder set a diagonal descent through an otherwise pale field. Its mean luminance of 0.614 is the chapter's brightest portrait mean; p95 reaches 0.922 while edge density stays at 0.026. The contrast between #393c37 and #ece9e2 makes the mouth, collar, and shoulder do more work than the minimal background.

<Plate slug="man-ray" id="08-arnold-schonberg" label="Arnold Schönberg, 1927" caption="A broad head contour, light-to-shadow transition, and jaw-to-collar turn create a quiet asymmetrical center." />

The surface of *Arnold Schönberg* appears frontal only at first. The broad ellipse holds the head, but the overlay's light-to-shadow path crosses the forehead and cheek, while the jaw-to-collar turn prevents the face from becoming a flat mask. Mean luminance is 0.322, p05–p95 is 0.082–0.863, and edge density is 0.059. Against a palette ranging from #251e11 to #ded0ba, the very dark left field gives the lit forehead and right cheek their unstable weight.

<Plate slug="man-ray" id="10-noire-et-blanche" label="Noire et blanche, 1926" caption="Kiki's face and the mask rhyme across the table edge while the hand and arm fix their unequal pairing." />

*Noire et blanche* restages the earlier mask pairing in a more exact, theatrical arrangement. The table edge divides the picture into a shallow upper stage and a broad pale foreground; the two ellipses name the face and mask without claiming they are interchangeable. Mean luminance rises to 0.583 and edge density falls to 0.019, so the image is governed by smooth tones rather than texture. The cool grayscale palette, from #2b3237 through #dde2df, makes the mask's dark surface and Kiki's pale cheek read as a controlled visual rhyme. As with the related image above, that formal rhyme exists alongside an unrecorded object provenance and a history of modernist appropriation, not outside it.

## when the camera disappears

<Plate slug="man-ray" id="09-champs-delicieux-rayograph" label="Untitled rayograph, from Champs délicieux, 1922" caption="A white disc, comb edge, and translucent diagonal make a camera-less composition from contact, opacity, and light." />

The *Champs délicieux* rayograph is the chapter's necessary break from portraiture and from lens perspective. The overlay follows the white disc, one actual comb edge, and the translucent diagonal; it does not promote the paper's incidental tonal divisions to a horizon. The source record identifies this work as a rayograph, a camera-less photogram, and as a gelatin silver print. Its measured p05–p95 span of 0.161–0.886 and 0.055 edge density explain the graphic force: opaque objects, translucent material, and bare paper produce a picture of contact and scale rather than depth. [Source notes](../../content/man-ray/sources.md)

<Callout>
The available records identify the rayograph's camera-less process and the selected work as a gelatin silver print, but they do not document a common process for the nine portraits. They also provide no verified camera model, lens, focal length, aperture, shutter speed, film stock, sensitivity, or exposure setting for this selection. The modern EXIF retained in a few web files records scanning/digitization, not Man Ray's equipment. [Research note](../../content/man-ray/research.md)
</Callout>

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Use a border as a second frame">
Photograph a small print, screen, doorway, or mirror inside a larger frame. Keep the inner boundary visible and decide whether it isolates the subject or makes the surrounding material part of the image.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Make a pair unequal">
Arrange two forms that rhyme in contour but differ in scale, tone, or orientation. Add a hand, edge, or other connecting element only if it makes the relationship deliberate rather than merely decorative.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Compose without a lens">
Make a simple photogram with opaque and translucent objects on photographic paper, or use a flatbed scan as a study. Build the image from overlap, contact, and tonal weight; do not rely on a horizon or vanishing point.
</ExerciseCard>
