# Man Ray — source notes

All downloaded files were opened after acquisition. The shortest long edge is 753px;
the remaining nine run from 810px to 5,332px. This is a deliberately early,
publication-led set: it establishes Man Ray's studio portraiture, Dada identity play,
and rayographic abstraction before a later build selects its teaching sequence.

## Acquired shortlist

### 01. *William Zorach* (c. 1917)

The tight, almost sculptural head study turns a dark ground and a tilted face into a
single compact mass—an early example of Man Ray making portrait lighting carry form.
Source: [Library of Congress, digital ID cph.3c37266](https://hdl.loc.gov/loc.pnp/cph.3c37266), downloaded through [Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:William_Zorach-by-Man_Ray.jpg).

**Use basis: open.** The Library of Congress/Commons record marks the file “no known
restrictions.” Credit: Man Ray; Library of Congress, Prints and Photographs Division,
digital ID cph.3c37266.

### 02. *H.D.* (1917)

The cropped half-length portrait makes the sitter's pale face and dark clothes a
high-contrast vertical rhythm, useful for discussing framing as a decisive cut.
Source: [*Tendencies in Modern American Poetry* at Internet Archive](https://archive.org/stream/tendenciesinmod00loweuoft), downloaded through [Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:H.D._in_Tendencies_in_Modern_American_Poetry,_1917.jpg).

**Use basis: open.** Published in the 1917 book and marked public domain by Commons.
Credit: Man Ray; scan of *Tendencies in Modern American Poetry* via Internet Archive.

### 03. *Alice B. Toklas and Gertrude Stein* (1922)

The paired portrait uses the two sitters' contrasting hats, collars, and gazes to set
up a deliberately unstable balance rather than a centered double portrait.
Source: [Wikimedia Commons file record](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Alice_B._Toklas_and_Gertrude_Stein,_1922,_Man_Ray.jpg).

**Use basis: open.** The source file is marked public domain on Commons. Credit:
Man Ray; digitization credited on the file record to 19h00s. The chapter retains the
record link with the work.

### 04. *Portrait of Rrose Sélavy (Marcel Duchamp)* (1921)

The fan, hand, and eye create a diagonal relay that makes masquerade legible as a
composition of cropped signs rather than as a straightforward sitter portrait.
Source: [Wikimedia Commons file record](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Portrait-of-rose-s%C3%A9lavy-1921.jpg) (source attribution there: WikiArt).

**Use basis: open.** The 1921 work is marked public domain on Commons. Credit:
Man Ray; source reproduction attributed to WikiArt on the file record.

### 05. *James Joyce* (1922)

Joyce's raised hand, bright shirt front, and the dark rectangle behind him form a
three-part counterweight—an economical lesson in a portrait's internal geometry.
Source: [*Shadowland*, September 1922, at Internet Archive](https://archive.org/details/shadowland07brew), downloaded through [Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:James_Joyce_-_Sep_1922_Shadowland.jpg).

**Use basis: open.** First published in the 1922 periodical and marked public domain
by Commons. Credit: Man Ray; *Shadowland* scan via Internet Archive.

### 06. *Kiki with African Mask* (1926)

This close pairing of Kiki's face and the mask makes the picture's repeated oval,
tilted cheek planes, and black/white tonal opposition immediately analyzable.
Source: [National Gallery of Victoria collection record](https://www.ngv.vic.gov.au/explore/collection/work/9783/), downloaded through [Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:May_Ray_Kiki_with_African_mask.jpg).

**Use basis: open.** The Commons file is marked public domain. Credit: Man Ray;
National Gallery of Victoria collection record. The source link is retained because
the work's cultural framing matters alongside its formal analysis.

### 07. *Catherine Hessling* (1927)

The magazine portrait compresses Hessling's head and the pale surround into a stark
single-figure silhouette, making negative space and print contrast the teaching focus.
Source: [*Cinégraphie*, September 1927, reproduction page](https://www.la-belle-equipe.fr/2018/11/02/entretiens-avec-catherine-hessling-cinegraphie-1927-et-pour-vous-1933/), downloaded from the public [Wikimedia Commons record](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Catherine_Hessling_by_Man_Ray_in_Cinegraphie_Septembre_1927.jpg).

**Use basis: fair-use.** Copyright notice is not supplied by the magazine scan; Man
Ray's estate is represented for this body of work by Man Ray Trust / ADAGP. This
chapter's use is criticism, scholarship, and transformative composition teaching; it
needs the complete frame to discuss silhouette and surrounding negative space; the
copy is web-sized (695 × 925px); and it links to and credits the public source rather
than replacing the original magazine or a licensed reproduction.

### 08. *Arnold Schönberg* (1927)

The long tonal gradient around Schönberg's head isolates the face while the turned
shoulder arrests an otherwise frontal portrait—useful for discussing a portrait's
asymmetrical center of gravity.
Source: [Arnold Schönberg Center file record](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Arnold_sch%C3%B6nberg_man_ray.jpg).

**Use basis: open.** The rights holder's permission is archived by Wikimedia's VRT;
the file is licensed CC BY-SA 2.0. Credit: Man Ray / Arnold Schönberg Center; licensed
CC BY-SA 2.0, with attribution and licence link required.

### 09. *Untitled rayograph*, from *Champs délicieux* (1922)

The rayograph converts translucent and opaque objects into floating, overlapping
shadows, making it the set's clearest lesson in camera-less composition and scale.
Source: [Art Institute of Chicago collection record, 1979.108](https://www.artic.edu/artworks/131709/untitled), downloaded through [Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Untitled_gelatin_silver_print_by_Man_Ray,_from_Champs_d%C3%A9licieux_(Delicious_Fields).jpg).

**Use basis: fair-use.** The Art Institute record carries the notice “© 2018 Man Ray
Trust / Artists Rights Society (ARS), New York / ADAGP, Paris.” This chapter's use is
criticism, scholarship, and transformative composition teaching; it needs the complete
rayograph to analyze object placement and photogram scale; the copy is only web-sized
(672 × 876px); and it links to and credits the Art Institute record rather than
replacing the original portfolio or a licensed reproduction. Commons additionally marks
the 1922 publication public domain in the United States, but this documented fair-use
route is used because the holding institution supplies the current estate notice.

### 10. *Noire et blanche* (1926)

Kiki's pale face and the dark Baule mask are locked into an almost exact visual rhyme;
the photo is indispensable for teaching tonal counterpoint, repeated contour, and the
ethical context of its modernist appropriation.
Source: [Google Arts & Culture public object display](https://artsandculture.google.com/asset/noire-et-blanche-0002/jgFtjUi0aPaZzA); the displayed, publicly retrievable 1200px rendition was downloaded directly. Supporting collection record: [Centre Pompidou](https://www.centrepompidou.fr/fr/ressources/oeuvre/cgj9Bne).

**Use basis: fair-use.** The Centre Pompidou record identifies the work as © Man Ray
Trust / ADAGP, Paris. This chapter's use is criticism, scholarship, and transformative
composition teaching; it needs the complete frame to discuss the face/mask pairing and
its implications; the copy is web-sized (1200 × 969px); and it links to and credits the
public collection/display records rather than replacing an original or licensed
reproduction.
