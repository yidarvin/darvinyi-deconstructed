# Cecil Beaton — research note

## Scope and records consulted

The local source dossier documents ten photographs made by Cecil Beaton for the Imperial War Museums: work made in Britain, India, China, North Africa, and a Wellington aircraft during 1940–45. Each selected file is publicly available through Wikimedia Commons with an IWM object record and a public-domain statement; the local files are unaltered, normalized copies of those display originals. The dossier records titles, dates or date ranges, object numbers, provenance, and credit as Cecil Beaton / Imperial War Museums. [Source notes](sources.md)

## Visual method and era

This is a wartime selection rather than a substitute for Beaton's later fashion and society archive. Across the group, he makes immediate conditions legible through framing: a broken arch encloses a distant cathedral, tram bars make shallow portrait cells, a door's studs set a tempo beside a still sitter, and aircraft controls crowd a working body. The chapter's claims about thresholds, repeated bars, hard light, faces, hands, and machinery are readings of the visible photographs and the deterministic image analyses; they do not claim undocumented intent or direction.

## Process, printing, and camera settings

The cited IWM and Commons records, source dossier, and normalized image files do **not** establish a camera model, lens, focal length, aperture, shutter speed, film stock, negative format, exposure index, lighting setup, original print process, paper, print date, or darkroom method for these individual frames. The ingest manifest records dimensions and no capture EXIF; absent or web-file metadata cannot reconstruct the historical exposure. No technical setting or printing recipe is inferred from grain, contrast, or the wartime subject matter.

It is supportable to discuss the visible effects of framing, tonal separation, repeated architectural elements, posture, and foreground machinery. It is not supportable here to convert those effects into claims about specific equipment, film, exposure, or printing choices.
