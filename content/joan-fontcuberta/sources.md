# Joan Fontcuberta — source shortlist

This eleven-work set follows Fontcuberta’s sustained use of persuasive photographic form to unsettle photographic authority: fabricated botanical and zoological specimens, an archival-looking natural-history sheet, altered photograms, a painted photographic body, a staged monster, and two late images where pattern seems to promise evidence. The Art Institute of Chicago collection API marks every selected work `is_public_domain: false`; all files were therefore acquired as the museum’s publicly served 843px-long-edge IIIF display JPEGs and are used on a **fair-use** basis. Each JPEG was opened and measured locally; all clear the project’s 640px floor.

**Rights basis:** Joan Fontcuberta is the credited artist on every record; the museum gives the listed collection credit but no separate copyright notice or named rightsholder. The rightsholder is consequently recorded conservatively as “Joan Fontcuberta / rightsholder not specified by the Art Institute record.” The assessment below is per work: this nonprofit book makes a transformative use for criticism, scholarship, and composition teaching; each underlying work is a creative photograph or photographic object; the complete frame is needed to analyze the asserted composition; and the credited, record-linked, web-sized copy neither replaces a print nor a licensed reproduction.

## Research context

The [Banco de España artist profile](https://coleccion.bde.es/wca/en/secciones/coleccion/artistas/joan-fontcuberta-779.html) identifies *Herbarium* (1982), *Fauna* (1987), *Frottogrames* (1987–90), *Palimpsests* (1989–93), *Sputnik* (1997), and *Googlegrams* (2005) as a sustained investigation of photography’s documentary, illusory, and contextual claims. [MACBA’s artist profile](https://www.macba.cat/en/actor/joan-fontcuberta/) likewise describes his work as questioning photographic truthfulness through parodic, institution-shaped presentations. Those accounts make this set’s complete frames—not cropped illustrative details—necessary evidence for the planned compositional criticism.
