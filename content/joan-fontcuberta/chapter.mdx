Joan Fontcuberta uses photography's visual authority as a material to test. These
works borrow the look of specimens, archive sheets, photograms, portraits, and
cosmic records, then let their improbable subjects or unstable surfaces expose
how quickly a photograph can be read as evidence. The overlays do not try to
solve the fictions. They name the visible decisions that make them persuasive:
an isolated silhouette, an enclosing curve, an old-paper frame, a centered
field, or a carefully measured tonal divide. [Source notes](../../content/joan-fontcuberta/sources.md)

## specimens that use blank paper as proof

<Plate slug="joan-fontcuberta" id="01-cala-rasca" label="Cala Rasca, 1984" caption="A wrapped base and flower are held apart by one long arcing stem, making the pale paper an active interval." />

In *Cala Rasca*, the overlay follows the arcing stem from the wrapped base to
the flower opening rather than mistaking the white page for empty background.
The analyzer finds just 0.011 edge density and a high mean luminance of 0.901:
pale #eff0ed and #e7e9e6 leave the darker stem and base to read as an unlikely
specimen suspended in a clinical field. The thirds grid is a measured fit, but
the composition gains its force from the stem crossing that regularity.

<Plate slug="joan-fontcuberta" id="02-supratex-horadatus" label="Supratex Horadatus, 1984" caption="A swollen head, thorned spine, and side growth turn an isolated biological silhouette into a precarious figure." />

*Supratex Horadatus* makes a single form sound diagnostic by giving it a broad
pale reserve. The overlay distinguishes its swollen head, specimen spine, and
side growth; their asymmetry prevents the centered silhouette from settling into
a neutral botanical illustration. The phi grid is the analyzer's better fit.
Near-white #ecede9 surrounds charcoal #3a3a39, and the 0.195 tonal spread keeps
the thorned edge legible without supplying any environmental context.

<Plate slug="joan-fontcuberta" id="03-pirulera-salbitana" label="Pirulera Salbitana, 1984" caption="Two long curves close around a tiny fly, making scale and empty paper the picture's central drama." />

The two marked arcs in *Pirulera Salbitana* meet near the small fly rather than
forming a complete enclosing circle. That withheld closure makes the fly a
pressure point inside an otherwise almost blank page. At mean luminance 0.909
and edge density 0.008, pale #f0f2ef dominates; dark #403e3d is reserved for
the curves and their small interruption. The phi fit is therefore less a rule
than a way of registering how the curve gathers toward the small subject.

## the archive performs its certainty

<Plate slug="joan-fontcuberta" id="04-pirofagus-catalanae" label="Pirofagus Catalanae, 1986" caption="A centered, worn sheet frames labeled studies of an invented creature in the grammar of natural-history evidence." />

*Pirofagus Catalanae* uses an archival sheet as an internal frame. Its measured
central axis steadies the separate studies and their annotations, so the viewer
encounters a coordinated document before reading individual marks. The source
record identifies it as a photographic work reproducing an ink-and-wash drawing;
that specific mediation matters more than any invented camera story. Cream
#ebe5d2, #dfd4b5, and brown-gray #5f5a4f keep the stains and inscriptions inside
a moderate 0.039 edge-density field.

<Plate slug="joan-fontcuberta" id="05-autumn" label="Autumn, 1989" caption="Upper and lower foliage masses create a dark interval, punctuated by a low white bloom." />

The documented gelatin-silver photogram on vinyl wallpaper, *Autumn*, makes its
central darkness feel staged by vegetal bands rather than by a conventional
landscape horizon. The overlay marks the lower foliage edge, central field, and
white bloom. It is a more crowded surface than the specimen plates: edge density
0.098, with charcoal #454239 opposed to pale #ede9cd and muted olive #636051.
The vertical axis is measured, but the bloom's low off-axis position keeps the
image from reading as pure symmetry.

<Plate slug="joan-fontcuberta" id="06-nu-couche" label="Nu Couché (Reclining Nude), 1993" caption="A face, hard black wedge, and broken body contour make the photographic surface feel like a bodily collage." />

*Nu Couché* compresses a painted body into a shallow, unstable field. The
overlay's black wedge is a real diagonal edge that forces the eye back toward the
face, while the marked contour shows how limbs and planes refuse a single clean
silhouette. The analyzer measures mean luminance 0.440, p05 0.051, and a palette
from near-black #181411 through taupe #746b5e to pale #bdbaa1. These broad tonal
planes, not a claimed camera technique, make the collage pressure visible.

## figures that are almost evidence

<Plate slug="joan-fontcuberta" id="07-tyrannosaurus-leg" label="Tyrannosaurus’s Leg, 1990" caption="A bent leg, low floor edge, and shadow double make identity hinge on outline and cast light." />

In *Tyrannosaurus’s Leg*, the overlay follows the bent leg to its
weight-bearing foot and holds it against the low, slightly tilted floor edge.
The adjacent shadow repeats the vertical drama without becoming a second body.
Pale #f2f4ed and warm #e3d2a8 make the leg's gray #52504c contour unusually
legible; p95 reaches 0.949 while the modest 0.018 edge density keeps the staged
room spare.

<Plate slug="joan-fontcuberta" id="08-cebus-nigittatus" label="Cebus nigittatus, 1990" caption="A face-like form hovers in a scratched, darkened image field whose wear appears to authenticate the fiction." />

The documented gelatin-silver photogram *Cebus nigittatus* refuses a stable
portrait. The overlay only names the face-like oval, the dark central tangle,
and the worn image field; it does not pretend that the scratches prove an
animal's presence. Brown-black #382f25, pale #d3cbc0, and near-white #eff0eb
span a wide p05-to-p95 range of 0.133–0.918. At edge density 0.034, damage and
silhouette remain distinct enough to imitate an evidentiary surface.

<Plate slug="joan-fontcuberta" id="09-garden-of-the-dragon" label="The Garden of the Dragon, 1991" caption="Pale fractures and a dense field of fragments keep the surface from resolving into a reliable figure-ground." />

In the gelatin-silver photogram *The Garden of the Dragon*, the overlay traces
two actual pale fractures before naming the larger dense field they interrupt.
This avoids treating the texture as a receding scene: it is a surface whose
overlapping fragments keep changing rank. It has the chapter's highest edge
density, 0.119. Dark #3f3d36, brown-gray #746d5f, and pale #f2f4ef make the
fractures visible without resolving the image's archaeological ambiguity.

## a field can look like a measurement

<Plate slug="joan-fontcuberta" id="11-apocalypse-st-john" label="Apocalypse St. John, 1999" caption="A black sky boundary, central field axis, and dotted lower expanse convert a celestial-looking image into a measured system." />

*Apocalypse St. John* gives an enormous black upper field a hard boundary at
y=0.424, then compresses the marked lower expanse beneath it. The overlay's
vertical axis and dotted-field center describe that ordering without claiming a
literal astronomical vista. Mean luminance is only 0.205, p05 is 0.000, and the
palette rises from #030101 through gray #7f7f7d to #b1b4b3; the image's 0.072
edge density is concentrated where the marks begin, making the dark sky feel
deliberately blank.

<Plate slug="joan-fontcuberta" id="12-light-calligram-ii-1" label="Light Calligram II (1), 1993" caption="An ink burst and one upper-right ray turn an almost empty sheet into a diagram of outward force." />

*Light Calligram II (1)* closes the selection by making a central burst look
both accidental and exact. The overlay marks its measured ray center, the nearby
ink burst, and one genuine upper-right ray. The phi grid fits the measured
distribution, but the eye follows the irregular splatters rather than a smooth
spiral. Mean luminance is 0.904 and edge density just 0.016: paper-like
#f1eee2 and #ece7d7 leave dark #3b3935 ink to do all the directional work.

technical claims to the documented record and visible photographic structure.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Make blank space do evidentiary work">
Photograph one ordinary object against a large pale field. Move it until the
space between its base and its furthest projection becomes as important as the
object itself. Keep the version in which the empty area changes the object's
apparent scale or authority without adding a caption.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Build an archive without claiming a fact">

Arrange two or three views, a label, and one visibly aged material on a single
sheet. Photograph the whole sheet. Then remove one item and compare whether the
remaining arrangement still looks like a document; identify which compositional
device, not the wording, supplied the authority.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Turn a surface into a question">

Make a close photograph of a cracked, layered, or marked surface. Use two real
lines or fractures and one tonal mass to organize it, but avoid claiming a scene
behind the texture. Compare a version that reads as depth with one that remains
visibly a surface.
</ExerciseCard>
