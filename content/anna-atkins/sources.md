# Anna Atkins — sources

Atkins's compositional method is the direct-contact photogram, not the camera: a pressed algae specimen laid straight onto cyanotype-sensitized paper and exposed to sun, so the plant's own silhouette becomes the image. There is no lens, no perspective, no depth — only a flat white form suspended in a field of deep Prussian blue, and the entire compositional interest lives in how the specimen's structure fills, balances, or radiates across that rectangle. Some plates isolate a single frond dead-center; others scatter three or four specimens of varying scale across the page like a loose constellation; the finest ones (*Dictyota dichotoma*, *Padina Pavonia*) turn a botanical silhouette into pure abstract pattern — branching, radial, or fan-form geometry decades before abstraction had a name. Atkins hand-lettered each specimen's Latin name in white ink directly onto the blue ground, so the caption itself sits inside the composition rather than below it.

**Rights basis:** Public domain. *Photographs of British Algae: Cyanotype Impressions* was privately printed in fascicles between 1843 and 1853; Atkins died in 1871, and the work is long out of copyright everywhere (life+70 and well past any term). The images below are sourced from the New York Public Library's Spencer Collection copy, digitized at high resolution and released by NYPL as public domain, with master scans mirrored to Wikimedia Commons. All eleven plates come from this single, complete, well-provenanced digitization, which lets every image in this chapter cite the same clean rights basis.

## Shortlist

### 01 — Sargassum bacciferum (1843–44)
- **Why it matters:** The book's opening plate — clustered berry-like bladders (Gulf weed) scattered in a loose, irregular constellation across the page, establishing the plate-as-specimen-map format the whole series follows.
- **Source:** https://digitalcollections.nypl.org/items/510d47d9-4b3d-a3d9-e040-e00a18064a99
- **Rights:** Public domain (NYPL Spencer Collection; Wikimedia Commons mirror)
- **File:** raw/anna-atkins/01-sargassum-bacciferum.jpg (3072px)

### 02 — Dictyota dichotoma, in the young state; and in fruit (1843)
- **Why it matters:** The single most reproduced Atkins plate — a large radiating, dichotomously branching frond fills the lower two-thirds of the page like a fan of lightning, with three smaller sprigs scattered above it for scale and counterweight.
- **Source:** https://digitalcollections.nypl.org/items/510d47d9-4adb-a3d9-e040-e00a18064a99
- **Rights:** Public domain (NYPL Spencer Collection; Wikimedia Commons mirror)
- **File:** raw/anna-atkins/02-dictyota-dichotoma.jpg (3072px)

### 03 — Himanthalia lorea (1843–44)
- **Why it matters:** Long strap-like thongs cascade the full height of the plate in loose parallel curves — the composition built entirely on line and length rather than mass.
- **Source:** https://digitalcollections.nypl.org/items/510d47d9-4b41-a3d9-e040-e00a18064a99
- **Rights:** Public domain (NYPL Spencer Collection; Wikimedia Commons mirror)
- **File:** raw/anna-atkins/03-himanthalia-lorea.jpg (3072px)

### 04 — Laminaria digitata (1843–44)
- **Why it matters:** A single large hand-shaped kelp blade planted near the frame's center, its finger-like lobes splayed symmetrically — the plate's boldest, simplest silhouette.
- **Source:** https://digitalcollections.nypl.org/items/510d47d9-4b4e-a3d9-e040-e00a18064a99
- **Rights:** Public domain (NYPL Spencer Collection; Wikimedia Commons mirror)
- **File:** raw/anna-atkins/04-laminaria-digitata.jpg (3072px)

### 05 — Delesseria sanguinea (1843–44)
- **Why it matters:** Broad, leaf-like blades with fine internal veining rendered as delicate tonal gradation rather than flat white — the closest Atkins comes to naturalistic modeling within the silhouette method.
- **Source:** https://digitalcollections.nypl.org/items/510d47d9-4aff-a3d9-e040-e00a18064a99
- **Rights:** Public domain (NYPL Spencer Collection; Wikimedia Commons mirror)
- **File:** raw/anna-atkins/05-delesseria-sanguinea.jpg (3072px)

### 06 — Padina Pavonia (1843–44)
- **Why it matters:** Three clusters of fan-shaped, concentrically ringed fronds (peacock's-tail algae) — the most purely radial, almost ornamental composition in the shortlist, each fan a study in curved repetition.
- **Source:** https://digitalcollections.nypl.org/items/510d47d9-4a92-a3d9-e040-e00a18064a99
- **Rights:** Public domain (NYPL Spencer Collection; Wikimedia Commons mirror)
- **File:** raw/anna-atkins/06-padina-pavonia.jpg (3072px)

### 07 — Ceramium rubrum (1843–44)
- **Why it matters:** Fine, hair-thin filamentous branching spread wide and airy across the page — a study in delicacy and negative space that contrasts sharply with the book's broader-bladed specimens.
- **Source:** https://digitalcollections.nypl.org/items/510d47d9-4ac9-a3d9-e040-e00a18064a99
- **Rights:** Public domain (NYPL Spencer Collection; Wikimedia Commons mirror)
- **File:** raw/anna-atkins/07-ceramium-rubrum.jpg (3072px)

### 08 — Chondrus crispus (1843–44)
- **Why it matters:** Dense, repeatedly forking fronds (Irish moss) packed into a compact fan mass — the composition reads as solid tonal weight rather than line, a useful counterpoint to the filamentous plates.
- **Source:** https://digitalcollections.nypl.org/items/510d47d9-4b2a-a3d9-e040-e00a18064a99
- **Rights:** Public domain (NYPL Spencer Collection; Wikimedia Commons mirror)
- **File:** raw/anna-atkins/08-chondrus-crispus.jpg (3072px)

### 09 — Polysiphonia violacea (1843–44)
- **Why it matters:** Wispy, hair-fine fronds drift loosely across the upper register of the page, leaving the lower two-thirds empty blue — an exercise in extreme compositional imbalance and negative space.
- **Source:** https://digitalcollections.nypl.org/items/510d47d9-4b43-a3d9-e040-e00a18064a99
- **Rights:** Public domain (NYPL Spencer Collection; Wikimedia Commons mirror)
- **File:** raw/anna-atkins/09-polysiphonia-violacea.jpg (3072px)

### 10 — Cystoseira granulata (1843–44)
- **Why it matters:** Dense, bushy, multiply-branching growth spreads almost symmetrically from a single root point — a study in radial fill closer to a tree silhouette than a botanical specimen.
- **Source:** https://digitalcollections.nypl.org/items/510d47d9-4b3f-a3d9-e040-e00a18064a99
- **Rights:** Public domain (NYPL Spencer Collection; Wikimedia Commons mirror)
- **File:** raw/anna-atkins/10-cystoseira-granulata.jpg (3072px)

### 11 — Plocamium coccineum (1843–44)
- **Why it matters:** Sharply angular, comb-like branching zigzags down the frame in a tight, graphic rhythm — the most pattern-like, almost typographic structure among the algae plates.
- **Source:** https://digitalcollections.nypl.org/items/510d47d9-4b1c-a3d9-e040-e00a18064a99
- **Rights:** Public domain (NYPL Spencer Collection; Wikimedia Commons mirror)
- **File:** raw/anna-atkins/11-plocamium-coccineum.jpg (3072px)
