Anna Atkins photographed nothing at all, not in the sense any other chapter in this project uses the word. Photographs of British Algae is built by contact: a real, pressed alga laid straight onto cyanotype paper and left in the sun, so the specimen becomes its own mask and its own image. There is no lens to square to a subject, no depth to recede into, and no horizon to level against a tripod head. The entire compositional question these eleven plates answer is how a flat white silhouette occupies a rectangle of Prussian blue, scattered loosely like a scrawled constellation, radiated from one point like a fan, drawn out as pure line, or planted dead center as a single dominant mass. Because the generic composition analyzer behind this project's overlays still measures every frame for a horizon and a vanishing point regardless of what made it, its readings need translating here rather than quoting straight. What it calls a horizon almost always lands on the album page's own mounted edge, a few percent off the top or bottom of the frame, not any depth in the picture. Where it does report a vanishing point, that point is nothing but the place two or more of the specimen's own branches happen to converge, a botanical reading standing in for an optical one. Its palette samples, though, are exactly what they claim to be: five swatches per plate, and on every one of the eleven they resolve into the same family of Prussian blues bracketing a near-black for the hand lettering and a near-white for the paper or the thinnest part of the specimen, the chemistry this chapter's research describes rendered as plain, repeatable measurement.

## the loose constellation

Two plates test how far a specimen can scatter across an empty page before the eye needs an anchor, and each supplies a different one.

<Plate slug="anna-atkins" id="01-sargassum-bacciferum" label="Sargassum bacciferum, 1843-44" caption="A ten-point growth axis climbs the page corner to corner from a base at (0.32, 0.705) to a tip at (0.762, 0.175), while an ellipse and a subject anchor single out the berry-like bladders that give the plate its name." />

Sargassum bacciferum opens the book, and the overlay reads it as a climb rather than a static cluster. An ellipse 0.368 wide by 0.270 tall rings the irregular mass of Gulf-weed bladders, but the ten-point growth axis is the real structure, ticking diagonally from that base at (0.32, 0.705) up through six intermediate points to a tip at (0.762, 0.175), the frond scaling the rectangle from lower left to upper right. A subject anchor at (0.427, 0.602) sits directly on the bladders, the one detail the eye is asked to settle on before the axis pulls it onward. The analyzer's own numbers agree with a composition built on climb rather than symmetry: a mid-bright mean of 0.30, reaching from a p05 of 0.098 up to a p95 of 0.83, and a vertical symmetry of 0.81 on an axis at x equals 0.53, high for a diagonal scatter because the frond's climb balances the page left to right even without a literal mirror in it. Its own converging-diagonal search, working from twenty lines, lands a vanishing point at x equals 0.28, y equals 0.54, low in the same lower-left region the axis starts from. The five sampled swatches run from a near-black #221e1b through blues at #1b5c9e and #6089b1 up to #e1edef, the palest hex sampled anywhere in this chapter.

<Plate slug="anna-atkins" id="09-polysiphonia-violacea" label="Polysiphonia violacea, 1843-44" caption="Twin mirrored sprays crowd the phi-marked upper register while a tiny frame-in-frame isolates the handwritten caption stranded two-thirds of the way down an otherwise empty page." />

Polysiphonia violacea takes the same problem to an extreme. The overlay's ellipse for the frond sits at (0.45, 0.43), just 0.28 wide by 0.18 tall, and a three-point polyline traces the twin mirrored sprays sharing one base, from (0.19, 0.53) through (0.46, 0.56) to (0.70, 0.48), all of it crowded into the page's phi-marked upper register while a tiny frame-in-frame just 0.316 wide by 0.049 tall marks the handwritten caption stranded at y equals 0.714, far below the specimen. Two-thirds of the plate is left as unbroken cyanotype, and the analyzer's tonal reading confirms it: a p95 of just 0.78, the narrowest highlight ceiling in the chapter, against a mean of 0.415, because so little of the page carries any white specimen at all. It also returns the highest symmetry pairing in the set, a vertical symmetry of 0.93 on an axis at x equals 0.54 and a horizontal symmetry of 0.97 at y equals 0.46, the two sprays balancing the frame almost exactly even as they abandon its lower half. Twenty-one converging lines put its vanishing point at x equals 0.43, y equals 0.42, inside the same crowded cluster the ellipse already rings.

## the radial fan

Three plates turn a single specimen, or several, into pure radiating pattern, the closest this book comes to abstraction.

<Plate slug="anna-atkins" id="02-dictyota-dichotoma" label="Dictyota dichotoma, in the young state and in fruit, 1843" caption="The book's most reproduced plate: a radiating frond mass fills the lower two-thirds on a bilateral symmetry axis at x0.55, balanced by three small counterweight sprigs traced across the top of the page." />

Dictyota dichotoma is the single most reproduced plate Atkins made, and the overlay measures why it holds. An ellipse 0.415 wide by 0.255 tall rings the radiating, dichotomously branching frond mass filling the lower two-thirds of the page, anchored by a subject anchor at (0.47, 0.585) on the branch point where the fan first forks, while a three-point polyline across the top, from (0.27, 0.25) through (0.50, 0.22) to (0.73, 0.30), marks the small counterweight sprigs scattered above it. The overlay hangs a bilateral symmetry axis at x equals 0.55, and the analyzer's own measured vertical symmetry lands at 0.81 on exactly the same line, x equals 0.55, the hand-set axis and the raw correlation peak agreeing to two decimal places. Tonally the plate runs bright and long, a mean of 0.34 stretching from a p05 of 0.10 to a p95 of 0.89, and the analyzer's converging-diagonal search, reading the fan's own branch geometry, places a vanishing point at x equals 0.59, y equals 0.40 from ten lines, close to the crown of sprigs the polyline already traces.

<Plate slug="anna-atkins" id="06-padina-pavonia" label="Padina Pavonia, 1843-44" caption="Three concentrically ringed, peacock's-tail fronds are set as a stable triangle, an ellipse over each cluster joined by a polyline running (0.486, 0.34) to (0.323, 0.566) to (0.636, 0.607) and back." />

Padina Pavonia sets three fronds of the peacock's-tail alga into a deliberate triangle rather than a grid. Three ellipses ring the clusters: an upper one 0.216 wide by 0.092 tall at (0.486, 0.34), a lower left 0.148 by 0.086 at (0.323, 0.566), and a lower right 0.177 by 0.105 at (0.636, 0.607), and a closed polyline running through all three centers and back names the triangular arrangement outright. No grid is drawn over this plate at all, since the composition is its own geometry. The tonal reading is close kin to Dictyota's, a mean of 0.38 with a p05 of 0.086 and a p95 of 0.89, and the vertical symmetry comes in at 0.86 on an axis at x equals 0.54, the highest bilateral balance of the three fan plates even though nothing here is a literal mirror, just three concentrically ringed fans holding the corners of a triangle in rough visual equilibrium.

<Plate slug="anna-atkins" id="08-chondrus-crispus" label="Chondrus crispus, 1843-44" caption="Three impressions of the same dense, forking frond sit as a mirrored triangle, a symmetry axis at x0.562 landing almost exactly on the analyzer's own measured vertical-symmetry peak." />

Chondrus crispus repeats one specimen three times instead of varying it. The primary fan mass, ringed by an ellipse 0.201 wide by 0.149 tall at (0.529, 0.34), anchors a triangular rhythm the overlay traces through (0.324, 0.629) and (0.777, 0.636) and back to the top, and a symmetry axis is hand-set at x equals 0.562. The analyzer's own vertical symmetry lands at 0.81 on almost the same axis, x equals 0.5625, the hand-placed line and the raw correlation peak a few thousandths apart. Tonally this is the deepest and widest-ranging plate of the three, a mean of 0.41 with the widest tonal spread in the whole chapter, a standard deviation of 0.28, running from a p05 of 0.11 to a p95 of 0.91, because Irish moss prints as dense, forking tonal mass rather than the thin line of the other two fans. A frame-in-frame at (0.135, 0.137), 0.795 wide by 0.721 tall, marks the photographed album leaf itself, and the horizon the analyzer reports at y equals 0.86 falls almost exactly on that frame's own lower edge, 0.137 plus 0.721 equals 0.858, confirming it as the mount boundary rather than any depth cue.

## line without mass

Two plates strip the specimen down to line alone, the opposite move from the fans, one built for length and one for delicacy.

<Plate slug="anna-atkins" id="03-himanthalia-lorea" label="Himanthalia lorea, 1843-44" caption="Two strap-like thongs cascade the full height of the plate in loose, roughly mirrored parallel curves, converging on one basal button at (0.536, 0.726)." />

Himanthalia lorea is composition built entirely on line and length. A thirteen-point polyline arcs one thong the full height of the plate, from (0.419, 0.11) at the top down through a long curve to (0.565, 0.718) near the bottom, and a second, equally long polyline loops a companion thong in loose parallel from (0.23, 0.17) down to (0.342, 0.766), the two curves converging on a subject anchor at (0.536, 0.726) marked as the basal button where every thong meets. A symmetry axis at x equals 0.562 names the loose left-right mirror of the two loops, and the analyzer's own vertical symmetry agrees closely, 0.81 on an axis at x equals 0.5625. Tonally the plate runs bright and long, a mean of 0.39 reaching from a p05 of 0.094 to a p95 of 0.91, one of the brightest highlight ceilings in the chapter, appropriate to thongs that are mostly open white curve against blue. No vanishing point is confident enough here to report; twelve lines are found but none converge tightly enough to call.

<Plate slug="anna-atkins" id="07-ceramium-rubrum" label="Ceramium rubrum, 1843-44" caption="A hair-thin frond radiates from one holdfast and cascades into a nearly empty field, the analyzer's edge density of 0.091 the highest in the chapter, a direct measure of the specimen's own filamentous delicacy." />

Ceramium rubrum answers Himanthalia's long curves with something far finer. An ellipse 0.38 wide by 0.31 tall, centered at (0.47, 0.49), rings the whole hair-thin, filamentous frond, and a six-point growth axis traces its cascade from (0.23, 0.205) at the top down to (0.47, 0.80) near the bottom, radiating from a single holdfast into an otherwise nearly empty field. The analyzer's edge density is 0.091 here, the highest of any plate in this chapter, a direct measure of how much fine filamentous branching this one specimen packs into the frame compared to the broader-bladed plates around it. Its tonal range is the deepest in the set too, a p05 of 0.031 against a p95 of 0.79, and its palette samples a near-black of #10100e, the darkest hex sampled anywhere in this chapter. Vertical symmetry here is the second-highest measured anywhere in the set, 0.91 on an axis at x equals 0.46. A frame-in-frame at (0.06, 0.10), 0.86 wide by 0.79 tall, marks the album page itself, and the horizon the analyzer reports at y equals 0.90 lands almost exactly on that frame's own lower edge, 0.10 plus 0.79 equals 0.89, the mount boundary again standing in for a horizon that was never there.

## the single blade, centered

Two plates plant one dominant form near the middle of the page and let its own internal structure, rather than any scatter or radiation, carry the frame.

<Plate slug="anna-atkins" id="04-laminaria-digitata" label="Laminaria digitata, 1843-44" caption="A single hand-shaped kelp blade splays its finger-like lobes at top left while a long diagonal axis carries the eye down to the small holdfast anchoring the frond near center." />

Laminaria digitata is the plate's boldest, simplest silhouette. An ellipse 0.27 wide by 0.24 tall, centered at (0.525, 0.54), rings the frond's broad blade, a three-point polyline marks the splayed lobes fanning open at top left from (0.066, 0.175) to (0.162, 0.117), and a long diagonal axis runs the frond's full length from (0.123, 0.222) through the blade to (0.797, 0.756), ending at a small ellipse at (0.596, 0.75), just 0.04 by 0.03, that marks the holdfast anchoring the whole plant. The analyzer's edge density is only 0.014, the lowest in the entire chapter, matching a silhouette with almost no internal branching to draw an edge around. Its vertical symmetry is also the lowest measured here, 0.76 on an axis at x equals 0.45, because a single splayed, asymmetric blade has none of the mirrored balance the fan and thong plates build in. Tonally it sits mid-range, a mean of 0.37 between a p05 of 0.11 and a p95 of 0.79, the narrowest highlight ceiling among the single-specimen plates.

<Plate slug="anna-atkins" id="05-delesseria-sanguinea" label="Delesseria sanguinea, 1843-44" caption="Three fronds fan across a thirds grid from a rachis fork the overlay places at (0.529, 0.589), the analyzer's own raw vanishing-point coordinate for this frame reused as a botanical, not optical, reading." />

Delesseria sanguinea is the closest Atkins comes to naturalistic modeling within the silhouette method, and the overlay's own vanishing-point marker makes the point literally. Three fronds fan across a rule-of-thirds grid: an ellipse 0.20 wide by 0.14 tall at (0.24, 0.27) rings the veined blade the overlay calls out for its tonal gradation, a subject anchor at (0.36, 0.694) sits on the lower cluster's flattened, coralline-overgrown mass, and a vanishing point at (0.529, 0.589) marks the rachis fork where the blades radiate. That coordinate is not invented for the overlay. It is the analyzer's own raw vanishing-point reading for this frame, x equals 0.529, y equals 0.589 from seven converging lines, repurposed here as a description of plant anatomy rather than depth, since Atkins's fronds genuinely do fork and radiate from that point the way perspective lines would converge in a camera image. The tonal reading is unremarkable next to that structural coincidence, a mean of 0.35 with a p05 of 0.098 and a p95 of 0.86, and vertical symmetry of 0.82 on an axis at x equals 0.53, close to the chapter's typical range for a page holding more than one specimen.

## twin fronds from one root

Two late plates in the shortlist share a root-and-fan structure, one keeping it loose and one turning it sharply angular.

<Plate slug="anna-atkins" id="10-cystoseira-granulata" label="Cystoseira granulata, 1843-44" caption="Two great fronds fan almost symmetrically upward from a single root at (0.578, 0.695), while a loose lower branch running to (0.02, 0.65) breaks the mirror before it can turn mechanical." />

Cystoseira granulata is the bushiest, most symmetrical growth pattern in the shortlist, closer to a tree silhouette than a botanical specimen, and the overlay reads it as almost, but not quite, a mirror. A three-point polyline fans twin fronds from a shared root, up through (0.172, 0.112) on one side and (0.758, 0.145) on the other, meeting at (0.578, 0.695), while a second, shorter polyline runs from that same root out to (0.02, 0.65), a loose lower branch that the overlay calls out by name as the detail keeping the radial growth from reading as mechanical, and a subject anchor at (0.579, 0.448) sits on the dense tangle where the fronds knot together higher up. The analyzer measures the brightest mean tone in the whole chapter, 0.42, with the widest highlight ceiling too, a p95 of 0.92, consistent with two large, brightly exposed fronds filling most of the page. Its vanishing point, built from twenty-seven converging lines, the most of any plate here, lands at x equals 0.56, y equals 0.42, close by the shared root the overlay already marks. Vertical symmetry measures 0.87, but on an axis at x equals 0.38, well left of every other plate in this chapter, which cluster between 0.45 and 0.56, because the root sits left of center even as the two fronds fan outward in rough bilateral balance above it.

<Plate slug="anna-atkins" id="11-plocamium-coccineum" label="Plocamium coccineum, 1843-44" caption="Three fronds balance across a near-central axis at x0.5375, but the upper-left specimen's zigzagging comb-branch is the plate's clearest instance of Atkins's typographic, almost lettered patterning." />

Plocamium coccineum closes the shortlist on its most graphic note. A symmetry axis at x equals 0.5375 balances three fronds across the page, and the analyzer's own vertical symmetry confirms it almost exactly, 0.89 on an axis at x equals 0.5375, the same figure to four decimal places. An ellipse 0.165 wide by 0.185 tall at (0.26, 0.36) rings the most angular of the three, and a six-point polyline zigzags its comb-like branch back and forth between x equals 0.15 and x equals 0.235 as it climbs from y equals 0.352 to y equals 0.137, the sharpest, most pattern-like structure in the chapter, closer to lettering than to a plant. A subject anchor at (0.503, 0.653) marks the densest branching mass elsewhere in the frame. Tonally the plate reads close to Dictyota and the fan plates, a mean of 0.34 between a p05 of 0.098 and a p95 of 0.83, and ten converging lines place a vanishing point at x equals 0.47, y equals 0.60, in the lower register the subject anchor already occupies.

<Callout>
A word on process and honesty. These eleven plates are cyanotypes, not photographs of a scene: Atkins coated the paper herself with iron salts, laid a real pressed alga on it under glass, and let the sun do the rest, so the paper is negative, mask, and finished print all at once, with no film or glass negative behind any of them to reprint from. That means no aperture, shutter speed, or exposure time survives for any image in this chapter, and none is invented here. What the historical record does support is qualitative: exposure ran by direct sunlight for several minutes up to a few tens of minutes, judged by eye as the paper darkened to the correct blue before washing. It also means every surviving copy of Photographs of British Algae is its own distinct, hand-processed object, not a print run off one master, and the eleven plates gathered here all come from a single well-provenanced source, the New York Public Library's Spencer Collection copy, released as public domain and mirrored to Wikimedia Commons, so the rights basis stays as clean and singular as the process that made the pictures.
</Callout>

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Compose without a lens">
Make a photogram. Lay a leaf, a flower, or any flat, translucent object directly on a sheet of photographic or sun-sensitive paper, weight it under glass, and expose it to light. With no lens and no viewfinder to compose through, you decide the picture entirely by how you place the object on the sheet, the way Atkins decided every plate in this chapter by hand.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Build a triangle from three of one thing">
Photograph three instances of the same object, three leaves, three tools, three cups, arranged so their centers form a triangle across the frame, the way Padina Pavonia's three fan clusters and Chondrus crispus's three fronds hold the page. Try it once with the three objects identical in scale and once with one noticeably larger, and compare which reads as more stable.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Let negative space carry the frame">
Photograph a small subject placed entirely in the upper third of the frame, and commit to leaving the bottom two-thirds empty, the way Polysiphonia violacea abandons the lower half of its page to unbroken blue. Resist the instinct to fill the empty space with anything, even a caption, and notice how much weight the small subject has to carry alone.
</ExerciseCard>
