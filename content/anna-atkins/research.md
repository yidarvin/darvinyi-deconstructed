# Anna Atkins — research

Scope: technique, era, printing, and recorded camera settings for the eleven
plates in this chapter. This is direct-contact cyanotype photogram work from
the 1840s, made without a camera and with no per-frame exposure record; where
a number is not documented, this says so.

## Who and when

Anna Atkins (1799–1871) was an English botanist and illustrator, not a
studio or field photographer in the sense the other chapters in this project
use the word. Her father, John George Children, was a chemist and Fellow of
the Royal Society who encouraged her scientific training from childhood — she
supplied the engraved plates for his 1823 translation of Lamarck's work on
shells — and through him she knew Sir John Herschel and William Henry Fox
Talbot personally. She married John Pelly Atkins in 1825 and, through the
1830s, built her own herbarium of pressed British algae, joining the
Botanical Society of London as one of its few women members. When Herschel
shared his newly invented cyanotype process with her in 1842, Atkins saw it
as a solution to a problem she already had as a botanist: algae specimens are
notoriously hard to draw or engrave accurately, and a photographic silhouette
could record a specimen's exact structure with no illustrator's hand
between. Beginning in October 1843 she self-published *Photographs of
British Algae: Cyanotype Impressions* in privately produced fascicles issued
through 1853, each plate a cyanotype she exposed and processed herself and
hand-lettered with the specimen's Latin name. It is generally credited as the
first book illustrated entirely with photographic images, predating the
first installment of Talbot's *The Pencil of Nature* by about a year. Atkins
later collaborated with fellow botanist Anne Dixon on two further cyanotype
volumes on ferns and flowering plants (1853–54). She died in Kent in 1871.

## Technique

The process is the cyanotype, invented by Herschel in 1842 and given to
Atkins directly by its inventor, a family friend. Paper was hand-coated with
a solution of iron salts (ammonium ferric citrate and potassium
ferricyanide) and dried in the dark. Critically, none of this is camera
work: there is no lens, no negative in the photographic sense, and no
darkroom exposure of a scene. Atkins laid a real, pressed algae specimen
directly on the sensitized paper, weighted it under glass, and set the whole
assembly in sunlight. Sunlight struck the paper everywhere except where the
specimen's own body blocked it, so the plant acted as its own contact mask —
a photogram, not a photograph of a subject at a distance. Where the
specimen was translucent (as in the fine branching filaments of *Ceramium
rubrum* or *Polysiphonia violacea*), some light passed through and modulated
the tone, which is why a few plates in this chapter (notably *Delesseria
sanguinea*) show internal gradation rather than a flat silhouette. This is
also why the eleven images have no perspective, depth, or camera framing to
analyze — the entire compositional question is how the flat specimen is laid
out against the rectangle of the page.

## Printing

The cyanotype is a printing-out process: the paper itself is the negative,
the mask, and the final print all at once, with no intermediate glass or
film negative to hold or reprint from. Exposure to sun turned the
uncovered paper a deep Prussian blue (ferric ferrocyanide); after exposure,
Atkins washed the sheet in plain water to dissolve away the unexposed,
still-soluble iron salt under the specimen's silhouette, leaving crisp white
plant-form against blue ground, then dried and mounted the sheet. Because
each plate was produced this way one sheet at a time, with no mechanical
reproduction, every surviving copy of *Photographs of British Algae* is a
distinct set of individually hand-processed prints rather than copies of a
single master; roughly a dozen complete or near-complete copies are known
across institutions today. The eleven images in this chapter are drawn from
the New York Public Library's Spencer Collection copy, one of the
best-preserved and most completely digitized sets, with master scans mirrored
to Wikimedia Commons; other institutional copies survive at the British
Library, the Natural History Museum (London), and the Metropolitan Museum of
Art.

## Camera settings

Not applicable, and not simply unrecorded: no camera, lens, shutter, or
aperture was used to make any of these eleven images, so there is no
f-stop, shutter-speed, or sensitivity value that could ever have existed for
them, and none is stated here. What can be said is qualitative and well
documented for the cyanotype process generally: exposure ran by direct
sunlight under glass, typically for several minutes up to a few tens of
minutes depending on the strength of the sun and the specimen's opacity, with
the printer judging completion by watching the exposed paper darken to the
correct blue before washing it out. That sun-timed, watched-by-eye exposure
is the only "settings" record this era's photogram work has, and it applies
uniformly across all eleven plates in this chapter.
