Ansel Adams makes landscape feel deliberate before it feels descriptive. Across this selection, a moon can be a small fixed point against a field of black, a cliff can become a single vertical slab, and a foreground boulder or tree can keep a distant mountain from becoming scenery. The overlays name those relationships without treating every detected edge as a fact about the picture. The tonal measurements describe the normalized museum display files used here; the dates, later print dates, and acquisition rationale are in the [source notes](../../content/ansel-adams/sources.md).

## small lights, immense fields

<Plate slug="ansel-adams" id="01-moonrise-hernandez-new-mexico" label="Moonrise, Hernandez, New Mexico, 1941" caption="The moon, low mountain ridge, and village create a ladder from tiny lights to an immense dark sky." />

*Moonrise, Hernandez, New Mexico* makes the moon seem both precise and precarious. The overlay holds it as a small circle above the mountain ridge and gathers the village beneath it, so the frame reads from the cemetery and buildings through the bright cloud band to the black field overhead. The normalized file is dark overall, with mean luminance 0.264, yet it reaches p95 0.867; pale cloud, moon, and village marks can therefore carry the image without weakening the nearly black #251b1f sky.

<Plate slug="ansel-adams" id="06-moon-and-half-dome" label="Moon and Half Dome, Yosemite Valley, 1960; printed 1975/80" caption="A small moon balances Half Dome's broad vertical rise and the hard rim of its crest." />

The later *Moon and Half Dome* compresses the same lunar device into a tall confrontation. The moon and the dome crest are the overlay's only two named poles, with the detected horizontal at y0.235 acting as the rim from which the rock rises. The display rendition has a mean of 0.376 and p05 of 0.161, with #2f2d2e darks set against #e4e5e1 highlights. The source record supplies a 1960 picture date and a 1975/80 print date, but no individual camera or exposure settings.

<Plate slug="ansel-adams" id="11-moon-and-clouds-northern-california" label="Moon and Clouds, Northern California, 1959; printed 1970" caption="The moon remains a tiny hold within a low cloud band, distant hills, and a large mobile sky." />

In *Moon and Clouds, Northern California*, the moon does not command the scene by size. It survives as a tiny marked point while the low cloud band sweeps across the frame and distant hills secure the lower edge. Its edge density is only 0.0293, the quietest of this sky sequence, so the white and gray strata matter more than descriptive texture. The palette moves from #333434 through #ebede6, and the wide p05-to-p95 span, 0.180 to 0.925, accounts for the atmosphere's force.

## rock as a vertical event

<Plate slug="ansel-adams" id="03-monolith-face-of-half-dome" label="Monolith, The Face of Half Dome, Yosemite National Park, 1927; printed 1959" caption="A severe monolith edge and a snow slope pull the eye into a towering cliff rather than across an open vista." />

The overlay for *Monolith* traces the huge outer edge of Half Dome and a measured snow slope that the analyzer connects to a convergence at x0.446, y0.470. The point is a structural reading of the recession below the rock, not a claim that the whole image is ordinary linear perspective. The cliff's scale comes from that small oblique path meeting an almost featureless sky, while the print's 0.116 edge density concentrates texture in the rock rather than distributing it evenly across the frame.

<Plate slug="ansel-adams" id="10-el-capitan-sunrise" label="El Capitan, Sunrise, Yosemite National Park, 1956; printed 1959" caption="The outer cliff edge and its sun-shadow boundary raise a bright granite mass above river and trees." />

*El Capitan, Sunrise* makes light itself a contour. One overlay path follows the outer edge of the cliff, another follows the sharp division between its bright face and dark neighboring wall, and a lower ellipse keeps the river and trees in view as the scale-setting base. With mean luminance 0.468, p05 0.173, p95 0.922, and a palette spanning #373130 to #e3e4df, the file gives the illuminated granite a decisive lift without clearing the valley floor away.

<Plate slug="ansel-adams" id="12-white-house-ruin-canyon-de-chelly" label="White House Ruin, Morning, Canyon de Chelly National Monument, Arizona, 1949" caption="A small ruin and a broad canyon shadow make human architecture register against a wall of geological scale." />

The small ellipse around White House Ruin is intentionally modest: it does not make the building larger than it is. The canyon-shadow path shows how the cliff's layered surface surrounds that human mark, while the low detected foreground line keeps the bright trees and ground from floating free. The source identifies this as no. 4 in *Portfolio Two: The National Parks and Monuments*. Its display file is restrained in edge density, 0.0368, and its palette from #393738 to #e7e7df makes the wall read as a succession of large value planes.

## weather, distance, and obstruction

<Plate slug="ansel-adams" id="02-clearing-winter-storm-yosemite" label="Clearing Winter Storm, Yosemite National Park, c. 1937; printed 1959" caption="Dark valley walls bracket a luminous clearing, so weather becomes the deep center of Yosemite Valley." />

The two valley-wall paths in *Clearing Winter Storm* do not converge on a forced point; they bracket the bright clearing, whose large ellipse holds the weather at the center of the frame. The detected valley-floor line sits at y0.614 and leans slightly upward, matching the photograph's broad, uneven basin rather than a rigid horizon. Its 0.0972 edge density is the highest in this landscape group, fitting the active mixture of cliffs, snow, trees, and cloud, while #353436 darks and #dadbd8 lights preserve a long tonal interval.

<Plate slug="ansel-adams" id="08-mount-williamson-from-manzanar" label="Mount Williamson, from Manzanar, Sierra Nevada, California, c. 1944" caption="A Sierra ridge rises beyond a dominant foreground boulder, preserving the tension between blocked access and distant scale." />

*Mount Williamson, from Manzanar* refuses the easy sweep of an unobstructed panorama. The ridge path crosses the clouded Sierra, but the ellipse around the nearest boulder gives the foreground a physical interruption and a unit of scale. The file's 0.0930 edge density comes from the field of stones as much as from the mountain, and its middle gray palette, #3b393c through #e5e3dc, gives the sky's breaks in cloud an equal visual role.

<Plate slug="ansel-adams" id="05-jeffrey-pine-sentinel-dome" label="Jeffrey Pine, Sentinel Dome, Yosemite National Park, California, 1940" caption="The pine's root, trunk, and two long branches act as a living counterweight to granite distance." />

The Jeffrey pine is not foreground decoration. The root-and-trunk ellipse makes its twisted weight explicit, while the upper and lower branch paths span the pale distance like two uneven beams. The normalized display file is bright, mean 0.555 with p95 0.929, but the branch silhouette remains legible because its palette includes a dark #3a3838 against #e4e5e0 highlights. No camera or printing details for this individual work are supplied by the source record.

## abstraction through contour and repetition

<Plate slug="ansel-adams" id="07-sand-dunes-sunrise-death-valley" label="Sand Dunes, Sunrise, Death Valley National Monument, California, 1948" caption="Dark dune and distant-ridge crests counter the bright dune edge, turning terrain into a sequence of tonal planes." />

*Sand Dunes, Sunrise* is a landscape reduced to boundary after boundary. The overlay separates a distant ridge, a dark dune crest, and the brilliant lit edge at right; each contour changes the apparent depth of the next. The file has the selection's lowest edge density, 0.0197, a useful confirmation that the image works through broad gradients instead of texture. Its p05 of 0.118 and p95 of 0.929 describe the unusually severe black-white range.

<Plate slug="ansel-adams" id="04-rose-and-driftwood" label="Rose and Driftwood, San Francisco, California, c. 1932" caption="A single pale rose is held against two runs of dark, directional wood grain." />

The close study *Rose and Driftwood* uses no scenic distance at all. A single ellipse encloses the flower, while two short grain paths demonstrate why the pale petals feel set against, rather than merely placed on, the wood. Mean luminance is 0.411 and the p05-to-p95 range is 0.180 to 0.878. The gray palette from #373431 to #ddded8 makes the rose's broad curves look unusually calm beside the repeated cut and swirl of the board.

<Plate slug="ansel-adams" id="09-forest-winter-yosemite" label="Forest, Winter, Yosemite Valley, 1922/52" caption="Snow-loaded branching, a dark trunk, and a low snow opening turn the forest into a dense rhythmic screen." />

In *Forest, Winter, Yosemite Valley*, the eye travels through interruption rather than toward a single landmark. A snow-loaded branch falls through the left half, a dark trunk holds the right, and the lower snow opening gives the dense web one moment of release. Edge density reaches 0.140, the highest of the selection, because trunk, twig, snow edge, and foliage all remain active. Yet the mean is a high 0.653 and p95 reaches 0.937, so the white field is structure rather than blank background.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Make a small light carry a large field">
Photograph one small bright element against a broad dark or quiet field. Before making the frame, decide what low band, silhouette, or foreground mark will keep that small light from becoming an isolated symbol. Compare a version with the anchor present to one without it.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Use a foreground obstruction">
Make a landscape in which a near object blocks part of the distant view: a tree, rock, fence, or wall. Keep the obstruction visibly heavy, then make sure the far distance still has one clear contour or tonal break. The test is whether the near object gives scale without simply hiding the subject.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Photograph a contour sequence">
Work with a subject whose form can be read as a few connected boundaries: dunes, snow, fabric, cliffs, or a tabletop still life. Build the image around three visible contours with different tonal weights. Do not rely on texture alone; make each line change the depth or balance of the next.
</ExerciseCard>
