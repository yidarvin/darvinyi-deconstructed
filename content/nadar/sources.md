# Nadar (Félix Tournachon) — sources

Nadar built his 1854–86 portraits on a discipline nearly opposite to the ornate backdrops and painted scenery standard at rival studios (Disdéri's carte-de-visite factory chief among them): a plain, undecorated ground — often no more than a swath of grey canvas or bare wall — lit by the broad, soft north light of a glass-roofed studio and left to fall away into unretouched black at the frame's edges. Sitters are isolated in that void, usually bust- or three-quarter-length, given an informal seated or half-turned pose rather than the rigid full-frontal stance of studio convention, so that attention concentrates entirely on the face, the hands, the particular gesture that reveals character. It is the first sustained "environmental void" portrait idiom — psychological rather than documentary — and it is why Nadar's sitters (Baudelaire, Sarah Bernhardt, George Sand, Delacroix, Dumas, Doré, Liszt) still read as the defining likenesses of Second Empire Paris. Beyond the studio, Nadar was also first to attempt photography from a tethered balloon (1858, no surviving plate) and first to use artificial light underground, descending into the Paris catacombs and sewers in 1861–62 with Bunsen-battery lamps — two technically pioneering, compositionally distinct bodies of work (a plan-view aerial geometry with no traditional subject; deep-black cave compositions built around a single lit island) that belong in the same chapter as the studio portraits.

**Rights basis:** Public domain. Nadar died in 1910, so his copyright expired in France/the EU in 1980 (life+70); every image below was made between 1854 and 1886 and is also public domain in the US as a pre-1929 publication. All eleven files were sourced as "Original file" masters from Wikimedia Commons, most of them digitized from the J. Paul Getty Museum, National Gallery of Art, or Bibliothèque nationale de France (Gallica) holdings of Nadar prints/negatives.

## Shortlist

### 01 — Self-Portrait with Wife Ernestine in a Balloon Gondola (c. 1865)
- **Why it matters:** The studio-built publicity image for the "Le Géant" ballooning ventures — a woven hamper rigged as a gondola, Nadar and Ernestine framed tight within its basketwork rim, the compositional shorthand for flight without an actual sky in the frame.
- **Source:** https://www.nga.gov/artworks/110211-self-portrait-wife-ernestine-balloon-gondola (National Gallery of Art; Wikimedia master: https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Nadar,_Self-Portrait_with_Wife_Ernestine_in_a_Balloon_Gondola,_c._1865,_NGA_110211.jpg)
- **Rights:** Public domain (National Gallery of Art open access; pre-1929 work)
- **File:** raw/nadar/01-self-portrait-balloon.jpg (3357px)

### 02 — Self-Portrait (c. 1854–55)
- **Why it matters:** Nadar in a painter's smock against a bare ground, the plain-void studio signature turned on himself — the same isolating darkness and soft north light he gave his sitters, applied to the photographer as his own first subject.
- **Source:** https://www.getty.edu/art/collection/object/39021 (J. Paul Getty Museum; Wikimedia master: https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Nadar_selfportrait.jpg)
- **Rights:** Public domain (Getty Open Content Program; pre-1929 work)
- **File:** raw/nadar/02-self-portrait-studio.jpg (4144px)

### 03 — Charles Baudelaire (1855)
- **Why it matters:** The sole surviving print from Nadar's first Baudelaire sitting (the negative destroyed) — hand tucked into the waistcoat, gaze averted just off-axis, the poet's face modeled by soft studio light against total black, nothing else in the frame.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Charles_Baudelaire_1855_Nadar.jpg
- **Rights:** Public domain (pre-1929 work; digitized print, public domain mark)
- **File:** raw/nadar/03-charles-baudelaire.jpg (6627px)

### 04 — Sarah Bernhardt (negative c. 1864, print c. 1924)
- **Why it matters:** A young Bernhardt, years before her stardom, posed reclining against drapery — informal, off-vertical, the body's diagonal doing the compositional work that a rigid seated pose would not; the Getty catalogs the negative to Félix Nadar with the surviving print made later by the Nadar studio under his son Paul.
- **Source:** https://www.getty.edu/art/collection/object/1045A6 (J. Paul Getty Museum; Wikimedia master: https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Sarah_Bernhardt,_par_Nadar,_1864.jpg)
- **Rights:** Public domain (Getty Open Content Program; pre-1929 work)
- **File:** raw/nadar/04-sarah-bernhardt.jpg (3932px)

### 05 — George Sand (1864)
- **Why it matters:** Bust-length, three-quarter turn, the writer's heavy-lidded gaze meeting the lens directly while the ground falls to black — a portrait built entirely on facial modeling with no incident to compete with it.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:George_Sand_by_Nadar,_1864.jpg
- **Rights:** Public domain (pre-1929 work; digitized print, public domain mark)
- **File:** raw/nadar/05-george-sand.jpg (2800px)

### 06 — Gustave Doré (between 1856 and 1858)
- **Why it matters:** The illustrator turned sitter, hand loosely at his chin, lit so the modeling runs from bright cheekbone to deep shadow collar — a compact demonstration of Nadar's single-source chiaroscuro applied to a working artist rather than a celebrity.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Photograph_of_Gustave_Dor%C3%A9_by_Nadar,_between_1856_and_1858.jpg
- **Rights:** Public domain (pre-1929 work; digitized print, public domain mark)
- **File:** raw/nadar/06-gustave-dore.jpg (3000px)

### 07 — Franz Liszt (March 1886)
- **Why it matters:** One of the last portraits made under the Nadar name — the aged Liszt seated frontally in three-quarter light, the void behind him now doing double duty as a memorial ground; jointly credited to Félix and Paul Nadar by the Getty, made months before Liszt's death.
- **Source:** https://www.getty.edu/art/collection/objects/39130/nadar-gaspard-felix-tournachon-paul-nadar-franz-liszt-french-1886/ (J. Paul Getty Museum; Wikimedia master: https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Franz_Liszt_by_Nadar,_March_1886.png)
- **Rights:** Public domain (Getty Open Content Program; pre-1929 work)
- **File:** raw/nadar/07-franz-liszt.jpg (2292px; converted from the Commons master PNG to JPEG, no other alteration)

### 08 — Eugène Delacroix (1858)
- **Why it matters:** The only known Nadar portrait of Delacroix — who reportedly demanded the print destroyed — three-quarter length, coat and cravat dissolving into the dark ground so only the face and the folded arms hold light; a rare case of Nadar's void style applied to a famously camera-averse sitter.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:F%C3%A9lix_Nadar_1820-1910_portraits_Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix.jpg
- **Rights:** Public domain (pre-1929 work; digitized print, public domain mark)
- **File:** raw/nadar/08-eugene-delacroix.jpg (2107px)

### 09 — Alexandre Dumas père (1855)
- **Why it matters:** Dumas turned three-quarter to the lens, coat and hair filling the upper frame, the same unbroken black ground pushing all the composition's weight onto the face and the loosely draped hand.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Alexander_Dumas_p%C3%A8re_par_Nadar_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg
- **Rights:** Public domain (Google Art Project digitization; pre-1929 work)
- **File:** raw/nadar/09-alexandre-dumas.jpg (3953px)

### 10 — Aerial view of Paris, Arc de Triomphe (1868)
- **Why it matters:** Nadar's oldest surviving aerial photograph (the 1858 first attempt does not survive) — shot from the gondola of "Le Géant," it flattens the Place de l'Étoile's radiating avenues into a plan-view star, a composition possible only from directly overhead.
- **Source:** https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Nadar,_Aerial_view_of_Paris,_1868.jpg
- **Rights:** Public domain (pre-1929 work; digitized print, public domain mark)
- **File:** raw/nadar/10-aerial-view-paris.jpg (2473px)

### 11 — View in the Catacombs (1861)
- **Why it matters:** Stacked skulls and femurs recede from a single magnesium-lamp island of light into total black — the composition Nadar's underground artificial-lighting experiments made possible, and a study in framing depth with darkness rather than perspective lines.
- **Source:** http://www.getty.edu/art/collection/object/45982 (J. Paul Getty Museum; Wikimedia master: https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Nadar,_View_in_the_Catacombs_-_Getty_Museum.jpg)
- **Rights:** Public domain (Getty Open Content Program; pre-1929 work)
- **File:** raw/nadar/11-catacombs-paris.jpg (4132px)
