# Nadar (Félix Tournachon) — research

Scope: technique, era, printing, and recorded camera settings for the eleven
plates in this chapter. This is wet-collodion glass-plate work from 1854–1886,
with one artificial-light exception (the 1861–62 catacombs series), and it
carries no per-frame exposure record; where a number is not documented, this
says so.

## Who and when

Gaspard-Félix Tournachon — Nadar — was born in Paris in April 1820 and died
there in March 1910. He came to photography sideways: he made his name first
as a journalist, caricaturist, and novelist in the Paris literary-bohemian
press of the 1840s, and he was already at work on the "Panthéon Nadar," a
vast projected lithograph of caricatured French writers and public figures,
when he took up the camera in the early 1850s to help build a reference
archive of sitters' faces for the drawing project. He was self-taught in the
darkroom, learning the wet-collodion process as it was becoming the studio
standard, and he opened his own photographic studio in 1854 at 113 rue
Saint-Lazare, moving in 1860 to larger, purpose-built quarters at 35 boulevard
des Capucines. A bitter dispute over rights to the "Nadar" name, waged against
his own photographer brother Adrien Tournachon (who had worked in the
studio), was still being litigated in the mid-1850s even as Nadar's own
sitting practice — Baudelaire (03), George Sand (05), Doré (06), Delacroix
(08), Dumas (09) among them here — built the reputation the chapter's studio
plates document. Beyond the studio he pursued two separate technical
ambitions covered here: manned flight, first attempted photographically from
a tethered balloon in 1858 (no plate survives) and pursued through the 1863
giant balloon "Le Géant," with the earliest surviving aerial photograph from
his gondola work dating to 1868 (10); and photography by artificial light,
which he patented in 1861 and used to descend into the Paris catacombs and
sewers in 1861–62 (11). He died in Paris in 1910; every image in this chapter
was made in his lifetime and is public domain in both France (life+70, expired
1980) and the United States (pre-1929 publication).

## Technique

All eleven plates are wet-collodion (wet-plate) glass-negative work, the
process Frederick Scott Archer introduced in 1851 and that had become the
dominant studio and field technique by the time Nadar opened his own rooms.
A glass plate was coated with iodized collodion, sensitized in a silver
nitrate bath, exposed while still wet, and developed within minutes, which is
why every sitting — studio or expedition — required a darkroom within steps
of the camera. The studio portraits (02–09) were made in Nadar's glass-roofed
rooms under broad, soft, north-facing skylight, with the sitter held still on
a stand camera against a bare backdrop; no headrest brace is visible in these
plates the way it often is in contemporary American studio work, consistent
with Nadar's preference for a more relaxed, conversational sitting. The two
technical outliers are documented separately: the catacombs series (11) used
no daylight at all, but the electric arc light Nadar patented in 1861 —
a Bunsen battery bank (as many as fifty cells wired in series) feeding an
electric regulator built with the engineer Victor Serrin — because sunlight
cannot reach an underground gallery; contemporary accounts of the project put
individual exposures at as long as eighteen minutes even with that artificial
source. The aerial plate (10) and the earlier, non-surviving 1858 attempt
required Nadar to sensitize, expose, and develop a wet plate in a portable
darkroom rigged into the balloon gondola itself, working against wind, motion,
and a strict window before the collodion dried.

## Printing

Nadar's studio produced both of the two major printing methods of the period
across the run of his career: his earliest 1850s prints, including the sole
surviving Baudelaire print (03), were varnished salted-paper prints made by
contact from the glass negative, while his later work follows the industry's
shift to albumen paper (egg-white-bound silver salts), the contact-printing
standard from the mid-1850s through the 1890s. There being no enlargers in
this era, every print here is a same-size contact print from its glass
negative. Surviving material for this chapter is scattered across
institutions rather than held as a single archive: the National Gallery of
Art (01), the J. Paul Getty Museum (02, 04, 07, 11), and Wikimedia-hosted
digitizations of prints held at other institutions — including a Google Art
Project scan (09) — account for the rest; several of the later Nadar-studio
sittings (07, and to some degree 04) are catalogued jointly to Félix and his
son Paul Nadar, who ran the family studio's later years and whose own prints
from his father's negatives sometimes postdate the sitting by decades, as
with the Bernhardt negative (c. 1864) printed around 1924.

## Camera settings

Not recorded. Wet-collodion photography of this era predates any per-frame
aperture, shutter-speed, or sensitivity log, and there was no exposure meter
on set or in the field; the operator judged exposure by experience with a
given plate's freshness, the light, and the lens. What can be said is
qualitative and specific to each body of work: the studio portraits (02–09)
required the sitter to hold a pose for several seconds under strong but soft
skylight; the catacombs plate (11) is the one case in this chapter with a
documented exposure figure at all, contemporary accounts giving individual
exposures of up to eighteen minutes under artificial arc light, which is a
statement about that project's process, not a per-frame camera setting; and
the aerial and balloon-gondola work (01, 10) demanded a compressed
sensitize-expose-develop cycle timed to the wet plate's working window, with
no further specifics on record. No f-stop, shutter speed, or sensitivity
value survives for any of the eleven images, and none is invented here.
