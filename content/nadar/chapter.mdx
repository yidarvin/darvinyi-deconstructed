Nadar ran his studio on a discipline nearly opposite the ornate, painted-backdrop convention of his Second Empire rivals: strip the set down to a bare wall or a swath of grey canvas, light it with the broad, soft north light of a glass-roofed room, and let the ground fall away into unretouched black at the frame's edges. The overlays in this chapter keep finding the same signature underneath that discipline, a vertical symmetry axis that the analyzer's own raw measurement confirms plate after plate, sometimes to three decimal places, even when a sitter breaks it with a turned pose or an averted gaze. Nadar was willing to bend that symmetry when a sitter demanded it, building a hard diagonal instead of a mirror for Sarah Bernhardt and Gustave Doré, and he was willing to abandon the studio altogether for two technically reckless experiments, a balloon gondola and the black of the Paris catacombs, that forced him to build depth out of light alone rather than any line the camera could trace on its own.

## the photographer in his own frame

Open with the picture least like the rest of the sitting practice: a publicity still for the "Le Géant" balloon ventures, not a portrait sitting at all. The overlay frames two nested rectangles, a painted sky backdrop whose own edge shows near the frame's corner (a frame within a frame at x equals 0.06, y equals 0.02, sized 0.89 by 0.88) and, inside it, a second, tighter frame at x equals 0.354, y equals 0.588 marking the basketwork rim that pens Nadar and his wife Ernestine into the gondola's weave. A subject anchor rings Ernestine's face, at x equals 0.591, y equals 0.394, "ringed by suspension ropes," and a vertical symmetry axis at x equals 0.438 runs through the rigging, close enough to the analyzer's own raw measurement of 0.4375 that the two numbers might as well be the same line: the picture's vertical symmetry actually measures 0.86, one of the highest in the whole chapter, even though the overlay's own label calls out "the uneven pair" that axis bisects, since Nadar and Ernestine themselves are no mirror at all. The analyzer also flags a horizon at y equals 0.19 with full confidence, high in the frame, the seam where the painted sky meets the backdrop's own canvas, a border no cloud ever crossed. Tonally the print runs warm and bright, a mean of 0.60 with a wide reach from a p05 of 0.26 up to a p95 of 0.85, the palette running from a deep #574e42 up to a near-white #fefdfd. It is Nadar the showman staging flight inside a studio, the geometry doing exactly what a stage set's geometry should.

Turn the same discipline on himself and the theatrics disappear. Self-Portrait pins Nadar's own face, in a painter's smock, against the same void he built for every sitter who followed him. The overlay's ellipse at cx equals 0.445, cy equals 0.34 models the face in soft light, and a short polyline traces the raised hand bracing it, rising from (0.304, 0.494) through (0.28, 0.419) to (0.246, 0.356). A vertical symmetry axis at 0.4375, the "void backdrop's centering axis," lands on the identical figure the analyzer measures independently, 0.4375, with a vertical symmetry score of 0.80. The tonal signature is the darkest and flattest print in the chapter outside the two expeditions, a mean of just 0.368 with a narrow standard deviation of 0.095, and an edge density of only 0.0058, the lowest of any of the eleven plates, because there is almost nothing in the frame to draw an edge except one hand, one collar, and one face. The subject anchor at x equals 0.528, y equals 0.507 falls on the starched collar, called out as "the frame's one hard edge," the single crisp passage in a print otherwise built entirely from soft transition.

## the face against nothing

Baudelaire's is the purest case of the void portrait. Made at Nadar's first sitting with the poet, the negative later destroyed so only this one print survives, the overlay sets a rule-of-thirds grid and centers a bare vertical symmetry axis at x equals 0.5 exactly, "figure centered in the void," while a subject anchor holds the face at x equals 0.483, y equals 0.456. The analyzer confirms real symmetry on that same axis, a looser 0.71, and a horizontal symmetry of 0.77 on an axis at y equals 0.55. Two small departures carry the whole psychological charge: a polyline running the gaze off from (0.50, 0.35) out to (0.12, 0.48), so Baudelaire looks past the camera rather than into it, and an ellipse at (0.26, 0.705) marking a hand tucked out of sight into the coat rather than posed. The analyzer also turns up a horizon at y equals 0.82, though at a lower confidence of 0.85, most likely the coat's own hem rather than any real horizon, since nothing in the frame sits further back than Baudelaire's shoulders. Tonally this is the widest-ranging print in the chapter, a mean of 0.42 but a standard deviation of 0.29, reaching from a near-black p05 of 0.031 to a near-white p95 of 0.94, the palette running the full scale from #161616 to #e3e3e3. Nothing here is built to be noticed except the face and the averted eyes. The axis and the void are simply the ground they stand on.

George Sand gets the same void with the turn reversed. The overlay's ellipse at cx equals 0.50, cy equals 0.27 models a head held against plain ground, and the subject anchor lands directly on the "heavy-lidded gaze, meeting the lens" at x equals 0.52, y equals 0.255, even though the body itself has swung three-quarter into a near-symmetric wrap. That wrap is read as its own long polyline, five points running from (0.02, 0.68) almost level across to (0.99, 0.72), a plain striped mass built to fill the lower two-thirds of the frame with nothing incident in it. A vertical symmetry axis at 0.4625 marks the drape's own near-mirror, matched exactly by the analyzer's own raw axis at 0.4625, though the overall vertical symmetry score here is lower, just 0.60, the lowest of any void portrait in the chapter, precisely because the turned pose breaks the strict frontal mirror the axis is drawn on. The analyzer's own line-finder independently turns up a vanishing point near that same lower register, at x equals 0.37, y equals 0.83, with seventeen separate diagonals converging on it, second only to a sleeve fold later in this chapter, evidence that the wrap's own folds really do converge geometrically and not just as a compositional reading. The busiest ground of any portrait here backs up that texture: edge density measures 0.128, more than twenty times the self-portrait's own. Tonally it sits mid-register, a mean of 0.38 with a spread from p05 0.13 to p95 0.70, palette running #272727 to #bababa. The face still does all the work. The turned body and the busy drape are simply what Nadar lets fall away from it.

## the diagonal breaks the mirror

Not every sitting holds the frontal mirror. Bernhardt, photographed years before her stardom, reclines against drapery, and the overlay reads her not on a vertical axis at all but on a diagonal: a polyline runs from her jaw at (0.44, 0.335) down through (0.53, 0.52) to the bare shoulder at (0.83, 0.54), doing the compositional work a squared seated pose would otherwise do. The subject anchor sits high on her face, at x equals 0.405, y equals 0.288, and a wide ellipse at cx equals 0.545, cy equals 0.26 gathers the loose hair mass above it. The overlay also marks a vanishing point at x equals 0.356, y equals 0.622, where the velvet's own folds are said to converge, and the analyzer finds the same point independently, to three decimal places, x equals 0.356, y equals 0.622, from twelve separately converging diagonals in the fabric. The frame's own vertical symmetry measures 0.78, but on an axis set well off center, at x equals 0.625, since it is the diagonal, not a mirror, doing the compositional work here. The print is the darkest of the studio portraits, a mean of just 0.23 with p05 at 0.09 and p95 at only 0.45, a tight, low-key palette from #1e1e1e up to a pale #d2d2d2.

Doré gets the same permission to break the frame's own symmetry. A vertical axis at x equals 0.5, the "plain backdrop's mirror axis," runs straight down the middle of the ground behind him, and the analyzer's own raw measurement agrees closely, a vertical symmetry of 0.83 on that same line. But the sitter refuses it: the subject anchor at x equals 0.471, y equals 0.43 catches the face meeting the knot of his scarf, a polyline sends his gaze out of frame, from (0.44, 0.25) to (0.04, 0.32), and a leading line cuts the one bold diagonal in the picture, the checked scarf dropping from (0.566, 0.394) down to (0.663, 0.755). Tonally it runs warmer than the void portraits around it, a mean of 0.44 with p05 at 0.25 and p95 at 0.88, a palette of browns and tans from #55453c to a pale #edddbf. The backdrop offers Doré the same bare, symmetric void as every other sitter. What makes this plate his own is that he, and the scarf, are the only things in it declining to hold still on the axis.

## two lit passages in the dark

By 1886 the discipline has narrowed to almost nothing but light. Liszt sat for one of the last portraits to carry the Nadar name, credited jointly to Félix and his son Paul, and the overlay's wide ellipse, centered at cx equals 0.475, cy equals 0.41 and sized 0.305 by 0.27, fuses his pale mane and face into one lit mass. A vanishing point at x equals 0.513, y equals 0.348 marks the eyes, "every crease converges here," and the analyzer's own line-finder locates the identical point independently, from ten converging diagonals in the wrinkles and hair. Below it a small subject anchor at x equals 0.563, y equals 0.57 picks out the collar as "the last bright note" before the coat and the darkening backdrop close around it. The analyzer's own measured symmetry axis for this plate sits well left of center, at x equals 0.375, with a vertical symmetry of 0.79, since Liszt is seated off the frame's own middle inside the ellipse the overlay actually draws. Tonally the print runs dark and wide, a mean of 0.35 with a standard deviation of 0.23, reaching from p05 0.094 up to p95 0.76, the palette darkest of any portrait at its low end, #25201b, up to a pale #cac1b8.

Delacroix, in the only Nadar portrait of him and one its sitter reportedly wanted destroyed, is built on exactly two lit passages and nothing else. An ellipse at cx equals 0.51, cy equals 0.265 rings the face, "the only fully lit passage," and a second subject anchor at x equals 0.394, y equals 0.536 finds the shirt front, "second lit note," the coat and the void around both dissolved to black. A polyline sends the gaze averted from (0.47, 0.215) out to (0.08, 0.335), off the lens entirely. The analyzer finds a real vertical symmetry here too, 0.87, the highest of any portrait in the chapter, on an axis at x equals 0.512, almost exactly where the overlay's own ellipse is centered. Tonally the print is dark and wide-ranging, a mean of 0.38 with the widest standard deviation of any portrait here, 0.24, from a near-black p05 of 0.098 to a bright p95 of 0.78, the palette running from #212121 to #cacaca. Two lit shapes, a real symmetric ground, and a sitter's eyes refusing to meet the lens: the void portrait pushed as far toward abstraction as Nadar took it.

Dumas gets the same two-passage economy with a third element folded in. The overlay's ellipse at cx equals 0.49, cy equals 0.205 lets his unruly hair fill the frame's own crown, and a subject anchor at x equals 0.488, y equals 0.305 marks the face as "the only fully lit passage," while a second at x equals 0.605, y equals 0.68 finds the hand tucked into its draped cuff. A four-point polyline, from (0.179, 0.779) through (0.321, 0.644) and (0.494, 0.681) to (0.601, 0.725), traces the sleeve's own fold carrying the eye down to that hand, and the analyzer's own line-finder confirms real convergence along that same fold, a vanishing point at x equals 0.528, y equals 0.376 built from twenty-two converging diagonals, the most of any plate in this chapter. The frame's own vertical symmetry measures 0.74, on an axis at x equals 0.4625, just off the void's exact center in keeping with the head's slight turn. Tonally this is the darkest studio portrait here, a mean of just 0.20 with p05 at 0.063 and p95 at only 0.47, a cool, low palette from #191518 up through #887f77 to a pale #cbc7bb. The void does not just isolate Dumas's face. By the time the eye has followed the coat down to the hand, it has traced almost the entire lit surface of the print.

## beyond the studio

Twice Nadar left the studio behind entirely to chase a technical first, and the geometry had to be rebuilt from nothing. Shot from the gondola of "Le Géant" in 1868, the oldest of his aerial photographs to survive, the Place de l'Étoile flattens into a plan-view star the overlay reads as two separate leading lines, one radiating boulevard rising from (0.734, 0.66) to (0.604, 0.165) and a second, shorter one from (0.5, 0.348) to (0.581, 0.271), both aimed at a frame within a frame boxing the Arc de Triomphe itself at x equals 0.556, y equals 0.025. It is by far the widest plate in the chapter, an aspect of 2.64, and the tonal range is severe, a mean of 0.37 with a standard deviation of 0.27, p05 at 0.035 and p95 at 0.85, an edge density of 0.18, the highest in the whole chapter, all of it the fine grain of rooftops and avenues seen from height. A horizon sits low, at y equals 0.87 with 0.86 confidence, the join between building rooftops and the pale sky above the boulevards' vanishing distance. The analyzer's own automatic search for a vanishing point, given only two confidently converging lines at just 0.4 confidence, lands far from the Arc entirely, at x equals 0.10, y equals 0.49, and its overall left-right symmetry measures a low 0.59, unsurprising for a photograph whose real subject is one radiating star of avenues rather than a mirrored facade. It is a reminder that the overlay's own hand-drawn convergence on the Arc is doing real interpretive work no automatic line-finder alone would have found.

The other experiment goes underground. In the catacombs, lit by nothing but the Bunsen-battery arc lamp Nadar patented in 1861, the overlay abandons converging lines for a chain of light and dark: a subject anchor at x equals 0.322, y equals 0.727 holds the stacked skulls in the near ground, a leading line runs the ossuary wall's own edge back from (0.398, 0.824) to (0.281, 0.341), a second, smaller anchor at x equals 0.641, y equals 0.559 catches a single pier lit by the lamp, and a frame within a frame at x equals 0.665, y equals 0.33 boxes the bone gallery fading to black beyond it. The analyzer's own vertical symmetry measures 0.79 on an axis at x equals 0.625, and its horizon sits at y equals 0.71 with full confidence, the ossuary floor reading as a clean luminance split even in near-total dark. Tonally the print is the most legible of the two expeditions, a mean of 0.55 with p05 at 0.23 and p95 at 0.85, a warm palette of bone and shadow from #543c2f up to #dfcfad. No sun reached this frame at all. Depth here is built entirely by where the light happened to fall, not by any line the camera could have drawn on its own.

<Callout>
On process and honesty about settings. All eleven plates are wet-collodion glass-negative work, sensitized, exposed, and developed within minutes in a darkroom kept within steps of the camera, whether that camera sat in Nadar's glass-roofed studio on the rue Saint-Lazare and later the boulevard des Capucines, rode in the gondola of a balloon, or was lowered into the Paris catacombs. No per-frame aperture, shutter speed, or plate speed survives for any of the eleven images, and none is invented here. The one documented exposure figure in the whole chapter belongs to the catacombs series, where contemporary accounts put individual exposures at up to eighteen minutes under the arc light Nadar patented in 1861, a statement about that project's process, not a camera setting. The earliest print here, the sole surviving Baudelaire sitting, is a varnished salted-paper contact print, while the later sittings follow the era's shift to albumen paper, still a same-size contact print from the glass negative since no enlarger yet existed. Several of the later negatives, including the Liszt and Bernhardt sittings, are catalogued jointly to Félix Nadar and his son Paul, whose own studio prints from his father's negatives sometimes postdate the sitting by decades, as with the Bernhardt print made around 1924 from a negative sixty years older. All eleven images are public domain, in France under the standard life-plus-seventy term that expired in 1980, and in the United States as pre-1929 publications.
</Callout>

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Build the void, then find the one hard edge">
Photograph a sitter against the plainest ground you can find, lit from one broad, soft source, with everything but the face and one other detail allowed to sink into shadow. Choose in advance which single element stays crisp, a collar, a hand, a piece of jewelry, the way the starched collar is the only hard edge in Nadar's own self-portrait. Check afterward whether your eye still finds that one detail first.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Break your own mirror">
Set up a plain, symmetric backdrop and center your camera on it exactly. Then pose your subject off that center, on a diagonal, a turned shoulder, or a gaze cast out of frame, the way Bernhardt's jaw-to-shoulder line and Doré's averted eyes both refuse the very symmetry the backdrop offers. Shoot one frame with the sitter squared to the mirror and one broken from it, and compare which one holds more tension.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Chain the light instead of the line">
Find a dark space, a stairwell, a garage, an unlit room, and light it with a single small source rather than the room's own fixtures. Compose so the eye travels from one lit passage to the next, a near object, a mid-distance object, a far one fading to black, the way the catacombs picture builds depth from a lit skull wall to a single lamp-struck pier to a gallery that simply goes dark. Do not use a single converging line anywhere in the frame.
</ExerciseCard>
