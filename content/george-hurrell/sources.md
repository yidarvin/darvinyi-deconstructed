# George Hurrell — source shortlist

All ten files below were acquired from Wikimedia Commons' public file service
on 2026-07-18 and verified to open locally. Commons records each as public
domain (either a pre-1978 publicity/publication image with no copyright notice
or a publication whose copyright was not renewed). Credit is retained here even
where Commons does not require attribution. The supplied 1933 Jean Harlow PNG
was converted to JPEG solely to meet the raw-file naming convention; its framing
and image content were not altered.

## Acquired photographs

1. **Bette Davis**, *Photoplay*, October 1938 — `01-bette-davis.jpg` (1665 × 2345).
   The steeply modeled head-and-shoulders arrangement is a concise example of
   Hurrell's key-light/shadow design and sculptural eye line. [Commons file
   record](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Bette_Davis_by_George_Hurrell,_Photoplay_October_1938.jpg).
   **Use basis: open.** Commons identifies George Hurrell as author, credits
   *Photoplay* (October 1938), and marks the work Public domain / not
   copyrighted; credit: George Hurrell, *Photoplay*.

2. **Conrad Nagel**, *The New Movie*, July 1930 — `02-conrad-nagel.jpg` (2406 × 3391).
   A tight head-and-shoulders portrait whose hair-part highlight, face, and
   shoulder mass make a controlled tonal construction. [Commons file
   record](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Conrad_Nagel_by_Hurrell.jpg).
   **Use basis: open.** Commons identifies George Hurrell as author, credits
   the digitized July 1930 *New Movie* issue at Internet Archive, and marks it Public
   domain / not copyrighted; credit: George Hurrell, *The New Movie*.

3. **Rita Hayworth**, *The American Magazine*, December 1942 — `03-rita-hayworth.jpg` (2000 × 4964).
   Its extraordinary vertical figure creates a useful lesson in how a long
   frame, dress contour, and bright face organize glamour without a busy set.
   [Commons file record](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Hayworth-American-1942.jpg).
   **Use basis: open.** Commons credits Crowell-Collier Publishing Company and
   George Hurrell, identifies the December 1942 magazine scan, and marks it
   Public domain / not copyrighted; credit: George Hurrell, *The American
   Magazine*.

4. **Jean Harlow**, 1933 — `04-jean-harlow.jpg` (1018 × 1339).
   Harlow's luminous face, soft halo, and diagonal shoulder form make the
   negative space feel theatrical rather than empty. [Commons file
   record](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Jean_Harlow_by_George_Hurrell_1933.png).
   **Use basis: open.** Commons identifies George Hurrell as author and records
   the publicity photograph as public domain for absence of a required copyright
   notice; credit: George Hurrell, Jean Harlow publicity portrait (1933).

5. **Jean Parker**, 1935 — `05-jean-parker.jpg` (677 × 881).
   The small, legible portrait shows a restrained three-quarter pose and facial
   highlight pattern that is useful for studying subject anchoring. [Commons file
   record](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Jean_Parker_(1935)_by_George_Hurrell.jpg).
   **Use basis: open.** Commons identifies George Hurrell as author, describes
   it as a 1935 publicity photograph, and marks it Public domain / not
   copyrighted; credit: George Hurrell, Jean Parker publicity photograph.

6. **Joan Crawford**, 1936 — `06-joan-crawford.jpg` (1070 × 1420).
   Her turned shoulder and direct glance demonstrate how a portrait can set up
   tension between profile, frontal gaze, and an enveloping black ground.
   [Commons file record](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Joan_Crawford_-_1936_-_Hurrell.JPG).
   **Use basis: open.** Commons identifies George Hurrell as author and lists
   the image under “PD US no notice”; credit: George Hurrell, Joan Crawford
   publicity photograph (1936).

7. **Karen Morley**, 1930s — `07-karen-morley.jpg` (802 × 994).
   The studio still makes a compact case study in rim light: its bright outline
   separates Morley's hair and shoulder from a near-black field. [Commons file
   record](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Karen_Morley_by_George_Hurrell,_1930s.jpg).
   **Use basis: open.** Commons identifies George Hurrell as author, credits an
   MGM still from the Marvin Paige Collection, and marks it Public domain / not
   copyrighted; credit: George Hurrell, MGM publicity still.

8. **Carole Lombard**, c. 1937 — `08-carole-lombard.jpg` (1234 × 1558).
   Lombard's repeated arcs of hair, neck, and sleeve reveal how Hurrell used
   curves to carry the eye through a closely cropped vertical composition.
   [Commons file record](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Lombard_by_Hurrell.jpg).
   **Use basis: open.** Commons identifies George Hurrell as author, dates the
   portrait c. 1937, and marks it Public domain / not copyrighted; credit:
   George Hurrell, Carole Lombard portrait.

9. **Myrna Loy**, undated — `09-myrna-loy.jpg` (2000 × 2632).
   The high-resolution publicity portrait lets the chapter isolate the quiet
   relation between a softly lit face, dark hair mass, and open background.
   [Commons file record](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Myrna_Loy.jpg).
   **Use basis: open.** Commons identifies George Hurrell as author and lists
   the work under “PD US no notice”; credit: George Hurrell, Myrna Loy publicity
   portrait.

10. **Olivia de Havilland**, *Photoplay*, August 1938 — `10-olivia-de-havilland.jpg` (839 × 1037).
    The image's bright cheek and shoulder triangle show how a single highlight
    can lock a pose into a stable, glamorous pyramid. [Commons file
    record](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Olivia_de_Havilland_by_George_Hurrell,_Aug_1938.jpg).
    **Use basis: open.** Commons identifies George Hurrell as author, credits
    *Photoplay* (August 1938), and marks it Public domain / not copyrighted;
    credit: George Hurrell, *Photoplay*.

## Selection notes

The source pass prioritized complete-frame, 1930s–42 studio/publicity portraits
that can support later composition analysis rather than collecting redundant
headshots. The ten-file set exceeds this photographer's required minimum of four
and contains no fair-use acquisition.
