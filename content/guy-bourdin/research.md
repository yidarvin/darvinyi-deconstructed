# Guy Bourdin — research notes

## Scope and provenance

Guy Bourdin (1928–1991) is represented here by twelve 1000px-long-edge display renditions from the Guy Bourdin Estate's public artwork page. The sequence includes undated Charles Jourdan campaign pictures, Charles Jourdan Spring 1978, French *Vogue* work from 1969, 1972, 1976–77, and 1977, a 1980 Pentax Calendar image, and *Vogue France* from 1983. `sources.md` preserves the links, credit, copyright notice, and image-specific fair-use rationale. MoMA's artist record corroborates the period and the French *Vogue* May 1977 and Charles Jourdan Spring 1978 series.

## What the available record supports

The Estate identifies itself as copyright holder and says modern Estate prints are numbered, stamped, and signed by Samuel Bourdin. That is the only printing information supplied for this chapter; it does not establish the printing process of any original work. The supplied images visibly support discussion of constructed fashion and editorial scenes: held photographs, extreme crops, mirrors, objects, hard colour fields, repeated forms, and deliberate product-to-figure scale shifts.

No available record identifies a camera body, lens, aperture, shutter speed, film stock, negative process, original paper, lighting diagram, or exposure setting for these individual photographs. The ingested JPEGs contain no EXIF. Observations about contrast, colour, or the appearance of illumination therefore describe these web reproductions only, not undocumented production settings or original prints.

Palette representatives reinforce the compositional contrasts visible in the display images: the sparse crop includes yellows such as `#dcb148` and red-brown `#8d150d`; the umbrella image is overwhelmingly near-black and blue-black, with a small pink `#cb9799` accent; the 1977 mirror scene pairs deep greens such as `#1a3135` and `#305c51` with red `#b30408`.

## Compositional throughline

Bourdin makes fashion images read as staged propositions rather than transparent displays. A held print changes scale and withholds a body; a red shoe dwarfs a second image; umbrellas build a field from which one eye appears; mirrors and flowers multiply a figure without stabilizing it. The overlay set names those visible relations, while declining false spatial claims produced by texture detectors in the glitter close-up and grass scene.
