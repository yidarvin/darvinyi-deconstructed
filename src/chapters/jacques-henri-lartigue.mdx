Jacques-Henri Lartigue's early pictures make speed feel less like a subject than a way of arranging a frame. A stair, a beach, a glider, a tire, a racing car, and an escalator each become a device for giving bodies a direction or for holding them against a much larger field. The overlays stay literal: a rail, ridge, horizon, tire ring, wheel, shopfront band, or figure. They make no claim that an incidental tonal seam is a horizon or that every diagonal is perspective. ## bodies in a moving frame

<Plate slug="jacques-henri-lartigue" id="01-bichonnade-in-flight" label="Cousin “Bichonnade” in Flight, 1905; printed 1972" caption="A stair rail rises through the frame while Bichonnade’s face and falling dress turn a descending movement into an improbable suspended pose." />

In *Cousin “Bichonnade” in Flight*, the rail gives the fall an upward counterforce. The face sits near the upper phi point while the dress breaks from torso to rightward hem, so the figure seems briefly detached from the steps beneath. With p05 0.125 and p95 0.882, the dark clothes separate crisply from the pale stone; edge density 0.109 is the chapter's most active, fitting the brickwork, balustrade, dress, and stair treads that keep the frozen instant unstable.

<Plate slug="jacques-henri-lartigue" id="02-simone-roussel-beach" label="Simone Roussel on the Beach at Villerville, 1906; printed 1972" caption="A low sea horizon holds Simone and a dog against an exceptionally open beach field." />

*Simone Roussel on the Beach at Villerville* makes restraint its action. The measured sea horizon at y=0.422 runs almost level behind Simone, while the dog provides the dark, central counterweight and the far wader keeps the space from closing. The mean luminance is 0.835 and edge density only 0.010; cream #eeeee4 and the dog's dark #62594b make the few interruptions unusually consequential.

<Plate slug="jacques-henri-lartigue" id="03-zissou-glider" label="My Brother, Zissou, Gets His Glider Airborne, Château de Rouzat, October 1908; printed 1972" caption="The glider wings and the ridge line hold a small crew between a large blank sky and the grass slope." />

The glider's stacked wings in *Zissou Gets His Glider Airborne* form a small angled construction above the ridge, with the handlers gathered at its bottom edge. That is a picture of lift built out of contrast in scale: nearly blank sky occupies most of the frame, while the grass and figures compress below y=0.672. The analyzer's edge density of 0.009 and p05–p95 span of 0.400–0.941 explain the spare, cutout-like clarity.

## apparatus and velocity

<Plate slug="jacques-henri-lartigue" id="04-race-course-auteuil" label="The Race Course at Auteuil, Paris, 1910; printed 1972" caption="Three oversized hats form an upper rhythm while a striped foreground figure holds the frame’s right edge." />

At Auteuil, motion is withheld rather than shown directly. The tall striped figure fills the right third, its hem marked as the image's lowest high-contrast endpoint; the three hats make a sloping upper cadence across the rail. A tonal span from p05 0.180 to p95 0.922 and edge density 0.059 carry the collision of striped cloth, lace, chairs, and the dark course beyond.

<Plate slug="jacques-henri-lartigue" id="05-woman-fox-fur" label="Woman with Fox Fur, Avenue des Acacias, 1911; printed 1972" caption="A dark upright woman and her dogs are tied together by a curved lead across pale pavement." />

The street photograph uses a curved, visible link to make a large relation. The lead drops from the woman’s hand, bows through the lower middle of the frame, and meets the dog pair, while the cars recede as secondary black forms. Her furred silhouette has the force of an anchor because it reaches p05 0.110 against a pale boulevard whose palette begins at #ebe4da; the low 0.016 edge density makes the few dark contours count.

<Plate slug="jacques-henri-lartigue" id="06-zissou-tire-boat" label="Zissou in his Tire Boat, Château de Rouzat, 1911; printed 1972" caption="The tire ring makes an internal frame around a seated figure on a centered vertical axis." />

*Zissou in his Tire Boat* turns play into a frame-within-a-frame problem. The oval tire contains the seated figure but leaves the surrounding water active on all sides; the centered axis is a stabilizer, not a claim that the water is symmetrical. Edge density 0.082, second only to the stair picture here, comes from the moving water and the tire's textured surface, while #ecebe1 flashes against the brown water palette.

<Plate slug="jacques-henri-lartigue" id="07-wheeled-bobsleigh" label="Wheeled Bobsleigh Designed by Jacques Henri Lartigue, 1911; printed 1972" caption="The driver, front wheel, and low chassis make the improvised vehicle cut across a deliberately blurred gravel foreground." />

The bobsleigh is photographed low enough that the gravel becomes a large, soft plane and the machine seems to skim its edge. The overlay follows the chassis only as a visible contour, with the near wheel and driver's face making the machine-human unit legible. Its p05–p95 range of 0.204–0.945 is broad, and edge density 0.074 records the contrast between sharp wheel spokes and an intentionally unfocused ground.

<Plate slug="jacques-henri-lartigue" id="08-grand-prix-dieppe" label="Grand Prix of the Automobile Club of France, Course at Dieppe, 1912; printed 1972" caption="A close front wheel and the racing car sit in a measured convergence field, while the blurred crowd makes speed visible at the frame’s edge." />

In *Grand Prix*, the very large wheel is not merely a detail; it makes the car occupy the foreground before the viewer can settle into a conventional view of the track. The analyzer finds a 20-inlier convergence at (0.665, 0.409), but the overlay names it only as a speed field, not as a literal road horizon. The car's bright body, dark wheel, and pale surroundings span p05 0.278 to p95 0.941 with a modest 0.018 edge density: blur suppresses detail so that leaning mass and wheel dominate.

## staging a city

<Plate slug="jacques-henri-lartigue" id="09-rowe-twins" label="The Famous Rowe Twins of the Casino de Paris, 1929; printed 1972" caption="The twin pair forms a near-symmetrical portrait, interrupted by the automobile’s large diagonal hood." />

The Rowe Twins present themselves as a pair, but Lartigue does not leave the portrait sealed. The twin oval names their staged grouping while the automobile’s hood rises from the lower right and cuts across it. The overlay follows that visible hood edge rather than claiming a false center between the sitters. p05 0.208 to p95 0.957 and the #f5efe6-to-#41402a palette give the bright hood enough force to make that interruption structural.

<Plate slug="jacques-henri-lartigue" id="10-bibi-london" label="Bibi, London, October 1926" caption="Bibi is held in the escalator opening while a shallow, angled shopfront band and street corridor pull the eye through the unusually wide frame." />

*Bibi, London* turns its wide proportion into a directional argument. The dark escalator mass occupies the left, Bibi appears in its opening, and the bright shopfronts and pedestrians recede through the right half. The measured shopfront break tilts only 2.8 degrees, enough to prevent the horizontal strip from becoming static. Its mean luminance is 0.430, lowest in the selection; dark #403d36 and light #f0f0e8 make the image a deep urban aperture rather than an empty panorama.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Make one direction do the work">
Photograph a figure beside a stair, rail, curb, or escalator. Let one visible structural edge set the direction, then remove every other competing diagonal from the frame.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Build a figure and field">
Make an image in which a small person or object is held against a large, low-detail field. Test a version with the horizon or ridge low and another with it high; compare where the subject gains force.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Use a machine as a frame">
Photograph a wheel, tire, window, or other enclosing shape with a person partially inside it. Keep enough surrounding space that the internal frame reads as a relation, not a crop.
</ExerciseCard>
