Carleton Watkins made the western survey photograph feel less like a view than a constructed place to stand. The nine plates here move from Yosemite's enormous walls to a stereocard, a mill town, a railroad cut, and a smelting works, but they keep returning to the same proposition: the frame needs a hard near fact before distance can become legible. A pine at the edge, a print mount, an oval aperture, a rail, a bridge, a tree trunk. The overlays follow those physical devices rather than treating every detector result as evidence. The analyzer can find an apparent symmetry in a valley or an apparent horizon in a caption strip; those are measurements to test, not claims to repeat. Where the geometry is real, though, Watkins gives the eye a route through scale that is almost architectural.

## a valley held by one tree

In *Yosemite Valley from the Best General View* the right-edge pine is not a decorative foreground. The overlay anchors its crown near x0.73, y0.13, and draws the two cliff walls down toward the waterfall in the central distance. The measured ridge/sky break sits at y0.288, exactly where the overlay's distant-ridge line runs. It is a huge scene made readable by a sequence of decreasing facts: pine, cliff, ravine, fall, mountain. Watkins even added the clouds from a second negative, a documented construction that makes the open upper field as deliberate as the dark foreground.

<Plate slug="carleton-watkins" id="01-yosemite-valley-best-general-view" label="Yosemite Valley from the Best General View, 1866" caption="The pine fixes the right edge while left and right cliff contours descend toward the distant waterfall below a ridge measured at y0.288." />

The analyzer records a restrained midtone mean of 0.47 and an edge density of 0.043. Its warm palette moves from #dac3a0 through brown-violet #483932. That quiet, hazed middle is the point: there are enough hard edges in the pine and cliffs to establish scale, then the valley can soften into air without becoming empty.

*Tutocanula, or El Capitan* makes a useful complication. This is a paired stereoscopic card, not one unframed landscape. The two rectangles, their central divider, and the yellow mount form the primary composition of the supplied object, which is why the overlay draws two internal frames and the divider rather than inventing a landscape horizon from the caption band. The broad, almost matched El Capitan views become a controlled double take: a monumental wall is repeated only a few inches apart for binocular depth. Its lower edge density of 0.020 and mean tone of 0.60 help keep the two panels calm enough to read as a pair.

<Plate slug="carleton-watkins" id="02-el-capitan-tutocanula" label="Tutocanula, or El Capitan, 3600 Feet, Yosemite Valley, 1861" caption="The two printed views and their central divider, not the detector's caption-band horizon, are the stereocard's governing structure." />

## water as a clearing

*Dams and Lake, Nevada County* makes depth by reversing the Yosemite formula. Here a dark, irregular forest occupies nearly all the frame; the lake is a narrow bright clearing inside it. The overlay circles that basin, traces its far shore, and marks the distant ridgeline at the analyzer's measured y0.26. It deliberately ignores the detected vanishing point in tree and shore texture, because none of those marks behaves as a coherent perspective system. The basin does not recede by rails or road. It opens as a small exposure in a dense dark field.

<Plate slug="carleton-watkins" id="03-dams-and-lake-nevada-county" label="Dams and Lake, Nevada County, California, Distant View, c. 1871" caption="A ridgeline at y0.26, the light lake basin, and its far shore clarify the layered opening inside a dark forest." />

This is the darkest plate in the group by mean tone, 0.38, and one of the more textured, at 0.077 edge density. The palette reaches a near-black #34180b before rising to #cdbca0 on the water. The contrast is not merely atmospheric: it gives the lake its role as a piece of cleared evidence inside the scene of an engineered intervention.

*Mirror Lake and Mt. Watkins* works by a related but gentler enclosure. A huge right-hand tree runs down the frame, while a granite slope at left falls into the bright shoreline opening. The overlay places the actual lake seam at y0.766, where the analyzer also sees its strongest horizontal change. It does not use the system's proposed vanishing point, which is assembled from branches and foliage rather than a visible recession. The eye moves sideways and down into an opening, not into a geometric tunnel.

<Plate slug="carleton-watkins" id="04-mirror-lake-mount-watkins" label="The Lake / Mirror Lake and Mt. Watkins, Yosemite, negative 1861; print c. 1867" caption="The dark right tree and a granite slope contain the sunlit opening above the measured shoreline at y0.766." />

The print has a wide tonal spread, standard deviation 0.27, and a high 0.179 edge density, mainly because the tree canopy and foreground vegetation stay active even as the water goes calm. Browns from #39190d to #ddc89d make the bright clearing look more like a withheld passage than an open vista.

## the camera turns upward

Getty records that Watkins tilted an 18 × 22-inch camera upward for *Multnomah Falls*, so the leaning trunks are an effect of camera placement rather than a careless horizon. The overlay therefore isolates the visible upper and lower falls and the dominant left trunk, not a false horizontal or a canopy-derived vanishing point. The scene is all climb and drop: the camera's altered axis turns the gorge into a tight vertical sequence.

<Plate slug="carleton-watkins" id="05-multnomah-falls-side" label="Multnomah Falls, Columbia River, Oregon / Multnomah Falls, Side, negative 1867; print 1881–85" caption="The visible upper and lower falls are held apart by a massive foreground tree rather than forced into a false continuous line." />

At 0.231, this is the highest measured edge density in the set, and its 0.24 tonal deviation is equally broad. Bark, spray, foliage, and the narrow white falls keep interrupting one another. The palette, from #391f13 to #d2c29c, captures the same dark-versus-water split the overlay names. The record supports the camera's size and tilt; it does not preserve aperture, exposure time, or focal length.

Watkins takes an even more explicit turn in *Among the Treetops, Calaveras*. He placed the 18 × 22-inch camera on its back, and the visible oval plate becomes the picture's decisive internal frame. Two trunks run inward toward the sky opening and a vertical axis divides that opening approximately in half. The overlay uses the oval and these physical trunks; it rejects the analyzer's supposed horizon and canopy vanishing point. Neither is a horizon in an upward-looking image.

<Plate slug="carleton-watkins" id="07-among-the-treetops-calaveras" label="Among the Treetops, Calaveras Grove, negative 1878; print 1880–90" caption="The oval plate aperture and opposed trunks organize an upward view around a central void of sky." />

The analyzer finds a bright mean of 0.63 but only 0.030 edge density, unusually low for a tree picture because the large sky opening overwhelms the fine canopy detail. Its pale #dfd3af and dark #472b19 palette turns the void into an active light field. In both waterfall and treetop photographs, the camera's unusual physical orientation is the compositional decision before any later reading begins.

## survey lines become infrastructure

*View on Lake Tahoe* converts the old landscape recession into a railway problem. The two track alignments converge at x0.459, y0.560, precisely where the analyzer finds a vanishing point from 22 inlying line segments. The upper rail curves in from the left; the lower rail climbs from the right; tiny figures wait at their junction. The photographer and mammoth-plate camera also cast a shadow at the bottom edge, a recorded presence that shows how close the instrument stands to the engineered terrain.

<Plate slug="carleton-watkins" id="06-view-on-lake-tahoe" label="View on Lake Tahoe, 1877" caption="Upper and lower rail lines converge at the measured railway junction, where the small figures supply scale." />

With a mean of 0.53 and standard deviation of 0.25, the print sits in a broad warm range from #433029 to #ead5a3. Its 0.120 edge density is substantial but ordered: ties, rails, trestle, and cut rock repeat instead of merely cluttering the field. The photograph makes industry legible by giving it the same clear route into distance that Yosemite's walls once supplied.

The *View in Los Angeles* is likewise a survey of settlement, but it first asks us to see the mounted print as an object. The overlay frames the printed image field, then follows the streetcar rails and right-hand street edge to their shared recession at x0.625, y0.474. Pico House remains the major architectural mass to the left of that route, but the eye is carried away from it and into the empty street. The analyzer reports no machine-confirmed vanishing point here; the overlay's claim is visual and limited to the plainly converging rails and street edges.

<Plate slug="carleton-watkins" id="09-views-in-los-angeles" label="Views in Los Angeles, c. 1880" caption="The mounted image field contains Pico House while rails and the street edge converge down the newly built city street." />

This is the brightest and smoothest plate, with mean tone 0.70 and edge density only 0.022. #dac9af, #b89d86, and #9e8270 dominate the palette. The low edge count is not lack of information; it lets the street's shallow, dusty recession read with remarkable calm.

*Smelting Works, New Almaden* ends the chapter by making the industrial basin itself into an argument. A rail bridge rises from the near corner toward the works, a hillside flume slopes down into the same cluster, and the valley ridge runs around the upper edge like a containing wall. The overlay omits the analyzer's small incidental vanishing point because it does not organize the full scene. Its real claim is logistical: bridge, flume, roofs, stacks, and ridge are an interconnected system visibly set inside the terrain.

<Plate slug="carleton-watkins" id="10-smelting-works-new-almaden" label="Smelting Works, New Almaden, 1863" caption="A rail bridge and a hillside flume direct attention to the works, held inside the broad contour of the valley ridge." />

The mean tone falls back to 0.46, with a wide 0.25 spread and #3c281f at the dark end of the palette. At 0.081 edge density, the plant is neither as busy as the falls nor as smooth as Los Angeles. That middle state is revealing: Watkins makes extraction a designed landscape, its machinery readable because it has been granted the same measured foreground, route, and enclosing horizon as the wilderness views.

<Callout>
The selected Getty objects are albumen silver prints. The negatives behind Watkins's western large-format photographs were wet-collodion glass plates, coated, exposed, and developed while wet, then contact-printed at their intended scale. This chapter does not convert that general process into fictional per-frame settings. No aperture, shutter speed, focal length, or exposure duration is recorded for these photographs in the supplied Getty records. What is documented is more useful: the tilted 18 × 22-inch camera at Multnomah Falls, the same camera placed on its back for the Calaveras treetops, the mammoth-plate camera shadow at Lake Tahoe, and the second negative used for Yosemite's clouds.
</Callout>

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Pin the distance with one near fact">
Find a broad scene with a very small distant subject. Put one unmistakable object at a frame edge, a tree, post, person, or rock, then make two or three visible contours lead from it toward the distance. Keep the object large enough to establish scale, as Watkins's pine does in Yosemite.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Make a route, then test it">
Photograph a rail, path, curb, or bridge approaching a clear convergence. Draw the route afterward and ask whether every marked segment sits on a real edge. If it does not, drop the mark instead of using a decorative diagonal. Compare the result with the two rails in the Lake Tahoe view.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Frame the object, not only the scene">
Photograph a scene through a window, doorway, circular opening, or on a printed page. First identify the frame inside the frame, then decide whether it changes the picture's center of gravity. Watkins's stereocard and oval treetop print show how the object can be the composition.
</ExerciseCard>
