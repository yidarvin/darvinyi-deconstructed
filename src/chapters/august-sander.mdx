August Sander's portraits make social description a problem of placement. Across this selection, clothes, tools, props, and posture do not explain a sitter from outside the picture; they are the picture's visible structure. *People of the Twentieth Century* is organized as a social atlas, but its force comes from a more exact decision: each frame makes a person and the material signs around them answer to one another. The overlays stay with that observable grammar rather than treating a detector's every proposed horizon or vanishing point as a fact.

## figure, tool, and type

<Plate slug="august-sander" id="01-young-farmers" label="Young Farmers, 1914" caption="A low field edge and the left cane hold three differently spaced figures in one advancing rhythm." />

In *Young Farmers*, the overlay places the field edge at y equals 0.292, where the pale sky breaks against the darker rural ground. The left cane rises through that band, while the first man's face receives the small anchor. The cane is not a decorative diagonal: it lets the three separated bodies read as one measured progression. The analyzer finds a broad tonal range, from p05 0.122 to p95 0.922, with a palette moving from #27251f through #8c8883 to #e6e5e2; dark coats can therefore carry their rhythm against an unusually bright open sky. [MoMA record](https://www.moma.org/collection/works/193647)

<Plate slug="august-sander" id="02-bricklayer" label="Bricklayer, 1928" caption="The centered body is made inseparable from the broad brick load crossing its shoulders." />

*Bricklayer* is a portrait in which work becomes architecture. The centered body axis runs through the cap and the vest buttons, while the bracketed brick load creates a literal horizontal pressure across the shoulders. That broad, segmented mass makes body and stack one construction rather than separating face, labor, and object into different subjects. It is the darkest plate here by mean luminance, 0.202, and its palette only reaches #9f9b92 at the light end; the low-key field gives the face and bricks their hard, carved relief. [MoMA record](https://www.moma.org/collection/works/193778)

<Plate slug="august-sander" id="03-proletarian-intellectuals" label="Proletarian Intellectuals [Else Schuler, Tristan Rémy, Franz Wilhelm Seiwert, Gerd Arntz], c. 1925" caption="Four faces are compressed into a shallow field, with the rear and right-hand sitters establishing its depth." />

The group in *Proletarian Intellectuals* refuses the usual single center. The rear sitter's higher face and the right-hand face receive separate ellipses, making the shallow but layered arrangement explicit without inventing an internal frame or a connecting contour. The other two sitters press into the interval between them, so the portrait's collective identity depends on differing posture, glasses, hands, and dress rather than a conventional central hero. The image has the set's highest edge density, 0.0541, and its #282621-to-#d4d2cf palette preserves those differences within the compressed arrangement. [MoMA record](https://www.moma.org/collection/works/193802)

## authority staged in the body

<Plate slug="august-sander" id="04-police-officer" label="Police Officer, 1925" caption="A blunt head-and-moustache accent sits inside the uninterrupted dark silhouette of the uniform." />

*Police Officer* reduces authority to a few insistently legible signs. The face-and-cap ellipse includes the unusually broad moustache, and the short polyline follows its lateral sweep; the larger bracket names the uniform silhouette that contains those accents. This is not a claim of literal bilateral symmetry, even though the front-facing presentation invites it. With mean luminance 0.629 and edge density only 0.0088, the pale studio surround and smooth dark uniform simplify the figure into a controlled emblem. [MoMA record](https://www.moma.org/collection/works/193906)

<Plate slug="august-sander" id="05-match-seller" label="Match-seller, 1927" caption="The seated seller occupies a dark niche while the step pushes the eye toward the open pavement." />

The horizontal *Match-seller* makes the setting share the portrait's work. The face sits inside the shaded niche at left, while the step edge opens the composition through the seated legs to the sunlit pavement. The overlay does not use the analyzer's detected vanishing point: the more truthful movement is the actual stone step and the contrast between enclosure and exposure. A restrained #36332e through #aba8a0 palette and mean luminance 0.333 keep the face distinct without separating it from the wall that frames it. [MoMA record](https://www.moma.org/collection/works/193993)

<Plate slug="august-sander" id="06-member-of-parliament" label="Member of Parliament (Democrat), 1927" caption="The hat, face, cloak, and upright umbrella create a compact political silhouette against a light ground." />

The Parliament member's umbrella is a second vertical body beside the cloaked figure. The large head-and-hat ellipse, the upright umbrella polyline, and the outer figure bracket make that relation explicit. The picture is not organized around a detector's weak portrait “horizon”; it is organized around the balance of dark clothing against the blank studio field. Its p05 of 0.157 and p95 of 0.925 describe the clear separation of light ground from a palette rooted in #312f28. [MoMA record](https://www.moma.org/collection/works/194000)

## faces, relation, and studio space

<Plate slug="august-sander" id="07-radio-secretary" label="Secretary at West German Radio, Cologne, 1931" caption="A turned face and raised hand keep the seated body active within a tall, sparse studio field." />

In *Secretary at West German Radio*, the raised hand prevents the close portrait from becoming inert. The ellipse follows the turned face, the polyline follows the bent arm and hand, and the bracket holds the seated silhouette within the tall frame. The tight, dark garment absorbs much of the image: mean luminance is 0.368 and p05 is 0.129, but the #c2beb5 light end preserves the face and the satin's small highlights. [MoMA record](https://www.moma.org/collection/works/193883)

<Plate slug="august-sander" id="08-painter-otto-dix" label="Painter [Otto Dix], 1924" caption="The close profile is built from a clean facial edge and the opposing diagonal of the jacket lapel." />

The close crop in *Painter [Otto Dix]* turns the sitter's profile into a sequence of edges. The ellipse names the profile as a whole; the small polyline follows the forehead, nose, mouth, and chin; the longer line picks out the opposing jacket lapel. The analyzer reports very low edge density, 0.0048, so those few contour decisions carry almost all the portrait's structure. Its light #e5e6e0 field and dark #45413a clothing make the profile read cleanly without adding narrative props that this particular frame does not show. [MoMA record](https://www.moma.org/collection/works/194061)

<Plate slug="august-sander" id="09-otto-dix-and-martha" label="The Painter Otto Dix and his Wife Martha, 1925-26" caption="A frontal face and a profile are held apart by the narrow overlap of their shoulders." />

The double portrait makes relation visible without a gesture. Martha's frontal face and Dix's profile receive separate ellipses; the short line follows the overlap of their shoulders below. The resulting difference is structural: one face meets the camera, the other turns across the frame. With p05 0.165, p95 0.788, and edge density 0.0139, the plate maintains a moderate tonal field in which the pale wall is not empty but a necessary interval between the two heads. [MoMA record](https://www.moma.org/collection/works/193821)

<Plate slug="august-sander" id="10-young-woman" label="Young Woman, 1929" caption="The face is counterweighted by one long arm and the dark curve of the chair beneath the light dress." />

*Young Woman* ends the sequence by making an open field part of the portrait. The face ellipse is counterweighted by the long arm and by the chair curve beneath the dress, so the pale right side of the frame remains active rather than vacant. The image's p05 of 0.153 and p95 of 0.788, together with #333029 and #c5c2ba at the palette's ends, make that contrast legible: dark chair and hair hold against the light dress and surrounding wall. [MoMA record](https://www.moma.org/collection/works/193857)

## process and settings

The supplied MoMA records place these photographs between 1914 and 1931 and identify them as part of Sander's larger social project. They do not document a camera, lens, aperture, shutter speed, film stock, negative format, or the print process for these web renditions; the ingested files likewise contain no camera EXIF. It would be false to infer settings from the portrait style. What can be stated is visual: Sander repeatedly makes dress, prop, bodily orientation, interval, and studio or street space into evidence that is both individual and typological.
