Bernd and Hilla Becher make industrial description into a discipline of comparison. A pithead, tank, or cooling tower is photographed as a whole, but its real argument emerges in the repeated field beside it: one form keeps changing while the format, distance, and pale ground hold steady. The overlays therefore mark the photographed print fields and their actual gutters. They do not mistake a row break for a single horizon, a detector's saliency point for a lone subject, or a serial array for a symmetrical building. ## comparison is the subject

<Plate slug="bernd-hilla-becher" id="01-pitheads-perspective-views" label="Pitheads: Perspective Views, 1981" caption="Fifteen closely matched prints turn changing hoist supports and headframes into a comparative field rather than a single industrial view." />

The fifteen-print field in *Pitheads: Perspective Views* makes its row and column gutters part of the composition. Each tower rises through an individual portrait cell while diagonal supports swing differently against pale sky. The overlay's central gutter and lower row break show how a fixed display gives those deviations equal visual weight. Mean luminance is 0.715, with a grey palette from #3c3c3c to #d0d0d0 and edge density 0.0941; dark trusses therefore remain active without overwhelming the repeated pale grounds.

<Plate slug="bernd-hilla-becher" id="02-blast-furnaces-heads-frontal-views" label="Blast Furnaces, Heads, Frontal Views, 1985" caption="Twelve mounted views keep a common print unit while pipes, ducts, and central furnace cores change from cell to cell." />

The twelve-print field in *Blast Furnaces, Heads, Frontal Views* first establishes equal mats and gaps, then lets each furnace resist that order. Tall cores recur, but ducts and pipes extend unevenly into their cells. The overlay identifies the central column and row gutters rather than claiming perfect frontal symmetry. Its mean of 0.659, p05 of 0.153, and p95 of 0.871 describe how dark machinery holds against the pale display space; #282119 and #d8d4d1 mark the range.

<Plate slug="bernd-hilla-becher" id="03-coal-tipple-goodspring-four-views" label="Coal Tipple, Goodspring, Pa. Four Views, 1975" caption="A four-view arrangement keeps one tipple in comparison while its angled silhouette and site relation change with viewpoint." />

The four-view field makes viewpoint a variable. The central vertical and horizontal gutters refuse a continuous panorama: each landscape is a fresh test of the same timber structure. The top-left view reads more narrowly, while the other views give its diagonal conveyor and lean more force. Mean luminance is 0.705 and edge density only 0.0406, so the dark tipple gains force from the open ground and pale tree-line rather than from an overloaded field of detail.

## the typology holds the difference

<Plate slug="bernd-hilla-becher" id="04-water-towers-international" label="Water Towers, Germany, United States, France, Belgium, Luxembourg, 1963–1995" caption="Thirty regular portrait cells compare water-tower contour, supports, and enclosure while suppressing the sites around them." />

The water-tower typology expands comparison until contour becomes a language. Masonry cylinders, lattice frames, bowls, globes, and tapered vessels remain centered inside thirty narrow cells; the selected gutters make the serial field, not a shared physical horizon, the governing structure. The analyzer finds a bright mean of 0.771 and p95 of 1.000, with #faf6f7 at the pale end and #48433f at the dark end. That broad, light field lets each silhouette arrive quickly.

<Plate slug="bernd-hilla-becher" id="06-winding-towers-belgium-france" label="Winding Towers, Belgium, France, 1967–1988" caption="Nine tall frames hold winding towers at a common scale so arches, pulleys, bracing, and diagonal hoist supports can diverge visibly." />

The nine-print field of *Winding Towers* sets a common tall format before any one tower is read. Within it, arches, pulley wheels, and braces change the internal grammar; slanting supports press against the upright shafts. The central gutter and row break make that shared format visible without assigning the whole sheet a false symmetry axis. With mean luminance 0.805, p05 0.247, and edge density 0.0578, dark machinery has ample separation from the near-white #fdfcfc ground.

<Plate slug="bernd-hilla-becher" id="07-gas-holders-telescoping" label="Gas Holders (Telescoping), Germany, Belgium, United States, United Kingdom, 1965–1992" caption="Nine centered cylinders turn rim height, horizontal bands, and outer guide frames into a measured set of differences." />

In the telescoping holders, repeated cylinders become a sequence of rims and bands. The overlay is deliberately spare: it marks the nine-print container and its two gutters, leaving the separate levels and external guide frames readable as the actual variations. The palette runs from #4b4642 to #fefdfd, with mean luminance 0.726 and edge density 0.0569. The pale upper backgrounds keep the layered dark bodies from merging across cells.

<Plate slug="bernd-hilla-becher" id="08-gas-holders-spherical" label="Gas Holders (Spherical), Germany, 1963–1993" caption="A common nine-print field makes globes, equatorial seams, supports, ladders, and catwalks comparable without calling them identical circles." />

The spherical holders demonstrate why the Bechers' regularity is not sameness. Globes repeat across nine cells, but seams, support legs, ladders, and catwalks vary around their edges; several apparent circles become visibly perspectival. The overlay's field and gutters keep the comparison honest. Mean luminance is 0.682, p05 is 0.224, and edge density is 0.0387, so broad tonal modelling does more work than fine texture across the muted #413c36-to-#f7f6f5 palette.

<Plate slug="bernd-hilla-becher" id="09-gas-holders-germany-united-kingdom" label="Gas Holders, Germany, United Kingdom, 1971–1997" caption="Nine cylindrical vessels turn repeated ribs, top rims, bases, and external fittings into a controlled study of proportion." />

Here, horizontal ribs accumulate into a measured skin. The serial field makes height, width, taper, and fittings visible without treating the right-edge detector response as a subject or calling the group perfectly symmetrical. The analyzer records the densest edges of this selection at 0.0775, appropriate to ribbing, ladders, and diagonal braces; #4e473c against #fefefd keeps those small structural differences legible.

<Plate slug="bernd-hilla-becher" id="10-cooling-towers-germany" label="Cooling Towers, Germany, 1966–1990" caption="Nine tapering forms make changes in flare, waist, rim, opening, and cross-bracing readable against a repeated pale ground." />

The cooling-tower field compares a single broad silhouette family. Each tower narrows and flares differently, while X-bracing lays a second, more regular structure over its curved body. The overlay identifies only the serial frame and gutters; a universal vanishing point or horizon would hide the work's actual comparison. A bright mean of 0.766, p95 of 1.000, and edge density of 0.0721 explain the crisp contrast between dark bracing and pale background.

<Plate slug="bernd-hilla-becher" id="11-coal-mine-tipples-pennsylvania" label="Coal Mine Tipples, Pennsylvania, United States, 1974–1978" caption="Nine related tipples keep a common serial field while collapse, stance, pulley wheels, cross-bracing, and conveyors alter each structure's profile." />

The final nine-print field returns to the tipple as a changing skeleton. Upper pulley wheels, timber or steel frames, and diagonal conveyors recur, but degree of collapse and viewpoint keep each construction provisional. The overlay's gutters make that serial pacing legible without forcing a common orientation on the objects. Mean luminance is 0.768, p05 is 0.251, and the #423c38-to-#fefdfd palette leaves the crossed braces present against the open sky.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Make a comparison field">
Photograph one class of ordinary structure from a controlled distance and framing position. Build a small grid of at least four views. Before choosing a favorite image, ask which repeated frame makes a real difference in contour, support, opening, or ground relation visible.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Hold one variable steady">
Make two short sequences of the same object. In the first, keep the camera position stable and let the object's differences carry the sequence. In the second, change your viewpoint deliberately. Compare whether the grid clarifies variation or begins to describe your movement instead.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Use gutters as structure">
Print or arrange a set of images with equal gaps. Mark one vertical and one horizontal gap on a proof. Do not treat those marks as a horizon or a subject; describe how the spacing changes the way you compare the pictures.
</ExerciseCard>
