Germaine Krull makes industrial matter refuse its ordinary scale. In this
1925–30 group, a gantry, crane, ride, tower, and advertising sign are never
shown as orderly things to be surveyed; they are cropped, tilted, multiplied,
and made to read as graphic pressure. That is the central promise of *Métal*,
the 1928 portfolio of industrial structures that the National Gallery of
Victoria records as sixty-four plates, and it carries into the carnival and the
Peugeot commission. The overlays stay with the visible argument: beams,
lattice bays, wheel orbits, and letterforms, not invented technical history.

## steel made abstract

<Plate slug="germaine-krull" id="01-abstract-industrial-building" label="Untitled (Abstract Industrial Building), 1925" caption="A canted overhead beam crosses a central gantry spine, making the cropped structure read as a stacked field rather than a stable facade." />

In *Untitled (Abstract Industrial Building)*, the steep overhead beam is the
first fact the eye receives. It runs across the pale field into the upright
gantry, while the overlay's vertical axis at x0.463 holds the support edge that
keeps the collision from flying apart. The small anchor names the gantry crown,
where riveted planes, cables, and the right-hand brace meet. The analyzer finds
a subdued edge density of 0.009 and a mid-bright mean luminance of 0.582, from
p05 0.369 to p95 0.851; ochre-brown (#6b6258), cream (#e6d2b8), and muted gray
(#847969) compress the object into a limited, almost painted range. The angle
does not disclose a building so much as force its steel planes to become the
picture.

<Plate slug="germaine-krull" id="02-metal-industrial-structure" label="Untitled (from Métal), 1925–1928" caption="A cropped boom, suspended load, and framed lattice bay divide the crane into dark architecture and a large open field of sky." />

*Untitled (from Métal)* is more severe: the crane's dark upright occupies the
right while the suspended load punctures a nearly empty left half. The overlay
marks the lattice bay and the cropped boom head, and retains the analyzer's
measured convergence at x0.248, y0.492, a geometric consequence of the boom
and rigging rather than a claim about a receding street. The image has a wider
tonal span than the preceding plate, p05 0.192 to p95 0.808, with dark brown
#413223 set against #dfc29c sky. Its modest edge density, 0.026, comes from a
few decisive contours rather than a busy field. Krull turns industrial function
into a cut-paper relation between black structure and open ground.

## the machine becomes a diagram

<Plate slug="germaine-krull" id="03-carnival-ride" label="Carnival Ride, 1928" caption="An overlapping wheel orbit, spokes, and cropped gondolas make the fairground ride read as radial machinery rather than a scene." />

In *Carnival Ride*, the same vocabulary becomes noisy and public. The overlay's
wide ellipse follows the overlapping wheel orbit, while an upper strut leads
toward the analyzer's six-line convergence at x0.692, y0.512. A smaller anchor
holds the central hub and rider, refusing to let the human scale disappear
inside the machinery. At edge density 0.050, nearly twice the crane plate's,
the many arcs and braces make the image visibly more active; yet its tones stay
close, from p05 0.278 to p95 0.714, in tan #baab92 and dark gray-brown #524c43.
The photograph has movement without needing to depict motion: overlapping
circles and diagonals supply it.

<Plate slug="germaine-krull" id="04-eiffel-tower" label="The Eiffel Tower, 1928" caption="A tower axis and one right-hand lattice sweep concentrate the upward view into a dense field of crossing ironwork." />

The Eiffel Tower is the portfolio logic at its most extreme. Rather than show
the monument's silhouette, Krull looks up into a packed interior of iron. The
overlay's central vertical axis at x0.5 follows the tower's axial pull; a
right-hand lattice sweep brings the eye inward to the marked knot of structure.
The surrounding frame-in-frame is not a second architectural opening but the
photograph's own lattice field, pressed hard against the paper edge. This is
the sharpest and busiest image here: edge density 0.101, mean luminance 0.513,
and a p05–p95 reach from near-black 0.122 to white 1.000. The palette's
#2f2823 iron, #c5bbab paper tone, and #fefefe highlights make the tower a
network of black marks on light.

## commercial light, the same pressure

<Plate slug="germaine-krull" id="05-peugeot-advertisement" label="Advertisement for the Six Peugeot 12-cylinder, 1930" caption="Repeated Peugeot lettering and badge forms layer into a tilted field of commercial light rather than a single readable sign." />

The Peugeot advertisement brings Krull's construction into a commercial night
scene. The overlay traces the tilted neon wordmark, circles an overprinted badge
cluster, and anchors the large foreground word. None of those marks describes a
single stable sign: the same name is repeated, inverted by reflection, and
broken by blur. The picture is the darkest of the five, mean luminance 0.332,
with a compact p05–p95 span of 0.263–0.600; gray #484542, #53504d, and dusty
#a69a8a keep even the lit lettering inside a restrained register. With edge
density 0.066, it is denser than either crane but less granular than the tower.
Advertising here behaves like *Métal*: letters are material, and material is
made to drift across the picture plane.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Let one beam disrupt the frame">
Photograph a stair, bridge, crane, or other structure from close enough that a
single diagonal crosses an upright support. Keep the subject partly cropped,
then decide whether that diagonal makes the structure feel more like a thing or
more like an abstract plane.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Make a radial machine">
Find a wheel, ride, fan, or circular guard with repeated spokes or braces.
Frame it so at least one circle is cut by the edge, then use the intersecting
arcs, not a centered view of the whole object, to organize the image.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Layer a word in light">
At dusk, photograph illuminated lettering through glass or alongside a
reflection. Move until the same word appears at least twice at different angles,
and preserve enough dark ground for the letters to read as floating forms rather
than as a clean advertisement.
</ExerciseCard>
