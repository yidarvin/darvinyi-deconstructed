Ben Shahn's October 1935 Pulaski County photographs do not arrange cotton work as a single emblem. They move between a waiting crowd, close portraits, a field, a scale, a family porch, and a wagon. The overlays keep that shift in scale visible. A porch eave becomes a measured boundary in the first frame; boards and posts make shallow stages in the portraits; the field's receding rows make work spatial; the scale turns a transaction into a triangle. The geometry is never an alibi for the social scene. It is how Shahn lets many people occupy a frame without reducing them to one type.

## the porch as a public room

The first three pictures work with the same wooden architecture in radically different ways. *Cotton pickers, Arkansas* lays a long porch under a gabled roof, with people dispersed from its dark interior into the open yard. The overlay takes the eave at y=0.303 and the left gable edge as the governing structure; its vertical balance axis at x=0.375 passes through the central porch mass rather than claiming that the people themselves are symmetrical. The analyzer finds a broad tonal scale, mean 0.464 from p05=0.031 to p95=0.910, and a neutral palette from #0e0e0e to #dedede. That is why the roof can hold a crowd without flattening it: black interior, bright sky, and pale ground remain distinct fields.

<Plate slug="ben-shahn" id="01-cotton-pickers-arkansas" label="Cotton pickers, Arkansas, October 1935" caption="The porch eave at y=0.303 and left gable edge make a large triangular roof over a dispersed crowd; the open foreground keeps the gathering from becoming a single mass." />

The close portrait narrows that public room to a worker seated against plank lines. The phi grid is the analyzer's better fit (0.350 versus 0.210 for thirds), while the small anchor at x=0.554, y=0.352 sits on his face. A porch-board line crosses behind the pale sack. This is the darkest photograph of the porch group, mean 0.382 with p05=0.102 and edge density 0.051; its palette runs from #202020 to #c5c5c5, so the white sack reads as a counterweight rather than a decorative highlight.

<Plate slug="ben-shahn" id="02-cotton-picker-arkansas" label="Cotton picker, Arkansas, October 1935" caption="The worker's face sits near the phi grid's upper intersection; porch boards and the pale sack make a diagonal counterweight around the seated figure." />

The 6:30 a.m. picture reverses that relation. Its nearest woman presses forward from the vertical siding while two other figures hold the shallow depth behind her. The overlay's polyline does not claim a literal perspective line; it follows the step from left figure to near figure to the right-hand figure. The low line at y=0.866 is explicitly the retained negative edge, not a horizon in Arkansas. The image is brighter and flatter than the close portrait (mean 0.506, edge density 0.031), with #242424 through #c9c9c9 holding bodies and boards in a tight value range.

## cotton as mass, then field

*Cotton on porch* almost removes people altogether. The overlay makes the modest claim the image can bear: an upper silhouette of piled cotton, the porch boards under it, and a vertical balance line through the mound. The high measured mean (0.663) and p95=0.890 explain the luminous field, while the low edge density of 0.022 means individual tufts merge into one nearly architectural volume. Its gray palette, #202020 to #d9d9d9, gives the dark doorway and boards just enough force to contain it.

<Plate slug="ben-shahn" id="04-cotton-on-porch-maria-plantation" label="Cotton on porch of sharecropper's home, Maria plantation, October 1935" caption="An upper silhouette and the porch boards below turn loose cotton into a single pale mass, held against the dark structure at left." />

In the Alexander plantation field, Shahn restores bodies and depth. A level field horizon at y=0.105 sits above the workers; a measured convergence at x=0.499, y=0.638 and a leading line along a foreground cotton row organize the near picker and receding field. This is the set's busiest image by edge density, 0.121. The palette runs from #141414 through #c2c2c2 and the mean is only 0.399, so leaves, stems, cotton, clothing, and soil stay legible as a thick field rather than dissolving into an idealized white crop.

## exchange and the family stage

The weighing station is not a quiet composition, but its A-frame makes an exact, visible armature. The overlay marks the field edge and both legs of the scale; the center axis is a balance reading of the apparatus, not a claim that the people mirror one another. At mean 0.456 with edge density 0.100, it is almost as textured as the cotton field. The five sampled grays (#161616 to #cbcbcb) split sacks, figures, timber, and the road into discrete weights.

<Plate slug="ben-shahn" id="06-weighing-in-cotton" label="Weighing in cotton, Pulaski County, October 1935" caption="The two scale legs make a tall A-frame around the suspended sack; figures and cotton bags break its otherwise mechanical balance." />

The porch family has the opposite rhythm: a broad horizontal opening, steps, posts, and people distributed in several registers. The measured phi grid is only a marginally better fit than thirds (0.212 to 0.210), so it is supporting structure, not a claim of ideal proportion. The important mark is the anchor on the central seated figure at x=0.507, y=0.351. Mean tone is 0.473, from #0d0d0d to #bcbcbc, and edge density falls to 0.036; the architecture turns the people into a frieze without making them uniform.

<Plate slug="ben-shahn" id="07-sunday-on-the-porch" label="Children of sharecroppers, Little Rock, Arkansas / Sunday on the porch, October 1935" caption="The porch opening and its step form a shallow stage; the central seated figure anchors an uneven family distribution instead of a symmetrical tableau." />

The mother-and-child portrait uses enclosure more quietly. An ellipse names the mother's face, a polyline follows the physical interlock of mother and child, and the doorway/chair rectangle gives the pair a frame within the negative edge. The measured center axis is a tonal balance through chair and doorway, not a literal mirror. A mean of 0.432 and edge density of 0.030 make this one of the calmer plates; #191919 through #cecece separates the dark doorway, the bright chair back, and the child's cloth with unusually spare means.

<Plate slug="ben-shahn" id="08-mother-and-child-little-rock" label="Mother and child, Little Rock, Arkansas, October 1935" caption="The mother's face, the child's diagonal body, and the doorway/chair frame make an intimate structure without turning the pair into an abstract pattern." />

The Sunday portrait is more frontal but not more static. The doorway frame surrounds the sitter, while the polyline runs from cap down the shirtfront to the clasped hands, recording a bodily rather than geometric line of attention. The analyzer's axis at x=0.412 describes the portrait's left-weighted tonal balance. This is the brightest portrait in the set (mean 0.604, p95=0.894) and also the least texturally busy (edge density 0.020); #080808 to #d3d3d3 gives the face and shirt room to meet the camera before the wall takes over.

<Plate slug="ben-shahn" id="09-sharecropper-on-sunday" label="Sharecropper on Sunday, Little Rock, Arkansas, October 1935" caption="A doorway frame and the line from cap to clasped hands keep the frontal sitter active; the measured balance axis is left of frame center at x=0.412." />

## a wagon, not a conclusion

The last photograph does not turn the day into a clean departure image. A wagon rail makes a shallow horizontal stage at y=0.454, and another line follows the side of the wagon. Bodies face different ways; the overlay anchors only the left worker, at x=0.351, y=0.254, and names the centered axis as broad wagon balance rather than social order. The phi grid is the better mechanical fit (0.442 versus 0.377 for thirds). Its mean 0.530, wide p05–p95 span of 0.035–0.910, and quiet edge density of 0.029 leave the figures cut against an almost blank sky and the planked wagon.

These are 35 mm nitrate negatives made in October 1935 for the Resettlement Administration. The local records document neither camera model nor per-frame aperture, shutter speed, focal length, sensitivity, or exact original printing process. `research.md` separates that historical record from the modern scan and from the analyzer's measurements; no camera settings are invented here.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Build a public room">
Photograph a group under one architectural boundary, a roof, awning, doorway, or platform. Leave enough foreground that the group does not become a solid block. Mark only the boundary that gathers the people and one line that gives the architecture its direction.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Let a task set the geometry">
Find a working scene with an actual apparatus, a counter, frame, cart, ladder, or table. Make the overlay follow the object that organizes the task instead of imposing a generic vanishing point. The scale in *Weighing in cotton* is useful because its triangle already exists in the scene.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Keep a portrait particular">
In a portrait, mark the face, one visible relation of body or gaze, and one enclosing structure. Do not call a tonal balance a literal symmetry unless the image actually mirrors. The goal is to show how a person occupies a frame, not to turn them into a diagram.
</ExerciseCard>
