Berenice Abbott's *Changing New York* turns description into pressure. The ten WPA Federal Art Project photographs here do not settle for a city inventory: they make the street behave as a canyon, a billboard as a plane of type, an elevated avenue as a moving diagram, and an industrial viaduct as a ceiling of repeated steel. The overlays stay close to those visible constructions. They mark measured edges, anchors, and convergences, while the prose follows how Abbott makes a viewer feel scale through compression, repetition, and light. The project dates from 1935 to 1938; the source records and open-use basis are collected in [the source shortlist](../../content/berenice-abbott/sources.md).

## the street as a constructed corridor

<Plate slug="berenice-abbott" id="01-broadway-thomas-street" label="Broadway and Thomas Street, Manhattan, 1936" caption="Unequal arcaded facades make a shallow urban canyon, their diagonals meeting at a compressed street pinch." />

*Broadway and Thomas Street* begins with a conflict between large, unequal faces. The left arcade holds the typewriter-shop sign and the right arcade pushes sharply into depth; their measured diagonals meet at x 0.436, y 0.370. The horizontal break at y 0.536 does not make the street calm. It separates the bright upper masonry from the active intersection and lets the pedestrians, shop fronts, and vehicles collect below. Its mean luminance is 0.435, but the p05-to-p95 span runs from 0.055 to 0.914, a range wide enough to keep white stone, dark storefronts, and the street's small moving figures distinct.

<Plate slug="berenice-abbott" id="02-cedar-street-william" label="Cedar Street, from William, Manhattan, 1936" caption="A narrow bright street opening is compressed between a dark wall at left and a heavily shadowed rounded corner at right." />

The vertical frame of *Cedar Street, from William* makes the same problem more severe. A white slot of sky and distant street rises almost the full height of the picture between two dark masses. Abbott gives the near right corner its own sculptural weight: curved stone bands and a small corbel accent interrupt the blank shadow. The image is the chapter's darkest by mean tone, 0.290, yet its p95 reaches 0.957. That extreme separation makes the bright slit feel less like atmosphere than a cut through architecture.

<Plate slug="berenice-abbott" id="04-spring-varick-streets" label="Spring and Varick Streets, Manhattan, 1935" caption="The long facade recedes as a window grid while the streetlamp makes a dark counter-diagonal across it." />

In *Spring and Varick Streets*, the city opens into a long perspective rather than a slot. The facade roofline and repeated window bands rise from the lower left toward the top center, while the lamp interrupts the regularity with an emphatic dark arm. The overlay traces the two measured recession lines, not every pane. With edge density 0.144, the highest in this group, the photograph has enough fine structure to suggest a grid without becoming a flat building survey. The point is the tension between a serial wall and the uneven activity at its base. [NYPL record](https://digitalcollections.nypl.org/items/510d47d9-4fba-a3d9-e040-e00a18064a99)

<Plate slug="berenice-abbott" id="05-starrett-lehigh-building" label="Starrett-Lehigh Building, 601 West 26th Street, Manhattan, 1936" caption="Receding floor bands and a rounded industrial corner pull a low-angle elevation into the frame." />

The *Starrett-Lehigh Building* turns floor bands into a scale machine. The long window line climbs toward a measured convergence at x 0.685, y 0.341, while the near corner sends a second band down through the picture. That cross-pressure makes the building read as both an enormous mass and a set of repeatable modules. The tonal range is also broad, from p05 0.035 to p95 0.937, so dark brick at left can counter the pale sky and bright facade without losing the fine window rhythm. [NYPL record](https://digitalcollections.nypl.org/items/510d47d9-4edd-a3d9-e040-e00a18064a99)

## crowds held against a fixed structure

<Plate slug="berenice-abbott" id="06-tempo-city-i" label="Tempo of the City: I, Fifth Avenue and 44th Street, 1938" caption="A cropped clock and its heavy stem hold the picture still while taxis and pedestrians spread across the lower street." />

*Tempo of the City: I* gives movement a fixed measure. The clock is cropped by the upper edge but its bright face and dark stem dominate the left half. The overlay anchors that face and follows the stem down into the pedestrian field, while a lower diagonal describes the crowd's entry from the left. At y 0.511, the traffic band runs behind the clock's base and turns taxis into a shallow horizontal counterweight. The analyzer records a mean luminance of 0.298 and a restrained palette from #2b231c to #cec7bb; within that dark range, the clock face and pale taxi roofs become insistent flashes.

<Plate slug="berenice-abbott" id="07-tempo-city-ii" label="Tempo of the City: II, Fifth Avenue and 42nd Street, looking west from Seymour Building, 1937" caption="An elevated view transforms streetcar, vehicles, and curbside crowd into intersecting avenues of motion." />

The second *Tempo of the City* removes the clock and raises the camera. The streetcar advances out of the lower-left field, lanes stretch toward the upper right, and a compressed band of curbside bodies weights the right edge. The overlay uses two real diagonals rather than inventing a single tidy vanishing point for this bend of traffic. Edge density rises to 0.091, almost double the value in the later avenue view, because vehicles, rails, pavement seams, and bodies all compete at once. The modest tonal spread, p05 0.259 to p95 0.800, keeps that competition legible as a single gray traffic field.

<Plate slug="berenice-abbott" id="08-seventh-avenue-south" label="Seventh Avenue looking south from 35th Street, Manhattan, 1935" caption="A sunlit avenue channel recedes through nearly black building masses and closes against a low foreground roof edge." />

*Seventh Avenue looking south* is quieter, but not less forceful. Dark buildings leave a sharply luminous avenue down the center; the line overlay names the two structural edges that drive toward the measured pinch at x 0.328, y 0.799. The low foreground roof provides the only firm horizontal closure. Its edge density is just 0.050, the set's lowest, which helps explain why the photograph works as a tonal channel before it works as an inventory of buildings. The palette moves from brown-black #312a23 to pale #cbc2b4, with the avenue taking almost all of the available light. [NYPL record](https://digitalcollections.nypl.org/items/510d47d9-4f83-a3d9-e040-e00a18064a99)

## surfaces that become arguments

<Plate slug="berenice-abbott" id="03-fish-market-south-street" label="Fish Market, South Street, Manhattan, 1935" caption="Painted commercial lettering, awnings, curb, and low facades hold the market in a long lateral rhythm." />

The fish market is a frieze, not a crowd scene. Abbott lets painted names, numbered doorways, chimneys, awnings, and parked trucks make the rhythm. The overlay follows the low market band, an awning edge, and the curb rather than claiming activity that the image does not visibly supply. The sign cluster at upper left starts the scan across the facade. With mean luminance 0.515, p05 0.051, p95 0.878, and edge density 0.073, the commercial surface has enough contrast to keep lettering and openings readable without breaking into visual noise. [NYPL record](https://digitalcollections.nypl.org/items/510d47d9-4f0e-a3d9-e040-e00a18064a99)

<Plate slug="berenice-abbott" id="09-advertisements-east-houston" label="Advertisements, East Houston Street and Second Avenue, Manhattan, 1937" caption="The Trommer's masthead and stacked ad panels turn a billboard wall into a steeply receding collage of type." />

In *Advertisements*, type becomes architecture. The huge Trommer's masthead tilts across the top, while smaller theater, magazine, drink, and parking panels tile the supporting wall. The large frame in the overlay identifies the masthead as one panel among many, and the two measured diagonal edges make the stack climb through the frame. The image is notably bright, with mean luminance 0.582 and p95 0.933, but its 0.118 edge density ensures that pale lettering remains an active texture instead of dissolving into a blank signboard. [NYPL record](https://digitalcollections.nypl.org/items/510d47d9-4f63-a3d9-e040-e00a18064a99)

<Plate slug="berenice-abbott" id="10-riverside-drive-viaduct" label="Under Riverside Drive Viaduct, 125th Street at 12th Avenue, Manhattan, 1937" caption="Repeated steel arches and trusses recede beneath the viaduct while a gas holder opens a bright industrial void at right." />

Under the Riverside Drive Viaduct, repetition becomes enclosure. The biggest arch frames a second arch, then a long retreat of smaller bays; a single measured diagonal follows that recession toward the street. At right, the gas holder's pale cylinder is visible through the steel and releases the dense ceiling into a bright void. Abbott does not flatten this structure into a simple grid: curved arch ribs, horizontal girders, diagonal braces, cars, signs, and street all remain in play. The middle-gray palette from #565556 to #e6e5ea and edge density 0.098 keep the layered construction readable. [NYPL record](https://digitalcollections.nypl.org/items/510d47d9-4eaa-a3d9-e040-e00a18064a99)

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Compress a street">
Stand at the edge of an intersection and frame two facades so that their strongest edges meet in a small central area. Keep people and traffic low in the frame. Review whether the built edges create a corridor before the street activity becomes the subject.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Give movement an anchor">
Photograph a moving sidewalk or street from beside one fixed object, a clock, pole, doorway, or sign. Make the fixed element large enough to hold one side of the frame, then let bodies or vehicles make a diagonal flow across the remaining space.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Make a surface carry scale">
Find an advertising wall, warehouse facade, or bridge understructure with a repeated unit. Choose one edge that describes its recession and one local detail that interrupts repetition. The picture should read as a structure at a distance and as a field of specific marks up close.
</ExerciseCard>
