David Octavius Hill trained as a painter, and every calotype he made with Robert Adamson carries a painter's discipline into a brand-new medium. Where a documentary eye might let a scene fall as it falls, Hill built each sitting the way he would have built an oil composition: two or three bodies closed into a triangle or a pyramid, a plain backdrop cleared of anything that might compete with the group, and Rock House's raking north light doing the work a warm palette would do on canvas. The soft, broken grain of calotype paper only helped the case, reading his sitters as connected masses of light and shadow rather than as sharp-edged documentary inventories. The overlays in this chapter keep finding the same structural moves under very different subjects: two fishwives fused into one silhouette, a churchful of ministers stacked into a diagrammatic crowd, a stone monument squared to its own central axis, a fisherman's labor staged as a single bent diagonal. Whether the sitting took an hour in the Rock House studio or an afternoon on location at Greyfriars or the newly finished Scott Monument, the same eye for closed, legible shape is doing the organizing.

## the fishwife triangle

The Newhaven fishwife pictures are the clearest statement of Hill's method: two figures, one plain ground, and a single closed silhouette built from whatever the women are already carrying.

## the crowd as diagram

Hill's other great problem was the opposite of the fishwife cell: not two bodies closing into one shape, but dozens of ministers who had to read as individuals and as a single Disruption event at once. The moderator's own portrait, made the same year, shows what the crowd work was built from.

## labor staged as diagonal

Away from the studio and the Disruption sittings, Hill and Adamson also worked genre pictures of Newhaven fishing life, and both surviving examples here turn a body at work into a single bent diagonal line.

## the monument's axis

Twice in this set the camera is pointed at a piece of stone architecture rather than a sitter, and both times Hill squares the structure to its own vertical axis while letting human incident sit off to one side.

## doorway and glass

Two informal pictures round out the chapter: Hill photographing himself, and Hill photographed among friends. Both trade the studio's plain ground for a real setting that the composition has to work with rather than clear away.

<Callout>
A word on process and honesty. These are calotype negatives, Talbot's 1841 paper process, contact-printed onto salted paper rather than the albumen paper that would replace it in the 1850s, and it is the paper negative's own broken fiber grain, not any lens or printing choice, that reads Hill's tightly posed groups as soft connected masses instead of crisp documentary inventories. No per-frame aperture, shutter speed, or paper sensitivity survives for any of the eleven plates here, and none is invented: calotype work of the 1840s predates any such log by decades, and exposure was judged entirely by the operator's eye and the day's light. What can be said plainly is that the Rock House sittings (01, 02, 05, 09, 10) held a sitter still for several seconds to perhaps a minute under strong north daylight, and the outdoor work (03, 04, 06, 07, 08, 11) required similarly long exposures with the camera leveled and stopped down for even focus across a deep or crowded scene, Willie Liston's coiled line included: a held pose, not a frozen instant, since no shutter of the era could freeze one. All eleven images are public domain, made between 1843 and 1848 by two men both long dead, drawn from Metropolitan Museum of Art Open Access CC0 prints and from Wikimedia Commons mirrors of Google Art Project and National Gallery of Art scans where the National Galleries of Scotland's own archive, the largest holding of this material, blocked automated access.
</Callout>

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Close the triangle">
Pose two people so that something they are already carrying, a bag, a shawl, a raised arm, closes the gap between their bodies into one continuous silhouette against a plain wall or sheet. Check afterward whether the shape reads as a single triangle rather than two separate figures, the way the overlay traces one unbroken outline through both Newhaven fishwives.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Square the monument, then add a diagonal">
Photograph a piece of architecture, a building, a gate, a monument, dead-center on its own vertical axis with the camera leveled. Then add one or more small figures along a diagonal line low in the frame, at the structure's base rather than its center, the way Hill sets his nieces along the Dennistoun tomb's diagonal. Compare the shot with and without the figures to see how much a single diagonal of human scale changes a purely architectural frame.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Stage labor as one diagonal">
Photograph someone in the middle of a repetitive task, coiling rope, kneading dough, stacking anything, and frame their bent body as a single diagonal line running from the eyes down to the hands doing the work. Hold the frame long enough that the pose, not a frozen instant, becomes the whole subject, the way Willie Liston's redding the line turns a still calotype into implied motion.
</ExerciseCard>
