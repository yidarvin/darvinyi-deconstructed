Nicéphore Niépce's two surviving pictures here do not yet behave like a
photographer's mature series. They are tests of whether light can leave an
image at all. But that makes their decisions unusually visible. In *View from
the Window at Le Gras*, the exposure is so prolonged that light seems to have
come from everywhere; in *La Table Servie*, every object is held in place so
the new process has something stable to describe. One picture discovers a
view; the other builds a stage for it.

## the window as armature

The overlay for *View from the Window at Le Gras* traces the two roof slopes
that enter from the upper corners and turn toward the faint distant roofline
at y0.331. They leave a pale opening between dark side masses, which is the
picture's real structure even when the courtyard itself is hard to parse.
The analyzer measures an extraordinarily low edge density of 0.012 and a
cool palette running from charcoal `#2c3132` through blue-gray `#656b6c` to
chalky `#cac6bf`; it also finds no vanishing point. That is persuasive
evidence for an image held together by broad tonal wedges rather than by a
clean perspective system. Its apparent vertical symmetry (0.982 around
x0.513) belongs partly to the plate and reproduction border, so the overlay
does not falsely turn that detector result into a claim about the courtyard.

## the table as proof

*La Table Servie* is much less accidental. The overlays mark the two side
edges and front edge of the cloth as a shallow tabletop stage, then anchor
the glass at x0.586, y0.587, the analyzer's strongest saliency region. The
objects do not recede far, but the table makes their sequence legible: bottle,
bowl, glass, and covered dish are held against an uneven dark ground. The
tonal spread is much wider than the window image: standard deviation 0.299,
from p05 0.008 to p95 0.820; the edge density rises to 0.049. Its palette
is nearly monochrome, from `#0f0f0f` through grays to `#c7c7c7`, so the glass
and white cloth register as the picture's small pool of light rather than as
local color.

<Callout>
The historical record does not preserve aperture, focal length, shutter
speed, or sensitivity for either image. The supplied sources describe the
window exposure only as lasting hours or days, and the files contain no EXIF
camera data. Those absences matter: the analysis can describe what the
reproductions visibly organize, but it cannot turn an experimental plate into
a modern exposure record.
</Callout>

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Build with two dark wings">
Frame a bright open area between two dark foreground masses. Do not look for a
subject first; make the negative space and the two entering diagonals carry
the image, as the window view does.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Give a still life a stage">
Arrange three or four objects on a cloth whose side and front edges remain
visible. Make the edges establish the shallow spatial box before you decide
where the focal object belongs.
</ExerciseCard>
