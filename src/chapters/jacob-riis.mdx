Jacob Riis's tenement photographs are organized by pressures: beds occupy one another's space, alleys funnel bodies toward a small opening, and doorways set a person against an impassable wall. The overlays name those visible relations, not diagnoses of the people in them. Riis used photographs alongside prose and public lectures to advocate reform, but that purpose did not neutralize the unequal conditions under which he and his collaborators made and captioned the pictures. In the Bayard Street image, the historical record is particularly stark: its surprise flash was made while sleepers were unaware. A formal reading has to hold both facts: the design is forceful, and the encounter was not an equal one.

The analyzer finds a constrained monochrome range throughout: charcoal #1a1a1a and #2f2f2f recur in the interiors and alleys, while flash or open sky reaches #b7b7b7 and #d2d2d2. The densest edges appear in *Pulling threads* (0.094), *Court at 24 Baxter Street* (0.092), and the Ludlow Street sweatshop (0.082); their rooms and yards are made from interruptions. *Five cents a spot* is unexpectedly less edged at 0.022, despite its crowding, because flash turns bedding and wall into broad tones. That difference is useful: density is not a moral measurement of hardship, but a way to see where detail, light, and enclosure carry the composition.

## crowded rooms, public claims

<Plate slug="jacob-riis" id="01-five-cents-spot" label="Five cents a spot, c. 1890" caption="An upper berth, a seated sleeper, and a crowded bed edge make the room read as stacked occupation rather than open depth." />

In *Five cents a spot*, the overlay frames the upper berth, circles the seated sleeper, and follows the shared edge of the bed beneath. The image's mean luminance is 0.55, with a broad p05–p95 range of 0.25–0.93; flash makes the pale foreground bedding push forward while the alcove retains its dark center. The low edge density is not a contradiction of the room's crowding. It describes the large tonal masses that allow the eye to register the stacked arrangement at once. The LOC and MCNY context matters here: this specific picture was made during a late-night police investigation with flash powder, and its occupants were unaware. The diagram marks beds and bodies without turning that fact into a spectacle.

<Plate slug="jacob-riis" id="03-bohemian-cigarmakers" label="Bohemian cigarmakers at work in their tenement, 1889" caption="A slanted worktable connects the working figure to a child and the adjoining domestic space." />

The cigarmakers picture links work and home through the worktable diagonal. The overlay connects its slant to the working figure, then marks the seated child as a second anchor. With mean luminance 0.38, p05 0.10, p95 1.00, and edge density 0.063, the flash creates a room of strong local contrasts: window, faces, tabletop, and bright right wall separate into zones. That visual clarity should not be confused with a complete account of labor. It is a selected, captioned reform image, and the overlay's claim is only that the furniture makes a route across the room.

<Plate slug="jacob-riis" id="04-pulling-threads" label="Twelve-year-old boy “pulling threads” in a “sweatshop,” 1889" caption="The foreground young worker interrupts a band of standing observers and a pile of work at the right." />

*Pulling threads* has the group's lowest mean luminance, 0.25, but one of its widest tonal spans (p05 0.08; p95 0.89) and its highest edge density, 0.094. The overlay circles the foreground worker, keeps the standing observers as a broad band rather than identifying individuals, and follows the work bundle at right. The frame is made of interruption: bright sleeve, dark coats, face, and piled material. The source title gives an age and labor description; the overlay does not infer more about the child or the surrounding people from the image.

<Plate slug="jacob-riis" id="08-sweatshop-ludlow-street" label="Sweatshop in Ludlow Street tenement, New York, 1889" caption="Two worktable runs and a seated worker divide the room into parallel bands rather than a single deep recession." />

The Ludlow Street room refuses a clean vanishing point. Two table contours run through the picture while the central standing figure blocks their apparent recession; the seated worker supplies a strong near-right anchor. The analyzer reports mean 0.32, p05 0.07, p95 0.80, and edge density 0.082. Its #1a1a1a to #646464 palette leaves the windows as bright interruptions, not neutral illumination. This is why the overlay declines the detector's proposed convergence: it follows actual tables and workers instead of making the room more orderly than the photograph is.

## alleys, thresholds, and distance

<Plate slug="jacob-riis" id="02-baxter-street-alley" label="Baxter Street Alley in Mulberry Bend, 1888" caption="Opposing alley edges narrow toward a bright opening, with a child held on the left stair." />

The central pressure in *Baxter Street Alley* comes from two ordinary edges: the right wall and left stair rail. They narrow toward the pale rear opening, while the child on the upper stair keeps the left foreground from becoming only a framing device. Mean luminance 0.44 and edge density 0.045 sit between the dark interiors and open yard views; #2f2f2f, #545454, and #7a7a7a separate wall, alley, and light without an artificial horizon. The overlay deliberately rejects the analyzer's high-confidence horizontal split because it is a brightness transition, not a visible skyline.

<Plate slug="jacob-riis" id="05-court-24-baxter" label="Court at 24 Baxter Street, 1888" caption="A staircase and rail cut through the yard toward a dark doorway, leaving children gathered at the lower wall." />

At 24 Baxter Street, the staircase is the picture's strongest diagonal event. The overlay tracks its rail toward the dark doorway and circles the children gathered beneath it. At mean 0.31, p05 0.07, p95 0.72, and edge density 0.092, the courtyard stays mostly dark while boards, laundry lines, railings, and faces keep breaking it into small incidents. The composition is not a survey plan of the yard. It is a compressed viewpoint in which the stair cuts across the available space and the doorway acts as an unlit stop.

<Plate slug="jacob-riis" id="06-mullens-alley" label="Mullen’s Alley, Cherry Alley, 1888" caption="The alley floor and right fence lead to a group of children below a blown-out opening." />

*Mullen's Alley* uses an almost theatrical light trap: the bright upper opening is too washed out to carry detail, so the eye returns to the children at the lower center. The overlay follows the alley floor and right fence as contours, then makes that group explicit. A mean of 0.43, p05 0.13, p95 0.83, and edge density 0.034 show how much of the picture works in broad light and dark rather than fine texture. The analyzer suggested symmetry, but the walls and illumination are not mirror images; the overlay leaves that false claim out.

<Plate slug="jacob-riis" id="10-baby-tenement-hallway" label="Baby in slum tenement hallway, N.Y.C., 1888" caption="A child stands at a doorway while the right-hand railing carries the hall away from the figure." />

The hallway image is built from an imbalance: nearly blank wall at left, a dark doorway with the child, and a sharp row of railings at right. The frame-in-frame and ellipse therefore do less than an invented perspective diagram; they identify the threshold where the child stands. Its mean of 0.56 is the highest in this group, but p05 0.07 and p95 0.87 reveal an extreme split between flare and shadow. The analyzer's proposed low “horizon” is the floor/print boundary rather than a meaningful structural line, so the overlay names only doorway and railing.

<Plate slug="jacob-riis" id="11-flat-hells-kitchen" label="Flat in Hell’s Kitchen, 1889" caption="An open doorway with people and a bed edge divide the small room into competing claims on space." />

In the Hell's Kitchen flat, the open doorway competes with the bed at right instead of leading comfortably into depth. The overlay frames the doorway, marks the people within it as a group, and follows the bed edge; these are three visible claims, not a story about their relationship. Mean luminance 0.48 and edge density 0.028 make it one of the less texturally dense images. The tonal range from #2b2b2b through #bebebe is carried by broad door, wall, bedding, and floor areas, while scratches in the reproduction are left unannotated.

## children, rooms, and repetition

<Plate slug="jacob-riis" id="07-playground-poverty-gap" label="Children’s playground, Poverty Gap, 1888" caption="A foreground child with a wheelbarrow stands before a distant band of children and buildings across a rough open lot." />

The Poverty Gap image provides an important spatial counterpoint to the alleys. Its open lot gives the foreground child and wheelbarrow room to register before the distant band of people and buildings closes the space. The phi grid fits the analyzer's measured structure better than thirds; the overlay uses it as a mild reference, not proof of intentional proportion. Mean 0.51, p05 0.15, p95 0.93, and edge density 0.064 place the picture among the brighter, more articulated views. The repeated wheelbarrows turn that apparent openness into a field of activity and work.

<Plate slug="jacob-riis" id="09-italian-mother-baby" label="Italian mother and her baby in Jersey Street, 1890" caption="The mother and baby are held between leaning boards and a dark doorway within a tightly bounded room." />

The mother-and-baby picture is modestly constructed but physically insistent. A short contour follows the actual boards leaning at left; an ellipse holds mother and baby together; the doorway at right is a black vertical counterweight. With mean 0.31, p05 0.09, p95 0.78, and edge density 0.075, the room is a dark set of walls, boards, fabric, and containers. The overlay does not extract the baby as a decorative focal point or claim a gaze; it keeps the pair within the enclosure that the photograph itself establishes.

<Plate slug="jacob-riis" id="12-going-to-bed" label="Going to bed in the Five Points House of Industry, 1890" caption="Rows of beds create a lateral rhythm, interrupted by a lit child at the front right." />

In *Going to bed*, repeated beds make a horizontal rhythm rather than a deep corridor. The overlay follows two bed-and-child alignments and marks the bright child near the front right. The picture has mean luminance 0.33, p05 0.11, p95 0.86, and edge density 0.038: its pale bedding pulls across the dark hall, while the children form a restrained counter-rhythm. A thirds grid is useful here because the relations are lateral and evenly interrupted; it is not a substitute for the actual bed rails and figures.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Map a room without making a diagnosis">
Photograph one small room using only a doorway, a bed edge, and one person as structural elements. Make an overlay that names visible relationships: edge, threshold, cluster, without assigning a social meaning to the person. Then remove any mark that is only a shadow, a scan blemish, or an assumed story.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Make an alley breathe">
Find a narrow passage and compose with two real edges leading toward an opening. Place one figure near either edge, not at a false central vanishing point. Compare a high-contrast version with a softer one: which preserves the corridor without hiding the person against the background?
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Use repetition and interruption">
Arrange repeated objects such as chairs, beds, tables, or containers across a frame. Add one person or object that breaks the rhythm. Mark the repeated contour and the interruption in an overlay, but do not add a symmetry axis unless the image truly mirrors itself.
</ExerciseCard>
