Vibeke Tandberg makes a photograph behave like evidence just long enough for it to become uncertain. Across the serial works selected here, a domestic room can contain two versions of the artist, a wedding portrait can keep its social grammar while changing its cast, and a frontal face can remain recognizably a portrait while its features stop agreeing. The source record documents staged personae, 1996 C-print digital montages, and specific black-and-white RC prints; the overlays stay with those visible arrangements rather than inventing undocumented technical procedure. [Source notes](../../content/vibeke-tandberg/sources.md)

## the ordinary room becomes impossible

<Plate slug="vibeke-tandberg" id="01-living-together-15" label="Living Together #15, 1996" caption="Two same-scale selves and the meeting of their hands turn a café table into a compact staged exchange." />

The doubled figures in *Living Together #15* lean toward the same table but do not simply mirror each other. Their ellipses make two separate bodies visible, while the small hand path supplies the hinge. The contrast is wide for such an intimate scene, from p05 0.118 to p95 0.976, and pale #f2f0ee window light is held against near-black #28272c and warm brown #615147. Ordinary perspective survives; singular identity does not.

<Plate slug="vibeke-tandberg" id="02-living-together-12" label="Living Together #12, 1996" caption="A broad room divide and measured recession hold two versions of a person in one plausible interior." />

Here the detector's room recession at x0.163, y0.797 and the lateral divide at y0.466 give the picture a stable spatial grammar. The two bodies disrupt that grammar by making one room contain incompatible presences. The pale blind field #e6e7e7 and dark purple #1c1021 make the figures' flesh and cloth unusually immediate.

<Plate slug="vibeke-tandberg" id="09-living-together-4" label="Living Together #4, 1996" caption="A domestic room divide and recession allow repeated figures to exchange across one coherent room." />

<Plate slug="vibeke-tandberg" id="10-living-together-6" label="Living Together #6, 1996" caption="Room recession, a bed edge, and repeated bodies make a bedroom scene materially credible and psychologically unstable." />

The later two domestic plates change the tempo. *#4* sends its room toward a low rightward recession, while *#6* turns the bed into a broad diagonal field. In both, the montage matters because furniture, wall planes, and light continue to behave consistently. Their dark bases (#181f23 and #1c1e26) let skin, bedding, and patterned cloth become the interruptions that prove the room's claim to ordinariness.

## a social template is repeated

<Plate slug="vibeke-tandberg" id="05-brud-5" label="Brud #5, 1993" caption="Bride and groom are presented as a paired vertical template, with close faces and costume doing social work." />

<Plate slug="vibeke-tandberg" id="06-brud-2" label="Brud #2, 1993" caption="The recurring bridal role is held against a changed partner inside the same formal portrait grammar." />

<Plate slug="vibeke-tandberg" id="07-brud-7" label="Brud #7, 1993" caption="Two closely placed portrait masses make serial variation visible inside an inherited wedding format." />

The *Brud* photographs use the compressed logic of the studio wedding portrait: faces close, clothing legible, background soft enough to suppress ordinary location. The overlays do not claim an actual symmetry the bodies fail to possess; they mark paired masses and their unequal head positions. White dress, dark suit, and flower accents concentrate attention in the figures against maroon and charcoal grounds. The change of partner is therefore not anecdotal; it is the change that the stable template makes unavoidable.

## portrait and persona are adjustable surfaces

<Plate slug="vibeke-tandberg" id="03-the-lost-ones-3" label="The Lost Ones #3, 2019" caption="A portrait axis and enclosing facial field make a localized deformation look all the more unstable." />

<Plate slug="vibeke-tandberg" id="04-the-lost-ones-7" label="The Lost Ones #7, 2019" caption="A frontal portrait format preserves the head's authority while its face refuses stable recognition." />

Both *Lost Ones* portraits use a calm vertical axis as a foil for facial mutation. Their palettes stay almost entirely inside black, gray, and pale gray (#0b0b0b to #ababab in *#3*; #0a0a0a to #a2a2a2 in *#7*). The sparse tonal language directs attention to contour and feature placement; the intervention is not hidden by visual noise.

<Plate slug="vibeke-tandberg" id="08-old-man-cowboy-5" label="Old Man Cowboy #05, 2022–23" caption="A riding axis, horse-and-rider mass, and raised rope pull a persona out of luminous smoke." />

*Old Man Cowboy #05* replaces the domestic room with a theatrical haze. The centered horse and rider become a dark vertical interruption against a nearly symmetrical smoke field, while the raised rope breaks that stability. Browns #4e463e and #766c62 shift toward the light #e8e3de behind the silhouette; the persona reads as an appearance produced by contrast and pose.

<Plate slug="vibeke-tandberg" id="11-old-man-staircase-79" label="Old Man Going Up and Down a Staircase #79, 2003" caption="A masked figure and two stair trajectories convert a landing into a steep performed descent." />

<Plate slug="vibeke-tandberg" id="12-old-man-staircase-82" label="Old Man Going Up and Down a Staircase #82, 2003" caption="A sloping handrail and masked figure organize a recurring old-man character inside a tall architectural frame." />

The stair images make costume into a directional problem. Rails and treads make a body answer architecture, while the suited, aged mask prevents the figure from settling into documentary character. Their monochrome palettes run from #181818 to #c1c1c1 and from #191919 to #f1f1f1: bright window and wall planes make the black suit a movable obstruction. The sources identify these 2003 works as unique black-and-white RC prints, but do not record the camera or exposure used to make them.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Make a room disagree with itself">
Photograph one room twice from a fixed viewpoint, changing only a figure's position or relation to another version of them. Preserve the furniture and light logic so the alteration is discovered through spatial consistency.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Repeat a social template">
Make three portraits with the same crop, background, and broad pose. Change one role-bearing detail, costume, partner, prop, or expression, and identify which fixed elements make the change read as a claim rather than a variation.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Let architecture direct a persona">
Place a costumed figure on a stair, corridor, or landing. Use one rail, seam, or run of steps as a real directional line, then position the body so the architecture changes the role rather than merely supplying a background.
</ExerciseCard>
