Jerry Uelsmann's pictures do not ask us to believe that the world was found this way. They ask a harder question: what makes an impossible world feel, for a moment, internally continuous? Across these combination prints, a horizon, mirror axis, piece of furniture, or cloud bank acts as a treaty between negatives. The diagrams identify those visible treaties; the documented printing context and image credits are in the [source notes](../../content/jerry-uelsmann/sources.md).

## continuity is the special effect

<Plate slug="jerry-uelsmann" id="01-magrittes-touchstone" label="Magritte’s Touchstone, 1965" caption="A low field-to-sky seam separates a sleeping face from a suspended rock without allowing either scale to settle." />

In *Magritte's Touchstone*, the field horizon is almost stubbornly ordinary. It divides sky from grass at y0.446, yet it also divides the picture into three incompatible scales: the vast hovering rock, the middle ground, and the near face. The overlay anchors the rock and rings the face because the photograph's tension is not an abstract surrealism; it is a precise disagreement about what is near. With p05 0.008 and p95 0.871, the display copy can keep black grass, pale cloud, and the rock's chipped highlights distinct enough for that disagreement to remain legible.

<Plate slug="jerry-uelsmann" id="02-poets-house" label="Poet’s House, 1965" caption="A tiny house is caught between a suspended tree crown and an inverted root mass." />

The house in *Poet's House* is deliberately too small to dominate by itself. It works as a hinge: the dark crown appears to float above it, while the branches below pull downward into an impossible root mass. The far tree band, measured at y0.445, is the quiet tonal boundary that permits the reversal. Its extreme reproduction range, p05 0.004 to p95 0.992, makes the white water and black foliage read as separate sheets rather than as muddy detail.

<Plate slug="jerry-uelsmann" id="03-untitled-1966" label="Untitled, 1966" caption="A giant palm contains a smaller standing figure while its lower edge hovers over the reclining body." />

The giant hand in *Untitled* is not merely a strange object placed over a portrait. Its spread fingers establish a canopy, and the lower palm edge makes a horizontal counterweight above the sleeper. The interior figure supplies a second scale inside that canopy. The overlay follows the palm silhouette and its rising diagonal so the eye discovers the inner figure before returning to the body below. This reproduction is comparatively open for the set, with edge density 0.084; the large tonal fields give the nested figures room to register.

## mirroring makes a scene less stable

<Plate slug="jerry-uelsmann" id="04-apocalypse-ii" label="Apocalypse II, 1967" caption="A doubled tree creates a single monumental axis above a low waterline and a small group of figures." />

*Apocalypse II* makes symmetry feel less like repose than a threat. The vertical tree axis is exact enough to turn branches into a single enormous canopy, while the waterline at y0.624 places five figures below it as a dark, human-scale interruption. The supplied grayscale palette runs from near-black `#09090b` through mid greys to `#cccbca`; against that range, the white limbs look simultaneously botanical and skeletal.

<Plate slug="jerry-uelsmann" id="05-small-woods-where-i-met-myself" label="Small Woods Where I Met Myself, 1967" caption="A reflection seam and mirror axis turn repeated figures and trunks into an enclosing forest." />

Here Uelsmann makes reflection a device for doubling a state of mind. A seam at y0.418 flips the woods into a dark lower register, while the central axis makes trunks and spaces echo across the frame without making every figure identical. The left upright figure and its inversion are a useful small counterpoint to that larger repetition. The high edge density, 0.225, records the visible thicket of trunks and branches in this display copy; the composition remains readable because the repeated verticals establish intervals rather than chaos.

<Plate slug="jerry-uelsmann" id="06-gemini-child" label="Gemini Child, 1968" caption="A paired body holds to a vertical axis, while the central disc makes symmetry into a dense psychological knot." />

*Gemini Child* lets bilateral doubling announce itself openly. The top and bottom figures meet through a vertical axis, but the central disc blocks any easy reading of the pair as a simple reflection. Its oval is a compact, dark counter-mass surrounded by pale sleeves and hands. The muted teal-grey display palette, `#272a2b`, `#344141`, `#486c75`, and `#64888a`, makes the warm brown disc feel even more inserted into the image's otherwise cold body of tones.

<Plate slug="jerry-uelsmann" id="07-untitled-1969" label="Untitled, 1969" caption="A floating island and rooted tree make a central axis out of mountain, water, and an impossible lower form." />

The 1969 *Untitled* layers several kinds of mirroring: mountain contours, water, the central tree, and the low seed-like form. The overlay keeps only the large island ellipse, its vertical axis, and the mountain-to-water band. Those few claims are enough to show how the picture holds its fantasy together: the island reads as a stable platform before its suspended tree and reflection disturb that stability. At edge density 0.091, this is much less busy than the wood scene, so the large tonal masses can bear more of the argument.

## rooms and landscapes become stages

<Plate slug="jerry-uelsmann" id="08-untitled-man-at-desk-1976" label="Untitled, 1976" caption="A genuine tilted tabletop, cornice, and fireplace opening make the cloud-filled room feel like an exposed theatrical set." />

The room in the 1976 *Untitled* starts with recognisable fixtures: a left cornice that turns into the rear wall, a patterned floor, mantel, fireplace, and a large tilted drafting table. The overlay follows the tabletop itself, separately traces the cornice's turn into the back wall, and frames the fireplace opening. Those ordinary anchors make the sky that fills the room feel more disorienting, not less. The display copy's broad luminance range (p05 0.012 to p95 0.671) keeps the table's bright plane distinct from the deep architectural framing.

<Plate slug="jerry-uelsmann" id="09-untitled-1982" label="Untitled, 1982" caption="A frontal house remains orderly only until its central axis continues downward as a root run." />

The house-root transformation succeeds because the upper façade is nearly ceremonial: frontal, centred, and bounded by a simple frame. Uelsmann then lets its centre continue down through a root run that cannot belong to architecture. The overlay avoids tracing every root; it names the façade, central axis, and one run so the decisive conversion remains visible. This display copy has an edge density of 0.176, enough texture to make the roots persuasive without overwhelming the clean gable silhouette.

<Plate slug="jerry-uelsmann" id="10-dream-theater" label="Dream Theater, 2004" caption="An arched inner stage and cathedral axis carry the gaze from an open book to a distant figure." />

*Dream Theater* is built from nested thresholds. The open book forms a near stage apron; a dark strip at y0.687 separates it from the luminous interior; the arch repeats around a tiny standing figure. The crow and the figure are small, but the architecture gives both a route through depth. With edge density 0.094 and palette steps from `#161513` to `#d2cfca`, the work has enough tonal restraint for the cloud mass to merge plausibly into the ornate interior rather than reading as a pasted background.

<Plate slug="jerry-uelsmann" id="11-voyager" label="Voyager, 2008" caption="A centred craft, its wake, and a mountain-water seam transform symmetry into a route toward uncertain scale." />

In *Voyager*, the eye first takes in a small hovering craft, then drops down the central axis to the solitary figure and spreading wake. The mountain-and-water seam at y0.747 stabilises that descent, while the radiating wake opens the foreground back toward the viewer. The image is sparse by this set's standards, with edge density 0.050; its pale palette (`#e6e3db`, `#c6c3bb`, `#a19d95`) gives the dark water and central forms unusual narrative force.

<Plate slug="jerry-uelsmann" id="12-forgotten-promise" label="Forgotten Promise, 2012" caption="A descending smoke column connects cloud ceiling, faint figure, distant field, and a grounded stone foreground." />

The late *Forgotten Promise* does not need a crisp architectural join. Its black smoke column bends down through a field of repeated clouds toward a translucent figure, then meets a band of distant land and close stones. The overlay follows that long column, the faint body, and the field band: three visible stages of a passage rather than a claim that the image has one ordinary perspective. Its p05-to-p95 range, 0.016 to 0.878, lets the photograph keep the smoke's black centre and the cloudy whites separate while the figure remains deliberately uncertain.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Make one seam carry two worlds">
Photograph a level horizon, wall edge, or table line. Make a second exposure whose tone could meet it, then test whether the seam feels structural before it feels like an effect.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Use a mirror axis without calming the picture">
Build a bilateral arrangement with a subject and its reflection or duplicate. Add one mismatched scale, object, or interruption at the centre so the symmetry creates tension instead of decoration.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Stage depth in three thresholds">
Place a nearby object, an intermediate frame, and a far target on one viewing axis. Before making the picture, identify the horizontal or vertical boundary that will let the eye travel from one threshold to the next.
</ExerciseCard>
