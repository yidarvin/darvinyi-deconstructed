Vivian Maier's pictures make looking feel conditional. A pane, mirror, screen, sign, wall, or bird often arrives before the supposedly central figure. The overlays do not claim a hidden universal geometry; they identify the actual barriers, apertures, and directional edges that make each view specific. Across self-portrait and street photograph alike, a person becomes vivid because the image does not give that person to us all at once.

## self-portraits made through surfaces

<Plate slug="vivian-maier" id="01-self-portrait-1954" label="Self-Portrait, 1954" caption="A shop window layers the photographer over cars and two seated women, making the central reflection an unstable public stage." />

In *Self-Portrait, 1954*, the large reflected photographer is only one layer. Cars cross the outer street while the outlined seated pair appears below her camera; the overlay uses that smaller rectangle to keep the image from reading as a solitary silhouette. A line at the street reflection makes the stack of transparent planes explicit. The analyzer's p05 of 0.110 and p95 of 0.965 describe a broad value range, while greys from #202020 through #a4a4a4 keep the layers distinct without assigning them separate worlds.

<Plate slug="vivian-maier" id="02-self-portrait-1971" label="Self-Portrait, 1971" caption="Reversed lettering, a ladder, circular window marks, and a small reflection make the storefront itself the picture's governing grid." />

In *Self-Portrait, 1971*, the figure is small enough that it must negotiate the storefront's other marks. The overlay traces an actual ladder leg, the transom, and the local panel containing the reflection. Reversed letters and repeated black circles keep redirecting attention across the broad glass before it returns to the camera. With edge density 0.0716, this is one of the more structurally busy pictures in the set; p05 0.035 and p95 0.933 account for the force of black lettering against the bright pane.

<Plate slug="vivian-maier" id="03-self-portrait-1961" label="Self-Portrait, 1961" caption="An interior window, an enlarged reflected face, and a bright round reflection turn the room into several overlapping depths." />

*Self-Portrait, 1961* does not arrange its reflections into a tidy illusion of depth. The window frame separates a small reflected photographer and the house across the street from a much larger face at upper left and the bright circular reflection at right. The overlay names those material apertures instead of treating the image as a single room. Its mean luminance of 0.385 and palette from #202020 to #adadad help explain why the bright circle reads as an interruption rather than a source of stable illumination.

<Plate slug="vivian-maier" id="04-self-portrait-1954-2" label="Self-Portrait, 1954" caption="A small tabletop mirror presents a face only after wire mesh has crossed the entire view." />

The force of this self-portrait is the refusal of clear access. The mirror is centered and visibly enclosed, but the mesh continues over both the reflected face and its boundary. The ellipse and frame do not call the mesh decorative: they show the two literal structures that make looking through the picture feel like looking through a second screen. This image has a low mean luminance of 0.252 and a relatively high edge density of 0.0793; bright mirror edges and the repeating wire do much of the structural work against near-black #0d0d0d.

<Plate slug="vivian-maier" id="05-self-portrait-1955" label="Self-Portrait, 1955" caption="A round mirror pulls the profile inward while the hand and camera break into the frame from above and below." />

The round mirror in *Self-Portrait, 1955* is not a neutral portrait device. It makes a dark enclosure around the profile, while the hand at the upper edge and blurred camera below deny the calm isolation the circle might promise. The overlay holds those three claims apart: mirror boundary, enclosing field, and edge intrusion. At edge density 0.0152, this is the least hard-edged image in the selection; the strong p05/p95 spread of 0.008 to 0.882 comes principally from broad black and pale passages rather than fine detail.

## streets structured by scale and obstruction

<Plate slug="vivian-maier" id="06-new-york-untitled" label="New York, NY, undated" caption="Library cornice, arches, and stair bands make the central passerby read against a civic architecture far larger than her." />

The New York Public Library façade supplies a strong system of repeated arches and columns, but Maier does not let it become an empty architectural study. A sharply dressed woman interrupts the façade at lower center, with a soft foreground band cutting across the bottom. The overlay follows the cornice, the arched field, and the stair rhythm because those are the actual large structures against which the figure must register. Its edge density is only 0.0318: the image depends less on a mass of detail than on a few broad, high-contrast architectural intervals.

<Plate slug="vivian-maier" id="07-new-york-fall-1953" label="Fall 1953, New York, NY" caption="Steam opens a pale space around a walking woman while the receding facade and dark doorway concentrate activity at right." />

In *Fall 1953, New York*, steam is compositional material. It clears a pale field at left, giving the woman walking toward the camera a space in which to separate from the surrounding men and masonry. The traced recession points through the pavement and façade to the doorway crowd, so the image does not flatten into a picturesque moment. The tonal span remains pronounced, from p05 0.027 to p95 0.835, and edge density 0.0569 is concentrated where steam gives way to coats, doorway, and brick.

<Plate slug="vivian-maier" id="08-milwaukee-untitled" label="Milwaukee, Wisconsin, undated" caption="A painted storefront and dark door make a narrow architectural stage for a standing smoker and seated companion." />

This Milwaukee photograph is ordered by its vertical partitions. Painted glass fills the left, a dark tiled corner cuts through the middle, and the decorated door gives the figures a hard right-hand boundary. The anchor sits on the smoking man's face rather than flattening the pair into a generic street group; the second person is deliberately lower and partially nested into the doorway. Its nearly black #121212, pale #d6d6d6, and edge density 0.0678 describe why the shopfront feels both graphic and materially worn.

<Plate slug="vivian-maier" id="09-chicago-march-1966" label="Chicago, IL, March 31, 1966" caption="A projecting sign and dark wall build a diagonal release into a narrow, bright street corridor." />

In *Chicago, March 31, 1966*, the man at left is not simply framed by the street; he seems to emerge from a dark vertical mass. The overlay traces the real sign-to-street diagonal and marks the wall and corridor as separate pressures. A second figure advancing from the distance makes the right side active, but the large black field keeps the nearer man's profile dominant. This is the set's highest edge-density result, 0.0918, with #101010 opposing #efefef and a p95 of 0.965, measurements consistent with the picture's sharp, compressed light.

<Plate slug="vivian-maier" id="10-new-york-untitled-2" label="New York, NY, undated" caption="Pigeons occupy the foreground in a wing-shaped sweep, delaying a clear reading of the young man behind them." />

The pigeons make the first claim on the frame in this last New York image. Their bodies and wings form a broad oval around the young man's lowered head and arms; the overlay follows that foreground field instead of pretending the face is the sole subject. The sign-filled street behind remains soft and secondary. With edge density 0.0362 and a grey palette from #151515 through #cbcbcb, the photograph retains enough detail to identify the birds while giving their blurred, close movement priority.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Make a surface do two jobs">
Photograph through a shop window, mirror, or screen. Let the surface both reveal a subject and interrupt that subject with a second space, text, or texture. Name the exact edge or reflection that makes the two spaces coexist.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Use architecture as pressure">
Place one person against a façade, doorway, stair, or wall that is much larger than the person. Make a version in which the structure merely backgrounds the figure, then make another in which its repeated elements actively direct the eye past or toward the person.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Let the foreground arrive first">
Use a near object, bird, hand, sign, or vehicle to occupy a large part of the frame. Keep the figure behind it legible but incomplete. Check whether the foreground creates a route through the image rather than simply obscuring it.
</ExerciseCard>
