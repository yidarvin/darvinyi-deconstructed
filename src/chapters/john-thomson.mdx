John Thomson's China photographs arrive here as damaged wet-collodion glass negatives, not as clean period prints. The plate edge, labels, scratches, and inversion are therefore part of what a reader sees. They also make the analyzer unusually fallible: most automatic horizons, symmetry axes, and saliency points follow the archival substrate rather than the photographed scene. These overlays name only features that survive visual review and use the plate boundary when the material object itself is the strongest frame.

Made between 1868 and 1872, the pictures belong to the treaty-port world, where a British commercial photographer selected and later captioned Chinese people and places for Western audiences. The archive does not generally preserve consent, names, or directions for sitters, so the chapter does not invent them. Wet collodion's portable-darkroom workflow helps account for the settled viewpoints, but it cannot settle questions of agency image by image.

## plate, portrait, and stereo pair

<Plate slug="john-thomson" id="01-great-wall-badaling" label="The Great Wall, Badaling, 1871" caption="The surviving negative is too faint for a strong scene claim; its plate boundary and distant tonal divide are the defensible structure." />

The Great Wall negative is almost consumed by its grey field. The overlay does not pretend that a corner artifact is a subject or that the detector's y=0.792 band is a horizon. A plate boundary, a faint tonal divide, and a broad ridge ellipse make an honest three-part reading. The analyzer's low edge density of 0.020 supports that restraint; the measured palette ranges from #72694c to #eef0ef, the image hovering between substrate and scene.

<Plate slug="john-thomson" id="02-manchu-bride" label="Manchu bride, 1871" caption="The bride's headdress forms the apex of a central portrait within a visibly damaged glass plate." />

In Manchu bride, the headdress, not the archival label in the upper corner, is the image's real apex. An ellipse holds the sitter, a short polyline follows the ornament's crown, and the plate boundary keeps the damage visible. The negative has a very low edge density of 0.013 and a light mean of 0.70; that thin tonal field makes the dark face and headdress concentrate the whole frame.

<Plate slug="john-thomson" id="03-manchu-lady-rock-garden" label="Manchu lady in a rock garden, 1869" caption="A standing figure and robe silhouette emerge from a damaged garden field rather than from an invented horizon." />

<Plate slug="john-thomson" id="04-mandarin-official" label="A Mandarin official, 1869" caption="A chair and portrait setting create a local frame for the seated official inside the larger glass plate." />

The two vertical portraits work by extraction. In the rock garden, an ellipse and robe contour find the woman inside a mottled field; in the official's portrait, a nested frame follows the chair setting around the seated figure. Their analyzer means, 0.53 and 0.51, are close, but the official's edge density of 0.031 is higher than the garden portrait's 0.021 because the chair and costume supply a firmer internal geometry. Neither reading relies on the scan's false symmetry or its handwritten margin.

<Plate slug="john-thomson" id="06-manchu-soldiers-self-portrait" label="Two Manchu soldiers with John Thomson, Amoy, 1871" caption="A central standing figure and paired seated figures make the stereo group legible as an arrangement, not as a detector artifact." />

This is the exceptional symmetry claim. The centered standing figure and paired seated figures visibly support the axis, unlike the generic axes proposed for most of the plates. The analyzer reports mean 0.59 and edge density 0.021; the broad stereo plate leaves enough empty field around the group for that arrangement to register.

<Plate slug="john-thomson" id="07-chinese-portrait-artist" label="Chinese portrait artist, Hong Kong, 1869" caption="Two stereo panels and their divider organize duplicated views of the artist at work." />

The portrait artist plate insists on being read as two images. Frames mark each stereo panel and a vertical polyline names their divider. This corrects the analyzer's high but misleading full-frame symmetry score: it is measuring the duplication, not a single composition. At mean 0.39 and edge density 0.063, the plate is the darkest and most textured of the set, its scratches competing with the little pictures inside the studio.

## relation, landscape, and the surviving field

<Plate slug="john-thomson" id="08-cool-shade" label="Cool shade, China, 1870" caption="A bridge curve and the small figures below it remain readable through foliage and the negative's damaged surface." />

<Plate slug="john-thomson" id="09-husband-wife-amoy" label="A husband and wife, Amoy, 1870–71" caption="Two sitters facing one another make an eye-line relation that is stronger than the glass plate's top label." />

Cool shade has a bridge curve and small figures beneath it, while A husband and wife is held by reciprocal looking. Both overlays refuse the analyzer's border-driven saliency: the bridge and figures occupy the lower right of 08, and the two faces hold 09. The latter's mean 0.49 and edge density 0.035 preserve a restrained tonal portrait; the former's mean 0.57 and edge density 0.030 leave foliage and plate damage almost equally present.

<Plate slug="john-thomson" id="10-praia-grande-macao" label="Praia Grande, Macao, 1870" caption="Far shore, mountain ridge, and a sailboat separate a harbour scene into three quiet bands." />

<Plate slug="john-thomson" id="12-travelling-fruit-seller" label="Travelling fruit-seller, Peking, 1869" caption="The seller and basket occupy the readable field left of a dominant archival strip." />

Praia Grande is the clearest landscape: two polylines identify the far shore and ridge, and a small ellipse locates a sailboat. Its mean of 0.59 and edge density of 0.038 describe a gentler transition from water to mountain than the analyzer's false lower-frame horizon. The fruit-seller plate requires the opposite strategy. The printed strip at right overwhelms the scan, so the overlay frames the photographic field to its left, then finds the seller and basket within it. Its low edge density, 0.011, explains why the plate label can so easily become the detector's false subject.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Separate image from substrate">
Photograph a picture, print, or screen with its border included. Before drawing a compositional guide, list what belongs to the depicted scene and what belongs to the object carrying it. Only annotate the latter when it changes the reading.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Build a relation">
Photograph two people facing one another. Let their eye-line or the space between them be the main structure. Do not impose a symmetry axis unless the arrangement visibly supports it.
</ExerciseCard>
