Timothy O'Sullivan's compositions run on one mechanism, repeated across a war and two federal surveys: set something small and legible against something too large to otherwise be read, and let the mismatch in scale do the work a designed composition would do elsewhere. A boat grounded on a canyon shore, a wagon and its own trailing footprints crossing a dune field, four tiny figures sheltering in a mission doorway, a survey member's silhouette dissolving into steam. Wherever a marker like that is available, the overlays in this chapter find it planted deliberately against rock, water, or sand that dwarfs it, and often placed by hand well away from wherever the analyzer's own raw saliency search happens to land, because the brightest patch of a frame and the point of the picture are frequently two different places. Where no such marker exists, as at the one frontal document shot of an inscribed rock face, O'Sullivan drops the device altogether and treats the wall as a wall, dead level, because the aim of that frame is legibility rather than scale. The two Civil War plates that open the chapter predate the survey work by several years but already carry its discipline: an elevated or oblique vantage that collapses a deep field into one legible plane, the same tool he would later turn on canyon walls instead of church pews.

## the mirrored canyon

Three plates hold a near-perfect vertical mirror down the center of the frame, and Black Cañon is the starkest of them. O'Sullivan's rented boat Picture sits grounded at x equals 0.39, y equals 0.64, the overlay's only scale mark against sheer canyon walls that the analyzer measures at a vertical symmetry of 0.93 on an axis at exactly x equals 0.5, the same axis the overlay draws as the canyon's mirrored walls. The overlay places its own vanishing point by hand at x equals 0.56, y equals 0.54, on the small notch of daylight between the converging walls, because the analyzer's own line-convergence search returns no vanishing point for this frame, unlike the raw finding in the Civil War council scene. The overlay also passes over the analyzer's raw saliency peak, which sits high on the canyon wall at x equals 0.71, y equals 0.32, in favor of anchoring the composition on the boat instead, since the brightest patch of rock is not the point of the picture. ## the measure on blank ground

<Callout>
On rights and camera settings, the honest record is thin and this chapter states only what survives. All eleven plates are United States government work made under military or Corps of Engineers commission, first the Union Army's Civil War photographic corps and then the King and Wheeler surveys, and none of it carries a copyright. Nine come from Library of Congress glass-negative holdings; two, the Massaponax Church interior and the Steamboat Springs fissure, survive as complete prints only in the Metropolitan Museum's Open Access collection, because their Library of Congress negatives are split or partial. Several Fortieth Parallel and Wheeler Survey negatives did not survive at all, glass being fragile and expendable once a print was pulled, which is part of why institutional holdings for this body of work are scattered across two collections rather than one. On exposure, wet-plate collodion predates any per-frame aperture or shutter record, and no light meter existed to log one. What can be said is qualitative: a stopped-down lens on a leveled stand camera held several seconds to tens of seconds of exposure even in strong daylight, long enough to smooth the Shoshone Falls water into a continuous sheet and to demand that any human figure in frame, at the boat, the wagon, or the mission doorway, hold still or register only as a small static mark. No specific f-stop, shutter speed, or sensitivity survives for any of these eleven images, and none is invented here.
</Callout>

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Plant one scale mark">
Find a landscape or interior too large or too complex to read as a whole, a canyon, a warehouse, an empty field. Place one small, legible object or person in it, a boat, a bag, a single figure, and let its size do all the work of establishing scale. Shoot the frame so the mark is unmistakably the smallest thing in it, the way O'Sullivan's boat sits grounded against the walls of Black Cañon.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Mirror the frame around a vanishing point">
Photograph a symmetric space, a hallway, an alley, a row of trees, from dead center so the two sides nearly mirror each other, and let whatever gap remains between them carry the eye back to a single vanishing point. Check your result against a vertical flip: if the two halves nearly match, you have built the same axis the analyzer measures at 0.9 or higher in Black Cañon, Vermillion Creek, and Shoshone Falls.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Shoot the same subject two ways">
Photograph one subject twice, once dead level and frontal, as if you were making a document or a record, and once from an oblique, elevated, or otherwise displaced vantage, as if you were catching an event in progress. Compare how differently the two frames organize the same information, the way O'Sullivan's frontal Inscription Rock and his raking Massaponax Church gallery view are separated by nothing but where he chose to stand.
</ExerciseCard>
