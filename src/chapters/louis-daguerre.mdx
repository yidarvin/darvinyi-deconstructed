Louis Daguerre's earliest surviving plates make duration visible as a
compositional tool. The street does not become empty because the city is
empty; it becomes empty because most of the city cannot hold still. In the
atelier, by contrast, every plaster face, basket, and frame is arranged to
stay put. These photographs make the fixed camera choose: architecture and
objects survive, while ordinary movement is edited out.

## a street cleared by time

In *Boulevard du Temple*, the left street edge runs from x0.036, y0.547 to
x0.455, y0.347, and the opposite roofline falls back from the right toward
the same narrow distance. The overlay labels that small opening rather than
pretending that the analyzer's strongest vanishing-point candidate, x0.700,
y0.246, is the street's literal center: it belongs to roof geometry at the
right edge, while the visible boulevard opens nearer the middle. The plate is
the chapter's brightest and busiest file, with mean luminance 0.518, standard
deviation 0.294, and edge density 0.131. Its palette spans `#202020` to
`#dcdcdc`, a harder black-and-white architecture than the other two plates.
The long exposure removes mobile traffic from that receding system, leaving
the street's fixed lines to do the work.

## an inventory of tones

The atelier still life takes the opposite approach: it converts the tabletop
into a deliberately layered test. The overlay follows the leaning relief from
the lower left to the cluster of casts, boxes the framed study above, anchors
a small plaster mask at x0.336, y0.640, and uses the tabletop as the lower
register. These are specific objects, not detector artifacts. The analyzer's
single broad saliency region covers the central group from x0.279 to x0.676,
and the tonal statistics run from p05 0.035 to p95 0.706, with a dark mean of
0.275. Its palette begins at `#111111` and never gets lighter than `#aeaeae`:
the picture makes pale casts emerge from a black room by value alone.

## across the river

The Pont Neuf plate is held by its foreground statue. The analyzer's saliency
peak lands exactly on it at x0.538, y0.706, so the anchor identifies the
Henri IV monument rather than forcing a false horizon through the image. From
there, a polyline follows the Pont Neuf deck across the middle distance and a
second climbs the right-bank roofs toward the dome. The file's edge density,
0.140, is the highest of the three; its p05/p95 range of 0.176 to 0.933 and
palette from `#363636` to `#e5e5e5` preserve both the bright sky and the dark
tree mass that encloses the river. The composition is therefore serial rather
than symmetrical: terrace statue, river, bridge, then skyline.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Let the fixed things compose">
From one viewpoint, make a picture in which a curb, roofline, or facade does
the directional work. Wait until moving figures stop being the main event,
then ask whether the permanent architecture still carries the frame.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Test a tonal range">
Build a small tabletop arrangement with one very light object, one very dark
object, and several midtones. Keep the arrangement still and judge it in
black and white: every object should remain legible through value and edge.
</ExerciseCard>
