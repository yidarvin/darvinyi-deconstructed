William Henry Jackson's Yellowstone photographs do not pretend that the land is immediately legible. They first give it a boundary: every one of these objects is a mounted albumen print, and the overlay begins by marking the printed image field rather than mistaking the mount edge for a natural horizon. Within that field, Jackson's views make geological scale readable through a few repeatable moves: opposed canyon walls that close toward water, a foreground rim that establishes a threshold, and a pale feature, whether river, fall, basin, or terrace, that holds the eye in otherwise overwhelming terrain.

## canyon as a funnel

*Great Falls of the Yellowstone* turns a vertical fall into the meeting point of two dark walls. The two traced canyon contours close toward the water without claiming an algorithmic vanishing point; the analyzer's proposed horizon at y0.816 is the lower edge of the printed photograph, not a landscape feature. The plate's restrained 0.016 edge density and warm palette, from #2e2c28 through #e2d4c0, leave the fall as a pale interruption inside a remarkably calm field.

<Plate slug="william-henry-jackson" id="01-great-falls-yellowstone" label="Great Falls of the Yellowstone, 1871" caption="The printed image field contains two canyon walls that funnel attention to the central fall; the detected lower-edge split is not treated as a horizon." />

*Columnar Basalts on Yellowstone River* uses a wider format and a gentler turn. The basalt brink travels across the upper left, while the river bends down through the middle into a dark stand of trees. Its mean tone is 0.67 and edge density only 0.013, a quiet tonal range that lets the angular geology register as an ordered shelf rather than a busy surface.

<Plate slug="william-henry-jackson" id="02-columnar-basalts-yellowstone" label="Columnar Basalts on Yellowstone River, 1878" caption="The basalt brink and the river's turn make a measured route through a wide canyon view." />

The two Grand Cañon views make the same operation clearer by changing distance. In the distant view, left and right descents make a deep V around the pale river; its #e5d7c3 to #35302b palette keeps the receding canyon in broad, separate value bands. From the riverbank, the walls instead rise around a bright water passage. The lack of a measured vanishing point in either analysis is useful: these are enclosures, not diagrams of linear perspective.

## water gives the landscape a center

At the Upper Falls, the overlay follows the river's bend and the left ravine edge. The image's 0.012 edge density is among the lowest in the group, so the large masses of forest and valley remain readable before the small white fall appears. The 1872 Great Falls view makes the opposite choice: its distant waterfall is the bright focal interruption, held against the right cliff face and a stepped foreground. The analyzer favours a phi grid for that plate; the overlay uses it, but the claim stays local to the visible fall and cliff rather than extending an abstract grid into the whole mount.

The Castle Geyser pair slows the sequence down. The 1878 crater plate has a bright mean tone of 0.75 and the narrowest tonal spread here, 0.17, so the low mineral mound reads as a quiet frontal still life. The earlier view places a large, visibly oval basin in the foreground and a low tree line behind it. The ellipse is an interpretive description of a real rim, not a claim that the basin is geometrically perfect.

## terraces as architecture

The Mammoth Hot Springs photographs turn mineral deposits into built form. In *Glen Spring*, a pale spring slope advances between dark branch screens; it is the busiest plate measured here, with an edge density of 0.029, because the branches make a textured foreground against the soft mineral rise. In the larger Mammoth view, the terrace edge repeats across the middle while the distant slope holds the upper right. Its brighter 0.72 mean and palette from #36312c to #e6daca make the terraces read as separate shelves of light.

The historical record supports a precise but limited technical account. The Library of Congress catalogs these 1871–78 Yellowstone survey objects as albumen prints, made during F. V. Hayden's U.S. Geological Survey of the Territories for the Department of the Interior. It does not record aperture, shutter speed, focal length, exposure duration, lens, negative format, or a per-image camera model for this group. The modern `Metis Systems` EXIF in the local manifest belongs to the scan, not to Jackson's camera, and no camera setting is inferred from it.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Make an overwhelming place legible">
Photograph a large landscape or built space with one pale or open feature inside darker enclosing masses. Before drawing any overlay, identify whether the eye reaches that feature by a wall, slope, shoreline, or true route. Draw only what you can point to.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Treat the object as part of the picture">
Photograph a print, page, window, or screen. Mark its internal image field first, then decide whether its border changes the composition. A detector may find a strong horizontal on the lower edge; that is evidence about the object, not necessarily a horizon.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Use value before detail">
Make one version of a scene with clear large light and dark masses, then inspect it in grayscale. If the route through the scene disappears without fine texture, simplify the composition until the major tonal structure carries it.
</ExerciseCard>
