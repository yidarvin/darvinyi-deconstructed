László Moholy-Nagy makes the camera impatient with ordinary eye level. These twelve works, made from 1922 to 1930, repeatedly exchange stable description for a visual problem: a cat becomes a tilted mass, a street becomes a plan, steel becomes a white lattice, and a target range becomes a diagram. The overlays make deliberately limited claims: they trace visible curbs, rope, rail, circles, figures, and contact edges, while rejecting detector “horizons” and vanishing points produced by texture or graphic overlap. The sources identify this period with the New Vision and, in the case of the photogram, with a cameraless process rather than lens perspective. ## a changed point of view

<Plate slug="laszlo-moholy-nagy" id="01-cat-seen-from-above" label="Cat, Seen From Above, c. 1926" caption="The cat's face anchors a high-angle view while its coiled body and whiskers drive back into the surrounding floor." />

In *Cat, Seen From Above*, the facial anchor lies near the lower middle; the two overlay paths follow the coiled body and whiskers back to it. The downward view denies the animal the usual portrait scale. Its measured tonal span, p05 0.314 to p95 0.937, and warm beige-to-dark-brown palette (#5f574b through #f7ece6) separate fur from floor with broad, quiet transitions; the low edge density, 0.014, keeps the figure massed rather than fussy.

<Plate slug="laszlo-moholy-nagy" id="02-climbing-the-mast" label="Climbing the Mast, 1928" caption="A climber's grip, taut rope, mast, and sail edge turn an upward view into a steep diagonal construction." />

*Climbing the Mast* puts the grip at the knot of rope, mast, and sail. The overlay follows those three long members rather than claiming a false spatial convergence from their intersections. Dark rigging against pale sail creates a wide p05–p95 span of 0.176–0.882; the charcoal #393231 is the needed counterweight inside a mostly pale palette. The camera’s upward angle makes the worker feel embedded in the construction rather than set before it.

<Plate slug="laszlo-moholy-nagy" id="03-7-am-new-years-morning" label="7 A.M. (New Year's Morning), c. 1930" caption="A walking pair, cyclist, far curb, tram track, and long shadow turn a street into a shallow oblique plan." />

In *7 A.M.*, the walkers and cyclist are tiny scale checks inside a large paving field. The far curb and tram track run as parallel, falling bands; the cyclist’s shadow makes the surface feel as active as the people. The restrained p05–p95 range of 0.380–0.733 and low 0.016 edge density explain the image’s unusual quiet: it is geometry made with slight changes in gray rather than hard contrast.

## circles, screens, and shadows

<Plate slug="laszlo-moholy-nagy" id="04-behind-the-back-of-the-gods" label="Behind the Back of the Gods, 1928" caption="A circular aperture, performer, suspension line, and crowd below keep the photograph's scale and orientation unresolved." />

The overlay for *Behind the Back of the Gods* stays with the circular opening and its suspended figures; it does not mistake the rim and rigging for a real vanishing point. An exceptionally bright mean luminance of 0.857 and a p95 of 0.941 leave the dark ring and crowd as compact interruptions in cream paper space. This is why the picture can feel both airy and disorienting.

<Plate slug="laszlo-moholy-nagy" id="05-decorating-work-switzerland" label="Decorating Work, Switzerland, 1925" caption="A roof worker, cast shadow, cable, and two windows make a facade read as an oblique worksite diagram." />

The worker at the roof window gives *Decorating Work* its human scale, but the wall is the larger actor. Cable, cast shadow, and the two window frames subdivide one pale plane into a construction. Its palette moves from deep #494641 to #f1ecd6, and p05 0.243 against p95 0.820 gives the window openings enough force to read as cuts, not decoration.

<Plate slug="laszlo-moholy-nagy" id="06-dolls-on-the-balcony" label="Dolls on the Balcony, 1926" caption="Two dolls are held in a balcony grid whose rail and lattice shadow turn the floor into an aggressive diagonal screen." />

The small dolls interrupt a much larger mesh of rail and shadow. Their two elliptical markers keep the claim literal; the diagonal rail and lattice shadow show how the balcony makes a cage-like field around them. At edge density 0.062, the photograph is far busier than the street or cat plates, and its p05 0.173 to p95 0.859 span lets the dark grid bite into the pale floor.

<Plate slug="laszlo-moholy-nagy" id="07-fischernetze-isola-bella" label="Fischernetze auf Isola Bella, c. 1930" caption="A worktable edge, fisher, hanging net curtain, and net-and-cloth diagonal make a screen the route into depth." />

*Fischernetze* makes a foreground obstruction do the work of perspective. The vertical net curtain and diagonal cloth force the eye through a mesh toward the fisher and the waterline. Its edge density is 0.068, among the chapter’s highest, and the dark brown-to-cream palette (#433c34 to #dacec0) is exactly the tonal opposition that keeps the loops legible instead of dissolving into texture.

<Plate slug="laszlo-moholy-nagy" id="08-fotogramm" label="Fotogramm, 1922" caption="A circular disc, bright mesh intersection, and two crossing mesh axes replace lens perspective with contact and light." />

*Fotogramm* is the important exception: it is cameraless. The overlay identifies a circular disc and crossing mesh rather than inventing a horizon or a perspective point. The analyzer finds the chapter’s darkest mean, 0.209, with p95 only 0.490; black-brown #272220 holds most of the field while the mesh intersection supplies a small luminous event. Contact, opacity, and translucency become the composition.

## looking down into machinery and control

<Plate slug="laszlo-moholy-nagy" id="09-from-the-radio-tower-berlin" label="From the Radio Tower, Berlin, 1928; printed c. 1940" caption="An elevated track, curving path, and circular plaza turn the city below into a shallow, diagrammatic field." />

The high view in *From the Radio Tower, Berlin* makes the elevated track a strong upper cut and the plaza a large low counterweight. The circle is not a target in a literal sense; it is a compositional anchor inside a city reduced to paths and dots. p05 0.196 to p95 0.902 gives the aerial field its bright, graphic separation. The supplied Met record dates the image 1928 and the print circa 1940.

<Plate slug="laszlo-moholy-nagy" id="10-pont-transbordeur-marseille" label="Pont Transbordeur, Marseille, 1929" caption="A truss diagonal, compact central mechanism, and descending tower leg make the bridge a near-abstract steel field." />

In *Pont Transbordeur*, the overlay uses one measured truss diagonal and the central mechanical concentration, with a broad ellipse only to name the field of steel. The high edge density, 0.061, and wide p05–p95 span of 0.153–0.937 measure why the bridge reads as a glare-and-shadow lattice rather than a distant view of a harbor.

<Plate slug="laszlo-moholy-nagy" id="11-scandinavia" label="Scandinavia, 1930" caption="A circular hatch, sailor, worker, foreground rail, and rope line compress a deck into intersecting planes and hard tonal cuts." />

*Scandinavia* is the set’s most tonally extreme camera-made plate: p05 0.039, p95 0.988, and edge density 0.083. The circular hatch and two people give the deck local anchors, but the foreground rail and rope line keep breaking them apart. Black #17140d, near-white #f9f6f0, and the intervening gray structure produce an almost Constructivist collision of planes.

<Plate slug="laszlo-moholy-nagy" id="12-target-practice" label="Target Practice (In the Name of the Law), c. 1927" caption="Three circular zones, a diagonal track boundary, and a mounted figure make a sparse field of loops, bodies, and control." />

*Target Practice* nearly empties the frame. The three circles isolate groups and a lone figure while the diagonal boundary and mounted figure give the blank field direction. The very high mean, 0.923, and narrow p05–p95 range of 0.729–0.980 make the black circles and bodies behave like drawn notation on paper rather than ordinary scene detail.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Refuse eye level">
Photograph a familiar subject once from above and once from below. In each frame, identify the one structural edge that takes over when ordinary scale disappears.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Make a screen do the work">
Use a fence, net, rail, or shadow lattice in the foreground. Keep one small figure or opening beyond it so the obstruction creates depth rather than merely blocking the view.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Compose without a lens">
Make a simple photogram with translucent and opaque objects on photographic paper, or simulate the exercise with a flatbed scan. Build the picture from overlap, contact, and tonal weight rather than perspective.
</ExerciseCard>
