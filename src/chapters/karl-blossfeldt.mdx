Karl Blossfeldt never called himself a photographer. He was a sculptor who taught modelling from living plants at the Berlin Museum of Decorative Arts, and he built his own copy-stand cameras for one purpose: to hold a single dried stem or seed head frontally in front of plain gray cardboard, at magnifications running from roughly 3x up to 30x or more, and record its structure for students to draw. That narrow discipline produced the most compositionally pure archive in this book. Strip a plant of its background, light it in flat diffuse daylight so nothing is raked into hard shadow, and center it, and what remains is geometry. The overlay analyzer, running blind on these scans, keeps measuring the same three things Blossfeldt was after: a vertical axis, a mirror across it, and a curve the plant grew by. This chapter follows those three signatures through the twelve plates.

## the specimen on the axis

The clearest place to start is the plainest specimen. Rough horsetail is a jointed stalk of repeating fluted segments, one length of it stood dead-center, and the analyzer returns the highest symmetry score in the set: 0.97 across a vertical axis pinned exactly at the frame center, with horizontal symmetry at 0.95 to match. That is close to a machine reading of perfect. The tonal range is bright and shallow, a mean near 0.80 lifting to a p95 of 0.98, so the pale column sits on an even paler ground with only the node rings and the terminal spore cone to break it. There is nothing here but the axis and the modular spacing along it, which is exactly the lesson.

Bear's breeches is the leaf that became the Corinthian capital, and Blossfeldt frames it to prove the debt. The analyzer locks a bilateral axis at frame center with a vertical symmetry of 0.92, and the overlay stacks identical spiny bract modules in vertical repetition along it. The tonal reach is wide, from a p05 of 0.14 up to 0.86, which is what gives the deep lobes their carved relief. Himalayan balsam works the same spine differently: a vertical stem carrying modular whorls of opposite branches, mirrored top to bottom, symmetry 0.86 about a center axis. The specimen reads like a wrought-iron candelabra precisely because Blossfeldt trimmed away everything that was not structure.

## the coil the plant already knew

Blossfeldt loved the curl a growing tip makes before it opens. Northern maidenhair fern is the textbook case: six croziers rising on S-curving stems from a common base, each unrolling on the same logarithmic spiral. The overlay draws that golden spiral onto the lead crozier and marks how the others repeat it. This plate also carries an honest warning about plain grounds. The line detector found a horizon at mid-frame and a vanishing point low in the image, both with high nominal confidence, but they are background false positives off the flat card and were dropped from the overlay. Symmetry falls to 0.65 here because a spiral is not a mirror, and that lower number is itself the evidence that this specimen belongs to the curve family rather than the axis family.

<Plate slug="karl-blossfeldt" id="03-adiantum-pedatum" label="Adiantum pedatum" caption="Six fern croziers repeating one logarithmic spiral on S-curving stems; a near-neutral gray palette and a symmetry of 0.65 mark this as a curve, not a mirror." />

Birthwort gives the coil a mirror. Two coiling stems flank a central vertical axis at frame center, symmetry 0.82, with a tendril crozier curling below, so the specimen is bilateral and spiral at once. The gourd tendril is the purest curvilinear form in the set: a straight vertical stem disciplines tendrils that spring off in every direction, a tight helical spiral lower-left, a bright-eyed loop upper-right, a looser loop upper-left. Its symmetry reads 0.71, lower than the axial specimens because the coils deliberately break the mirror, and the warm cream-and-brown palette keeps the bright tendril eye as the saliency peak.

## mirror pairs and the ribbed capsule

Two plates put the coil and the mirror in tension. Cut-leaved teasel makes its central stem the mirror line: two thorn-edged bracts inroll into crozier coils that answer each other across a bilateral axis at frame center, symmetry 0.88. It is also one of the highest-contrast compositions here, a deep p05 of 0.08 against a bright ground for a wide tonal reach, which is what lets the thorns read as sharp edges. The star-shaped seed capsule of Blumenbachia hangs pendant from its pedicel, bilaterally symmetric at a striking 0.95 while its ribbed surface twists in a spiral, so radial order and a rotational twist sit in the same object. Its ground is the darkest yet, mean 0.57, which pushes the pale capsule forward.

## radial crowns from a single node

The last structural idea is the burst: many parts fanning from one anchor. Ornamental garlic sends a single stem up to a ribbed capsule that opens into a broad radial umbel of buds, the overlay reading one mirror axis and a leading stem into the crown, symmetry 0.91. The Oriental poppy capsule is the canonical radial seed head, a fluted crown bursting atop an ovoid urn, all organized on one vertical spine at 0.83. Poppy is also the tonal outlier: a low mean of 0.35 with a compressed p05-to-p95 reach of 0.13 to 0.71, so the whole specimen lives in the dark register and the palette leans to deep browns rather than the usual creams. Moon carrot pushes the idea into architecture: a fluted trunk on the vertical axis forks and erupts into a crown of pointed buds. Its symmetry is only 0.65, but its edge density of 0.201 is by far the highest measured, a reading of just how many fine bud tips crowd the frame.

## the comparative plate and the gray ground

Blossfeldt also taught by lining specimens up. The bottlebrush buckeye plate sets three parallel buds in serial rhythm, each a bilaterally symmetric bud-head crowning a node-ringed stem, symmetry 0.89 about an axis shifted right of center to 0.62 because the trio is read as a group rather than one centered form. Serial repetition was his way of turning a single portrait into a chart.

<Plate slug="karl-blossfeldt" id="10-aesculus-parviflora" label="Aesculus parviflora" caption="Three parallel specimens in serial rhythm, each a bilaterally symmetric bud-head on a node-ringed stem; the mirror axis sits at 0.62, reading the trio as a group." />

Across all twelve, the flat cardboard ground is the quiet engine. The palettes cluster into warm creams and browns for most plates and near-neutral grays for the fern, and the grounds hold an even middle-to-high gray that never competes with the specimen. That is why the line detectors sometimes hallucinate a horizon on an empty card, and why the symmetry numbers are trustworthy: there is nothing else in the frame to measure. Blossfeldt removed the world so the geometry could not hide.

<Callout>
A note on exposure: none of these plates carries a recorded camera setting. Blossfeldt built his own bespoke cameras and left no per-frame log of aperture, shutter, or focal length, and the digital scans hold only scanner metadata. What follows from the method is qualitative only. High-magnification close-up work in flat diffuse daylight implies small working apertures for depth of field and correspondingly long exposures of dead-still specimens. Any specific f-stop or shutter speed would be invented, so none is given.
</Callout>

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Find the axis, then break it">
Photograph one plant part frontally against a plain wall or sheet of card, centered, in soft window light. Shoot it twice: once as a strict bilateral mirror like the horsetail, once with a coil or fork that deliberately breaks the mirror like the gourd tendril. Compare how centered the axis feels in each.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="One tone, one form">
Pick a pale specimen and a flat mid-gray ground and expose so the object separates on tone alone, no cast shadow. Aim for a long even gray scale from deep shadow to near-white, the reach that makes Blossfeldt's stems look carved. Then try a dark specimen like the poppy capsule and notice how much narrower the usable range becomes.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="The comparative plate">
Choose three examples of the same structure at different stages, a bud opening for instance, and line them up in serial rhythm as Blossfeldt did with the buckeye. Keep the spacing even and the scale matched so the eye reads the sequence as a single chart of one form changing.
</ExerciseCard>
