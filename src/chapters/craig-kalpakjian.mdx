Craig Kalpakjian’s interiors are persuasive enough to make ordinary architectural
logic feel unreliable. A corridor, lobby, ceiling, or dark service space supplies
the familiar vocabulary, but every relationship is unusually clean: a floor edge
becomes a route, a reflected wall doubles the room, or a single circular fixture
takes over as the image’s only event. The source record identifies these as
computer-generated works, so the chapter does not mistake their apparent
photorealism for evidence of a camera encounter. Instead, the overlays name the
visible seams, apertures, axes, and interruptions that make the spaces work.
[Source notes](../../content/craig-kalpakjian/sources.md)

## rooms that steer the eye

<Plate slug="craig-kalpakjian" id="01-lobby" label="Lobby, 1996" caption="A ceiling reflection and a low glass edge split the room into two oblique registers, making a shallow lobby feel spatially doubled." />

*Lobby* makes its broad floor look calm only until the glass wall begins to repeat
the ceiling. The overlay’s ceiling-to-wall register at y0.394 gives the reflection
a stable base, while the two diagonals separate the reflected room from the visible
one. With mean luminance 0.405, p05/p95 0.184/0.698, and edge density 0.0271, its
gray field is restrained enough that a few bright panels and black seams can do all
the spatial work.

<Plate slug="craig-kalpakjian" id="02-hallway" label="Hallway, 1996" caption="A lit threshold, a doorway lintel, and a nested opening turn an apparently empty room into a sequence of withheld access." />

In *Hallway*, the bright opening is not a destination so much as a structural
puncture in a dark field. The lintel at y0.300 and the internal frame make the
viewer register one enclosure through another. Its low edge density, 0.0079, and
compressed p05/p95 range of 0.224/0.545 make that small threshold more forceful
than a busy architectural view would be.

<Plate slug="craig-kalpakjian" id="03-corridor" label="Corridor, 1997" caption="A curved floor-edge route and a measured pull at the bend make the bright corridor lead forward without promising an exit." />

The apparent openness of *Corridor* is disciplined by curvature. The floor-to-wall
register at y0.542 runs into the bend at x0.569, y0.551, while the left floor edge
supplies the approach. The analyzer’s p05/p95 values of 0.329/0.886 and palette of
#4d4d4b, #727069, #908f87, and #d7dfe0 describe a luminous gray system rather than
a naturally changing scene.

## containment, reflection, and control

<Plate slug="craig-kalpakjian" id="04-corridor-ii" label="Corridor II, 1998" caption="A centered aperture and low terminal seam pull the dark image toward one carefully framed zone of light." />

*Corridor II* is nearly a diagram of containment. The central axis and receding
aperture hold the dim room upright; the seam at y0.653 establishes a late, low stop
before the reflection resumes below it. Mean luminance is only 0.112, with p05/p95
0.051/0.365, so the lit ceiling break is not incidental decoration but the limited
evidence that the space continues.

<Plate slug="craig-kalpakjian" id="05-monitor" label="Monitor, 1998" caption="A reflective ceiling dome interrupts the grid and encloses a second, smaller interior within its dark curved surface." />

The supplied *Monitor* image does not offer a conventional object against a room;
it makes a dark reflective dome the room’s visual event. The ellipse names the
fixture rather than forcing a fictive corridor onto its reflection, while a ceiling
seam and vertical axis show how the grid intensifies its oval interruption. Mean
luminance 0.190 and p05/p95 0.071/0.267 keep the reflection guarded, with the small
bright opening inside it doing the narrative work.

<Plate slug="craig-kalpakjian" id="06-hvac" label="HVAC, 1999" caption="A bright central axis and a gridded chamber hold the near-black image in a severe, frontal mechanical order." />

*HVAC* gives repeated bars and a bright central core the force of a façade. The
framed chamber and central axis make the image frontal even while reflections and
dark margins loosen its geometry. This is the selection’s most textural plate by
the analyzer’s measure: edge density 0.0972, with a range from black p05 0.000 to
p95 0.584. The high contrast makes the repeated grid read as pressure rather than
information.

## small interruptions in blank systems

<Plate slug="craig-kalpakjian" id="07-hall" label="Hall, 1999" caption="A floor-edge route and a small first ceiling light establish the long hall as a controlled recession rather than an open passage." />

In *Hall*, the rightward corridor is made from very few decisions: a sloping
ceiling-wall edge, one floor edge, and lights shrinking toward the distance. The
overlay’s route stays on the literal floor boundary; it does not claim that the
image has a theatrical central vanishing point where the visual evidence is less
clear. The smooth result is measured by edge density 0.0077 and p05/p95
0.259/0.565, letting the repeated white lights act like a metronome.

<Plate slug="craig-kalpakjian" id="08-dark-hall-ii" label="Dark Hall II, 2000" caption="A subtle shadow threshold and two receding boundaries make a nearly black frame release only a narrow amount of usable space." />

*Dark Hall II* tests how little visible information an architectural image can
retain. The line at y0.405 identifies a shadow threshold; the wall and floor
boundaries only gradually disclose the room. Its mean luminance is 0.101, p05/p95
0.047/0.145, and edge density just 0.0010. Those readings support the overlay’s
conservatism: darkness, not a hidden event, is the composition’s primary material.

<Plate slug="craig-kalpakjian" id="09-stair" label="Stair, 2001" caption="A ceiling-plane seam and a circular opening make the cropped passage feel like a suspended architectural puzzle." />

The plate titled *Stair* reduces circulation to a single tilted circular fixture
below a long horizontal seam. The overlay lets that fixture be the subject rather
than inventing a complete stair that the frame does not show. The palette stays in
dark olive-grays (#1b1a15, #28261e, #444234, and #686553), and p05/p95 0.094/0.278
hold the small bright interior of the opening in check.

<Plate slug="craig-kalpakjian" id="10-co" label="C.O., 2002" caption="One diagonal ceiling corner turns nearly blank planes into a cropped, unstable enclosure." />

*C.O.* makes blankness precise. The diagonal ceiling corner is the dominant event;
the pale planes remain deliberately spare rather than resolving into a fully
described room. Mean luminance rises to 0.801, with p05/p95 0.663/0.945 and edge
density 0.0062: this is the lightest, smoothest kind of constraint, where a small
directional edge has to carry the whole image.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Turn one seam into a route">
Photograph or render an interior with a floor edge, glazing seam, ceiling joint, or
baseboard that crosses the frame. Make a version where that line leads somewhere
and a version where it stops abruptly. Compare which version makes the room feel
more controllable.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Let a fixture become the subject">
Use a small architectural interruption, such as a vent, mirror, light, camera dome,
or opening, as the only strong object in a broad field. Keep the surrounding planes
simple enough that the fixture changes the scale of the whole image.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Build depth from controlled repetition">
Work with ceiling panels, lights, doors, tiles, or posts. Set one repeated sequence
on a clear route, then alter one interval, brightness, or angle. Keep the frame in
which the variation changes how far the eye believes the space extends.
</ExerciseCard>
