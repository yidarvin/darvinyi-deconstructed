Eugène Atget photographed an empty city on purpose. Working a large wooden view camera on a tripod in the first light of morning, from about 1890 until his death in 1927, he built a vast survey of old Paris: shopfronts, cobbled alleys, courtyards, staircases, park statuary. The streets are swept clean of traffic and people because the light was flat and the plates were slow, and what that emptiness leaves behind is pure structure. Run the analyzer across twelve of his plates and the same two instincts keep surfacing in the numbers. One is deep one-point perspective: lines that converge, again and again, on a single measured vanishing point. The other is the layered vitrine, where a pane of shop glass folds three planes of the world into one frame. This chapter follows both.

## the vitrine that collapses three planes

Atget's storefronts are the clearest statement of his method because the glass does the composing for him. In the Gobelins plate the overlay marks a frame within the frame -- a reflected arch -- sitting under a polyline frieze of four suited mannequin heads, and beneath them a leading line drawn along the display floor. That floor line drives to a vanishing point the analyzer places at x 0.7, y 1.05, which is to say below the bottom edge of the picture. The perspective is real but its target is off the stage entirely. The tones stay in a narrow warm band, mean near 0.40 with a p05-to-p95 reach of 0.21 to 0.75, and the palette is all browns, from a near-black 483b37 to a pale cbbcaf, the register of a warm contact print rather than a scene lit for drama.

The Boulevard de Strasbourg coiffeur works the same seam differently. Here the analyzer settles the frieze of mannequin busts on the horizon line at eye level, y 0.44, with the lead bust anchored left at x 0.26. The uncanny stare is set exactly where a passerby's eyes would meet it, while the Paris street ghosts across the glass as a faint reflection. The palette runs the same warm browns; the busts read as a proscenium of heads staged on a phi grid rather than as merchandise.

## the perspectival throat

Take the glass away and the same drive toward a vanishing point structures Atget's open streets. The corner of the rue de Seine and the rue de l'Échaudé funnels a cobbled alley down to a luminous passage; the overlay reads the cobble rut and the left wall base as two leading lines converging on a vanishing point at x 0.74, y 0.63, with a frame within the frame at the far passage. The tonal reach is long, 0.07 to 0.72, so the dark alley walls hold their shadow while the distant opening blows bright. Two children watch from a high window, small enough that the analyzer never flags a face -- the composition is the street, not the sitters.

<Plate slug="eugene-atget" id="02-corner-rue-de-seine-echaude" label="Corner of the rue de Seine and the rue de l'Échaudé, 1924" caption="A cobbled alley funnels to a luminous passage, cobble rut and wall base converging on a vanishing point two-thirds down the frame." />

Place Saint-Médard tilts the same throat sideways. The rue Mouffetard recedes to a vanishing point at x 0.30, y 0.52, and here the horizon itself carries a measured tilt of nearly 5 degrees, the ground line running downhill as the market square actually did. This is the brightest of the street plates, mean 0.53 and a top end of 0.87, the sunlit foreground crowd anchoring human scale between the billboard wall and the church.

<Plate slug="eugene-atget" id="05-place-saint-medard" label="Place Saint-Médard, 1898 to 1900" caption="The rue Mouffetard recedes to a vanishing point left of center on a ground line that tilts nearly five degrees, the sunlit crowd holding the human scale." />

The Cour de Rohan is the cleanest convergence in the set. Read as one-point perspective, the flaking left wall and the right rooflines funnel to a vanishing point dead on the eye-level line at x 0.56, y 0.53, and the analyzer fits it with sixteen inliers at full confidence -- an unusually strong lock. This plate also has the widest tonal reach of any here, 0.11 to 0.94, the flaking plaster carrying both deep shadow and near-white highlight, and a vertical symmetry score of 0.90 that confirms how squarely Atget boxed the courtyard.

<Plate slug="eugene-atget" id="06-cour-de-rohan" label="Cour de Rohan, 1915" caption="An empty courtyard read as one-point perspective, left wall and rooflines funneling to a vanishing point centered on the eye-level line with a strong sixteen-line fit." />

## the frontal stage

When Atget turns to a doorway or a staircase he flattens the world into a shallow stage and lets one interior line do the perspective work. In the Hôtel de Brinvilliers the straight upper rail recedes to a one-point convergence high in the frame at x 0.40, y 0.26, then the analyzer hands off to the curving wrought-iron banister that sweeps down to the newel. Eleven of the twelve detected lines are diagonal: the whole image is motion, coiling, yet nobody is on the stairs. The stone-arched doorway frames it like a set waiting for an actor.

<Plate slug="eugene-atget" id="07-staircase-hotel-de-brinvilliers" label="Staircase, Hôtel de Brinvilliers, rue Charles V, 1900" caption="The straight upper rail converges high in the frame, then hands off to a curving iron banister sweeping to the newel, an empty stage framed by the arched doorway." />

The rue Asselin doorway pins three women into that same shallow box. The overlay is a frame within the frame -- the dark opening -- with a polyline running across three faces in a horizontal row and a horizon set on the crumbling stone base at y 0.69. The faces sit in the upper third, the subject anchor at y 0.33, so the women read as a frieze pressed forward by the void behind them. As with the alley children, the analyzer registers no face at all; the architecture wins.

<Plate slug="eugene-atget" id="11-three-prostitutes-rue-asselin" label="Three Prostitutes, rue Asselin, 1924 to 1925" caption="A frontal doorway presses three women into a shallow stage, their faces running in a horizontal row across the upper third above the seam of the stone base." />

## still water and radial nature

Two of Atget's obsessions loosen the grid without abandoning it. The Pont Neuf plate is his deep-space quay: moored barges recede toward the distant bridge across the Seine, a lone mast standing as a vertical caesura, the city dissolving in fog. The waterline sits low at y 0.64 and the frame is nearly mirror-perfect, a vertical symmetry score of 0.94 -- the highest in the chapter -- because the long exposure has stilled the river into a reflecting plane.

<Plate slug="eugene-atget" id="04-pont-neuf" label="The Pont Neuf, 1902 to 1903" caption="Moored barges recede toward the distant bridge, a lone mast a vertical caesura, the still Seine returning a near-perfect vertical symmetry." />

Saint-Cloud Park pushes the same stillness into a decayed formal garden. A ruined ivy-clad balustrade recedes right past a massive stone plinth, braced by the vertical ivy trunk under backlit winter trees; the leading line of the balustrade and the vertical symmetry of 0.91 hold the picture between recession and mirror. The tonal reach is long, 0.09 to 0.91, backlight burning the sky white behind the near-black ivy.

<Plate slug="eugene-atget" id="09-saint-cloud-park" label="Saint-Cloud Park, 1924" caption="A ruined ivy-clad balustrade recedes past a stone plinth under backlit winter trees, held between deep recession and a near-vertical mirror symmetry." />

The tree-roots study is the one plate where Atget drops architecture entirely, and the analyzer answers with its densest edge reading, 0.143, more than double the storefront plates. Instead of converging on a distant point, the roots radiate from a crown at x 0.49, y 0.60, with the trunk running down the vertical spine as a symmetry axis and the roots sprawling outward as leading lines. It is a vanishing point turned inside out: a burst instead of a funnel.

<Plate slug="eugene-atget" id="08-tree-roots-saint-cloud" label="Tree Roots, Saint-Cloud, 1919 to 1921" caption="A radial burst rather than a funnel: the trunk descends the vertical spine to a root crown from which roots sprawl outward across the densest-edged frame in the set." />

## the frieze of upturned faces

Two crowd plates close the chapter by treating people the way Atget treats mannequins: as a horizontal frieze read against deep architecture. The Fête du Trône carousel is shot just off frontal, so the analyzer finds both a central symmetry axis, vertical score 0.91, and a canopy rim that throws a one-point perspective converging at x 0.79 where the ladder rests. The platform reads as an ellipse, the empty cobbled foreground as a deserted stage floor -- a fairground caught before the crowd arrives.

<Plate slug="eugene-atget" id="10-fete-du-trone" label="Fête du Trône, 1925" caption="A radially symmetric carousel shot just off frontal, its canopy rim throwing a one-point perspective toward the resting ladder over an empty cobbled foreground." />

L'Éclipse, the plate Berenice Abbott loved, makes the frieze human at last. A row of upturned faces reads as a single polyline vector below the empty Paris skyline, while the corner building's cornice recedes to a vanishing point at x 0.34, y 0.53. It is the brightest plate here, mean 0.67 with a top end of 0.95, the overexposed spring sky pulling every gaze up out of the shadowed street. The eclipse is off-frame; the composition is the crowd's collective glance and the architecture behind it, which is the whole Atget argument in one picture.

<Plate slug="eugene-atget" id="12-eclipse-avril-1912" label="L'Éclipse, avril 1912" caption="A frieze of upturned faces reads as one vector below the skyline while the corner cornice recedes to a vanishing point left of center, the eclipse itself off-frame." />

<Callout>
A word on exposure. Nothing in these captions cites an f-stop or a shutter speed, because none is recorded. Atget worked large glass plates, roughly 18 by 24 cm, in dim morning light, and the negatives carry no per-frame log; the digital files hold only scanner metadata. What the record does support is qualitative: slow plates in flat light mean small apertures for depth of field and exposures measured in seconds. That is why the streets are empty, why moving figures blur or vanish, and why the park water goes glassy and still. The evidence is in the pictures, not in a number someone would have to invent.
</Callout>

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Find the vanishing point, then hide it">
Photograph a street or corridor in deep one-point perspective and trace where your converging lines meet. Now make a second frame where the vanishing point falls outside the picture, as in the Gobelins storefront where it lands below the bottom edge. Compare how the two frames feel: a target inside the frame resolves, a target outside it pulls the eye off the stage.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Compose with a pane of glass">
Find a shop window and stand where the glass layers three things at once: the goods behind it, the street reflected across it, and the dark void between. Frame so a row of objects reads as a frieze at eye level. You are building Atget's vitrine, letting the reflection do part of the composing.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Turn a crowd into a frieze">
Photograph a group all looking the same direction and arrange their faces into a single horizontal row, as in L'Éclipse. Keep the subject that draws their gaze out of frame. The picture becomes the shared vector of attention set against whatever architecture stands behind it.
</ExerciseCard>
