Gustave Le Gray photographed the sea because his materials would not let him photograph it whole. The wet-collodion and waxed-paper negatives of the 1850s could not hold a bright sky and a dark sea in one exposure, so Le Gray exposed the two separately and rejoined them on the print, and the overlays gathered here keep finding the same habit of mind wherever he turned the camera next. A hard, nearly drawn line divides sea from sky, or a lone trunk from open clearing, or stacked masonry courses from a narrow strip of air, and each half of the frame is left to hold its own tonal register while one small, off-axis form, a sun's glow, a hollow in a trunk, a drum tower, keeps the geometry from going inert. The analyzer's own numbers trace the same discipline under very different subjects: symmetry scores that cluster high across seascape, tree, and rampart alike, horizons pinned to a small number of measured lines, and in nearly every case a frame built from a handful of large primitives rather than a crowd of small ones.

## the combination-printed horizon

Three seascapes carry the device in its purest form: a horizon fixed dead level, sea and sky each independently toned, and one small break in the symmetry to keep the eye moving.

<Plate slug="gustave-le-gray" id="01-great-wave-sete" label="The Great Wave, Sète, 1857" caption="A dead-level combination-print horizon splits the frame while the sun's glow drifts right of the mirror axis and a small anchor marks the wave breaking over the rocks." />

The Great Wave, Sète is the clearest statement of the method. The overlay's horizon sits at y equals 0.425, tilted a bare 1.1 degrees, and the analyzer confirms the same line with full confidence from both its luminance-split and gradient methods. A vertical symmetry axis runs straight up the center at x equals 0.5, and the analyzer measures a real mirror there, a vertical symmetry of 0.84, against a weaker horizontal echo of 0.69 pinned lower, at y equals 0.61, because sea and sky are two independently balanced planes rather than mirrors of each other. What breaks the calm sits off that axis: an ellipse rings the sun's diffused glow at x equals 0.55, and a small anchor at x equals 0.88, y equals 0.83 marks the wave breaking over the foreground rocks, close to the analyzer's own raw saliency peak at x equals 0.945, y equals 0.834. Tonally the print runs a full middle register, a mean of 0.47 with a wide reach from a p05 of 0.18 up to a p95 of 0.86, the palette moving from a warm brown, #463532, up to a pale #e6ded0. The overlay's own best-fitting grid is phi, scoring 0.28 against thirds at 0.10, the golden section rather than the plainer thirds division is what actually organizes this horizon.

<Plate slug="gustave-le-gray" id="03-mediterranean-mount-agde" label="Mediterranean with Mount Agde, 1857" caption="A dead-level horizon holds flat sea against flat sky, broken only by Mount Agde's low silhouette and a nearer rocky headland crowding the right edge." />

Mediterranean with Mount Agde takes the same formula and empties it further. The horizon sits lower here, at y equals 0.637, and the frame holds almost nothing else: a flattened ellipse just 0.068 wide and 0.016 tall marks Mount Agde's low silhouette at x equals 0.646, and a short polyline traces a nearer headland at the frame's right edge. The vertical symmetry axis sits right of center, at x equals 0.625, matching the analyzer's own strongest mirror correlation of 0.87 on very nearly that same line, an asymmetry the flat sea and sky can absorb because there is so little else in the frame to unbalance. Tonally this is the darkest of the three combination prints, a mean of just 0.38 with p05 down at 0.13 and p95 at 0.73, the palette led by a near-black #312d25. Phi again edges out thirds as the better-fitting grid, 0.375 to 0.333, a narrower margin than in the Great Wave but a margin still.

<Plate slug="gustave-le-gray" id="04-french-english-fleets-cherbourg" label="The French and English Fleets, Cherbourg, August 1858" caption="A scattered fleet strings along an uneven horizon while a hand-placed anchor holds the eye on the central three-decker rather than the empty glare the raw saliency signal actually finds." />

The French and English Fleets, Cherbourg scales the formula up. The horizon tilts more sharply, at minus 3.8 degrees, sitting at y equals 0.565 by both the overlay and the analyzer's own measurement, and a seven-point polyline threads the fleet along that uneven line instead of a still coastal edge. The overlay's subject anchor sits on the central three-decker at x equals 0.411, a hand-placed choice: the analyzer's own raw saliency signal actually peaks in an empty corner of the frame, at x equals 0.086, y equals 0.99, a patch of bright water with nothing to look at, so the overlay overrides it to hold the eye on the ship that matters. Vertical symmetry measures 0.99 here, on an axis at dead center, x equals 0.5, and the tonal range is the widest of the three seascapes, a mean of 0.61 reaching from p05 at 0.19 up to p95 at 0.89, the palette warm through #ceb697 to a near-black #3f2e28. Phi is again the favored grid, at 0.25 against a thirds score of just 0.07.

## the single negative, same discipline

Not every seascape here needed two negatives. Two more marine pictures, one a private study and one a court commission, win the same reductive calm from a single exposure and a level horizon alone.

<Plate slug="gustave-le-gray" id="02-brig-on-water" label="Brig on the Water, 1856" caption="A single becalmed brig, reduced to pure silhouette, sits just under a horizon on the lower third, isolated by the sea's own reflected glare rather than a second negative." />

Brig on the Water proves Le Gray did not need combination printing to win the same effect. The horizon here is level, at y equals 0.665, and the composition rests on one small ellipse, just 0.034 by 0.028, ringing the becalmed brig in silhouette at x equals 0.578. A five-point polyline traces the sea's reflected glare running out toward the right edge. The analyzer's own raw saliency signal ignores the ship almost entirely, its peak landing in the extreme corner at x equals 0.99, y equals 0.99, the brightest patch of glare rather than the vessel, so the overlay's choice to anchor on the brig itself is a judgment about what the eye wants, not a readout of where the pixels are brightest. Tonally this is a narrower, calmer print than the combination prints, a mean of 0.46 with the tightest standard deviation of any seascape in the chapter, 0.17, running from p05 at 0.22 to p95 at 0.77, the palette earthbound, #523b33 through a pale #d6c7b0. Thirds, not phi, is the best-fitting grid here, 0.44 against 0.27, the single ellipse sitting nearer a lower-third intersection than a golden section.

<Plate slug="gustave-le-gray" id="05-reine-hortense-yacht-havre" label="La Reine Hortense, Yacht de l'Empereur, Le Havre, 1856" caption="The hull sits on a diagonal against a level waterline while the mainmast is driven straight up the frame, a court commission handled with the same reductive marine geometry." />

La Reine Hortense answers with a court commission handled the same way. The horizon sits low, at y equals 0.742 by both the overlay and the analyzer, and two leading lines carry the geometry: the hull set on the diagonal from x equals 0.296 up to x equals 0.622, and the mainmast driven almost straight up the frame at x equals 0.495. Edge density here is the highest of any seascape in the chapter, 0.064, the rigging and reflected hull detail giving the analyzer far more to trace than the emptied seascapes do. Vertical symmetry still measures a respectable 0.88 on the frame's center axis, but the horizontal echo pulls well off center, to y equals 0.375, the yacht's mass sitting high rather than splitting the frame evenly. Tonally it is a warm, bright print, a mean of 0.56 with a palette running from a pale #e5cdab down to a near-black #34251f. Neither grid scores well against this frame, thirds at 0.23 and phi at a flat 0.0, which is why the overlay drops the grid altogether: the diagonal hull and the vertical mast supply enough geometry on their own.

## the solitary tree, three ways

Away from the coast, Le Gray applied the identical figure-against-void logic to a single organic subject, three times, across two negative processes and two different visual registers.

<Plate slug="gustave-le-gray" id="06-tree-study-fontainebleau" label="Tree Study, Forest of Fontainebleau, ca. 1856" caption="A solitary tree stands ringed by open clearing rather than forest clutter, its trunk driven straight up the frame and its branches converging on the analyzer's own measured vanishing point." />

Tree Study isolates a single organic subject the way the seascapes isolate a horizon. An ellipse 0.155 by 0.32 rings the tree's crown and trunk near the frame's center, at x equals 0.53, and a vertical leading line runs the trunk itself straight up from the base to y equals 0.63. The analyzer independently finds a vanishing point where the branches converge, at x equals 0.592, y equals 0.464, built with full confidence from ten separate converging lines, and the overlay's own diagonal, tracing the soft backdrop receding from the upper left, points at exactly that convergence. The horizon sits pinned near the very top of the frame, at y equals 0.117, so nearly the whole picture is tree and clearing rather than sky, an inversion of the seascape's even split. Tonally it is a middling print, a mean of 0.40, but the busiest of the tree studies by edge density, 0.11, the foliage giving the analyzer plenty to trace even against open ground. Symmetry is telling here too: horizontal symmetry measures a high 0.92 while vertical symmetry falls to 0.59, since the canopy echoes itself top to bottom far more than the tree's asymmetric branching mirrors left to right. Phi is again the better-fitting grid, 0.16 against thirds at 0.085.

<Plate slug="gustave-le-gray" id="07-oak-tree-rocks-fontainebleau" label="Oak Tree and Rocks, Forest of Fontainebleau, 1849-52" caption="A forked oak radiates branches to every edge while twin boulders seal the base, the frame packed solid edge to edge with barely a gap of sky, Le Gray's earliest and densest manner." />

Oak Tree and Rocks runs the same figure-against-void logic in reverse, and it is worth reading directly against Tree Study, since it is Le Gray's earliest surviving manner, a waxed-paper negative of 1849 to 1852, rather than the wet-collodion plates that dominate the rest of this chapter. Here the vanishing point where the trunk forks and the branches radiate sits at x equals 0.497, y equals 0.471, drawn from thirteen converging lines, the most of any plate in the set, and a leading line rides one branch clear to the upper frame edge, from x equals 0.309 up to x equals 0.714. Two subject anchors mark the frame's only openings, a gap of sky at x equals 0.555, y equals 0.271 and a lichen-mottled boulder sealing the base at x equals 0.724, y equals 0.789, both landing almost exactly on the analyzer's own two strongest saliency regions. The frame around those two small openings is otherwise packed solid: edge density measures 0.18, the busiest of any plate in this chapter, and vertical symmetry drops to 0.56, the lowest measured here, since there is no calm mirror to find in a tangle of forked branches and boulders. The palette's fifth swatch, a near-pure black at #040303, is the paper negative's own harder register, and that same hex value recurs in only one other plate in this chapter, the Ramparts of Carcassonne, photographed on the same process within two years of this one.

<Plate slug="gustave-le-gray" id="08-hollow-oak-tree-fontainebleau" label="Hollow Oak Tree, Fontainebleau, 1855-57" caption="The ancient oak is planted dead center on the vertical axis, its trunk read as a single sculptural mass with the hollow that names it sitting as a dark aperture at its base." />

Hollow Oak Tree closes the sequence by making the tree the whole architecture of the frame. The overlay's symmetry axis sits at dead center, x equals 0.5, matching the analyzer's own measured vertical-symmetry axis exactly, though the correlation score there, 0.73, is more modest than the placement implies, since a single lit, off-center trunk shape does not produce a true mirror the way flat sea and sky do. An ellipse 0.125 by 0.195 traces the trunk's sculptural mass, and a small frame-in-frame rectangle picks out the hollow itself, the cavity the title names, low in that mass. The analyzer finds a vanishing point too, at x equals 0.659, y equals 0.397, from nine converging lines in the branches overhead, but the overlay does not use it, the composition is built on the trunk's frontal bulk rather than on depth. Tonally the print runs a wide middle register, a mean of 0.44 with edge density at 0.16, second only to Oak Tree and Rocks for texture, and a palette running from a deep #523a30 up to a pale #e1d5c1.

## the leading line and the banded wall

Depth and structure, rather than a level horizon, organize the next pair: a path pulling the eye into the wood, and a fortress wall read as pure stacked geometry.

<Plate slug="gustave-le-gray" id="09-fontainebleau-sandy-path" label="Chemin Sablonneux Montant, Forest of Fontainebleau, ca. 1856" caption="The sandy path climbs as a single leading line into the wood, framed by an arching canopy above and a storm-broken oak fork blocking the near approach." />

Chemin sablonneux montant, the sandy path climbing, turns the same forest into pure depth. A four-point leading line climbs the path itself from the frame's bottom edge up to x equals 0.58, y equals 0.71, and a seven-point polyline arches the canopy overhead from one side of the frame to the other. The analyzer finds its own vanishing point off to the right, at x equals 0.844, y equals 0.391, built from six converging lines that trace almost exactly the canopy's own upper arch, confirming that the trees framing the path are doing real perspectival work rather than simple decoration. A storm-broken oak fork, rendered as a narrow ellipse at x equals 0.44, y equals 0.50, blocks the path's near approach and forces the eye up and along the receding line instead of straight through. Horizontal symmetry measures a high 0.906 here, the arching canopy nearly mirroring itself top to bottom, while vertical symmetry sits lower, at 0.66. It is also the darkest print among the Fontainebleau studies, a mean of just 0.35, with the widest edge density of any of them, 0.14, sand, bark, and canopy all giving the analyzer plenty of texture to read.

<Plate slug="gustave-le-gray" id="10-ramparts-of-carcassonne" label="The Ramparts of Carcassonne, 1851" caption="The curtain wall is read frontally as stacked horizontal courses of stone climaxing in the drum tower's rounded mass, the same planar, banded geometry Le Gray built his seascapes from." />

The Ramparts of Carcassonne, made on the same waxed-paper process as Oak Tree and Rocks during Le Gray's 1851 Mission Héliographique survey, reads the fortified wall as pure stacked geometry. An eight-point leading line climbs the skyline from the lower left up to the great tower, and two shorter leading lines mark the coping courses of the inner and outer walls running level across the frame. A tall ellipse, 0.125 by 0.30, rings the drum tower's rounded mass at x equals 0.73. The horizon sits very high, at y equals 0.198 by the analyzer's own gradient reading, so the sky occupies barely a fifth of the frame and the stone courses do the rest of the work; neither the thirds nor the phi grid scores above zero against this composition, which is why the overlay drops the grid entirely and lets the wall's own coursing supply the structure instead. Tonally it is the widest-ranging print in the chapter, a standard deviation of 0.29 and a p95 of 0.92, the brightest highlight measured anywhere in these twelve plates, stone catching hard sun above deep masonry shadow. And its palette carries the same near-black #040303 found in Oak Tree and Rocks, the two paper-negative plates in this chapter sharing, down to the hex value, the harshest black either process produced.

## the raw material and the marching rank

The chapter closes with the technique's own raw material and its one true exception: a cloud study banked for future combination printing, and the single composition here built on depth through repetition rather than a still horizon.

<Plate slug="gustave-le-gray" id="11-cloud-study" label="Étude de Nuages (Cloud Study), 1856-57" caption="The cloud's own jagged mass anchors the frame at its sunlit breach, with light falling straight down to a glint on the water below, exactly the raw material Le Gray banked for combination printing." />

Étude de nuages is the working material behind the seascapes, shown here as a composition in its own right. A jagged polyline traces the cumulus mass across the upper frame, and the subject anchor sits precisely on the sunlit breach in the cloud, at x equals 0.632, y equals 0.447, exactly the analyzer's own raw saliency peak at those same coordinates, the one plate in this chapter where the overlay's chosen anchor and the raw signal agree to the pixel. A leading line then carries that light straight down the frame to a glint on the water, through three more points to x equals 0.688, y equals 0.878. The horizon sits high, at y equals 0.256, so the cloud itself, not the sea, occupies most of the picture. Tonally it is a print of real contrast, a standard deviation of 0.26, second only to the ramparts and the cavalry scene in this chapter, and its palette carries one swatch unlike anything else in these twelve plates, a near-neutral gray, #c8c6c1, among four otherwise warm, sepia-toned browns and tans, the one place in the chapter where cloud vapor rather than toned paper sets the color. Thirds is the marginally better-fitting grid, 0.44 to phi's 0.40, the closest contest of any plate here.

<Plate slug="gustave-le-gray" id="12-cavalry-camp-chalons" label="Cavalry Maneuvers, Camp de Châlons, 1857" caption="Mounted ranks diminish by scale and haze rather than a vanishing point, receding from a hand-placed anchor on the massed near rank toward the far horizon." />

Cavalry Maneuvers, Camp de Châlons closes the chapter on the one composition built from repetition rather than a still horizon read as a symmetry device. The horizon still sits where it always does in this chapter, level at y equals 0.575, tilted a single degree, but the actual subject is the leading line of massed ranks, receding from a near anchor at x equals 0.797 to x equals 0.205 in the haze, four points marking riders that diminish by scale rather than converge on a vanishing point the analyzer can lock onto; none is reported here, depth is read from size and haze alone. Vertical symmetry measures 0.992, tied with the Cherbourg fleet picture for the highest in the chapter, on the frame's dead-center axis, even though nothing here is a literal mirror, just the evenly spaced ranks balancing left and right. It is also the flattest print by texture of anything in this chapter, an edge density of just 0.004, the lowest measured here, consistent with a flat parade ground read through haze, and it carries the widest tonal spread of any plate, a standard deviation of 0.32, from a p05 of 0.16 up to a p95 of 0.90. As with the Cherbourg fleet, the analyzer's own raw saliency signal lands on an empty corner of bright sky, at x equals 0.992, y equals 0.99, so the overlay again overrides it, anchoring instead on the massed near rank where the actual subject sits.

<Callout>
A word on exposure and process. No aperture, shutter speed, or plate speed survives for any of these twelve images, and none is invented here. Both processes at work in this chapter, the waxed paper Le Gray refined around 1851 (Oak Tree and Rocks and the Ramparts of Carcassonne) and the wet-collodion glass plate he adopted by the mid-1850s (every other plate here), required exposures of several seconds to tens of seconds on a leveled stand camera, long enough for the deep fields in the landscape and architectural plates to read sharp front to back, but slow enough that the galloping cavalry at Châlons would have genuinely blurred at any faster gait than these ranks show. The seascapes' hard, independently toned horizon is not incidental: a single collodion exposure could not hold both a lit sky and a dark sea, so Le Gray exposed sea and sky separately, sometimes from the same scene and sometimes, as with the cloud study in this chapter, pairing a genuine seascape negative with a stock cloud negative shot elsewhere, then printed both onto one sheet with a mask at the horizon. All twelve prints are held by the Metropolitan Museum of Art and released under its Open Access, CC0 policy, and this chapter follows the Met's own dating and attribution throughout.
</Callout>

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Expose the sky and the ground separately">
Photograph a scene with both a bright sky and a darker foreground, a shoreline, a field, a street, twice: one frame metered for the sky, one metered for the ground. Blend the two behind a hard horizon line in post, the way Le Gray masked two negatives together at the seam. Compare the blended print against a single, compromise exposure of the same scene, and notice how much more the sky and the ground can each say once neither is compressed to protect the other.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Isolate one form against open ground">
Find a single tree, post, or other solitary vertical form and photograph it with as much open sky, field, or clearing around it as you can manage, the way Tree Study and Hollow Oak Tree let a lone trunk stand against emptied ground instead of forest clutter. Shoot it once with a busy background left in and once with the background cleared, and compare which frame actually reads as the subject.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Build depth from repetition, not a vanishing point">
Photograph a row of people, vehicles, or objects strung out away from the camera, close ones large and near, far ones small and hazy, without relying on a single converging vanishing point to carry the depth, the way the cavalry ranks at Châlons recede by scale and haze alone. Then, separately, photograph a wall, fence, or facade square on and read it as stacked horizontal bands, as in the Ramparts of Carcassonne. Compare the two ways of building depth in a frame, one through diminishing repetition, the other through frontal, banded flatness.
</ExerciseCard>
