James Casebere photographs constructed rooms at the point where their material certainty becomes unreliable. These eleven views from 2000–03 offer no bodies to scale the spaces, but every door, arch, stair, and window is made to do that work. The overlays describe a repeated system: a waterline interrupts an architectural elevation, a threshold promises access, and reflection turns the room into a second, less stable model. The analyzer finds relatively sparse edge fields in the tunnels and rooms: only 0.011 in *Tunnel #2* and 0.013 in *Dorm Room*, so small bright boundaries carry the pictures' structure. ## Water turns an interior into a diagram

<Plate slug="james-casebere" id="01-pink-hallway-3" label="Pink Hallway #3, 2000" caption="A centered doorway and a low waterline turn a rose-colored passage into a vertical double." />

*Pink Hallway #3* is not simply symmetrical: it makes symmetry feel precarious. The overlay's axial doorway and inner threshold hold the upper room together, while the waterline at y=0.721 releases that order into a wavering reflection. The analyzer's vertical symmetry measure is 0.845, but the palette moves from near-black #060302 through muted pink-gray #9e9093 to #dbc7d2. The organized doorway has to compete with an animated, darker lower half.

<Plate slug="james-casebere" id="02-nevisian-underground-1" label="Nevisian Underground #1, 2001" caption="A receding arch and a tilted waterline hold a flooded subterranean chamber between lateral openings." />

In *Nevisian Underground #1*, the arch is a device for withholding rather than admitting. The overlay frames the receding arch and marks the low waterline, which tilts about four degrees rather than sitting as a clean horizontal. The strongest tonal values are concentrated around a dark brown-black base (#060100, #361b06) and a pale upper range (#c5b1ab); the mean luminance remains only 0.222. Reflection does not open the chamber. It makes the architectural floor feel like another opaque ceiling.

<Plate slug="james-casebere" id="03-monticello-1" label="Monticello #1, 2001" caption="Three arched window bays cross a measured waterline, with their tall white divisions restated below." />

*Monticello #1* uses repetition more openly. A window-bay axis at x=0.512 and the arched-window enclosure are laid over a reflection whose waterline lands at y=0.595. The analyzer measures a wide tonal span from p05 0.004 to p95 0.816, and its blue-gray palette (#313f4a, #626e80, #c9c6db) makes the white trim look coldly constructed. The reflection doubles the bays but breaks their straight geometry into a liquid counter-rhythm.

<Plate slug="james-casebere" id="04-monticello-3" label="Monticello #3, 2001" caption="An oblique run of arched windows meets a broken reflective contour and a bright door at the edge." />

The companion *Monticello #3* abandons the frontal balance. The overlay names the window rhythm, then follows the reflected edge as it rises through the water toward the bright far door. Its color range remains restrained: deep #060d0b and #293339 against pale #b8c3e0, and its edge density is only 0.064. That restraint gives the diagonal reflected contour unusual force: one shallow physical room reads as though it has been pushed sideways.

## Stairs and apertures redirect the eye

<Plate slug="james-casebere" id="05-yellow-hallway-2" label="Yellow Hallway #2, 2001" caption="A tilted door interrupts a centered opening; rippled water disperses the room's yellow planes." />

*Yellow Hallway #2* makes the central opening look reliable only long enough for the tilted door to spoil it. The overlay pairs the central vertical division with that tilted inner frame and a waterline at y=0.558. Olive-black #140f01 and yellow-brown #3f370b carry the shadowed walls, while #cfcdca and the water's pale highlights keep rising from the floor. The picture's phi grid best fit suits this imbalance: its order is lateral, not perfectly centered.

<Plate slug="james-casebere" id="06-green-staircase-1" label="Green Staircase #1, 2002" caption="A stair rail climbs from the flooded foreground toward the crossing of stair and door geometry." />

The stair in *Green Staircase #1* gives the water a direct opponent. A rail runs from the lower left to the high landing, while the analyzer's five-line convergence falls at x=0.678, y=0.530, by the door-and-stair nexus. This is the chapter's busiest structural plate, with edge density 0.082 and a p05–p95 range of 0.110–0.788. Pale violet-gray #cec7d7 and deep green-black #282b20 hold its two conditions together: hard risers point upward as the floor undoes them.

<Plate slug="james-casebere" id="07-green-staircase-4" label="Green Staircase #4, 2003" caption="A diagonal stair edge cuts across an arched doorway and a broad curving soffit." />

*Green Staircase #4* reverses that exchange. Here the stair's edge climbs from the flooded foreground toward the right, and the large arch behind it becomes an enclosing counter-curve. The overlay calls the shallow upper boundary a curved soffit rather than a horizon: it is an architectural edge, not a distant landscape. At mean luminance 0.248 and edge density 0.038, the image remains quiet enough that the stair reads as one decisive incision through a green-black enclosure.

<Plate slug="james-casebere" id="08-dorm-room" label="Dorm Room, 2003" caption="A pair of high windows and their cast shadows compress a dark room into a shallow trapezoid." />

*Dorm Room* strips the earlier rooms down to nearly nothing. The window band at y=0.385 cuts a dark interior, the window pair supplies its only framed opening, and the analyzer gives the field a near-minimal edge density of 0.013. The palette runs from #0b100a to a nearly white #f6f2f2. The bright windows do not describe a view; their projected rails and dark lower wall make light itself the room's primary occupant.

<Plate slug="james-casebere" id="09-turning-hallway" label="Turning Hallway, 2003" caption="A stepped landing turns toward a gridded window held inside a deep, monochrome corridor." />

In *Turning Hallway*, the titled turn is the picture's argument. The overlay's corridor axis is measured at x=0.475, but the route is checked by the step landing and redirected toward the narrow window exit. The source image is nearly achromatic, from #131313 through #d3d3d3, with a mean luminance of 0.236. The empty floor makes the camera's path legible, while the window is too remote and too high to function as a simple release.

## Nested darkness, staged light

<Plate slug="james-casebere" id="10-tunnel-2" label="Tunnel #2, 2003" caption="A lateral wedge of light and repeated lit arch edges describe a narrowing approach to a dark terminus." />

*Tunnel #2* is Casebere's most stripped-down instance of the nested frame. The overlay encloses the dark terminus, traces the lit arch edges at right, and identifies the lateral wedge from the high opening. Its edge density is just 0.011, so the rim lights matter more than surface texture; #1d1507, #3d3118, and #d0c7c0 move from earth-darkness to a small illuminated permission. The repeated arches do not produce a measured vanishing-point claim here: their visual pull is persuasive, but the analyzer's low-confidence candidate does not agree with the actual doorway the eye follows.

<Plate slug="james-casebere" id="12-spanish-bath-vertical" label="Spanish Bath (Vertical), 2003" caption="A central arch, inner chamber, and waterline stack a tall bath into receding reflective zones." />

*Spanish Bath (Vertical)* closes the group by making reflection vertical rather than merely deep. The central arch axis at x=0.575, inner chamber, and waterline at y=0.562 layer a bright lamp, niche, pool, and reflected lamp into a single narrow progression. Warm #392b1c and #5f5344 are interrupted by blue-white #cdd3d8 at the side opening; the palette keeps the spatial promise divided between interior glow and an unreachable exterior. The room is a model, but the repetition of its arch and light is what makes it behave like a remembered place.

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Let reflection contradict structure">
Build or find a simple doorway, window row, or stair with a reflective surface below it. Make one frame in which the reflection merely repeats the room, then move the camera or disturb the surface until the repeated geometry becomes less certain than the built geometry. Keep the version in which the reflection changes the space rather than decorating it.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Make an exit withhold release">
Photograph a passage with a visible door, window, or lit opening. Arrange the foreground edge, a step, or a turn so that it redirects the eye before it arrives at that opening. Do not add a person; make the route itself create the sense of access or refusal.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Use one bright edge as a ruler">
In a dark room or tunnel, reduce the scene until one band of light or one illuminated contour carries the depth. Check whether the edge names a real plane, arch, or turn. If it cannot be named, simplify until the light describes the space rather than merely dramatizing it.
</ExerciseCard>
