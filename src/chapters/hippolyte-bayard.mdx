Hippolyte Bayard made photographs for barely a dozen years, and in almost every one of them he reaches for the same two tools. When he wants a mirror, he builds one: a body, a shelf of plaster casts, or a monument column planted on the frame's own vertical axis so the picture folds cleanly in half. When there is nothing to mirror, he flattens instead, laying a level horizon across the frame and letting atmospheric gray do the work that converging lines do for other photographers. The overlays in this chapter keep finding those two moves and almost nothing else. Even his one deliberate fiction, a self-portrait staged as a corpse, is built on the same frontal, symmetrical discipline he later gave to fountains and columns, which is exactly why the invention reads as documentary fact.

## the staged tableau

Bayard's earliest and strangest picture is also his most theatrical. Self-Portrait as a Drowned Man restages his own body as evidence, and the overlay reads the props as carefully as a stage manager would: an ellipse 0.065 by 0.085 rings the head lolled onto the shoulder at the frame's right, and a second, larger ellipse, 0.115 by 0.13, marks the hat set aside at left like a grave marker. A polyline traces the drape falling in a diagonal behind the pose, from a low point at (0.40, 0.63) up to (0.79, 0.19), the one line in the frame that is not dead level or dead vertical. The analyzer's own numbers back the staging: vertical symmetry measures 0.94 on an axis at dead center, x equals 0.5, and the print is bright and almost shadowless, a mean of 0.78 within a tight standard deviation of 0.076, the shadow value never sinking below a p05 of 0.69 while the highlights clip to a full white p95 of 1.0. Edge density measures just 0.0025, the lowest of any plate in this chapter. That flatness of light is the whole trick: nothing in the frame casts the kind of hard, particular shadow that would mark a live sitter holding a pose, so the fiction of the corpse has no visible seam.

Still Life with Statuary stages people out of plaster instead of flesh. A frame within the frame, 0.76 wide by 0.75 tall, marks the parted curtain that turns a shelf of casts into a proscenium, and inside it the overlay's symmetry axis sits at x equals 0.5, exactly where a portrait sitter's own axis would fall. The tall standing ellipse at the center, 0.11 wide by 0.27 high, is the Venus de Milo, elongated to read as a figure rather than an object, while a smaller roundel above it, at x equals 0.565, y equals 0.185, marks a cupid medallion. The analyzer's own raw correlation is far gentler than the staged axis implies, a vertical symmetry of only 0.61 and a horizontal symmetry of 0.67, since the casts themselves sit at different heights and depths on their brackets rather than truly mirroring each other. Tonally this is the widest-ranging print in the group, a mean of 0.54 but a standard deviation of 0.23, running from a p05 of 0.22 up to a p95 of 0.85, and the analyzer's own strongest saliency spike, at x equals 0.54, y equals 0.21, lands almost exactly on that cupid roundel, the one bright, human-scaled face in a frame otherwise built from cool plaster.

Self-Portrait in the Garden moves the staging outdoors and off-axis. The analyzer's own symmetry axis shifts right here, to x equals 0.5875, at a measured vertical symmetry of 0.82, because Bayard's figure, ringed by a small ellipse at x equals 0.595, y equals 0.19, stands well right of the frame's true center. He rests on a barrel, marked by a wide, flattened ellipse at y equals 0.503, while a leading line traces the ladder's diagonal from (0.093, 0.52) up to (0.214, 0.104), a line the analyzer independently confirms: it finds a vanishing point at x equals 0.159, y equals 0.714 built from four converging diagonals, almost exactly where that ladder line points. A small frame within a frame, just 0.057 by 0.058, isolates one cell of the trellis lattice behind him. ## the oval mask and the vertical axis

Four plates put architecture through the same drill Bayard gave his own body: plant it on the vertical axis, let it fill the frame, and let a soft oval vignette, the era's standard printing mask, echo that symmetry at the edges. Construction Worker, Paris sets the device against a rare piece of social observation. A shuttered window sits on a symmetry axis at x equals 0.5, its shadowed lintel marked by a horizon line at y equals 0.127, and the analyzer confirms both numbers closely: vertical symmetry of 0.92 on that same axis, and a horizon confidence of 0.99 at y equals 0.127. Below it, a barred window frames a grid within the grid, 0.31 wide by 0.32 tall, and an ellipse at x equals 0.285, y equals 0.765 marks the worker himself, standing on a low scaffold. The analyzer's own raw saliency spike, tellingly, sits nowhere near him, at the frame's far right edge, x equals 0.989, on a patch of bright stone rather than the man doing the labor; the overlay places its real anchor on the worker instead, since he is the documentary subject the picture is actually about. Tonally the print runs a mean of 0.56 with a standard deviation of 0.19, p05 at 0.23 and p95 at 0.78.

Cirque National, Paris gives the vertical axis to a whole rotunda. A wide, low ellipse, 0.33 by 0.18, traces the domed drum sitting on a symmetry axis at x equals 0.5, matched by the analyzer's measured vertical symmetry of 0.87 on that line, so the round architecture and the round vignette mount share one radial symmetry. The overlay hand-places its subject anchor at x equals 0.325, y equals 0.631, on the porticoed entrance, well away from the analyzer's own raw saliency peak at the frame's far left edge, y equals 0.336. The horizon reads at y equals 0.779 with full confidence, and the print sits at a mean of 0.67 with std 0.15, p05 at 0.35 and p95 at 0.81.

Column of barriere du Trone is the purest statement of the whole group: nothing but a single fluted shaft, planted dead center. The overlay's symmetry axis sits at x equals 0.513, matched almost exactly by the analyzer's own measured 0.85, and a small ellipse, 0.045 by 0.05, rings the statue crowning the capital at y equals 0.112, nearly touching the top edge. A horizon line at y equals 0.785, confirmed by the analyzer at full confidence on that same value, marks the gatehouse cornice, and a wide frame within the frame, 0.40 by 0.125, isolates the twin-pedimented gatehouse at the column's foot, echoing the shaft's own mirror symmetry at ground level. Tonally this is one of the quietest prints in the chapter, a mean of 0.72 with a tight standard deviation of just 0.11 and an edge density of 0.011, second lowest in the set. The analyzer's own raw saliency spike falls to the bottom left corner of the frame, near the pavement, not the capital, but the geometry the overlay measures runs the opposite way, straight up the shaft to the statue at the top.

## flattened perspective

Where Bayard has no vignette and no single body to mirror, he reaches for the opposite move: a dead-level horizon and an even, atmospheric tone that flattens deep space into bands. Windmills, Montmartre is the clearest case and also the most symmetric print in the entire chapter. Two ellipses, each about 0.06 wide by 0.10 tall, mark the windmill silhouettes bracketing the frame at x equals 0.393 and x equals 0.701, and a gently curved polyline holds the rooftops level between them. The analyzer's own vertical symmetry measures 0.97 on an axis at x equals 0.513, the single highest score anywhere in this chapter, and the horizon sits at y equals 0.88 with full confidence, pushed low so the pale sky dominates. It is also the brightest print here, a mean of 0.83 with p05 at 0.63 and p95 at 0.96, an airy tonal range consistent with the soft, low-contrast rendering of Bayard's own direct-positive process, and edge density measures a mere 0.0048, the town reduced to dark silhouette beneath an almost featureless sky.

<Plate slug="hippolyte-bayard" id="02-windmills-montmartre" label="Windmills, Montmartre, 1839" caption="Two windmill silhouettes bracket a level rooftop line, the highest vertical symmetry score in the chapter at 0.97, banding the built town dark beneath a vast pale sky." />

Panorama of Paris with Louvre and Tuileries in Foreground pushes the same idea to an extreme format, an aspect ratio of 3.33, wider than any other plate here. A long polyline of fourteen points traces rooftops, a dome and spires wandering along one nearly level line, broken only by a small ellipse marking the Louvre's turreted silhouette at x equals 0.48, y equals 0.53. Below it, a wide frame within the frame, 0.94 by 0.26, isolates the flat, dark Tuileries foreground, a mass with almost no internal detail. The analyzer measures a horizon at y equals 0.114 with full confidence, a vertical symmetry of 0.95, and one of the tightest tonal spreads in the whole chapter, a standard deviation of just 0.083, behind only the drowned-man self-portrait's 0.076. Mean tone sits at 0.70, p05 at 0.53 and p95 at 0.83, and edge density measures only 0.012. There is no vanishing point in this picture, by design or by measurement: the analyzer finds none, because depth here is built entirely by that narrow, even tonal recession rather than by any converging line.

Rue Royale is the one plate in this chapter that reads as true depth rather than a flattened band. Two leading lines, the receding roofline and the shopfront cornice below it, run five points apiece from the upper left down toward the lower right, sloping together toward a vanishing point the overlay implies just beyond the frame's right edge. A small frame within the frame marks an arched shop entrance at x equals 0.373, y equals 0.653, and a subject anchor at x equals 0.069, y equals 0.655 calls out the painted "DENTISTE" signage. The analyzer's own vertical symmetry measures only 0.72 here, on an axis pulled well off center to x equals 0.613, the weakest mirror match of the three flattened-perspective plates in this section, which is exactly what a raking street view rather than a frontal facade should produce. Tellingly, the analyzer's own line detector finds only horizontals and verticals in this frame, no diagonals at all, so it registers no measured vanishing point even though the eye reads one easily in the raking lines the overlay traces by hand. Edge density measures 0.035, busier than either of the other two flattened views, the repeating window bays and hand-painted signs filling the frame with incident that the tonal-recession plates keep bare.

<Callout>
A word on process and honesty. The two earliest plates here, the drowned-man self-portrait and the Montmartre windmills, are direct-positive prints: there was no negative, so each is a unique, unrepeatable object, and the softer, lower-contrast look you can see in their tonal numbers, especially the drowned man's shadowless mean of 0.78, comes from that chemistry rather than from any darkroom choice. The remaining eight plates are calotype paper negatives contact-printed onto salted paper, with no albumen coating, giving their matte, ink-like surface. No aperture, shutter speed, or sensitivity survives for any of the ten images: there was no light meter and no shutter beyond a lens cap held open by hand, and stand-camera exposures of this era are understood only qualitatively, from several seconds to well over a minute. That is consistent with everything the overlays measure here, static tableaux and dead-level architecture, held still long enough for the paper to catch it, but no specific number is invented where none survives.
</Callout>

## Exercises

<ExerciseCard n="01" title="Stage a fiction">
Build a small tableau around your own body: a prop set aside like evidence, a pose held longer than a snapshot would need, lit as flat and even as you can manage so no single hard shadow gives away the staging. Aim for the kind of frontal calm Bayard used to turn a corpse pose into believable documentary fact.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="02" title="Double the mask">
Photograph a symmetrical subject, a building facade, a monument, a fountain, straight on so its own architecture falls on the frame's vertical center. Then crop or vignette the print into an oval or circle centered on that same axis, so the mask repeats the subject's own mirror symmetry instead of fighting it, the way Bayard's oval-printed monument studies do.
</ExerciseCard>

<ExerciseCard n="03" title="Flatten with tone, not lines">
Photograph a deep scene, a street, a skyline, a row of rooftops, using a dead-level horizon and even, atmospheric light rather than a strong converging line. Push the exposure so foreground detail goes dark and featureless while the distance stays pale, and see how far tonal recession alone can carry the illusion of depth without a single vanishing point.
</ExerciseCard>
